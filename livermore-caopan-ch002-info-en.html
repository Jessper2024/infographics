S SituationStocks, like people, have their own character. When a stock enters a clear trend, it moves automatically — rising on large volume, retracing on reduced volume. This is the market's natural breathing.
C ComplicationMost speculators trade every day, fixating on minor fluctuations while missing major moves. Human weaknesses — wishful thinking and hesitation — prevent them from exiting when they should.
Q QuestionHow can one decide trading timing with objective rules rather than emotions — knowing when to wait patiently and when to exit decisively?

01
Trend Rhythm

The Natural Breathing of Trends: Normal Retracements & Volume

Trends have an intrinsic rhythm — rising on volume, retracing on lighter volume, then accelerating to new highs. Never fear normal retracements, but be extremely wary of abnormal ones.

Livermore illustrated with a stock starting at $50: Phase 1 rises to $54, normal retracement to $52½; Phase 2 accelerates to $59–60, intraday pullbacks of only 1–1.5 points; Phase 3 leaps from $60 straight to $68–70 with barely any retracement — the stock becomes "scarce." Only then does a larger 5-point retracement occur. Volume shrinks from enormous → moderate → thin — the hallmark of a healthy trend.

🌊 Phase 1
50→54, retrace to 52½
Enormous volume
Perfectly normal
⚡ Phase 2
54→60, 1~1.5 pt pullbacks
Moderate volume
Stock becomes scarce
🔥 Phase 3
60→70, barely pulls back
Thin volume
Trend accelerates to climax

In a clear trend, each normal retracement should fall along the same trend line. The gap from one high to the next widens over time — this is where the time element enters the picture.

02
Danger Signal

Abnormal Retracements: The Market Is Flashing Red

When the market makes a new high and then reverses 6+ points within the same day — something that has never happened before in this move — it is flashing a danger signal. Never ignore it.

Livermore described the classic danger-signal scene: the stock surges 6–10 points on extreme activity, but in the final hour, an abnormal 7–8 point break appears. The next morning it slips another point, then rallies to a firm close. But on the third day, it fails to hold its gains. This is an imminent danger signal.

"When I see a danger signal handed to me by the market, I don't argue with it. I get out! A few days later, if everything looks all right, I can always get back in again."

✅ Normal Retracement

Occurs naturally within the trend
Volume far below rally volume
Moderate, falls along trend line
No need to fear — stay patient

⚠️ Abnormal Retracement

New high then 6+ pt intraday reversal
Completely unexpected
Never before seen in this move
Exit decisively — don't hesitate

Livermore used the train analogy: if you're walking down a railroad track and an express train barrels toward you at 60 mph, you jump off — you don't stand there. Once it passes, you can step back anytime. No rule is 100% accurate, but consistently heeding danger signals pays enormously over the long run.

03
Human Weakness

Wishful Thinking & Hesitation: Your Worst Enemy Is Yourself

Wishful thinking must be eliminated entirely. Human weakness — hesitation and the gambling instinct — is every speculator's greatest enemy, more destructive than any market risk.

Livermore painted the classic psychological trap: the speculator sees a danger signal but hesitates. The market moves against him by many points. He tells himself, "I'll sell on the next rally." When the rally finally comes, he forgets his plan because "the market looks right again." Tragically, that rally is only a brief upward flicker — the market then truly declines, and he's still in, because he hesitated.

📛 The Classic Trap
"I'll sell on the next rally" → Rally comes, forget the plan → Market falls further → Helplessly trapped
🎯 The Right Approach
Follow a rule → The rule tells you when to exit → Act decisively → Save money and relieve anxiety

Livermore emphasized: after a big advance, a stock will of course rally from some level — but what makes you think it will rally exactly when you want it to? More likely, it won't. Those who treat speculation as a serious business must eradicate wishful thinking entirely.

04
Time Element

Less Is More: Sitting on Your Hands Is Part of Trading

There are only a few opportunities each year — perhaps four or five — when you may permit yourself to take a position. During the intervals, you should stand aside and let the market brew its next big move.

Livermore recounted the story of a legendary speculator living in the California mountains who received quotes three days late. Only two or three times a year would he phone his broker with orders. When asked his secret, he replied: "A real movement does not end in one day. By living far from the city, I give these movements the time they need to complete their course."

"I make speculation my business. The market is always a maze of complications. If I let myself get tangled in them, I'll be ruined — so I ignore minor movements. I like to stand aside and think in peace." — The Mountain Speculator

📝 Keep Records
Record price movements systematically; study them deeply
⏳ Factor In Time
Integrate the time element carefully; let trends run their full course
🎯 Strike Precisely
Seize only 4–5 major opportunities per year; stand aside the rest of the time

"While you are standing aside, those other speculators who feel they must trade every single day are laying the foundation for your next venture. You will profit from their mistakes." The first position should be profitable from the very start. After that, all you need to do is stay alert for danger signals.

💡
Key Takeaway

Trends have rhythm, and signals can be true or false. Hold patiently through normal retracements; exit decisively at abnormal danger signals — like jumping off the tracks before an oncoming train. Seize only 4–5 major opportunities per year. The rest of the time, stand aside, keep records, and let the time element work for you. Rules are more reliable than feelings. Discipline outlasts talent.
