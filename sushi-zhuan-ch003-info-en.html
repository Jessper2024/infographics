Core QuestionHow did a disheartened literatus banished from the capital by political infighting complete his spiritual healing and reach the pinnacle of poetic achievement among Hangzhou's landscapes?

Core AnswerIn the winter of 1071, Su Shi arrived as Vice-Prefect of Hangzhou. West Lake's four-season beauty, the Zen stillness of Gushan Temple, and the joy of literati gatherings reignited his vivacious, open-hearted nature. From visiting monks in mist-shrouded solitude to watching rainstorms from Wanghu Tower, he ultimately achieved perfect fusion of poetry and nature in "Drinking at the Lake, First Clear Then Rainy" — Hangzhou shaped Su Shi's poetic soul, and Su Shi reshaped Hangzhou's cultural DNA.

1
Nature's Healing

Landscape as Medicine for Political Wounds

The moment Su Shi arrived in Hangzhou, he "could not help but become intoxicated by the lake and mountain scenery," feeling a serenity he had not known for so long — his troubles dissolved imperceptibly among the hills and waters as pristine nature reignited his lively, open-hearted, active spirit and poetic inspiration.

Hangzhou was "bright with mountains and limpid with waters, prosperous and bustling." West Lake glittered like "a radiant pearl," while the Qiantang tidal bore surged "like ten thousand horses charging, unstoppable." Even Emperor Renzong had praised it: "With Wu Mountain's beauty, the Southeast's foremost prefecture." Su Shi even moved his desk to the lakeside, handling official business while enjoying the scenery — "his brush flew like the wind, settling all matters with effortless clarity amid laughter and conversation."

2
Visiting Monks

The Hermit's Spiritual Dialogue at Mist-Shrouded Gushan Temple

Shortly after arriving, Su Shi visited the poet-monks Huiqin and Huisi, whom Ouyang Xiu had repeatedly recommended. At Gushan Temple, "papered windows and bamboo rooms, ancient cypresses reaching to heaven," he conversed with the two monks "slapping thighs and talking endlessly, feeling deep regret they had not met sooner."

On that day "about to snow but not yet snowing," West Lake appeared "veiled in cloud and mist, pavilions and terraces appearing and disappearing like the fabled island of Penglai." Following winding mountain paths through clouds, Su Shi found transcendent stillness in the temple's seclusion. Returning home, "as if waking from a beautiful dream," he wrote "Visiting Huiqin and Huisi at Gushan on the Eighth Day of the Twelfth Month," capturing "waters clear, rocks revealed, fish can be counted; woods deep, no people, only bird calls echo" — a unique winter mountain landscape painting in verse.

"I write poetry urgently, as if chasing a fugitive — once the pure scene vanishes, it is hard to capture again." — Su Shi feared the transcendent beauty would slip away, rushing to preserve it in verse
3
Poetic Summit

West Lake Immortalized: "Lightly Adorned or Richly Groomed"

West Lake's picturesque beauty found its most perfect, most vivid expression in Su Shi's brush. "Drinking at the Lake, First Clear Then Rainy" became an immortal masterpiece through its accessible language and striking metaphor: "I compare West Lake to Xi Zi, the beauty of old — lightly adorned or richly groomed, she is always enchanting." Since then, "Xi Zi Lake" has been West Lake's poetic name.

Su Shi captured the lake's four-season spirit: summer "waters deep and mysteriously blue," autumn "golden winds bring cool, lotuses bloom in succession," winter "clouds and snow merge sky and water," spring "new cattails emerge, willows reflect upon the surface." From Wanghu Tower he watched storms: "Black clouds like spilled ink, not yet covering the hills; white rain like dancing pearls, splashing wildly into boats."

4
Literati Gatherings

Fine Scenes, Fine Company: The Joy of Hangzhou's Social Circle

With his "warmth, wit, and broad heart," Su Shi quickly integrated into Hangzhou's literati circles — "at every kind of banquet and gathering, everyone loved having him present." Surrounded by beautiful scenery, fine cuisine, and kindred spirits, he nearly attained the ancients' ideal: "fine times, beautiful scenes, joyful hearts, pleasurable affairs — the four things hard to have together."

He formed deep friendships with Prefect Chen Xiang and his successor Yang Yuansu. At Youmei Hall atop Phoenix Hill, "amidst the clinking of wine cups, suddenly a thunderclap shook heaven and earth" — the Qiantang River surged upright before the wind. Su Shi watched the moonrise: "The new moon is like a beauty, emerging from the sea to display her colors." Moonlit boating: "Released fish and turtles approach the people; ownerless lotus blooms everywhere."

5
Bittersweet Farewell

Parting Sorrow: From Hangzhou Paradise to Mizhou's Barren North

At the end of his three-year tenure, Su Shi reluctantly bid farewell. At the farewell banquet with Yang Yuansu, he wrote: "At the ends of the earth, we share the sorrow of drifting and falling; my native mountains still hold an unkept lifelong vow" — the simple pain of parting deepened into profound meditation on life's transience and the sorrow of rootless wandering.

Liu Shu and Zhang Xian escorted Su Shi as far as Songjiang's Rainbow Pavilion. The 85-year-old Zhang Xian sang his own lyrics, then "counting silently on his fingers, knowing this parting was a life-and-death farewell, tears streaming, he grasped Su Shi's sleeve" — unwilling to let go, aware he would never see him again. Su Shi had planned to visit his brother Su Zhe in Jinan by way of Haizhou, but the canal froze solid — he "had no choice but to brave the biting wind and head directly to Mizhou."

Seven years later, learning that Rainbow Pavilion had been swept away by the tides without a trace, Su Shi wrote "Remembering Our Excursion to Rainbow Pavilion" with boundless melancholy — time takes everything; only poetry endures.

KEY TAKEAWAY

The three Hangzhou years were Su Shi's "healing period": West Lake's landscapes not only soothed the wounds of political exile but ignited his most brilliant poetry. The line "I compare West Lake to Xi Zi" forged a mutual achievement — West Lake gained its immortal poetic alias "Xi Zi Lake," while Su Shi, through profound communion with nature, Zen stillness, and kindred friends, completed his metamorphosis from disheartened official to transcendent poet.
