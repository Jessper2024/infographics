Core AnswerIn April 1079, Su Shi set out on his third journey along the Bian River to assume office. At Yangzhou's Pingshan Hall, mourning his departed mentor Ouyang Xiu, he wrote: "Say not all things turn to emptiness when you turn your head — even before you turn, all is already a dream." Friends Departed

Three Times at Pingshan Hall: "Even Before You Turn, All Is Already a Dream"

Passing through Yangzhou on his third posting journey, Su Shi came to Pingshan Hall — the scenic landmark built thirty years earlier by his mentor Ouyang Xiu. Ouyang's hand-planted willows still stood before the hall; his calligraphy on the wall still seemed freshly brushed — yet the man was gone: "Three times I have passed beneath Pingshan Hall; half a lifetime has vanished in the snap of fingers. Ten years I have not seen the old immortal; his dragon-serpent brushwork still flies across the wall."

Su Shi took Bai Juyi's line "A hundred years pass in the blink of an eye; all things turn to emptiness when you turn your head" and pushed it further: "Say not all things turn to emptiness when you turn your head — even before you turn, all is already a dream." At that moment, his spirit roamed ten thousand li, spanning a thousand years — completely unaware of the crowd gathered round, straining to watch him write.

2
Night Boat Zen

"Deep Night, All Creatures Leave Each Other Alone": Ecstasy of Spirit Merging with Nature

Aboard the boat on the water, Su Shi tossed and turned, unable to sleep. Deep into the silent night, all creatures slumbered — "there was no strife, no clamor, no scheming" — he experienced the joy of spirit merging seamlessly with nature: "Deep night, all creatures leave each other alone; I alone keep company with my own shadow in delight."

In "Rising at Night on the Boat," Su Shi opens with "A faint breeze rustles the reeds; I open the door to see rain, moonlight filling the lake" — creating an atmosphere vast and strange, ethereal as immortal verse. The night tide silently rose like a winter worm's murmur; the moon hung on willow tips like a spider suspended on silk — "time seemed to have stopped; an instant became eternity." Yet "roosters crow, bells ring, the hundred birds scatter; at the boat's prow, drums beat out their call once more" — the clamorous day would soon return.

"This life hurries by amid care and trouble; pure scenes pass before the eyes in mere moments." — In this hurried, toil-filled life of cares, transcendent Zen moments are fleeting; the poet knew the brief stillness would soon give way to endless worldly turmoil

Philosophical Summit

"Neither Must One Serve, Nor Must One Retire": The Ideal Life's Balance

In his "Record of the Zhang Family Garden at Lingbi," Su Shi crystallized years of contemplation into a classic formulation: "The junzi of old neither felt he must serve, nor felt he must not serve" — all must follow the natural course, finding balance between the permissible and impermissible, preserving individual spiritual independence while never shirking social responsibility.

He elaborated: "Open the door and go forth to serve — then one walks step by step through the marketplace of court; close the door and return to reclusion — then one gazes up and down among mountain forests." Service and reclusion are not an either/or choice — one can flexibly shift between them in different times and spaces to "nourish life and cultivate character, practice righteousness and pursue aspirations." This was the life wisdom Su Shi distilled through two decades of officialdom's storms — the spiritual pillar that would sustain him through greater tribulations to come.

4
Huzhou Arrival

Calm Before the Storm: "I Have Nothing to Miss But These Clear Waters and Mountains"

On April 20, 1079, Su Shi arrived at his Huzhou post. "Huzhou's customs were peaceful and prosperous, its mountains and waters clear and distant" — he eagerly took a sedan chair to explore the city, intoxicated by scenery where "for thirty li around the city walls, every spot was exquisitely beautiful," his heart brewing many new plans: serving the people, singing the landscape's praises.

As in Hangzhou, Mizhou, and Xuzhou, he wanted to "extend the court's benevolence above and comfort the elders' hopes below." He wrote: "I have nothing to miss but these clear waters and mountains. New poems fly from my hand like pellets, never stopping." Yet he had no inkling that in the capital, a "sudden, severe political blow" was about to shatter every one of his beautiful hopes.

At this moment, Su Shi was full of anticipation, planning waterworks and cultural projects for Huzhou — a natural continuation of his achievements in Hangzhou, Mizhou, and Xuzhou. He firmly believed he was beginning yet another term of beneficial governance.
5
The Trap Tightens

Political Enemies: How Petty Men Made Su Shi Their "Thorn in the Eye"

The focus of Yuánfēng-era politics was no longer the reform debate but pure bureaucratic infighting. The core figures of both the reform and anti-reform camps had all departed — Wang Anshi had retired to Jiangning, Sima Guang was writing in seclusion — yet court factional struggles only intensified. Prime Minister Wang Gui, to secure his power, allied with Censorate officials Li Ding, Shu Dan, and He Zhengchen, who were eagerly hunting for opportunities to crush potential anti-reform resurgences.

Wang Gui was famously called the "Three Edicts Prime Minister" — request the edict, receive the edict, transmit the edict — possessing zero political vision but consummate bureaucratic cunning. Together with Vice Councilor Cai Que, they formed an "offensive-defensive alliance" with Li Ding's faction. Li Ding and company had risen to high positions through supporting the New Policies; seeing no strongman left to lead the reform camp, they "lived in constant anxiety," terrified that Emperor Shenzong might turn to opposition figures — and Su Shi, the "thorn in their eye, the spike in their flesh," became their perfect target.

"Ever since Su Shi shocked the capital in the 1057 jinshi examination, for various reasons he had long been denied significant court appointments" — yet in his enemies' eyes, his very talent was the greatest threat of all

KEY TAKEAWAY

Chapter 5 is Su Shi's watershed: the first half sees him achieve his philosophical summation — "neither must one serve, nor must one retire" — reaching the most harmonious balance between engagement and withdrawal; the second half reveals the capital's dark political reality, where petty men led by Wang Gui and Li Ding are weaving a net from his poetry. Philosophically, Su Shi was now prepared to face any storm life might bring — but he did not yet know that the coming "Crow Terrace Poetry Trial" would be the ultimate test of all his wisdom and courage. The frost of the Censorate was already creeping closer.
