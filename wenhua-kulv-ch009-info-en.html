Wenhua Kulv · Ch. 9 "Western Kashgar"

The ultimate crossroads of civilizations: an epic between the Tianshan and Kunlun Mountains

Core insight: Kashgar became the "center of centers" because it fulfilled both requirements for non-military civilizational exchange — a sparsely populated zone far from every empire's capital, plus a spirit of tolerance that put all travelers at ease — making it the only place in human history where Indian, Persian, Arab, Greek, and Chinese civilizations all shared the same stage without conquest.

QuestionWhy did historian Arnold Toynbee say, "If I could live my life again, I would wish to be born in the Western Regions"?

AnswerKashgar was the ancient world's "World Expo" — goods, religions, languages, and arts converged here at massive scale without war. From Zhang Qian's diplomatic missions to Ban Chao's garrison, from Buddhism's eastward spread to Islam taking root, and finally to the 19th-century Anglo-Russian "Great Game," for millennia this land has been the pivot of civilizational exchange.

1
The Ultimate Question

Why did Toynbee choose the Western Regions?

British historian Arnold Toynbee's answer stunned scholars worldwide: if he could live again, he would wish to be born in the ancient Western Regions of China — because it was a "blessed land of cultural convergence."

An ancient Chinese literatus, wherever he wandered, longed in old age only to return home. But modern scholars with world-historical vision were different — they would distill time and space through a lifetime of learning, then stand at the study window and ask: if life could be relived, where would I most wish to be born? Toynbee pointed to the Tarim and Yarkand river valleys of Xinjiang.

2
Civilizational Mechanism

What conditions enable non-military exchange?

The great civilizations converged in the Western Regions because it uniquely satisfied two demanding conditions: a sparsely populated zone far from every civilization's capital, posing no threat; and a spirit of tolerance that reassured every traveling party.

India, Persia, Babylon, Arabia, Egypt, Greece, Rome — each was self-contained, each enjoyed its own prestige. None would willingly stoop to fuse with others except through war. But the lure of trade and travelers' tales was irresistible, and they all quietly developed the same desire: to find a place for non-military exchange. Only the vast desert between the Tianshan, Kunlun, and Tarim met this need.

3
Corridor of Faiths

How did Kashgar become a spiritual hub?

Kashgar was not just a trade hub — it was a distribution center of spiritual culture. Buddhism's eastward spread used it as a corridor, Islam took root here as its forward base, Nestorian Christianity and Zoroastrianism also left their marks — all connecting Central and South Asia to West Asia and Europe.

Kumarajiva studied Hinayana Buddhism here at age twelve before converting to Mahayana; both Faxian and Xuanzang passed through on their pilgrimage journeys. The Karakhanid dynasty produced the Kutadgu Bilig (Wisdom of Royal Glory) and the Diwan Lughat al-Turk. Marco Polo recorded that Nestorian Christians here held "complete services." Yu Qiuyu notes: Indian and Chinese civilizations were both "doubly noble," and the place where their mutual wariness was first overcome was Kashgar.

4
Imperial Chessboard

Why did empires fight over Kashgar in the 19th century?

In the late 19th century, Kashgar became the front line of the Anglo-Russian "Great Game" — the Russian consulate garrisoned sixty Cossack cavalry, the British consulate was twice its size (described as "the only Union Jack between India and the North Pole"), while archaeologists Sven Hedin and Aurel Stein unearthed artifacts proving the land's "unparalleled importance."

The Russian consul Petrovsky was described as "a capable, arrogant, cunning, and seductive schemer," aiming to detach Xinjiang's westernmost oasis from China. Yet the archaeologists knelt in the deep desert, brushing away sand with bare hands — "archaeologists represent modern humanity kneeling in gratitude." Historian H.G. Wells concluded: the Tarim basin is more important than the Jordan or Rhine valleys.

5
First City of Kunlun

What does Kashgar's magnificence signify?

Among the "ancestral sites" of Chinese civilization, the Western Regions are the most breathtaking — the Tianshan, Kunlun, and Taklamakan "arrayed together, intersecting, echoing one another" — a sequence of unsurpassable extremes that surround, conquer, crush, and embrace you.

The author inscribed for Kashgar the words "First City of Kunlun" (昆仑第一城). Beside the Tianshan and Kunlun, China's Five Sacred Mountains look like bonsai; beside the Taklamakan, traditional desert poetry seems childish. The Twelve Muqam of Shache evokes the Shule and Qiuci music that stirred Tang Dynasty Chang'an — in the grand chords of China's greatest dynasty, the leading notes were mostly from Western Region melodies.

💡
Key Takeaway

Kashgar is a rare experiment in human civilization: it proves that pluralistic cultures can fuse without conquest, relying solely on geographic openness and cultural tolerance. From Zhang Qian to Toynbee, from Kumarajiva to Sven Hedin — no one seeking to understand "macro-history" across two millennia could bypass this border city beneath the Kunlun Mountains.

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Wenhua Kulv · Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
