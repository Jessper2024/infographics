Overview: Have a Target, Have a Method, Know "Enough"

S Situation

Before every trade, a trader must answer three questions: where to take profit, where to place a stop, and what is the risk/reward ratio. Professionals constantly ask these; gamblers never do.

C Complication

Amateurs avoid setting profit targets, believing it caps upside. Greed drives them to want more after winning, causing them to hold too long until profits evaporate into regret.

Q Question

How can traders systematically set and execute profit targets across different market phases? Which tools and mindsets ensure disciplined exits?

A Answer

Elder presents three technical profit-taking tools (MAs, Channels, Support/Resistance) matched to market phases, underpinned by the mental discipline of "knowing enough" (zhī zú). Every trade should pursue two goals: making money, and becoming a better trader.

01
Technical Tool

Moving Average Profit-Taking — Value Regression

When a market transitions from bear to bull, prices typically trade below moving averages. The approach: buy below the weekly value zone (between two EMAs), set profit targets near that value zone or the slow EMA. The Triple Screen system calls for weekly charts for strategy and daily charts for tactics. Moving averages represent the consensus on long-term value — price regressing to value is the core driver of a bull market's first phase.

Technical Tool

Channel Profit-Taking — Selling Overbought Zones

Once the bull market is established, prices stay above moving averages for extended periods, making MA-based targets ineffective. Switch to channels (envelopes): buy at the value zone (between EMAs), sell above the upper channel. Channels auto-adjust their width with volatility — targets shift upward as swings expand. This "riding the invisible rail" pattern repeats throughout bull trends. Elder warns: it may feel like an ATM, but the market is never that simple.

Technical Tool

Support & Resistance Profit-Taking — Long-Term Targeting

Deer hunters need bigger guns than rabbit hunters. Long-term trades (months to years) require larger targets set via support and resistance zones. Support and resistance are built on two emotions: pain and regret. Those who bought near the upper edge and suffered drawdowns wait painfully for breakeven; those who missed shorting at the top regret it deeply. These emotions coalesce into barriers. Elder emphasizes: S/R is not glass — it's more like a wire fence that both bulls and bears can lean against.

The psychological challenge of long-term trading: for weeks or months, you do nothing. Even when you spot short-term tops and bottoms, you cannot act — you are waiting for the long-term target. That is precisely why setting a price target is so important: it builds the conviction needed to hold. Elder held STTSY for two years, sold at $8.39 (target $16), made $27K — and "learned far more than I earned."
04
Mental Discipline

Contentment — Greed Is Profit's Worst Enemy

The most important word in trading — and in life — is "enough" (zhī zú, 知足). Greedy traders want more after a win, furious at themselves for not buying the exact bottom or selling the exact top. This resentment makes them buy too early and sell too late. Elder's advice is blunt: you must decide how much money will make you happy, and set your targets accordingly. Those who demand too much become slaves to greed; those who decide what is enough gain freedom.

✅ The Contented

Set profit targets and execute with discipline. If the market weakens before target → accept less, exit early. If the market gives more → don't regret. A bird in the hand is worth two in the bush. Score every trade in a journal. Learn from mistakes without self-punishment.

❌ The Greedy

Avoid setting targets, believing they'll "exit when the time is right." Profit triggers desire for more → hold too long → profits evaporate. Miss the top → pound the desk in regret → psychological imbalance → reckless next trade → deeper losses. Endless wanting erodes both reason and humanity.

Case Review

Trade Review — Continuous Evolution Through Your Journal

Elder highlights three core functions of a trading diary: ① Grade every trade on three dimensions (entry, exit, total), aiming to keep the average above 50%; ② Monthly reviews with hindsight — but without regret, which poisons your trading mindset; ③ Track P&L by idea source so you know whose advice to follow. If you learn from experience continuously, every day you become a better trader than the day before.

CORE TAKEAWAY

Profit-taking is not about predicting tops — it is about executing a plan. Three technical tools match three market phases: MAs for bear-to-bull transitions, channels for established trends, and support/resistance for long-term positioning. The mindset of "enough" (zhī zú) runs through them all: decide how much makes you happy, set your target, execute without regret. Every trade has two goals: make money, and become a better trader. The second one you can always achieve.
