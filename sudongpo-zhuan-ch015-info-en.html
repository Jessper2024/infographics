The Gay Genius · Ch.15 "The Crow Terrace Poetry Trial"

One phrase, four indictments, and the literary inquisition that changed Chinese literary history

📖 Overview

This was the greatest crisis of Su Dongpo's life — imprisoned for his writing. In 1079, censors extracted four sentences from his official memorandum and several poems, accusing him of "satirizing current affairs with hidden malice." During six to seven weeks of interrogation, Su Dongpo believed he would be executed and wrote farewell poems to his brother. He was ultimately spared by Emperor Shenzong, who admired his talent, and banished to Huangzhou — in what became the Northern Song's most infamous literary inquisition.

Core Question: How does a man face life and death for simply telling the truth?

Key Insight: Su Dongpo's humor and candor in the face of death — comforting his family with a joke about "losing one's old scalp" — outshines even his poetic genius.

01
TRIGGER

Four Words That Lit the Fuse

A single phrase — "ill-suited to accompany the upstarts" — turned Su Dongpo from prefect of Huzhou into a prisoner.

In his routine memorandum thanking the emperor for his new post in Huzhou, Su wrote that he was "ill-suited to accompany the upstarts (xinjin)" — a term Wang Anshi had used for "untalented juniors suddenly promoted." Published as a public notice, it instantly made the court's political climbers laughingstocks among the literati. Four censors, including Shu Dan and Li Ding, jointly submitted indictments citing four volumes of Su's poetry.

Every piece of Su's writing was scoured for evidence — official memoranda, poems, even a stone inscription quoting Mencius was twisted into an accusation of mocking the court.

02
ARREST

The Arrest: Official Robes and a Joke

In his darkest hour of arrest, Su Dongpo made his weeping family laugh with a single story.

When the imperial messenger Huangfu Zun arrived with guards in Huzhou, Su assumed he would be executed. He stepped forward: "I know I have offended the court. I believe I cannot escape death. I am not afraid, but please let me bid my family farewell." The messenger coldly replied: "It is not so serious." As his whole family wept, Su told them the story of Yang Pu — a reluctant scholar summoned to court whose wife wrote a farewell poem ending with: "Today they haul you off to the officials' lair / This time you'll surely lose your old scalp there."

Arrested July 28, imprisoned August 18, 1079. On the journey, he nearly jumped into Lake Tai, but feared implicating his brother. His family burned most of his manuscripts; only one-third survived.

03
TURN

The Smoked Fish Blunder That Moved an Emperor

A meal-delivery mix-up sent Su's two farewell poems to the emperor's desk — and saved his life.

Su and his eldest son Su Mai had a secret code: ordinary meals meant all was well, but fish signaled bad news. When Su Mai left the capital to borrow money, a friend substituted as meal-bearer and, unaware of the code, brought smoked fish. Convinced death was near, Su wrote two heartrending poems to his brother Ziyou, thanking the emperor for past kindness and vowing to be brothers through all future lives. The prison warden forwarded them to the throne as required. Emperor Shenzong was deeply moved.

Ziyou sobbed uncontrollably upon reading them. Despite the censors' pressure, the emperor — moved by both Su's talent and the poems' sincerity — ultimately showed clemency.

04
LAW

The Trial's Core: Honest Criticism or Malicious Slander?

The trial hinged on a single question: were Su's poems honest criticism, or malicious slander of the government and emperor?

Zhang Fangping argued in Su's defense that even the Book of Songs, edited by Confucius himself, was filled with political satire — a good government should tolerate frank criticism. Censor Shu Dan countered that Su's writings were "unprecedented in their concealed malice, resentment toward the sovereign, and shameless mockery," even declaring that "ten thousand deaths would not suffice." Another censor twisted Su's quotation of Mencius into a crime.

✅ Zhang Fangping's Defense

"The Book of Songs is full of political satire. A good government tolerates honest criticism." There is a fundamental difference between honest critique and malicious slander.

❌ The Censors' Accusation

Four indictments, four volumes of poetry: Su was accused of "concealed malice" and lacking "the restraint proper to a subject," demanding capital punishment.

05
PEOPLE

The People Wept; the Officials Hid

When Su was arrested, the local officials hid in fear — but the common people poured out to see him off, their "tears falling like rain."

According to the local gazetteer, the people came to bid farewell to their prefect, all weeping. The guards were arrogant and rough; Su later wrote to a succeeding emperor that "they arrested a prefect as if seizing a bandit." Only the Wang brothers and Chen Shixi held a farewell banquet. This stark contrast reveals the enormous gap between Su's standing among ordinary people and the hatred of his political enemies at court.

After Su's arrest, more guards were sent to search for poems and letters. Soldiers surrounded the boat, ransacking everything. The women and children were terrified. His wife raged: "This all comes from writing books!" — and burned even more manuscripts.

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

The Crow Terrace Poetry Trial was not fundamentally a legal proceeding — it was a contest over whether intellectuals could criticize power. Su Dongpo proved, through the raw sincerity of his farewell poems and his humor at the moment of arrest, that true literary integrity lies not in the sharpness of one's pen, but in the ability to comfort one's family with a joke at the edge of death.
