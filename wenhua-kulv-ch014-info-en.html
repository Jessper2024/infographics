Wenhua Kulv · Ch.14 "Ninggu Pagoda"

Exile destroys civilization, but nobility amidst suffering kindles flames beyond political persecution

Core Insight: Qing-dynasty exile was the systematic destruction of civilization — among 1.5 million exiles, countless talents and beauties were ground to dust, yet the nobility displayed by some literati in suffering kindled civilizational flames; we can endure suffering, but we will never glorify it.

QuestionHow did exile destroy civilization, and how did literati, stripped of everything, create culture amidst suffering?

AnswerExile destroyed human dignity through collective punishment, abuse, and forced labor. Yet some literati, drawing on inner nobility, composed poetry at the edge of survival and transmitted knowledge — "For culture and civilization, they set aside material desire and utilitarian calculation, pressing forward without hesitation, generation after generation."

1
Land of Exile

What made Ninggu Pagoda a realm of terror?

The three characters "Ning-gu-ta" were the most ominous talisman in the hearts of Qing officials nationwide — not a pagoda (Manchu for "six"), but an abyss worse than death: anyone could, overnight, form a lifelong bond with this place.

Historical records describe exiles devoured by wild beasts en route, or even eaten by starving locals. Compared to the notorious Shangyang Fort prison colony, Shangyang at least had shelter — it was "like heaven" by comparison. Beautiful women were casually violated; inconvenient husbands were killed first. Surplus exiles: women sold to brothels, men traded for horses.

"Southern beauties fill the northern frontier; Central Plains scholars half populate Liaoyang." — Qing poet Ding Jie

Qing-era northeast exiles alone exceeded 1.5 million — "no province lacked its quota." A large proportion were distant relatives caught in collective punishment, who may never have met the convicted party.

2
Tragedy of Collective Guilt

How did examination scandals expose ancient judicial logic?

The 1657 "Southern Examination Scandal" exemplifies Qing verdicts: a mere suspicion that one candidate might be distantly related to the chief examiner — after the candidate's father had disproven it — resulted in both examiners beheaded, all 18 sub-examiners hanged, 8 candidates beaten, and all their families exiled to Ninggu Pagoda.

This was the logic of ancient Chinese justice: the emperor had already removed the examiners — if no crime existed, where would imperial face be? So "the entire court insisted: your families must be related, cannot possibly be unrelated, no reason to be unrelated, why would you not be related?" The punishment was ludicrously disproportionate — those 20 examiners should have been China's finest scholars.

The most bewildered were literati caught in literary inquisitions and examination scandals — "overnight becoming criminals, stumbling northeastward with crowds of the collectively punished, most not understanding their own cases."

3
Power of Friendship

How did exile produce China's most precious friendships?

When rank, identity, and property are stripped one by one, what remains is life calling directly to life — the most precious, most moving friendships in Chinese history were surely forged in the exile lands of the north and south.

The anti-Qing loyalist Hanke, exiled in the northeast, founded the "Ice and Snow Poetry Society" with dismissed Qing officials as core members — political enemies vanished, antagonism dissolved, leaving only friends in utter sincerity. Most moving: Gu Zhenguan's rescue of Wu Zhaoqian, for which he knelt at countless mansions and wrote two "Golden Thread" poems to Nalan Xingde — "Is Jizi safe? Should you return, how could a lifetime's affairs bear recalling!" Nalan wept upon reading and pledged ten years.

"Words cannot exhaust — Guan bows his head." — Gu Zhenguan, "Golden Thread"

Yu Qiuyu asserts: "Today's northeastern frankness, hospitality, friendship-loyalty, and sense of honor must bear some connection to the spiritual legacy of the exiles. Exile created a richly flavored spiritual world from which we benefit to this day."

4
Civilizational Sparks

How did exiles become the northeast's cultural pioneers?

Amidst suffering, exiles still sought to do something irreplaceable — "Surely there must be something higher than gathering wild vegetables, collecting horse dung, and burning lime?" All such acts were connected to culture, and thus constituted a return — a cultural return.

📖 Teaching and Transmitting

Hong Hao transcribed the "Four Books" on birch leaves to teach village children. Zhang Shao lectured on the Book of Changes — "listeners gathered in droves." Zhang Taiyan noted: "Qiqihar's literacy was pioneered by the exiled descendants of Lü Yonghui."

📝 Scholarly Works

Fang Gongqian's Gazetteer of Ninggu Pagoda, Wu Zhenchen's Brief Account, Zhang Jinyan's Landscape Records, Yang Bin's Notes from the Willow Frontier — these works overcame Chinese scholarship's deficit in field research, producing a delightful chapter in academic history.

The Lü family of Zhejiang, the Fang family of Anhui, the Yang family of Zhejiang — southern families exiled for generations became seasoned "natives" who, in the cultural sense, were "heroic occupiers."

5
Nobility in Suffering

Why "not glorifying suffering" yet "affirming nobility"?

Exile, in any form, constitutes the destruction of civilization — that some exiles struggled from their wounds to create culture does not add luster to exile itself. A place like Ninggu Pagoda could never produce Li Sao, the Records of the Grand Historian, or Dream of the Red Chamber.

Yet the reason some literati could manifest humanity and create civilization amidst suffering lies in their inner nobility — "their external identity could change endlessly, even trapped lifelong in prison, but their inner nobility was never wholly eroded." What moves us most is nobility in suffering; what reveals nobility's nature is precisely this nobility. Through it, people composed poetry at the edge of survival, melted others' frozen hearts with their own warmth, and used humiliated bodies to ignite civilizational sparks.

"Civilization may arise from barbarism, but it never loves barbarism. We can endure suffering, but we will never glorify suffering. We do not fear persecution, but we will never affirm persecution."
💡
Key Takeaway

Ninggu Pagoda reveals three layers of truth: first, ancient China's exile system was the systematic destruction of civilization and humanity; second, some literati, drawing on inner nobility, kindled civilizational flames amidst suffering; third, we will never glorify suffering — but we must salute those souls who maintained their nobility within it.

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Wenhua Kulv (A Bitter Journey Through Culture) · Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
