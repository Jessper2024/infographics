Wenhua Kulv · Ch.10 "The Cold Eye of the Abandoned Well"

The millennial ruin of an Asian metropolis leaves behind only the indifferent gaze of an ancient well

Core Insight: The destruction of Balhae from a "Prosperous Eastern Kingdom" to ashes at the hands of the Khitans reveals a civilizational pattern — when a highly advanced city is conquered, the victors can neither control, integrate, nor reform it; annihilation becomes the only path.

QuestionWhy did the Balhae capital, once a peer of Chang'an, leave behind only a few stone foundations and one abandoned well after a thousand years?

AnswerWhen a civilization far surpasses its conquerors, destruction becomes the only "governance plan"; the Eight-Treasure Glazed Well's cold eye has witnessed cycles of collapse: Balhae → Liao → Jin → Yuan → Qing.

1
Chance Discovery

Who discovered a lost capital on the exile road?

Seven Qing-dynasty exiled scholars, dressed like beggars, identified palace pillar bases and carved patterns on massive stones while resting from carrying firewood — they were once China's top intellectual elites, narrowly escaping execution in imperial examination scandals.

These former examiners had been reduced to lime-kiln labor and horse-tending slaves. After four years, cultural memory seemed erased — until the stone carvings brought everything rushing back. One broke the silence with four words: "Li Bai's drunken letter." Another immediately responded: "The Balhae Kingdom!"

Legend says Balhae sent a letter to Tang China in an unknown script. None at court could read it — except the drunk poet Li Bai, who demanded that the powerful minister Yang Guozhong grind his ink and the eunuch Gao Lishi remove his boots before he would translate. Whether myth or not, the Balhae Kingdom itself — and its vast ruins — were no myth.

2
Prosperous Kingdom

How magnificent was the Balhae civilization?

During the reign of King Dae Insu (9th century), the Balhae capital "Sanggyeong Yongcheonbu" stood alongside Chang'an as one of Asia's two great civilizational centers — one in the east, one in the west.

The capital featured triple-walled rings of outer, inner, and palace cities, with the outer wall spanning over 15 kilometers. Five great bridges crossed the Mudan River — today, modern life with tens of thousands of residents needs only one bridge. It served as Northeast Asia's trade hub, linking distant Chang'an with Japan along an economic corridor.

🏛️ Balhae at Its Peak

Fully adopted Tang civilization, used Chinese as official script, operated under Tang governance models. Known as the "Prosperous Eastern Kingdom" in the 9th century.

⚠️ Hidden Peril

Surrounding tribes remained nomadic. Admiration and imitation masked intense jealousy and hatred — gleaming eyes grew ever more numerous.

3
Logic of Destruction

Why are advanced cities always annihilated?

Throughout human history, highly civilized cities invariably suffer the most brutal fates after conquest — the victors discover a civilizational order far beyond their own; they can neither control, integrate, nor transform it. Destruction is the only option.

In 926 CE, the Khitans conquered Balhae. They found not only cold stares from citizens but silent defiance in every brick and paving stone. After looting, they ordered mass evacuation and relocation southward, then set the entire city ablaze.

Archaeological excavations reveal bricks, tiles, and stones fused together by intense heat; massive road stones cracked apart. The water in the Eight-Treasure Glazed Well surely boiled dry during that fire — we will never know how long it burned.

4
Millennial Cold Eye

What cycles of rise and fall has this well witnessed?

The Eight-Treasure Glazed Well, with its basalt stone walls virtually undamaged after a millennium, gazes like a cold eye — one that has seen too much and grown tired of watching.

The Khitans destroyed Balhae — exactly two centuries later, the Jin destroyed them. Jin lasted only 120 years before the Mongols annihilated them. So many extinctions, each accompanied by infernos and the boiling, then drying, of that ancient well — yet groundwater always returns, bringing back ripples, but the gaze remains cold.

5
Transcendent Wisdom

How did ruins grant the exiles equanimity?

Confronted with only scattered stones and an abandoned well where a great city once stood, the injustices of examination scandals and the wrongful deaths of loved ones shrank to mere dust in history's folds.

The scholars' firewood collection point happened to be a volcanic crater. Against volcanic time, the scale shifts dramatically — dynasties measure in centuries, volcanoes in tens of thousands of years. A volcanic crater, too, is an abandoned well — its cold eye makes even the Earth shiver.

"History is indifferent. In most cases, it cares nothing for right or wrong, reason or emotion. More indifferent than history is nature."

Yu Qiuyu believes that had those exiled scholars seen the cold eye of the well, they would have embraced their suffering with greater equanimity — this is the ultimate insight that ruins bestow upon us.

💡
Key Takeaway

The Eight-Treasure Glazed Well in the Balhae ruins is a cold eye that has watched a millennium of rise and fall. It tells us: civilization can be created and destroyed; history is indifferent; human nobility lies in the capacity to transcend personal tragedy with equanimity, even in the face of total ruin.

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Wenhua Kulv (A Bitter Journey Through Culture) · Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
