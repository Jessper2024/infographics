Su Shi: A Life · Ch.2 — The World's Burdens Are Hard to Bear

In 1068, Su Shi set out for the capital for the third time — unknowingly his last embrace of his native soil. A thousand li away, a fierce factional struggle between Reformers and Conservatives had already begun, brewing a storm that would reshape his life's entire trajectory.

▎Core Question
How did the Song dynasty's structural crisis of "poverty and weakness" give birth to Wang Anshi's reforms — and why did these reforms drag Su Shi into an inescapable vortex of factional conflict?
The early Song "ancestral institutions" secured centralization but planted structural flaws: over-concentration of military power weakened defense, mutual oversight between officials bred bureaucratic bloat, and excessive fiscal extraction emptied the treasury. Facing internal decay and external threats, the young Emperor Shenzong joined forces with Wang Anshi to launch sweeping reforms. The resulting polarization between Reformers and Conservatives confronted Su Shi — just entering officialdom — with his first great political dilemma: "Seeing the times too hard for one man's strength, yet unable to retreat from imperial grace."
01
Structural Roots

"Ancestral Institutions": The Built-In Flaws of Centralization

The centralized system so carefully designed by the Song founders, while consolidating unity, also planted irreconcilable internal contradictions — which only worsened with time.

⚔️
Military Centralization

"Dismissing generals over wine" → civilian command → troop rotation. Prevented warlordism but caused "soldiers not knowing officers" — poor training, weak combat power.

🏛️
Political Overlap

Prefect + Controller check each other — the Controller reported directly to the emperor. Dual staffing caused bureaucratic bloat and internal friction. A joke of the era: "Just give me a post with crabs but no Controller."

💰
Fiscal Extraction

Most local revenue sent to central coffers, fueling elite extravagance. "Generosity to officials knew no limit; extraction from commoners left no surplus." By Renzong's reign, the treasury was empty.

🛡️
Defense Weakness

Weakened military, paying massive annual indemnities to Liao and Western Xia — 200K taels of silver + 300K bolts of silk — yet still no border peace. Neither peace nor war was feasible.

02
National Crisis

Poverty and Weakness: The Hidden Crisis Beneath Surface Calm

When Shenzong took the throne, the Song had reached a perilous state of "imminent internal and external crisis" — financial exhaustion, military defeat, and surging reformist pressure like magma beneath the earth.

The failed "Qingli Reforms" of 1043 had only deepened social tensions. The Song paid annual indemnities of 70,000 taels of silver, 5,000 bolts of silk, and 30,000 catties of tea to Western Xia — plus 200,000 taels of silver and 300,000 bolts of silk to the Liao. These crushing payments compounded the fiscal crisis. A swollen bureaucracy, an empty treasury, and mounting popular distress formed a perfect storm.

⚠️
Historical Inflection Point: A new wave of reform sentiment was bubbling vigorously among scholar-officials — "like surging magma underground, awaiting only a fissure to burst through." That fissure would be the historic meeting of the young Emperor Shenzong and Wang Anshi.
03
Historic Alliance

Shenzong and Wang Anshi: A Once-in-a-Century Meeting of Minds

The twenty-year-old emperor, humiliated by national weakness and isolated by the conservatism of elder ministers, remembered the extraordinary man who "regarded wealth and rank as floating clouds."

Shenzong consulted veteran statesmen: Fu Bi counseled "twenty years without speaking of war"; Sima Guang offered platitudes about self-cultivation. To Shenzong, they had all lost the reforming spirit of the Qingli era. Wang Anshi, meanwhile, had built an unparalleled reputation — repeatedly declining prestigious posts, living frugally, renowned for profound learning. Scholar-officials hailed him as "a sage reborn." When sovereign and minister finally met, Wang declared with bold optimism: "The time for great action is now — today!"

Shenzong once donned military attire to visit his grandmother, demonstrating his resolve to restore national strength. Recounting how Emperor Taizong had been wounded by Liao arrows and the humiliation of annual tribute, he wept. The union of this young emperor and the "man of the age" struck the opening chord of the Xining Reforms.

04
Reform Blueprint

Swift and Decisive: Finance and Military on Parallel Tracks

In the second month of 1070, Shenzong appointed Wang Anshi Vice Grand Councilor and immediately established the Finance Planning Commission — launching an eighteen-year reform movement.

The New Policies fell into two categories. Fiscal measures for "enriching the state": the Green Sprouts Loan, the Hired Service System, the Equitable Transport System, the Market Exchange System, the Square-Field Tax Survey, and Water Conservancy incentives. Military measures for "strengthening the army": troop reduction and consolidation, the General Command System, the Horse-Guarantee System, and the Mutual Security System.

Yet from the very start, the reforms were engulfed in controversy. "The court and the country were stunned; public opinion raged." Even the question of staffing sparked fierce divisions — one minister praised Wang as "talent fit for grand councilor," while another denounced him as "obstinate and self-opinionated, with impractical ideas." Emperor Shenzong "often lay sleepless through the night."

05
Personal Destiny

Su Shi's Dilemma: Between Serving the World and Retreating From It

Caught between the warring Reform and Conservative factions, Su Shi fell into a profound inner conflict — yearning to serve the state while fearing the political maelstrom.

En route to his post, Su Shi stopped at Jinshan Temple in Zhenjiang, where the sunset over the Yangtze inspired his most intimate self-portrait. He marveled at the "ripples on ten thousand acres like the grain of leather" and the "broken sunset clouds red as a fish's tail." But as darkness fell, he witnessed a mysterious torch blazing from the river's heart — "neither ghost nor man, what could it be?" He interpreted this as the river god's warning: the landscape is so beautiful, yet he clung to worldly ambition, unwilling to retire.

"As a feudal scholar-official, once he set foot on the path of officialdom, his life no longer belonged to himself" — therein lies the profound meaning of this chapter's title: "The World's Burdens Are Hard to Bear."
💡
Key Takeaway

The Xining Reforms represent a classic case of the "reform dilemma" in Chinese history. The Song dynasty's structural flaws — military centralization breeding defense weakness, political cross-checking spawning bureaucratic infighting, and fiscal extraction causing treasury crises — gave the reforms genuine historical legitimacy. Yet once launched, reform entangled interests, ideologies, and personal grudges, rapidly polarizing into irreconcilable factional warfare. For Su Shi, just beginning his official career, this reform was not an abstract policy debate but the core variable that would determine the rises and falls of his next thirty years. His vow to "retreat" made before Jinshan Temple and the undying ember of his "service" formed the twin themes that would echo through the symphony of his remaining life.
