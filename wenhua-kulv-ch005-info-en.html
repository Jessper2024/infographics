Wenhua Kulv · Ch. 5 "The Daoist Pagoda"

The Dunhuang calamity was not one man’s crime—an ignorant custodian, colonial scholar, and cultural comprador formed a triple tragedy

Core insight: Even the smallest person can cast a long shadow across the desert. Wang Yuanlu’s ignorant destruction, Stein’s colonial plunder, and Jiang Xiaowan’s cultural betrayal—three men acted out one of history’s most unequal ‘transactions’ at Mogao in 1900. But the deepest tragedy: even if the caravan were stopped, China at the time could not have protected those scrolls.

QuestionWho bears responsibility for the massive loss of the Library Cave’s treasures?

AnswerWang Yuanlu’s ignorant destruction opened the door, Stein’s colonial plunder walked through it, and Jiang Xiaowan’s cultural betrayal greased the wheels—three men each played a role. Nine thousand scrolls and five hundred paintings for thirty pounds. But the deeper tragedy: the scrolls sent to Beijing were plundered en route by officials and torn into shorter pieces to fudge the count. This was the powerlessness of an era—cultural protection first requires the protectors of culture to be protected.

1
Tragic Guardian

Wang Yuanlu: Destruction by an Ignorant Custodian

Even the smallest person can cast a long shadow across the desert. Wang Yuanlu—an illiterate Daoist priest, custodian of Mogao—whitewashed exquisite murals with lime, smashed statues with iron hammers, and connected caves into crude corridors. His ignorance was itself a form of violence.

Wang was originally a farmer from Macheng, Hubei, who became a soldier, then a Daoist priest, and eventually custodian of Mogao. He raised funds through alms, then hired local workmen to whitewash walls and paint Journey to the West tales. ‘The accounts are told calmly, but every time I read them, my mind turns as white as lime—a blank, blinding white. I can barely speak or move; before my eyes those brushes and hammers keep swinging.’ (Yu Qiuyu)

2
Historic Coincidence

The Day the Library Cave Opened, the Eight-Nation Alliance Decided to Attack

On June 22, 1900, Wang Yuanlu opened the Library Cave—on the same day, foreign powers in Beijing decided on a joint military intervention. Months earlier, oracle bones had been discovered. The coincidence is chilling: just as the nation revealed evidence of its cultural confidence, it faced its deepest crisis.

‘It was as if a decision in the foreign legations at Dongjiaominxiang instantly pierced the nervous system of a vast organism—and the door of a cave in the northwestern desert suddenly opened.’ (Yu Qiuyu) ‘Like the oracle bones, the Library Cave epitomized a nation’s cultural confidence. It had to appear, violently, at the very moment that confidence was about to be lost.’ (Yu Qiuyu) This is one of the most dramatic coincidences in Yu Qiuyu’s writing.

3
Three-Man Tragedy

The Transaction: Wang Yuanlu · Aurel Stein · Jiang Xiaowan

The Dunhuang calamity was not Wang Yuanlu’s crime alone—Stein cloaked colonial arrogance in scholarly professionalism, while interpreter Jiang Xiaowan, an anomaly among Chinese compradors who ‘felt almost no moral anguish,’ used the story of Xuanzang to trick open the cave door.

Jiang Xiaowan deceived Wang by claiming Stein would return Xuanzang’s sutras to India. Working through cold desert nights while Stein and Wang slept, Jiang alone could read the scrolls—he made the selections. ‘On those freezing desert nights, Stein and Wang Yuanlu both slept, while only Jiang Xiaowan remained busy. The two sleeping parties understood nothing of the contents; only he understood, and he alone made the choices.’ (Yu Qiuyu) This bridge figure ‘collapsed one bank of the river entirely.’

4
Cultural Plunder

Nine Thousand Scrolls and Five Hundred Paintings for Thirty Pounds

Stein acquired over 9,000 scrolls and 500 paintings for £30, packed into 29 large wooden crates. It was one of history’s most unequal ‘transactions’—‘there, an ancient nation’s wound was bleeding.’

The caravan departed at dusk as Wang Yuanlu stood respectfully by the roadside, considering ‘Sir Stein’ a benefactor. The author imagines standing in the desert, arms outstretched, blocking the caravan: ‘Ripping cultural relics from their native soil is a double loss—for both the artifacts and the land.’ But he then recalls: the scrolls sent to Beijing were plundered en route by officials, torn into shorter pieces to fudge the count. ‘Here it’s difficult, there it’s difficult—after thinking it through, I could only kneel in the desert and weep. My cries, like a wounded wolf howling into the darkness.’ (Yu Qiuyu)

5
Historical Judgment

Why Does Stein’s Epitaph Omit Dunhuang?

Stein’s tombstone inscription lists all his expeditions—India, Xinjiang, Persia, Iraq—but deliberately omits Dunhuang, the discovery that made him famous in the West. ‘His actions at Mogao have drawn increasingly severe condemnation from the civilized world.’

In 1943, at age 82, Stein died in Kabul, Afghanistan, buried in a cemetery surrounded by ‘poverty, war, and religious extremism.’ Yu Qiuyu speculates his soul most yearns to ‘find a dusk, just like the one when he departed with his caravan, and slip back to Dunhuang.’ Meanwhile, Wang Yuanlu’s Daoist pagoda ‘draws crowds of tourists daily, though none cast a respectful glance.’ And Jiang Xiaowan? ‘As for where Jiang Xiaowan’s grave is—I have absolutely no idea. If anyone knows, could you tell me?’ (Yu Qiuyu) History delivers its judgment through silence.

💡
Key Takeaway

Daily crowds visit the Daoist Pagoda, beneath which lies the wound of an ancient civilization. The Dunhuang tragedy was not one man’s crime but a triple tragedy of an era: destruction by an ignorant custodian, plunder by a colonial scholar, and betrayal by a cultural comprador. Yu Qiuyu forces us to ask: when cultural treasures meet a collapsing world, is there really a clear moral boundary between preservation and loss?

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Wenhua Kulv · Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
