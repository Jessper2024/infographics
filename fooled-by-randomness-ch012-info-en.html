Fooled by Randomness · Chapter 12

Gamblers' Superstitions and Caged Pigeons
SCQA Framework: Financial markets are full of random noise. Rational traders search for signals amidst the chaos every day.
But Skinner's 1948 pigeon experiment reveals a brutal truth—when food is delivered entirely at random, pigeons develop complex superstitious rituals.
The central question: When faced with randomness, are highly educated professionals truly more rational than caged pigeons?
The answer is no—knowing probabilities doesn't change behavior. Accept that we are animals, and use practical tricks instead of moral lectures. That is the pragmatic solution.

02
Self-Observation
The Trader's Awakening: I, Too, Am a Superstitious Pigeon

At age 25, the author worked as a derivatives trader in New York. One day, a taxi driver who couldn't understand English drove an extra block, forcing him to enter through the 52nd Street entrance—and that day, volatile exchange rates made his portfolio a lot of money.

🚕 Taxi Entrance Superstition
Behavior

The next day, he deliberately told the driver to drop him at the same entrance—and found himself wearing the same coffee-stained tie

👓 Eyeglasses Superstition
Behavior

After getting glasses, his income rose—though he only needed them for night driving, he kept wearing them, believing deep down in a link to performance

"I was unsettled by how my behavior resembled that of a charlatan, like an actor playing someone else's role. On one hand, I spoke like a probabilist with precise scientific standards; on the other, I was no different from blue-collar floor traders, still clinging to blind superstitions."
— The Author's Self-Reflection

What's more troubling: superstition had quietly seeped from daily "cultural rituals" into professional life. Intelligence grew, randomness kept coming—and superstition only deepened.

03
Psychological Mechanism
Causal Illusion: Our Brains Are Wired for Self-Deception

When observing events A and B, humans find it nearly impossible not to assume "A caused B, B caused A, or they influence each other." This is not a cultural trait—it is biological instinct. We are still processing random events with the "remnants of our old brain."

🧬
Biological, Not Cultural
Superstitions are rooted in biological makeup. Over thousands of years, something inside us hasn't evolved properly.
🕊️
We're No Better Than Pigeons
Skinner's experiment proved that even lower life forms build causal links in random events. Humans are no more sophisticated.
📊
Innate Statistics Can Deceive
"My innate statistical instincts were not suppressed by my expertise in hypothesis testing." Small sample sizes create illusions.
🏛️
Pyrrho's Insight
The Greek philosopher Pyrrho advocated a tranquil life, yet lost composure when chased by a bull. He said: it is hard to entirely abandon one's nature.
The Core Truth: We are born unable to view different events independently. The difference between the ignorant and the intelligent lies only in knowing how to act—but before causal illusion, everyone is equal.

05
Pragmatic Solutions
Embrace Human Nature: Skip the Lectures, Use Behavioral Tricks

The solution isn't about mastering the mind, nor about listening to moralizing. We must first accept that we are merely animals, and that we need low-level tricks to help us—not grand lectures.

❶
Physical Isolation: Don't expose yourself to performance reports until they reach predetermined thresholds. Just like craving chocolate—never keep chocolate under the trading desk.
❷
Accept Your Emotions: Having emotional reactions is normal. Most energy and drive come from emotion. The key is preventing emotions from driving irrational decisions, not suppressing the emotions themselves.
❸
Tricks Over Lectures: Most people know how they should behave. The problem is "not following through." What's needed are low-level behavioral tricks, not the same tired platitudes.
❹
A Cautionary Reference: Smokers know one in three will develop lung cancer, yet nurses at cancer centers still smoke outside the entrance. The gap between knowing a risk and actually changing behavior is far wider than we imagine.
This Chapter's Ultimate Insight: Rational knowledge ≠ behavioral change. We are no smarter than caged pigeons. Admit you're an animal. Use physical isolation and behavioral tricks to bypass instinctual traps—that's what a probabilist should truly be doing.
