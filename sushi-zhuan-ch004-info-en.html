Core QuestionWhen a disheartened literatus was transferred from paradise Hangzhou to locust-ravaged, barren Mizhou, how did he choose between personal despair and his people's survival?

Core AnswerArriving in Mizhou in winter 1074, Su Shi set aside personal grief. With "shrewd competence and diligent governance," he led a locust extermination campaign and drought relief. Confronting corpses in the fields and abandoned children throughout the city, he "wept as he gathered abandoned infants along the city walls" and established a government grain subsidy system that saved thousands of children. He proved that the greatest poetry need not be written on paper — it can be written upon the land itself.

1
Heaven to Hell

From Paradise to Wasteland: Mizhou's Harsh Reality

From warm, prosperous Hangzhou to remote, desolate Mizhou — "bitter winds howled, the landscape lay bleak" — Su Shi sank into melancholy, recalling his youthful ambition of "a thousand characters at the tip of my brush, ten thousand scrolls in my breast," now "face covered in dust, body weary," feeling that "life's road grew ever narrower."

Yet upon entering Mizhou territory, a strange sight caught his eye: though it was the farming off-season, men and women, young and old, still worked in groups in the fields — turning soil deep to bury locust eggs, cutting grass sod to burn into ash — visible for over two hundred li. Su Shi keenly realized: this year's locust invasion would be ferocious. In that moment, "a heavy burden settled on his shoulders, and personal sorrows were set aside."

2
Locust Control

Leading from the Front: "My servant's hands were calloused, my horses exhausted"

Immediately upon taking office, Su Shi investigated and found a shocking figure: peasants had already reported killing 30,000 hu of locusts! Yet local officials remained indifferent, some even flattered superiors by claiming "locusts come to weed the crops for the people" — Su Shi furiously rebutted them, citing what he had personally witnessed in Hangzhou: locusts blotting out the sun.

He formulated a systematic extermination plan: before spring warmth arrived, he mobilized the populace to burn and bury locust larvae, specially allocating grain to reward active participants. He himself "hurried through the fields from dawn to dusk, inspecting and supervising," writing: "Driving out locusts is recorded in the statutes — how can farming matters be neglected? My servant's hands were calloused, my horses were exhausted too."

3
Humanitarian Light

"Weeping, He Gathered Abandoned Infants": Saving Thousands of Children

Years of locusts and drought created widespread famine — "corpses lay everywhere in the fields, abandoned children could be seen all around" — with heavy heart, Su Shi "wept as he gathered abandoned infants along the city walls," then after much effort allocated hundreds of dan of grain specifically for their care, ultimately saving thousands of children teetering on the brink of death.

He posted public notices stipulating: families willing to adopt abandoned children would receive six dou of grain monthly from the government, encouraging people to cherish these small lives. Over time, "adoptive parents developed deep bonds of affection with the children they raised, refusing to abandon them even when life grew harder" — through his great humanitarian spirit, Su Shi rebuilt the warmth of family at the institutional level for these orphans.

4
Governing Banditry

Compassionate Rule: Even "Risking Death as Bandits" Is "Only Natural"

In these famine years, banditry surged. But Su Shi understood deeply that poverty drives rebellion: those who "risk death as bandits" may die; those who "fear the law and refrain from banditry" may also starve — many turning to banditry was "only natural." Merely "expanding rewards for informants and tightening arrest procedures" treats only symptoms, not the root cause.

While formulating rigorous bandit-suppression plans and "sharpening swords to pursue desperate bandits into the valleys," he fundamentally addressed the issue through compassionate governance. He submitted truthful reports to the court about the disaster and called for tax relief. In Su Shi's view, the foundation of public order was simple: let the people survive — only then would they "refrain from banditry even if pushed."

5
Poet's Compassion

"The Elders Asked Me": Shared Joys and Sorrows with Common Folk

During his two years in Mizhou, Su Shi "longed for auspicious snow and sweet rain every bit as anxiously as a farmer": winter snow falling, he thought "buried locusts should enter the earth a thousand feet deep, how many homes will see wheat clouds next year?"; spring rain clearing, he lamented "Heaven's intentions are truly hard to fathom, now it clears into splendid spring weather." Every raindrop and snowflake tugged at his heart because he knew they meant life or death for the common people.

He frequently consulted old farmers for agricultural wisdom, learning that "locusts and drought always reinforce each other" and that Mount Chang prayers for rain were most efficacious. At the crisis peak, he bathed, burned incense, observed vegetarian fast, and made a devout pilgrimage to Changshan. In his poetry, he captured a rare scene of official-commoner intimacy: "The elders asked me: 'Where is our Prefect?' This year has fine wind and snow — we shall see wheat piled high in heaps."

"The rugged paths of the world I have nearly all traversed; in this lonely mountain dwelling, I grow accustomed to growing old" — Amid mountain rain-prayers, Su Shi fused personal loss with compassion for all living beings, completing his tempering from literatus to people-centered official

KEY TAKEAWAY

The two Mizhou years marked Su Shi's transformation from "literary prodigy" to "people-centered official." Plunged from paradise Hangzhou into locust-and-drought-ravaged wasteland, he did not wallow in personal misfortune but poured all his energy into disaster relief: burning and burying locusts, fasting and praying for rain, weeping as he gathered abandoned infants, establishing grain subsidies for adoption. He proved through action: a truly great scholar-official writes immortal verse not only on paper, but upon the land itself — compassion for farmers is the Confucian spirit's highest poetry.
