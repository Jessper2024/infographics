The New Sell & Sell Short · Chapter 5 "Stop Losses"

A stop loss is the seatbelt of your trading system — sign the divorce agreement before you enter, so you can exit mistakes with dignity

The Core Problem

A trading system without stop losses is like racing without a seatbelt

Every trade must have a stop loss — this is the baseline of trading integrity. Yet most traders either refuse to stop out due to the "endowment effect," or place stops at the most obvious level just below the prior low — exactly where professional traders hunt. This chapter systematically answers three questions: Where to set the stop? How to execute it? When to move it? The answers span the Iron Triangle quantification method, Nick's Stop technique, market/hard stop principles, and trailing stop strategies for trending markets.

01
Foundation

The Essence of Stop Losses & the Iron Triangle

A stop loss connects the trader to reality — you must answer a fundamental question: is the potential profit worth the risk? Trading without a stop is gambling. Holding a losing position not only costs money but also consumes attention, preventing you from taking better trades. Like a toothache that steals more focus than expected, a losing position shackles the trader's ability to act.

02
Execution

Market Orders vs. Limit Orders for Stops

All orders fall into two categories: market orders and limit orders. Market orders guarantee execution but not price; limit orders guarantee price but not execution. Traders must choose which matters more — execution or avoiding slippage.

✅ Market Stop (MIT)

Guarantees immediate exit, accepting slippage cost. When trouble arrives, don't haggle — get out. Protective stops must use market orders — missing an exit can lead to far greater losses.

⚠️ Limit Order as Stop

To save a few dollars on slippage, you risk major losses. In fast declines, limit orders won't fill — price can gap down well below your stop. Stops aren't perfect, but they're the best tool we have.

⚠️
Elder's approach: Use limit orders for entries and profit targets ("the market either trades at my price or walks the plank"), but always use MIT market orders for protective stops — never negotiate when you're running for the exit.
03
Discipline

Hard Stops vs. Soft Stops

A hard stop is an order placed in the market, executed by a broker. A soft stop is a price the trader keeps in mind and manually acts upon when reached. Which one you choose depends on your expertise and ability to watch the market.

04
Placement

The Art of Stop Placement: Don't Be the Pros' Prey

Setting a stop one tick below the prior low is the worst approach. That's the most crowded stop zone in the market — professional traders wait to trigger those stops and scoop up discounted shares. A double bottom with bullish divergence, where the second bottom is slightly lower, is often a pro's buy signal but an amateur's stop-out point.

✅ Nick's Stop Method (Recommended)

Find the bar with the lowest low. Take the two adjacent bars, locate the lower of their two lows, and place the stop just below it. This tight-trailing stop tells the market: hit my stop if you must (it's small), or give me my gains.

❌ One Tick Below Prior Low

A cluster of stop orders sits here. When triggered, price cascades like a punctured balloon — heavy slippage. Even worse, price often rebounds immediately after. You were right about direction but still lost money.

💡
Slippage reduction trick: Place the stop exactly at the prior low (not below it). Price rarely stops right at the low — it either bounces before reaching it or breaks through. Setting at the exact low dramatically reduces slippage.

Trend Amplifier

Trailing Stops: The One-Way Street to Bigger Profits

A stop is a one-way street — you can only tighten it, never loosen it. When long, move stops only up; when short, only down. If the price falls and you lower your stop, you are indulging your mistake. Never do that.

💡
Key Takeaway

A stop loss is an inseparable part of any trading system. Before entering, complete the Iron Triangle calculation (per-share risk × max loss → position size). Never place stops at the market's most crowded obvious levels — use Nick's Stop Method to dramatically reduce the risk of being shaken out. For execution, stops must use market orders (MIT), and beginners must use hard stops. In trending markets, the stop is a one-way street that only tightens — master trailing stop strategies to let your profits run.
