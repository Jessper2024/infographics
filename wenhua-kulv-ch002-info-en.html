Wenhua Kulv · Ch. 2 "Temples"

Temple lamps and school lamps lit the countryside together—traditional faith and modern education reached the same destination by different paths

Core insight: Wushan Temple’s Buddha lamps and the school’s kerosene lamp—one sacred, one learned—each illuminated a countryside haunted by banditry. Seemingly opposed, traditional faith and modern education reconciled on the shared ground of ‘cherishing life’—Master Hong Yi (Li Shutong), both artist and monk, is the ultimate proof.

QuestionAre temple and school, traditional faith and modern education, opposites or complements?

AnswerThe teachers went from rejecting the temple to crossing mountains seeking Master Hong Yi, students sang Li Shutong’s songs as a ‘contest’ with monks, and a bandit was transformed inside a temple fair—faith and education reconciled on the shared ground of ‘cherishing life.’ As Teacher He said: ‘Only by coming and going freely do both the person and the place come alive.’

1
Parallel Worlds

Temple Bells and School Bells: Two Civilizations Side by Side

Every morning, grandmothers hobbled to temple to chant sutras while children ran to school—the contrast between the black-garbed, bound-foot matrons and the schoolchildren at the paifang gate captured two civilizations sharing the same village.

Grandmothers changed into clean black robes and walked in a line to Wushan Temple. Children took a different path to school, laughing and playing. Teacher He stood at the gate checking uniforms as temple bells rang. At night, the village had known only bandit lanterns and temple lamps; after banditry faded, the school’s lamplight joined in—evil, sacred, and learned lights together illuminated the darkness.

2
Spiritual Power

Why Did a Bandit Change After Entering the Temple Fair?

A student was kidnapped by a bandit who carried him into a crowded temple fair—and emerged a changed man, returning the boy home. The chanting was ‘like the tidal roar of Shanglin Lake’; the child fell asleep and awoke to find the bandit standing dazed before a Buddha statue. This story made Teacher He reconsider the temple.

At age four, a student was snatched by a bandit. The bandit squeezed through the temple crowd, moving ‘slower than monks chanting sutras.’ After hearing the story, Teacher He reflected: ‘The old bandit was washed away by Shanglin Lake’s tide.’ She then visited the temple herself. ‘She looked slowly. After coming out, she told the student: you can go there more often.’ (Yu Qiuyu)

3
Cultural Unity

Li Shutong Is Master Hong Yi: Tradition and Modernity Are Not Divided

Students sang Li Shutong’s ‘Farewell’ in the temple as a ‘contest’ with the monks. Master Xingchan loved it—and when the teachers learned that Li Shutong was none other than Master Hong Yi, they trekked across mountains to Wulei Temple, discovering that tradition and modernity were never truly separate.

He Ying led the singing: ‘Beyond the long pavilion, along the ancient road, fragrant grass stretches to the sky’s edge…’ Master Xingchan was so moved he wrote ‘Li Shutong’ with his brush. Teacher He leapt up: ‘Li Shutong is Master Hong Yi!’ The three teachers crossed mountains to Wulei Temple. They began to ponder: why would a modern educator embrace Buddhism? ‘Is this regression or elevation? It must be elevation—we are simply too shallow to understand it yet.’ (Yu Qiuyu)

4
Fluidity

‘The Noble Person Cherishes Virtue, the Petty Person Clings to Soil’

When graduating students promised to ‘come back after middle school,’ Teacher He stopped them—‘Don’t make that promise. Only by coming and going freely do both the person and the place come alive.’ Monks, nuns, Master Hong Yi, female teachers—it was the flow of outsiders that revitalized a closed village.

Teacher He quoted Confucius: ‘The junzi cherishes virtue, the petty man cherishes his native soil. Don’t cling too tightly to your hometown.’ As she spoke, temple bells rang and chanting rose softly—‘Alright, the Buddha is saying: time to set out.’ (Yu Qiuyu) Monks protected animals, teachers protected trees and children—two civilizations’ shared ‘cherishing of life’ reached a deeper harmony.

💡
Key Takeaway

Temple and school were not opposites but complements. A bandit transformed inside a temple fair, Master Xingchan admired ‘Farewell,’ and Teacher He crossed mountains seeking Hong Yi—traditional faith and modern education reconciled on the shared ground of ‘cherishing life.’ Yu Qiuyu reminds us: the flow of outsiders (monks, nuns, teachers, Hong Yi) is what truly animates a village.

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Wenhua Kulv · Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
