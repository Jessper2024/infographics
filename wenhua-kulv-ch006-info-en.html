Wenhua Kulv · Ch. 6 "Mogao Caves"

Mogao is a thousand-year-old life—four civilizations layered in the desert, two ‘Mr. Chens’ defended its remaining dignity

Core insight: From 366 CE, over a thousand years of continuous construction, Mogao is not an archaeological specimen but a living history of the Chinese soul, carved in stone, still breathing. The most moving element is not the Tang dynasty’s splendor but the two ‘Mr. Chens’—Chen Yinke, who went blind witnessing ‘our nation’s scholarly heartbreak,’ and Chen Wanli, who with a single tip-off defended Mogao’s last remaining dignity.

QuestionWhy did Mogao make proud men of every era bow their heads for a thousand years?

AnswerBecause it concentrated the power of religion and time: generation after generation of Buddhas ‘stand dignified in the caves, profound yet accessible, smiling,’ cooling the hottest hearts into awe. From the Sixteen Kingdoms to the Yuan dynasty, Indian, Greek, and Chinese civilizations layered atop each other. The Tang dynasty is the most brilliant chapter—‘true joy cannot be repeated.’ But when decline came, the plunderers arrived. Langdon Warner peeled murals with chemical solvent; translator Chen Wanli, with a single tip-off, defended the last dignity.

1
Living Art

Mogao: Not a Thousand-Year Specimen—a Thousand-Year-Old Life

From 366 CE, across Sixteen Kingdoms, Northern Wei, Sui, Tang, Five Dynasties, Song, and Yuan—over a thousand years of accumulation. Buddhist cave art underwent a fusion of three civilizations (India → Greece → China), ultimately ‘connecting to the bloodstream of Chinese history’—this is art history carved in stone, still breathing.

Early Sixteen Kingdoms caves—bodhisattvas with Western Region features, ‘deep lines, bold strokes, strong three-dimensionality, still bare-chested, carrying the heat of the Ganges.’ Wei-Jin period—dark brown hues, elegant slender figures, even a sickly beauty. Sui dynasty—flying apsaras dance, ‘heralding an omnidirectional movement.’ Each generation’s style transitions seamlessly: ‘Generation after generation of Buddhas stand dignified within the caves, profound yet accessible—smiling, joyful, moving, suffering, sacrificing.’ (Yu Qiuyu)

2
Civilizational Crossroads

Three Civilizations Meet in the Desert: Indian · Greek · Chinese

The Buddha cave alone already encapsulates both Indian and Greek civilizations. After Buddhism entered China, it absorbed increasingly rich Chinese secular civilization. Mogao is the only cave complex in the world carrying the visual legacy of three great ancient civilizations simultaneously.

Early Buddha statues show Gandharan Greek sculptural influence—‘one can often find clear traces of Greek sculpture there.’ Buddhism upon entering China ‘immediately understood this was a nation that needed stories told in accessible, vivid ways,’ so cave statuary absorbed Chinese secular civilization. Add to this the Central Asian merchants and Silk Road elements in the murals, and Mogao actually carries the intersection of four civilizations. These caves ‘sit in desolate wilderness, looking unremarkable from afar—who could know the brilliance of great civilizations contained within?’ (Yu Qiuyu)

3
Power & Faith

Why Did Every Warlord Bow Before Mogao?

From Northern Wei royalty to Mongol khans—political factions fought to the death, yet all contributed to Mogao’s preservation. ‘The power of religion and the power of time were enough to cool the hottest hearts, producing awe.’

After the An Lushan Rebellion, Tibetan forces occupying Dunhuang and Zhang Yichao’s army driving them out—mortal enemies—both restored Mogao. The Cao regime of Five Dynasties made major contributions; Western Xia and Mongol rule caused no damage. Buddhism here shed profound doctrine to connect with common people: karma, blessings, prosperity. ‘Yet popularization did not reduce it to a village temple fair—learned monks, eminent scholars, and illustrious families always sustained Mogao’s spiritual core.’ (Yu Qiuyu)

4
Tang Dynasty Peak

Tang Dynasty Joy: True Humanity Cannot Be Formulaic

Mogao’s Tang caves never repeat, never stiffen—women dominate, confidence makes expressions serene, even the folds of robes flow like music. ‘True joy cannot be repeated, just as true humanity cannot tolerate rigidity.’

Tang murals reveal the bone-strength of Wu Daozi’s school and the landscape brilliance of the Li Sixun-Li Zhaodao tradition. ‘Joy thus entered the classical canon—and even in the canon, it kept rejoicing, without a trace of pretense.’ (Yu Qiuyu) The An Lushan Rebellion was the watershed—afterward, murals added themes of helping exiled parents restore their kingdom. The Song dynasty brought cold impoverishment; the Yuan introduced the mystery and terror of Tibetan Tantric Buddhism.

5
Plunder & Protection

Warner’s Chemical Solvent vs. Chen Wanli’s Tip-Off

Harvard scholar Langdon Warner peeled murals off with chemical solvent. On his second trip, he brought a solvent specialist—but an ordinary translator, Chen Wanli, ‘left the very next day after arriving at Dunhuang, claiming his mother was ill,’ and tipped off the villagers. Dozens of villagers showed up to monitor; not a single drop of solvent was used.

Warner’s first stolen Mogao murals caused a sensation in the West; he regretted not bringing enough solvent. On his return, he was met by ‘dozens of villagers who dropped their work and came from fifteen kilometers away to monitor our movements… ready to attack or expel us by force.’ Chen Wanli was not just a translator but a doctor and scholar. Another Mr. Chen—Chen Yinke, later completely blind—said: ‘Dunhuang is the history of our nation’s scholarly heartbreak.’ ‘The artifacts Chen Wanli and the villagers could not save still drift far away… we gaze at them through thick museum glass, with long stares and whispered farewells.’ (Yu Qiuyu)

💡
Key Takeaway

Mogao is cultural sedimentary rock layered by four civilizations (Indian, Greek, Chinese, and Silk Road). What moves us most is not only the artistic brilliance but the two ‘Mr. Chens’—Chen Yinke, who went blind witnessing ‘our nation’s scholarly heartbreak,’ and Chen Wanli, who with a single tip-off defended Mogao’s remaining dignity. Cultural protection has never been the work of experts alone.

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Wenhua Kulv · Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
