Core Insight: In Hangzhou's "Heaven on Earth," Su Dongpo transformed hardship into poetry and navigated contradictions with humor—living his most radiant life under the shadow of political strife.

Core QuestionHangzhou was paradise, but the New Policies harmed the people and prisons overflowed—how could a poet find his footing between beauty and reality?

Core AnswerHe immortalized West Lake as Xi Shi in verse, found both wisdom and joy among monks and courtesans, stayed true to himself through his wife's trust, and ultimately transcended political bitterness with poetry and wit.

01
Poetry

West Lake as Xi Shi

One quatrain defined West Lake for a millennium—comparing it to the legendary beauty Xi Shi.

"The shimmering water delights under clear skies; misty mountains enchant in rain. I would compare West Lake to the Lady of the West—equally lovely whether in light makeup or full splendor." Whether sunny or rainy, the lake had intoxicating charm. Su Dongpo shaped its scenery with perfect artistry—Su Causeway, the Three Pools Mirroring the Moon, and willow-lined banks remain his legacy. He grasped nature's contours and worked minimally, only tightening, loosening, or strengthening a line here and there.

Hangzhou won his affection, and he won the people's love. A thousand years after his death, lakeside shopkeepers still mention "Su Dongpo—Su Dongpo." Tell them he was from Sichuan, and they'll be displeased.

02
City Life

A City of Pleasures

Hangzhou was a "Heaven on Earth"—Su Dongpo immersed himself fully in the city's daily delights.

From his hillside residence on Phoenix Hill, he could see boats on the Qiantang River, misty West Lake, temples, and villas. Night markets ran until two or three in the morning with candy vendors blowing sugar into animal shapes. Boatmen caught fish for tourists to release as Buddhist merit. Spring and autumn saw all of Hangzhou playing on the lake; even snowy winter days drew sightseers. Su Dongpo went out with family or friends, often returning half-drunk through brilliantly lit night markets.

The Song-era book "Dreaming of Hangzhou" by Wu Zimu listed exquisite snacks in far greater detail than Marco Polo's later account. Su Dongpo almost believed he had lived there in a previous life—he once correctly described 92 stone steps leading to a temple hall he'd never visited before.

03
Company

Monks & Courtesans

To Su Dongpo, the sensual life and the spiritual life were one and the same—monks and singing girls, wisdom and pleasure in harmony.

He once brought a singing girl into the temple of the strict Abbot Datong, made her sing an apology poem, and even the stern old monk burst out laughing. He traded witty repartee with the monk Foyin—"Who does Guanyin worship?" "Herself—it's better to rely on oneself." He judged the case of Monk Liaoran, who killed a courtesan, writing a verdict in verse. He persuaded the talented courtesan Qincao to leave her trade and become a nun. The twelve-year-old Chaoyun later became his concubine.

"Whose tune does the Master sing? Whose school does he follow? Lend your clappers and drumsticks—I'm just improvising, don't be suspicious." — Su Dongpo teasing Abbot Datong

Song courtesans pioneered the new verse form ci (lyrics). Su Dongpo elevated the genre from love songs to a vehicle for any thought or emotion—his finest lyrics would later depict Red Cliff.

04
Family

A Wise Wife's Harbor

Madame Su protected her husband with the cleverest strategy—complete trust, never pushing him toward the courtesans.

She knew her husband was someone neither wife nor emperor could restrain. She wrote no poetry and sang no songs—she simply cooked his favorite Meizhou dishes and ginger soup. When he was restless and depressed, she said: "Come, let me warm some wine for you." He once wrote a poem thanking her, saying she was far better than the wife of Liu Ling, who forbade her husband to drink. On a spring moonlit night she remarked: "I like the spring moon. The autumn moon is too sad. The spring moon makes everyone happy and content."

🏠 Her Wisdom

Daughter of a scholar, literate but uncompetitive. At night she endured his famous snoring—especially when he'd been drinking—as he wrapped and arranged himself carefully before sleep.

💭 A Secret Love

Su Dongpo's feelings for his cousin were buried in two poems. "Tired of chasing youthful pleasures, I idly face palace flowers and recognize an old fragrance." He once stayed three months at her home—yet never mentioned her husband.

05
Nature

A Child of Nature

A great philosopher who was no puritan, a Confucian who was no drunkard—Su Dongpo possessed a "uniquely wholesome, mystical outlook on life."

He often wandered alone in the hills, composing poems on remote peaks. At temples he would borrow a monk's reclining chair and move it into a bamboo grove, stripping off his official robes to nap half-naked. Young monks peeped in awe at this great scholar and saw—or thought they saw—seven black moles on his back arranged like the Big Dipper. On the Double Ninth Festival, he refused the customary banquet and went boating alone, rising before dawn to visit two monks on Solitary Hill.

"West Lake is the finest spring under heaven; visitors come, wise and foolish alike. Each takes what their depth allows—who can know it fully?" — Su Dongpo to Chao Duanyan

So close was he to nature, the seasons, rain, snow, mountains, and valleys—accepting their healing—that he could never have possessed a closed mind or a constrained outlook.

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

Heaven on Earth is not a place but a state of mind. Su Dongpo's three years in Hangzhou prove that true freedom lies in finding—amid circumstances you cannot change—a corner where you can fully express yourself: through art, beauty, and humor.
