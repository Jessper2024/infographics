苏东坡传 · 第29章「Return North」

In 1100, Emperor Zhezong died at just 24. Huizong succeeded him, a new Empress Dowager took power, and all Yuanyou officials were pardoned. Su could finally leave Hainan — but faced a new choice: join his brother in Yingchang, or return to his Changzhou lakeside farm? Yet summer heat and river dampness were silently consuming his life. How much time did the returning exile have left?

01
Era of Change

Death of Zhezong

Zhezong died at just 24, leaving behind a depleted generation of scholars. Huizong ascended and pardoned the Yuanyou faction — Su was finally permitted to leave Hainan.

Huizong would later leave behind 31 sons, several fine paintings, and a nation in chaos — employing the same faction, continuing state capitalism. But in this brief window, a new Empress Dowager protected Yuanyou scholars. When the wandering Taoist Wu Fugu brought news of the pardon, he vanished again — a lifetime of voluntary wandering.

02
Journey Home

Triumph and Farewell

Su's journey north was a triumph — everywhere crowds welcomed him, begged for his poetry and calligraphy. But it was also a journey of intense farewells: six servants died of plague, old friend Wu Fugu closed his eyes before him.

Reuniting with family in Guangzhou, he wrote 'as if waking from a long dream.' Stranded in Gan County for 70 days, he treated locals with medicine. When someone joked they thought he had died, Su replied: 'Indeed — I died, met Zhang Chun on the way to the underworld, and decided to turn back.' Pure Su Dongpo humor — never losing wit even facing death.

03
Final Journey

Killed by Amoeba, Not Exile

Su Dongpo did not die at the hands of emperor or Zhang Chun, but from a microscopic amoeba — June heat, riverbank humidity, he fell ill on his boat.

On the third of the sixth month, he likely contracted amoebic dysentery, self-diagnosing 'excessive cold ingestion.' He took astragalus root — in Chinese medicine, a tonic for blood and organs. The painter Mi Fu visited several times, bringing 'ophiopogon soup.' 'Last night I didn't close my eyes, sat upright feeding mosquitoes,' he wrote in his final letter. His 64-year-old body could endure no more.

04
Final Moments

Farewell to a Brother

Su Dongpo's final regret was not seeing his brother — 'I resent that Heaven won't let us reunite — what can I do against Heaven?' He spent his last days in Changzhou.

Ziyou had returned to the Yingchang farm, urging Dongpo to join him. Su wanted to but hesitated — thirty family members would burden his brother. He decided to settle in Changzhou first, then reunite. But on July 28, 1101 AD, he passed away. Huizong would later lose his throne and die a Jurchen captive. Su Dongpo's name, however, remains forever in the Chinese heart.

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

Su Dongpo proved with his life: a man can be defeated but never destroyed. He died from an amoeba — an enemy too small to see — not from political persecution. This seems anticlimactic, yet reveals life's deepest truth: the greatest enemy is never external suffering, but mortality itself. In 64 years, Su lived a longer life than most emperors — because his name still lives in every Chinese heart today.
