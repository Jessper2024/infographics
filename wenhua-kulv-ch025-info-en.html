Cultural Perplexity · Ch.25 "A Eulogy for Mother"

A woman who never raised her voice in ninety years answered every suffering with a smile and silence

Core insight: Mother's greatness lies not in what she did dramatically, but in how she quietly dissolved every hardship with a lifetime of smiling, listening, and silence — proving a strength that needs no resistance, a power deeper than any sharpness.

QuestionHow did a daughter of a wealthy urban family support a household of eight through war, famine, and the Cultural Revolution — while raising a son who would dare to stand alone against the world?

AnswerFive "unsolvable puzzles" thread through mother's entire life. She never explained, never complained, never shed a tear (except the day father died). What she upheld was not the argument over right and wrong, but something more fundamental — the Way of Heaven and the order of things, and the simple creed: "Whatever others do, just live on well."

1
Five Puzzles

How does one woman's life answer the impossible?

Mother left behind five "forever unsolvable" puzzles — each seemingly impossible, yet she accomplished them all without ever explaining how.

1

Returning to the village: A wealthy city daughter chose to live alone in an impoverished village to support her family during wartime — every detail of daily life reverted to the Stone Age.

2

Single-handedly enlightening: As the only literate person for miles, she ran literacy classes and wrote letters and kept accounts for every household — though no one asked her to.

3

Early education: She completed her eldest son's foundational education in that barren village, and at age seven had him replace her as the village scribe — a decision clearly meant to cultivate a sense of responsibility.

4

Anticipated success: When her son won first place in the citywide essay competition and a major math prize, she showed no surprise — as if it had all been part of her plan.

5

The 26-yuan riddle: During the Cultural Revolution, a family of eight survived on 26 yuan per month — a mathematically impossible equation that she somehow solved day by day, and no one starved.

2
Power of Silence

What lay behind her smile?

Mother's smile and shyness were not weakness — quite the opposite, she achieved a "special kind of strength": there exists another language between heaven and earth, not of resistance, but of silent perseverance.

When "revolutionary masses" came to threaten that "if father doesn't confess, he'll die without a burial place," she listened with her head lowered, never contradicting, thinking only of the next meal. When her uncle was hounded to death, she held the urn in silence, letting them berate her however they pleased.

Yu Qiuyu later understood: those people were not worth debating. "Once you debate, you enter their logical system and inevitably fall into their trap. Mother upheld a different set of fundamental human principles — the Way of Heaven. Only silence can make room for Heaven's Way, and leave space for history's judgment."

3
Life's Support

What did those warm hands carry?

Mother's hands, mother's feet, mother's shoulders — these bodily images formed the entire life-support system of a family in catastrophe.

🖐 The warmth of two yuan

During the Cultural Revolution, mother walked to her son's college, found him long unfed, and amid blaring loudspeakers, pressed her warm palm against his — a two-yuan note wedged between. He later discovered she had been washing iron sheets barefoot in winter at a small factory, drenched from head to toe.

💪 Shoulders carrying a table

The family ancestral altar table seemed to move by itself — mother was underneath it, carrying it on her shoulders. Family members had been imprisoned, driven to death, or exiled. Not a single hand to help.

🍱 The gray food bag

No matter how illustrious the son's reputation grew, every few days mother would arrive with a gray food bag slung over her shoulder to cook for him. "Fully half the weight of everything was borne by those aging shoulders."

4
Mountain Path

Where does audacity come from?

At age six, mother crossed two mountains to visit her uncle's home. Wanting to surprise her, Yu Qiuyu set out alone across the mountains to meet her — there were wild animals, and he was not afraid. When mother saw him on the mountain path, she was not startled, not reproachful, not inquisitive. She simply said with delight, "Qiuyu has come," took his hand, and walked home warmly together.

The scene perfectly fits Brecht's play title: Mother Courage and Her Children. Yu Qiuyu says: "All the audacity of my life, at its root, comes from mother." When, years later, he risked his life traveling tens of thousands of kilometers through terrorism-ridden regions to investigate ancient civilizations, international media called him "the world's most courageous humanities professor" — "but every one of those steps was still led by mother's warm hand from all those years ago."

Mother refused all collective grief. At father's funeral ten years earlier, she did not shed a tear throughout — only back home, before father's tiny desk, did she suddenly burst into violent sobs, crying like a little girl. "All the tears of her life ended with father."

💡
Key Takeaway

Unlike father's Confucian creed of "cultivate the self, regulate the family, govern the state, bring peace to the world," mother chose a more fundamental power — whatever others do, just live on well. She proved that true strength needs no shouting; a smile in silence can dissolve all malice. She embodies a neglected wisdom in Chinese culture: answering the age not with words but with life itself.

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Cultural Perplexity (文化苦旅), Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
