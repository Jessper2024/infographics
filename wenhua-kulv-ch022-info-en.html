Cultural Perplexity · Ch.22 "Huang Zuolin's Last Words"

When a city forgot its greatest theater master at the turn of the century, his final testament traveled 56 years to become another man's raft across dark waters

Core insight: Huang Zuolin embodied a lifetime proof that "art shines brightest in darkness" — from bidding farewell to George Bernard Shaw to serve his nation in peril, to proposing the world-shaking "Three Dramatic Perspectives" during a famine, he answered history's cruelest questions with art.

QuestionWhy was a Chinese master who made milestone contributions to modern world theater theory collectively forgotten during the centennial celebration of Chinese spoken drama?

AnswerBecause he always insisted on "only doing art, never being a tool," refusing to attach himself to any political faction. This independence made him marginalized through successive political campaigns and rendered him unplaceable in official cultural history — but it also gave him the most complete and beautiful artistic life of all.

1
1937 Choice

Why did he go back when war broke out?

Three days after the July 7 Incident, Shaw's protégé Huang Zuolin came not to ask his teacher to help him stay in England — but to say goodbye. He was returning to a homeland turning into a battlefield.

Shaw was astonished: "It's already a battlefield... you're not a general or a soldier, what are you going back for?" Huang could not explain the weighty Chinese concept of fu guonan — "answering the nation's peril" — in English. He simply said: "When something like this happens, most of us Chinese go back."

Shaw wrote: "Arise, China! The future of the Eastern world is yours. If you have perseverance and courage, it will be Chinese drama that makes the coming pageant more magnificent."

Fifty-six years later, at age 80, Huang recounted this scene to Yu Qiuyu in Singapore with crystal clarity. A theater apprentice chose to walk toward the gunfire at his country's darkest hour.

2
Island Miracle

How was a golden age of theater created under bombardment?

Art's greatest mission is to warm hearts in a cold world in chaos — Huang Zuolin inaugurated the golden age of Chinese theater in Shanghai's "solitary island" period.

One relatively minor production, The Inspector, ran 77 performances in 40 days. His lead actor Shi Hui was hailed as the "Emperor of Spoken Drama" — a title no one has surpassed to this day. Veteran artists recall: "Those performances were the best they could possibly be."

This confirms Yu Qiuyu's thesis: the noblest art does not come from massive investment, official patronage, or media campaigns. Rather, it is often a direct answer to harsh circumstances. Art's best backdrop is not gold, but black.

3
Theory Landmark

Why is the "Three Perspectives" theory a milestone?

On April 25, 1962, Huang published "A Casual Talk on 'Dramatic Perspectives'" — proposing that Stanislavski, Brecht, and Mei Lanfang represent the three great dramatic perspectives of the 20th century. It is a theoretical creation that the world has accepted.

Five contributions: framing theater history through "illusion" and "breaking illusion"; borrowing the "fourth wall" concept to demarcate theatrical space; elevating Brecht's innovative function to "historical periodization"; the grand framework of the three perspectives; and the aesthetic creation transitioning toward "xieyi" (ideographic) dramatic perspective.

Yu Qiuyu asked cultural figures across the country: in the past decades, has any achievement in Chinese cultural theory surpassed it? The answer: not a single one. Yet today's media, when listing "memorable cultural events," never mention this essay.

4
Character Stands

How did he draw the line with art as his compass?

During faculty title reviews, Huang Zuolin softly asked in a Hamlet-like line: "To create work, or to destroy people — that is the question." With one sentence, he vetoed the "attack specialists" from every political campaign.

On Cultural Revolution rebels: "We are not political examiners; we only evaluate professional work. But how can art be separated from character?" On performers from "Revolutionary Model Operas": "They should know what kind of torment their peers were suffering. They cannot pretend not to have seen it."

Yet Huang's greatest pain came from realizing he had started caring about politics. His answer was to return to himself: "Whatever they say, I'm returning to art." — A great artist is ultimately a child.

5
Last Testament

What did the testament that crossed half a century say?

In 1993, from his hospital bed at age 87, Huang wrote to Yu Qiuyu — then besieged by a national media frenzy — gifting him the three lines carved on Shaw's mantelpiece: "They have said. What said they? Let them say!"

These three lines traveled 56 years — from Shaw to Huang Zuolin, and on to Yu Qiuyu — becoming a raft across heavy black waters. Huang added about himself: "I am 87 this year, yet still unaware that old age approaches. I still dream of writing a book."

Huang died the following year. Yu Qiuyu wrote: "He gave Chinese theater, Chinese art, Chinese culture, and the Chinese people one more reason to be proud. He was a towering peak, adding an extra mast to the battered ship of modern Chinese culture — a mast weathered by wind and rain, pointing straight to the sky, visible from near and far."

💡
Key Takeaway

Huang Zuolin was one of the very few Chinese artistic giants to have lived a "complete and beautiful life" — answering the nation's peril, creating a golden age, proposing the Three Perspectives theory, interrogating the intellectual conscience, and enjoying a childlike yet revered old age. His forgetting is not his regret, but the regret of Chinese cultural history. A history of Chinese spoken drama that has lost Huang Zuolin "would make even the souls of Jiao Juyin, Cao Yu, Tian Han, and Lao She panic in heaven."

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Cultural Perplexity (文化苦旅), Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
