Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 26 "Appendix: Jesse Livermore Chronology"

Jesse Livermore's life is the most legendary canvas in the world of speculation — entered the trade at 14 as an office boy, rose and fell four times, made $1 million shorting in 1907, profited $100 million in the 1929 crash, and ended in bankruptcy and tragedy. This chronology records not just his trading milestones but serves as a mirror reflecting the fate of every speculator.

▎S — Situation
Born July 1877 in South Acton, Massachusetts. At 14, worked as an office boy at Paine Webber, copying the latest prices from the ticker tape onto quotation boards. At 15, executed his first trade — Burlington Steel, netting a profit of $3.12. A legend was born.
▎C — Conflict
Livermore's life encompassed four great rises and falls: from bucket-shop prodigy to NYSE bankruptcy, from millionaire to debt-ridden, from resurgence to final tragedy — each rise planted the seeds of the next fall. He wrote down every trading principle one must follow, yet could not consistently adhere to them in practice.
▎Q — Key Question
What enabled the legendary trader's repeated successes? And what caused his ultimate failure and self-destruction?
▎A — Answer
Livermore's successes were without exception rooted in: relying entirely on his own judgment, strictly following his trading method, going with the market trend, and cutting losses decisively without hesitation. His failures were without exception rooted in: listening to others' opinions, violating his own trading rules, adding to losing positions instead of cutting losses, and departing from the objective judgment of the ticker tape. The code behind his four rises and four falls was exactly the same — the only difference was whether he remained faithful to his own principles.
01
First Rise & Fall

1891–1901: From Prodigy to Bankruptcy

At 14, Livermore worked as an office boy at Paine Webber. At 15, he traded Burlington Steel for a $3.12 profit. He then accumulated $1,000 then $10,000 at bucket shops — "the first rising phase."

At 21, banned from bucket shops for overtrading, he took $2,500 to Wall Street for the first time. At the NYSE he suffered his first Waterloo: the exchange's delayed order execution was incompatible with his rapid-fire trading style, and he lost all his funds — "the first falling phase." He himself concluded: "The tape was so far behind the actual market that it killed my trading."

At 23 (May 9, 1901), he went from $50,000 back to bankruptcy in a single day — "The numbers printed on the tape were worlds apart from the actual prices, and ruined me." This devastating loss cost him dearly but also made him realize: when the trading environment changes, the method must change with it. His first lesson: Don't cling stubbornly to familiar tools.

02
Second & Third Rises & Falls

1901–1914: A Millionaire's Two Downfalls

After returning to bucket shops and fake brokerages for a year, Livermore again accumulated substantial funds, bought an automobile, and enjoyed a luxurious life — his second rise. In 1906, acting on a premonition before the San Francisco earthquake, he shorted the market for a $250,000 profit. On October 24, 1907, during the market crash, he shorted heavily and earned his first $1 million, buying a yacht — the peak of "the third rising phase."

However, in 1908, meeting Percy Thomas became the greatest turning point of his career. Led into a state of blind following by this "commodity expert of extraordinary persuasiveness," Livermore committed his most fatal error: he violated his trading rules by buying cotton, then violated them further by adding to the losing position, while simultaneously selling his profitable wheat position — "the result was bankruptcy."

From millionaire to bankrupt again — all because of one betrayal of his principles. The rule he would later emphasize throughout the book — "don't listen to tips" — was precisely where he himself took his biggest fall.

💀
The Most Expensive Lesson: "Don't listen to tips" is a principle Livermore repeatedly warns readers about in the book — yet he violated it completely with Percy Thomas. Even the master who wrote down this wisdom could not escape the trap of human weakness. This precisely proves the book's most important thesis: in the future, people will continue to repeat the mistakes of the past.
03
Fourth Rise & Fall

1914–1940: The Final Glory and the End

In 1914, Livermore declared bankruptcy — "Freed from the burden of debt, my mind and body are now liberated to devote themselves entirely to trading." After several years of a dull market he rose again. In 1917 he repaid all his debts. During the 1929 Crash he shorted the market for a $100 million profit, reaching the absolute peak of his wealth — his fourth and final brilliant rise.

Yet in 1934 he was bankrupt again. On November 28, 1940, Livermore shot himself at New York's Sherry-Netherland Hotel, leaving behind the note: "My life has been a failure." A legend who went bankrupt four times and rose four times — his ultimate enemy was not the market, but himself. In an irrepressible trading compulsion and a constant departure from his principles, he walked step by step into the abyss.

04
Pattern Summary

The Same Code Behind Every Rise and Fall

Looking across Livermore's four rises and four falls, a clear pattern runs throughout:

📈 Rising Phase = Following Principles

Relying entirely on his own judgment; strictly following his trading method; going with the market trend; cutting losses decisively without hesitation. These four points run through all his successes from age 15 to 53.

📉 Falling Phase = Departing from Principles

Listening to others' opinions (Ed Harding, Percy Thomas); violating his own trading rules; adding to losing positions instead of cutting losses; departing from the objective judgment of the ticker tape.

Livermore's entire life verified his own most famous statement: "The principles that guide success in stock speculation are based on the assumption that in the future, people will continue to repeat the mistakes they have made in the past." His greatest tragedy was that he himself became the most thorough footnote to this very statement.

“
My life has been a failure.
— Jesse Livermore, November 28, 1940
💡
Key Takeaway · BLUF

The Livermore chronology is the most profound parable in the world of speculation: four rises came from "following principles"; four falls came from "departing from principles." Even the master who wrote down this wisdom could not consistently adhere to his own principles in the heat of battle. The ultimate lesson of this chronology is not to teach you how to trade — but to warn you: the greatest enemy of trading has never been the market, but the person in the mirror. His life proved in blood that knowing the principles and following the principles are two entirely different things.
