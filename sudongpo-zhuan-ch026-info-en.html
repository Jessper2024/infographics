苏东坡传 · 第26章「Life in Huizhou」

Su Dongpo arrived in Huizhou to find oranges, sugarcane, and lychee orchards everywhere — nothing like the miasmic wasteland imagined. 'Spring colors in every household.' But when the court declared Yuanyou officials 'never to be reinstated,' all hope of returning north vanished. Banished forever, how does one build a life in the abyss?

▎CORE QUESTION

Su Dongpo decided to become 'a Huizhou man.' He built White Crane Residence atop a small hill — twenty elegant rooms with citrus, lychee, bayberry, and loquat trees. He practiced alchemy, health cultivation, solitary sleep, fasting for hemorrhoids, and built a life-release pond with Chao Yun. When he wrote 'Suppose I were just a Huizhou scholar who never passed the exams,' his transformation to settler was complete.
01
Building Home

White Crane Residence

Su Dongpo built White Crane Residence atop a four-zhang hill east of the river — half an acre, twenty elegant rooms, with a stunning view of the river curving northeast. This house still stands today.

The house adapted to narrow terrain, with two kind neighbors — Scholar Zhai and old Mrs. Lin the wine-maker. He dug a four-zhang well for shared use; neighbors let him drink on credit. The south yard bloomed with citrus, pomelo, lychee, bayberry, loquat, cypress, and gardenia.

02
Health Cultivation

The Study of Pure Thought

Su named his study 'The Studio of Pure Thought,' believing simplicity and pure thought were the foundation of longevity — he slept alone for eighteen months, fasted for months to treat hemorrhoids, yet discovered glory and immortality can't coexist.

He discussed cinnabar preservation with his brother but also wrote 'the golden elixir cannot be made.' He mocked: 'Bai Juyi tried alchemy too — his furnace broke the day he accepted a new post. Glory and immortality cannot coexist.' His health wisdom ultimately returned to simplicity: 'Guard the One, nurture with harmony,' plus spring water and morning sun.

03
Social Network

Huizhou Circle

Five neighboring prefects sent constant gifts of wine and food; the monk Canliao dispatched messengers with medicine from Hangzhou; the Buddhist devotee Zhuo Qishun walked 700 li from Suzhou with family letters — exile was not lonely.

Prefect Zhan Fan of Huizhou and Magistrate Lin Bian of Boluo became his closest friends. When Canliao offered to visit in person, Su humorously replied he was now 'like a Lingyin Temple monk who retired to a village courtyard, cooking coarse rice in a broken pot.' Though banished, Su's personal magnetism ensured he was never short of friends.

04
Spiritual Shift

Redefining Identity

'Suppose I were just a Huizhou scholar who never passed the exams — what's wrong with that?' — Su Dongpo's greatest spiritual victory in the face of permanent exile.

In 1095, a general amnesty was declared — but Yuanyou officials were excluded. The news paradoxically settled Su's mind. He wrote: 'I've abandoned hope of returning north. Yet my heart is profoundly at peace.' To another friend: 'Since return has no date, I now call myself a Huizhou man.' He had fully accepted a new identity — not an exiled convict, but a free resident.

"
Seeing recent events, I've abandoned all hope of returning north. Yet my heart is profoundly at peace — not through profound philosophy, but simply: suppose I were just a Huizhou scholar who never passed the exams — what's wrong with that?
— Su Dongpo, Letter to Cheng Zhicai

KEY TAKEAWAY

The twenty rooms of White Crane Residence were not built with bricks and tiles, but with a heart at peace. In Huizhou, Su Dongpo accomplished the most remarkable thing: not building a house, but building a new self. When he said 'suppose I were just a Huizhou scholar,' he embodied the highest wisdom — finding peace wherever you are. Not passive endurance, but active creation.
