苏东坡传 · 第24章「Fighting Famine」

Recalled to the capital amid hostile enemies, Su was demoted to Yingzhou within three months. What he saw was more shocking than politics: peasants stripping bark from elm trees to make gruel, families fleeing prosperous harvests to escape debt collectors. Against a system that trapped people at every turn, what could one man's protest achieve?

▎CORE QUESTION

Su Dongpo spent two years petitioning the court to forgive twenty years of accumulated civilian debt. He exposed the absurdity: millions ruined, trade paralyzed, the government wealthy while the nation went bankrupt. Though bureaucrats obstructed, memorials went missing, and decisions were endlessly delayed, he never stopped fighting.
01
Systemic Failure

Trapped: Famine or Prison

The aftermath of Wang Anshi's reforms created an absurd trap: starve in lean years, go to prison in good years — Chinese peasants had to 'choose' between them.

Su wrote in his Yangzhou memorial: 'Sick in both famine and plenty.' Green wheat fields stood beside empty farmhouses — peasants dreaded harvests because officials came to collect loan principal with interest. Trade collapsed, wine-tax fell from 300,000 to under 200,000 strings. Government records were chaos.

⚠️
Double bind: Starve in lean years, die in prison in good years. The peasants' plight was not natural disaster but man-made catastrophe — every policy harvested those at the bottom.
02
Bureaucratic Obstruction

108 Days of Silence

Su Dongpo's memorial requesting debt forgiveness met 108 days of silence. When he followed up, the Ministry of Revenue claimed the document was 'lost' and asked him to resubmit.

He resubmitted with notes explaining that only restoring public credit could revive revenue. Nearly two more years passed with no action. Bureaucrats played word games over 'registered seizure' vs. 'discounted seizure' — a semantic distinction that cost countless families their homes.

03
On the Ground

New Year's Eve Rescue

On New Year's Eve, Su Dongpo climbed the city wall, saw 20,000 refugees trudging through deep snow, stayed awake all night planning relief, and woke his colleague at dawn for immediate action.

'I couldn't sleep all night,' Su told Zhao Lingshi. 'We must find a way to save them.' Zhao proposed using thousands of dan of grain from public granaries and hundreds of thousands of jin of coal. 'Good,' Su said. 'We act now.' He continued writing to stop grain taxes so food could move freely.

04
Historical Lesson

One Man's War

Su pointed out: had his preventive measures been adopted earlier, the cost would have been less than half of current relief spending — 'Small minds save money for the court but not lives for the sovereign.'

The 1092 famine was devastating — over half the population of Suzhou, Huzhou, and Xiuzhou perished. 'Land with no people, people with no grain, grain with no seed, seed with no oxen.' He estimated a decade for recovery even with full government support.

KEY TAKEAWAY

Su Dongpo's famine-fighting journey reveals an eternal truth: good policies need people of conscience to implement them. When institutions become shackles and bureaucrats become obstacles, one person's persistence may not change the entire system — but it shows posterity what justice looks like. He spent two years writing unanswered letters. That silence, in itself, was the loudest answer.
