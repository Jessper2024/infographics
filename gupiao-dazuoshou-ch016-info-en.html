Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 16

Government Intervention Tips the Scales—Unforeseen Disasters Always Lurk

A · Answer: Add "unforeseeable calamities" to the list of stumbling blocks. Never forget that "profit not yet deposited in the bank doesn't count." Always leave ample room for black swans in every trade.
01
Background

Coffee Fundamentals: The Logic Was Flawless

This wasn't speculation—it was a conservative investment. Coffee was the only commodity that hadn't risen, and supply reduction was inevitable.

After war broke out, all commodities rose 100%–400%—except coffee. The reason: the European market was closed, flooding the U.S. with massive coffee shipments. But German submarine warfare was escalating, and cargo ships were dwindling fast. The supply-demand deduction was crystal clear: imports falling → inventory depletion → prices rising. Livermore considered this "a banker's operation, not a gambler's bet."

02
Surprise

The Underdog's Ploy: The Truth Behind the "Patriotic Petition"

The shorts packaged themselves as patriots "protecting America's breakfast," successfully winning government price controls on the only commodity that hadn't risen.

The German-American roasters who had shorted coffee to Livermore knew they couldn't deliver—the coffee they'd bought in Brazil had no ships to carry it. They went to the War Industries Board with a "patriotic petition": claiming Livermore was cornering coffee and would force Americans to pay sky-high prices. The government was compelled to set a maximum coffee price and a final settlement deadline—effectively shutting down the exchange.

⚠️ The irony: Green coffee beans remained stuck at 9¢/lb, while roasted coffee followed every other commodity straight up. The real beneficiaries were the coffee roasters—American consumers ultimately paid even higher prices for their coffee.
03
Impact

Millions in Profit Vanish into Thin Air

The price-fixing panel didn't even wait for coffee to start rising before stomping on the brakes.

Livermore was forced by government order to sell all his contracts—the millions in profit that, exactly as he'd predicted, should have been his, vanished. Even more ironic: when the coffee exchange reopened after the price controls, coffee had risen to 23¢ a pound—because the original ceiling was too low to cover the high shipping costs needed to guarantee imports. The American public still paid high prices, and Livermore got nothing.

❌ Result of price controls

Exchange shut down → supply couldn't arrive → price eventually hit 23¢ → the public paid more

✅ If left unregulated

Coffee rises naturally → high prices attract supply → price stabilizes at a reasonable level → Livermore earns his deserved profit

04
Warning

The Speculator's Force Majeure Checklist

In speculation, you can never be absolutely certain of anything—black swans are always lurking.

Livermore solemnly added "unforeseeable calamities" to his list of speculative stumbling blocks: government price controls (coffee), exchange rule changes (twice encountered), unforeseeable sudden events (the Lusitania, the German submarine ultimatum). Every time, he stood on the right side of the market using honest, fair methods—yet profit was stripped away.

He also delivered a scathing critique of the "bear raid" myth: of 100 so-called bear raids, 99 are simply the acceleration phase of a legitimate decline. It's merely a psychological prop—a "tip" in reverse—providing comfort to those who refuse to think and want an excuse for their losses.

💡
Key Takeaway

Forces outside the market—government, war, rule changes—can overturn a perfect trade at any moment. Profit only counts once it's in the bank. A speculator's margin of safety should cover not just price fluctuations but force majeure as well. Crying over spilled milk is a waste of time, but adding each "unforeseeable" event to your checklist is the only correct response.
