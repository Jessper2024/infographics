1
Identity Shift

Tilling Dongpo: From Scholar-Official to Farmer

Su Shi spent most of his time on the Eastern Slope — tilling by day, reading and writing at Snow Hall by night. A wine flask always at his side, he would drink a few sips when tired, then sprawl casually on the grass, head pillowed on a broken brick, sleeping soundly.

He left at dawn and returned deep into the night. The city gate guards came to know him well. "A hundred years hence, elders will speak of this former lord. Since ancient times, which worthy has not walked this road?" He told himself: for those destined to achieve eternal fame, is suffering not a necessary path?

That long yellow-mud road to Dongpo — dusty on clear days, muddy in rain — revealed moving beauty through Su Shi's enlightened eyes: "The great river surges coiling leftward, distant cloud-waves rolling and unfurling." Day after day he traveled it, "Mornings playing on Yellow Mud's white clouds, evenings lodging in Snow Hall's green mist."

2
Life's Pleasures

Snow Hall Life: Forbidden Wine, Roast Beef, Wall-Climbing

Snow Hall became a gathering place — they secretly drank contraband homebrew, and a western neighbor slaughtered his ailing ox for a barbecue. Belly full and happily drunk, they staggered through the wilderness singing and strolling, scaling the city wall to return home after the third watch.

Another night became legendary: after repeatedly getting drunk and sobering up at Snow Hall, Su Shi returned to Lingao Pavilion to find his servant snoring, with no answer to his knocking. He simply stood by the river, leaning on his staff listening to the waves. Moved, he wrote: "Long have I resented that this body is not my own — when shall I forget the hustle and bustle?" The next day the whole city spread rumors he had fled downriver in a boat. The prefect rushed over — only to find Su Shi still snoring thunderously.

Those who truly knew Su Shi would not make such a mistake. The multiplicity of his life's reflections made his character diverse and rich — he had a poet's innocent abandon and a recluse's otherworldly yearnings, yet remained anchored in reality, acutely aware of his responsibilities to society and family.

3
Declaration of Character

Caught in Rain: An Unfettered Declaration

On March 7, heading to Sand Lake to inspect farmland, Su Shi was caught in a sudden downpour. While all his companions were flustered, he remained utterly unperturbed — straw sandals, bamboo staff, chanting and strolling slowly. Soon the clouds parted and sun emerged. This fleeting storm spawned "Calming Wind and Waves," a timeless declaration of equanimity.

"Pay no heed to the sound of rain beating through the forest — why not chant aloud and stroll at ease? Bamboo staff and straw sandals, lighter than a horse — who's afraid? One straw rain-cloak, a lifetime of mist and rain." When the storm raged, he had the courage of one unmoved even if Mount Tai collapsed before him; when the setting sun smiled, he didn't exult — he had transcended external influences entirely.

Though the farmland purchase fell through, the trip brought the acquaintance of the famous physician Pang Anchang, who was deaf but brilliantly perceptive. Writing instead of speaking, "with but a few characters, he deeply understood my meaning." Together they toured Clear Spring Temple, where Su Shi, seeing the west-flowing Orchid Stream, wrote: "Who says life has no second youth? The stream before my door still flows westward!"

4
Immortal Masterpieces

Three Red Cliff Odes: The Pinnacle of Literature

On the night of July 16, Su Shi and guests drifted in a boat beneath Red Cliff — a cool breeze came, waves were still. Amid wine and poetry, one of the supreme works of Chinese literature emerged: the First Red Cliff Rhapsody. Three months later, on October 15, revisiting Red Cliff produced the Second Rhapsody and "Charm of a Maiden Singer."

The First Rhapsody unfolds philosophical discourse through host-guest dialogue: from lamenting "like mayflies between heaven and earth, a single grain in the vast sea" to finding present-moment aesthetic salvation in "the cool breeze on the river and the bright moon among the mountains." The Second Rhapsody depicts a moonlit mountain climb with a startling long whistle, presenting an even more carefree Su Shi. "The Great River flows eastward, its waves washing away a thousand ages of heroes" opens the ci poem with world-encompassing grandeur.

Together the three Red Cliff works form a complete philosophical system: the First Rhapsody resolves emotion through reason, from "lamenting our fleeting existence" to "all things and I are inexhaustible"; the Second Rhapsody conveys meaning through imagery; "Charm of a Maiden Singer" weaves history into lyric — the heroic passion of Zhou Yu's Red Cliff and the ultimate awakening that "life is but a dream" shine in mutual reflection.

5
Spiritual Elevation

Moonlit Dongpo: Crystal Joy in the Staff's Tap

Only in the deep quiet of night would Su Shi walk back to Lingao Pavilion by the pale moonlight — listening all the way to the crisp sound of his staff striking the uneven road, his heart brimming with crystalline joy. Suffering had been fully transmuted into aesthetic experience; resentment and fury had become things of the past.

"Rain-washed, Dongpo's moonlight is clear; the city folk are gone, the rustic walks alone. Don't despise this rough and rocky hillside road — I love the tink-tink of my trailing staff." This short poem "Dongpo" distills Su Shi's state of mind — wasn't that bumpy hillside road the very image of his life's rocky path? Yet what he heard in it was crisp, pleasing music. He had become brighter, warmer, kinder, more tolerant, filled with a mature humor gleaming with wisdom.

From his arrival in February 1080 to 1082, Su Shi had spent nearly three years in Huangzhou. External circumstances remained unchanged, but his inner world was utterly transformed. As he wrote: "Hell and Heaven are already equal to me" — he had completely transcended worldly concepts of failure and success, perceiving beauty and grasping philosophy in any circumstance. Transcendent equanimity had become the mainstream of his character.

💡
Key Takeaway

In his third Huangzhou year, Su Shi completed the most astonishing transformation in Chinese literati history: from the lonely indignation of "choosing among cold branches, unwilling to perch" to the unfettered equanimity of "one straw rain-cloak, a lifetime of mist and rain," to the cosmic vision of "The Great River flows eastward." The three Red Cliff odes are not merely a literary peak but the construction of an entire philosophical system. Those two moonlit nights — July 16 and October 15 — left an inextinguishable radiance in the sky of Chinese literature. As he said: "Rain-washed, Dongpo's moonlight is clear" — suffering had been cleansed into moonlit beauty.
