Trading for a Living II: The Art of Selling · Ch.1 "Buying"

Financial markets are vast and complex—no one can master everything. Just as modern doctors must specialize, traders must make deliberate choices about their method, style, and decision process.

▎Core Question
How should a trader choose across key dimensions to build a solid foundation?
The answer lies in deliberate decisions across four dimensions: analysis method (technical vs. fundamental), trading style (trend vs. counter-trend), decision process (discretionary vs. systematic), and a focused, deep toolkit—with every choice aligned to your personality, not blindly copied from others.
01
Mindset

Specialization Is a Requirement

Trading requires both confidence and humility. The market is too vast for one person to master. A modern doctor cannot simultaneously be a surgeon, psychiatrist, and pediatrician—the "renaissance physician" of centuries past no longer exists. Traders must become experts in a specific domain while remaining humble about everything else.

"
It is absolutely beneficial for a trader to understand their own temperament, preferences, and trading style.
— Alexander Elder
02
Analysis

Technical Analysis vs. Fundamental Analysis

The first critical choice: does price already reflect everything, or should you investigate intrinsic value? Both camps have strong advocates. Rigorous traders often combine the two.

📊 Technical Analysis

Price reflects all information. Study charts and indicators to determine which side—bulls or bears—will prevail. Signals are clear and quantifiable.

📈 Fundamental Analysis

Study company value and supply-demand balance. Focus on "what it should be worth," not "what the market is doing." Slower feedback loop.

💡
Common Ground: Rigorous fundamental traders check charts. Rigorous technical analysts monitor fundamentals. These methods are complementary, not opposing.
03
Style

Trend Trading vs. Counter-Trend Trading

Every chart consists of trending moves and consolidations. Trends attract beginners because they look obvious in hindsight—but trend trading is like riding a wild horse that wants to throw you off. Counter-trend trading exploits oscillations, lasts shorter, and yields smaller returns.

Richard Weissman identified three trader types in Mechanical Trading Systems:

04
Process

Discretionary Trading vs. Systematic Trading

Discretionary traders read charts and make progressive decisions—an engaging, hands-on process. Systematic traders study historical data, design robust systems, and let them generate signals. Don't analyze cost/benefit here—follow your temperament, just like choosing where to live or whom to marry.

🧠 Discretionary

Progressive analysis: assess direction first, then timing, step by step. Risk: beginners make impulsive decisions.

🤖 Systematic

Rule-based execution relying on historical testing. Risk: beginners fall into the curve-fitting trap.

⚠️
Convergence at the Top: Elite systematic traders act like discretionary ones when allocating capital across systems. Savvy discretionary traders follow mechanical rules. At high levels, the boundary blurs. Match it to your personality.
05
Toolkit

Sharpen Your Tools: The Five-Bullet Rule

A single candlestick gives only five data points: open, high, low, close, volume. Elder's "Five Bullets" rule caps indicators at five per chart. He uses four: Moving Averages, Trading Channels (Envelopes), MACD, and Force Index. He distrusts classical patterns (head-and-shoulders, triangles) as too subjective. He prefers calculated indicators—their signals are unambiguous.

Key Takeaway

None of a trader's choices—analysis method, trading style, decision process, or toolkit—are inherently right or wrong. The only criterion that matters: does it fit your personality perfectly? Only tools you've researched and built yourself can sustain your confidence during adverse markets. Specialize, know yourself, and keep your toolkit lean. Combine these three, and you have a good trade.
