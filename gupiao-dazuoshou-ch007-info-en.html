Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Chapter 7

On the eve of the 1906 San Francisco earthquake, Livermore shorted Union Pacific on pure intuition, earning $250,000—only to lose $40,000 at Saratoga by listening to a friend’s advice. A pivotal battle between instinct and discipline.

Overview

Is trading intuition superstition or subconscious accumulation?

Intuition is compressed experience. Livermore felt a powerful urge to short Union Pacific with no fundamental reason whatsoever—and was proven right the next day when the San Francisco earthquake struck. Yet at Saratoga, he abandoned his own judgment, heeded Ed Harding’s advice to close out and reverse, only to watch the stock soar on a surprise 10% dividend. Intuition earned him his biggest profit to date; blind trust in others cost him dearly.

Core thesis: A trader’s intuition is the product of subconscious pattern recognition built over years—far more reliable than tips from others. After this lesson, Livermore abandoned “impromptu” trading forever and began focusing on broad market conditions.

01
Intuition

Where Does Intuition Come From? A “Groundless” Short

Livermore’s intuition defied logic—but it worked repeatedly. In spring 1906, while on vacation in Atlantic City with no position, he stared at Union Pacific’s ticker and felt an overwhelming urge to short it. He couldn’t explain why—he just “wanted to sell.” He did, shorting 3,000 shares, then another 2,000. The next day: news of the San Francisco earthquake shook the world.

Union Pacific was among the hardest-hit railroads. Livermore pyramided his short to 15,000 shares and netted $250,000 within days—his largest profit to date. He reflected that over a dozen similar intuitive flashes had occurred, and each time he ignored one, he regretted it.

Costly Lesson

The Saratoga Mistake: When Intuition Surrendered to Others

A $40,000 lesson: trusting others over yourself. That same summer at Saratoga, Livermore read the tape and concluded that Union Pacific was being accumulated by big money. He began buying around 160. Ed Harding called long-distance, begging him as a friend to close out immediately—“the insiders are dumping it on you.” Livermore listened, liquidated his longs, and even went short 4,000 shares.

03
Turning Point

No More “Impromptu” Trading: The Final Lesson

The Saratoga episode was the watershed of Livermore’s trading career. He realized: despite the $40,000 loss, had he stuck to his own judgment, he would have cleaned up. This insight was worth more than money—it completed his trading education. From then on, he abandoned “impromptu” trading, focusing instead on broad market conditions rather than individual stocks.

The core lesson: A trader’s greatest enemy is not the market—it’s surrendering one’s own judgment. Others’ opinions, however well-intentioned, are noise. Livermore bought a simple truth for $40,000: The tape is truth. Independence is power.

Key Takeaway

Intuition is the subconscious rapid compression of vast market signals—not magic, but the natural product of professional training. Yet intuition must be governed by discipline: trust your own judgment, reject others’ noise. This was the lifelong creed Livermore bought with $250,000 in profit and $40,000 in loss. Saratoga became his last ‘impromptu’ trade; thereafter he turned to systematic analysis of broad market conditions.
