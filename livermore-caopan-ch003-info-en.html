01
Fatal Error

Spreading Too Thin: The Biggest Trap After a Winning Streak

Whenever a speculator has a run of success, the market releases a seductive haze that breeds complacency and inflated ambition — and spreading too thin across too many stocks is the classic symptom.

Livermore warned: handling a few stocks at once is manageable, but handling many simultaneously is overwhelming. He made this mistake himself during the wild bull market of the late 1920s — after seeing copper stocks' rally end and auto stocks peak, he prematurely concluded it was safe to short anything.

✅ The Right Way

After spotting a reversal in one stock group, wait patiently until other groups also signal that their decline or advance has ended. Act only when the time is right.

❌ The Wrong Way

After seeing one or two groups turn, immediately adopt a uniformly bearish or bullish stance on the entire market and rush to play everything. Impatience replaces common sense and judgment.

Livermore confessed the lesson was extremely costly: his trades in the first and second stock groups were profitable, but because he entered positions in other groups before their turning points arrived, he gave back a large chunk of those profits.

02
Costly Lesson

Big Copper & Auto Profits vs. Big Utilities Losses: A Textbook Cautionary Tale

Livermore accumulated massive paper profits shorting copper and auto stocks, but because he prematurely shorted utilities, the losses from the latter exceeded the profits from the former.

Ultimately, when utilities and other groups did top out, Anaconda (which he was short) had already declined 50 points from its peak, and auto stocks had fallen by a similar percentage. His directional call was perfectly correct — but his timing was fatally early. He should have waited until utilities emitted a clear topping signal before acting.

"Do not permit yourself to act in the same way in other stock groups unless you have plainly seen the signal that the latter group has begun to follow. Patience. Wait. Watch your timing — do not spread yourself too thin across the market."

03
Core Principle

Follow the Leaders: Concentrate Firepower on the Market's Most Prominent Stocks

"Concentrate your attention on the stocks that are the most prominent in that day's trading. If you cannot make money out of the leading active issues, you are not going to make money out of the stock market as a whole."

Just as women's fashions in clothing, hats, and jewelry change over time, the stock market continually discards past leaders and new leaders replace them. Railroads, American Sugar, and Tobacco were once leaders; then steel stocks took over; later automobiles moved to the fore. In Livermore's era, four groups dominated: steels, automobiles, aircraft, and mail-order stocks.

If you can correctly analyze the direction of just two stocks within those four key groups, you need not worry about the rest. The core principle — "follow the leaders" — while keeping your mind flexible. Remember: today's leaders may not be the leaders two years from now.

⚠️ Critical Warning: Attempting to follow too many stocks at once is definitely unsafe — you will become overworked and confused. Analyze only a relatively small number of groups. You will find it far easier to obtain the true picture of the market this way than by slicing it into many small pieces.

04
New Era

The Modern Speculator's Survival Code: Patience, Judgment & Right-Sized Positions

Today's market conditions no longer permit the large-scale manipulation of old — this brings safer opportunities for the wise, diligent, and competent speculator, but demands more prudent position sizing.

In the old days, market breadth and liquidity were ample — a speculator could take 5,000 or 10,000 shares without noticeably affecting the price, and exit easily if proved wrong. Today's markets are narrower: if an initial position proves untenable, liquidating it may incur severe losses. The intelligent speculator thus concentrates on a limited number of stock groups and their leaders.

Livermore's journey — from his first real trade (jointly buying 5 shares of Chicago, Burlington and Quincy Railroad with a friend, netting a $3.12 profit) to becoming a qualified speculator — was a lifelong practice of this principle. A new era has arrived in the stock market, bringing safer opportunities to the wise, diligent, and competent investor and speculator.

🔑
KEY TAKEAWAY

Follow the leaders — don't spread yourself too thin. Concentrate on the most prominent stocks of the day within a few key groups. Wait patiently for clear follow-through signals before acting. Keep your mind flexible — today's leaders are not tomorrow's leaders. This is the only sound path to safe profits in the new era of stock market conditions.
