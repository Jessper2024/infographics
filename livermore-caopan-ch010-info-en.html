Market action itself is the only signal — no need to wait for an external "good reason"

▎SCQA Framework
How can traders systematically record and interpret price movements to identify trend changes without relying on any external information?
Livermore spent over thirty years answering this question: S (Situation) — The market always operates in trends. Every shift between bull and bear leaves traces in price action. This chapter presents 16 complete chart manuscripts of U.S. Steel from March 1938 to February 1940. C (Conflict) — Most traders get lost in daily noise, waiting for external "good news" before acting, not realizing that market action itself is the only signal. Q (Question) — How can a systematic recording method reduce complex price movements into actionable trading signals? A (Answer) — Build a six-column table (Uptrend / Downtrend / Natural Rally / Natural Reaction / Secondary Rally / Secondary Reaction), use 6 points (single stock) or 12 points (composite price) as reversal thresholds, 3 points for entry confirmation, and distinguish trend phases with black ink, red ink, and pencil — letting the market tell you when to act through price behavior alone.

Recording System

The Six-Column Method: Livermore's Trend Dashboard

The six-column classification is Livermore's systematic framework for reducing complex price movements into actionable signals. He decomposed each leading stock's price action into six states — Secondary Rally, Natural Rally, Uptrend, Downtrend, Natural Reaction, Secondary Reaction — forming a complete trend-monitoring system.

📐
Reversal & Confirmation Rules: A single stock uses 6 points as the reversal threshold — if it retraces more than 6 points during an uptrend, it moves to the Natural Reaction column. Composite prices (synthesized from two stocks) use 12 points to filter out false signals. Regardless of direction, a 3-point breakthrough at the pivotal point is required to confirm the new trend.

Uptrend and Downtrend are recorded in ink because they represent clear directional trends; the other four columns are in pencil — these are merely adjustment phases that have not yet formed a trend and require further price action to confirm direction.

03
Trend Continuation

Adjustments Within a Trend: Distinguishing Normal Pauses from Dangerous Reversals (Charts 6–10)

An uptrend cannot advance in a straight line — through Charts 6–10 (Nov 12, 1938 – May 31, 1939), Livermore shows how to distinguish between "normal resting" and "trend termination" during corrections. The essence of these charts is not predicting when an adjustment will end, but clarifying which signals are worth acting on.

✅ Natural Reaction (Normal Correction)

During an uptrend, price declines more than 6 points and moves to the Natural Reaction column. As long as it doesn't further evolve into a downtrend, this is merely a normal pause on the upward path. Livermore's strategy: hold position, observe closely.

⚠️ Secondary Reaction (Deepening Correction)

After a Natural Reaction, prices continue downward — the correction deepens and moves to the Secondary Reaction column. Heightened vigilance is required: if this further evolves into a downtrend, the uptrend is over. Livermore's strategy: watch for breakdown, be ready for either direction.

🔑
Core discipline during adjustments: Do not enter on a whim during corrections — unless the signal that the adjustment has ended is unambiguous. Track closely when the trend is clear; observe carefully for signs of adjustment termination and trend continuation or reversal when in a consolidation phase.

05
Trading Wisdom

Four Core Lessons Revealed by the Manuscripts

Livermore's manuscripts are not merely technical records — they are a textbook on market psychology and discipline. Beyond the six-column table and reversal rules, these 16 charts reveal profound insights about the nature of markets.

✅ What Livermore's System Captures

Market action itself is the only signal — steel stocks warned of trouble in September 1939, four months before the reason (UK and Canadian government selling) was publicly revealed

Price patterns repeat — identifying key psychological moments matters more than chasing daily fluctuations

Composite prices outperform singles — synthesizing two stocks effectively filters false signals

❌ What Most Traders Get Wrong

Waiting for external "good news" to act — while the market had already turned long before the announcement

Chasing daily fluctuations — unable to see the forest for the trees, missing trend-level opportunities

Relying on a single ticker — frequently misled by false breakouts and noise

💡
Livermore's counsel: Maintaining the price record by hand is itself a form of training — you must personally select the data, personally fill in the record book, personally mark the pivotal points. In this process, the market speaks to you through your pen. The six-column recording method is not a prediction tool — it is a tool for listening to the language of the market.

In the presence of these 16 manuscripts, any "inside information" or "expert advice" pales into insignificance — because price has already said everything.

🔑
Key Takeaway

The essence of Livermore's six-column recording method: Reduce price movements to Uptrend (black ink), Downtrend (red ink), and four adjustment states (pencil), with 6 points (single stock) or 12 points (composite) as reversal thresholds and 3 points for breakout confirmation. The 16 chart manuscripts prove three timeless truths: First, market action itself is the only and earliest signal — steel stocks warned of trouble four months before the public news; Second, price patterns repeat — recognizing key psychological moments is more important than chasing fluctuations; Third, composite prices effectively filter false signals. Maintain your own price records by hand, and let the market speak to you through price behavior — this is the most valuable legacy Livermore left to traders.
