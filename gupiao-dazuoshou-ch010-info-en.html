Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Chapter 10

Returning from a Florida fishing trip, Livermore leveraged his deep understanding of monetary tightening to become ‘King of the Market’ during the 1907 Panic—his first perfectly executed strategic campaign, earning over $1 million in a single day.

Overview

How to Foresee a Liquidity Crisis from Fundamentals?

Fundamental conditions are the strongest ally. Seeing the market’s sharp rebound from Florida, Livermore realized the bear market wasn’t over. He profited quickly from Anaconda, then shorted broadly. When call loan rates hit 100-150% and the money crowd at the Exchange vanished, he knew panic had arrived. On October 24, 1907, at J.P. Morgan’s request and his own strategic judgment, he covered all shorts and went long—netting over $1 million that day.

Core thesis: Study the overall conditions, rank them by magnitude of influence, and you can anticipate the market’s probable future. This is not just trading—it’s understanding an era.

01
Crisis Foresight

From a Dividend Notice to Full-Scale Shorting: The Logic Chain

The insiders’ desperation is the short-seller’s signal. While vacationing in Europe, Livermore read in the Paris Herald that Smelters had declared an extra dividend. He immediately understood: the bull clique was fighting a desperate rear-guard action in a bear market, trying to push prices up to distribute before the storm. He cabled sell orders at once. His fills came back 6 points below the quoted price—itself evidence of market weakness. He canceled his vacation and rushed back to New York.

02
Panic Scene

October 24, 1907: Money Dries Up and Morgan Rescues

That morning, whatever rate the money brokers quoted, borrowers had no choice but to pay. Funds were insufficient for all liquidity needs. After the afternoon delivery hour, over a hundred money brokers crowded around the money post—Union Pacific had not a single bid, at any price. A broker told Livermore: “The whole Street is broke.”

J.P. Morgan stepped in. He sent Van Emburgh & Atterbury to announce $10 million in loans. When bankers protested they were “already below legal reserve limits,” Morgan barked: “Use the reserves! That’s what reserves are for!” Ultimately about $20 million in reserves saved the stock market.

03
Market King

Profiting a Million and Going Long: The Market King’s Strategic Choice

“If I had ten million dollars, I’d put every cent of it up right now.” Livermore was sitting on massive paper profits—just 10,000 more shares sold could trigger total collapse. But he made a wiser choice: cover all shorts and go long 100,000 shares. Three reasons: further declines might delay recovery; a panic would cause widespread national harm; and there were cheap buying opportunities at the bottom. J.P. Morgan sent word asking him to stop selling—but he had already begun buying.

The true meaning of ‘Market King’: “For a little while that day, the stock market was at the mercy of anyone who wanted to hammer it.” But Livermore’s greatest gain was not the million dollars—it was that he had “at last learned to trade intelligently in a big way,” forever transcending the level of a gambler.
💡
Key Takeaway

On October 24, 1907, Livermore completed his transformation from speculator to master trader. He not only foresaw the liquidity crisis through fundamental analysis, but at its climax made a strategic choice that transcended profit—covering and going long. This marked the completion of his trading system: study overall conditions → rank influencing factors → anticipate future trends → patiently await timing → systematically build positions → exit at the right moment. The million dollars was merely the result; the true ‘crown’ was this replicable cognitive framework.
