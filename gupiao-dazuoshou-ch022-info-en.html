Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 22 "Imperial Steel Operation Succeeds, Petroleum Products Disappoints"

With just 7,000 shares, Livermore pushed Imperial Steel up over 30 points — a textbook demonstration of how to awaken a comatose stock. The Petroleum Products failure, conversely, proves that an operator must have absolute trust in the client.

▎S — Situation
Imperial Steel was a widely acknowledged valuable but dormant stock: insiders held 70% they couldn't sell, investors found it "boring," and speculators found it "asleep." The stock entered hibernation the moment you bought it — no market, no liquidity.
▎C — Conflict
Insiders had to sell in the open market, but the stock barely traded. All the talk about earnings and prospects brought no buyers — "you could tell nothing but the truth and no one would buy." They needed someone who could awaken a sleeping asset. In contrast to Imperial Steel, the Petroleum Products operation failed because the client betrayed the operator.
▎Q — Key Question
How do you activate a dormant stock with minimal shares while protecting yourself from client betrayal?
▎A — Answer
A four-step operating system: ① Thorough due diligence — independent investigation confirms real value; ② Lock-up to prevent dumping — trust agreement locks 70% of float, eliminating backstab risk; ③ Gradual demand creation — from sluggish trading to active trading to price rise; ④ Staged liquidation — sell a little at each price advance. Throughout the process, only 7,000 shares were needed to push the price up over 30 points. The Petroleum Products comparison proves: trust is an irreplaceable foundation.
01
Preparatory Work

Due Diligence: Not by Feeling, But by Facts

The first thing Livermore did after taking the project was not trade — it was to send professionals to conduct on-site research into Imperial Steel's production, commercial, and financial departments, delivering independent investigation reports. "My purpose was not to discover strengths or weaknesses, but to grasp the facts — as they were."

The reports confirmed it was a valuable asset — buying at the current market price was advantageous, provided the investor was willing to wait patiently for a short while. Livermore's conclusion was emphatic: "I could see no reason why I could not, in good conscience and with confidence, undertake the project of an advancing market for Imperial Steel." Note the key phrase "in good conscience" — he was not speculative pumping, but creating a legitimate market for a genuinely valuable asset.

02
Risk Control

Trust Lock-Up: I Am Not a Dumping Ground

Livermore's first line of defense before launching the operation: "I had them place 70% of their holdings under a trust agreement. I had no intention of becoming a dumping ground for what the large shareholders wished to sell." This move firmly strapped down the vast majority of potential selling pressure — the remaining 30% of dispersed holdings were all he needed to manage as market risk.

This decision embodies Livermore's core risk-management philosophy: an experienced speculator never expects a completely risk-free project, but will eliminate controllable risks wherever possible. He uses a brilliant analogy — just as life insurance policyholders won't all die on the same day, dispersed shareholders won't all dump onto the market simultaneously. Reducing risk to a calculable scope is an essential quality of the professional operator.

Livermore used only 7,000 shares (just 7% of his option entitlement) to complete the entire push-up process — true power lies not in the amount of capital, but in the precise grasp of market psychology's rhythm.

04
Cautionary Tale

Petroleum Products: The Cost of Broken Trust

In the same chapter, Livermore contrasts another operation — Petroleum Products. Same strategy, same methods, same Livermore — but a completely different outcome. The cause lay not in the strategy, but in "people."

After seeing Livermore successfully push up the price, Petroleum Products insiders let greed override their contract — they secretly dumped massive quantities of their own shares onto the market, thoroughly destroying the market structure Livermore had painstakingly built. The operator's system, no matter how perfect, is no match for the client's betrayal.

⚠️
Comparative Lesson: The same operating system — Imperial Steel succeeded, Petroleum Products failed. The difference lay not in strategy, but in "people." Choosing the right partner is as important as choosing the right stock. A trust agreement is not legal decoration — it is a survival necessity.
💡
Key Takeaway · BLUF

With 7,000 shares, Livermore levered a 30-point advance — his secret lay not in capital size but in the four-step rhythm: "lock up first, create demand, then push up, finally distribute." The most important investment wisdom in this chapter may not be the operating strategy itself, but that trust is the most fundamental asset: without trust in the client relationship, even the most perfect operating plan is just waste paper. The Imperial Steel vs. Petroleum Products comparison is the best textbook.
