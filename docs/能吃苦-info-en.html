Endure Hardship, Get More Hardship

Mao Bidao · June 17, 2026 · WeChat Official Account

In 2026, China's A-share market is dominated by a single tech sector. The STAR 50 Index has surged 36% year-to-date, yet the market median has fallen 1.27%—nearly 70% of stocks are in the red. Retail investors, clinging to the belief in mean reversion, hold onto laggards and find themselves crushed by the Matthew Effect. Those who endure hardship are getting even more hardship. This essay travels from the football pitch to the stock market, from Zidane's four sons to Musk's trillion-dollar fortune, revealing a brutal truth: effort and endurance do not always yield reward—sometimes they simply keep you pointed in the wrong direction for longer.

01
Talent Is Random
Superstar genes + elite academy ≠ guaranteed success
Talent is random—effort and resources do not determine who inherits it. Zinedine Zidane has four sons, all placed into Real Madrid's academy through their father's connections. The results: the eldest retired at 29, never making it professionally; the second plays goalkeeper in Spain's second division; the third is equally unremarkable in the second division; the youngest, now in his twenties, still languishes in the reserves. The best genetics in world football plus the best youth system produced a ceiling of a second-division goalkeeper.

02
Matthew Effect
70% of stocks generate no real returns over 15 years
The 80/20 rule in A-shares is far more extreme than most investors realize. Mao Bidao's large-sample analysis shows that over 70% of listed companies fail to beat bank deposit rates over a 15-year horizon. Only 10–15% of stocks actually drive index gains, rising 10x, 50x, even 100x. Structural divergence is not an anomaly—it is the norm. As he puts it: "If even a socialist state cannot eliminate wealth inequality, how could a capital-driven stock market possibly rise and fall in unison?"

03
Mean Reversion Trap
Selling winners and holding losers = cutting yourself off from the 10–15%
The single most destructive retail behavior is selling winners to fund losers. Most investors operate with an implicit mean-reversion logic: what goes up must come down, what goes down must come up. They sell profitable positions and double down on losers—systematically offloading the very 10–15% of stocks capable of multibagger returns while pouring capital into the 70% that destroy value over time. This is not bad luck. It is behavior that structurally repels success.

04
Character Is Destiny
80% trail the index—yet refuse to simply buy the ETF
In a structurally polarized market, being on the wrong side is more lethal than lack of effort. In 2026, the STAR 50 is up 36%, the ChiNext 30%, and the CSI 500 15.5%. Eighty percent of retail investors trail all three. Yet most of them refuse to just buy an index ETF—they insist their bad habits can be overcome through more trading. They are on the wrong side of the trend, fighting the market's direction, yet too proud to index. This is not a technical problem. It is a character problem.

05
Asymmetric World
An overnight celebrity goalkeeper and a trillion-dollar centibillionaire
The scale of success depends not on effort but on where you stand. Cape Verde's goalkeeper Vozinha became an overnight sensation at 40 during the World Cup, amassing 11.6 million Instagram followers—more than Jay Chou. His country derives half its GDP from tourism, and his influence alone can reshape its destiny. Meanwhile, Elon Musk's paper wealth has hit $1.4 trillion—exceeding South Korea's entire annual GDP—driven by SpaceX's stock surge. Success in this world is never evenly distributed. Those who endure hardship get more hardship. Those standing in the right place don't need to.
"If your child didn't inherit the talent, they didn't inherit it. You'd be better off making peace with that than tormenting them trying."
🔑 Key Takeaway
Enduring hardship leads to more hardship—because where you endure matters more than how much you endure. The Matthew Effect in A-shares means the longer you hold onto losing stocks, the more you miss the 10–15% that actually drive the market. Random talent, concentrated capital, and winner-take-all dynamics are the bedrock rules of this world. Accepting that you cannot beat the index—buying ETFs, aligning with the trend, and knowing when to take profit—is the first step toward breaking the cycle of suffering.
