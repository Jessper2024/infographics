A Very Special True Story

By Mao Bidao · 2024/09/28 · WeChat Official Account

What begins as a casual financial journal turns into a story that the author himself admits he "cannot relate to." The first half is an honest family asset allocation review—from maturing municipal bonds to a risk-tiering framework and the iron rule against cross-tier capital flows, followed by shrinking crypto yields and a distressed-asset investment lesson. The second half recounts a true story from Linhai: a barber who gave free haircuts to seniors for a decade, diagnosed with ALS, was taken in by a police auxiliary officer and his wife—who have now cared for him for 11 years. The author admits he admires but cannot understand such extreme self-sacrifice, and leaves the question open for every reader.

Family Asset Allocation Framework
Real estate is modest; most assets are deployed across multiple financial markets.

02
The Iron Rule of Cross-Tier Capital Discipline
Capital can flow within the same risk tier, but never across tiers.

This is the core discipline: selling A-shares can fund more US equities (same tier), but proceeds from bond redemptions will never buy stocks. When ¥10M in municipal bonds plus 6.7% interest matured, the money was temporarily parked in a 3% short-term product for the holiday—and will only be redeployed into bond-like instruments afterward.

Bond yields have fallen sharply—comparable products now offer only ~5%. To boost overall returns, part of this capital will be directed to on-chain assets. But this adjustment stays within the low-risk tier; no boundary crossing.

03
Shrinking Crypto Yields and a Distressed-Asset Lesson
Both on-chain and off-chain, the era of easy riches is over. Stepping on landmines is a matter of probability, not ability.

On-chain yield farming has collapsed from 50–60% at its peak to 20–30%, and even those pools require scrambling to get in. The obvious question: if crypto yields so much more than bonds, why not go all in? The answer is the discipline from the previous section—crypto is in the 15%+ high-risk tier, with price volatility and hacking risk on top. Low risk and high returns are mutually exclusive; you must find balance on a seesaw.

💣 Distressed Asset Case: The author invested ¥3M in a distressed-asset pool—an imprisoned owner's 6–7 Beijing properties were bundled as collateral. About ¥2.6M was recovered through auctions of the first few properties. Then the owner was released from prison, demanded extra payment, and the remaining two properties are stuck in negotiation. Whether the rest comes back is unknown.

The core lesson: accidents happen in investing—like random probability, no one can avoid landmines forever. That's exactly why you diversify, maintain proportional controls, and aim for balanced risk-adjusted returns.

04
The Linhai Story: Ten Years of Free Haircuts, Eleven Years of Care
A barber's decade of community service was repaid with a couple's eleven-year devotion to his life.

The story came from a director friend of the author's, told over drinks in Linhai—100% true, well-known locally.

Barber Wu was a warm-hearted man. Starting in 2003, he organized weekly free haircuts for seniors over 60 and kept it going for ten years. In 2013, he was diagnosed with ALS—the degenerative disease with no cure. He quietly withdrew from the公益活动, intending to live out his remaining days in silence.

But a fellow volunteer—a police auxiliary officer—learned of Wu's condition and was deeply moved. He offered to care for Wu through his final years, invited him to move in, and his wife fully supported the decision. Their simple motivation: good people deserve good endings. Everyone expected Wu to pass within a year or two. Instead, thanks to the couple's extraordinary care, he is still alive today—11 years later. The couple gave Wu their largest bedroom, while they and their daughter squeezed into a 10 m² second bedroom. Nighttime care needs mean they get up whenever Wu requires help.

05
"Is This a Good Story?" — An Honest Dilemma
The author admits admiration without understanding; a director searches for the right cinematic angle; the answer is left to every reader.

When the director asked the author what he thought, the response was unexpectedly candid: "I feel this act of kindness went too far. Our simple Chinese value is that helping others is virtuous, but one should not suffer enormous losses in the process. This couple's moral altitude is extraordinary—I deeply admire them—but I cannot understand it, nor can I put myself in their shoes."

🐱 The Author's View
Admires the moral height, but extreme self-sacrifice exceeds the simple boundary of "helping others brings joy."

Still searching for the right angle to adapt the story cinematically—to show the shining side of human nature.

The article ends with an open question, thrown to every reader: "Dear readers, do you think this is a good story? I want to know your first, honest reaction."

Core Takeaway

This essay's essential tension lies not in financial methodology, but in the contrast between the first half's risk discipline (no cross-tier capital flows, diversification, accepting probabilistic landmines) and the second half's moral choice (a couple trading 11 years of their youth for a stranger's continued existence).

The former is a cold but sustainable rules-based system—it has boundaries, discipline, and admits accidents happen. The latter is a kindness so hot it burns—boundless, reckless with cost, exceeding what most people would accept.

Mao Bidao offers no answer. He simply records his own "I can't relate" and the director's "I want to film it." A good story doesn't have to feel comfortable—it just has to make you pause and think.
