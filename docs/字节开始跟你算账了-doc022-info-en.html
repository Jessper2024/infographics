The free strategy helped ByteDance seize the AI entry point in two years. But with user growth plateauing (CNNIC: 602M gen-AI users nationwide, 42.8% penetration), ByteDance no longer wants to swallow the full cost alone—paid tiers shift some burden to power users.

The Seedance video paradox compounds the issue: generating 100 seconds of video costs ~¥100. A ¥200/month subscriber making 2 minutes of video already incurs ¥120 in raw costs—the more they sell, the more they lose. Users still need to buy credits on top of membership.

The gap widens not just in access speed but in depth of thinking, productive output, and value creation—a new form of means-of-production stratification.

Volcano Engine president Tan Dai is explicit: B-end is the real token economy. Mainstream model prices per token are rising, but value per token is rising faster. ByteDance isn't betting on subscriptions to turn a profit—this is a cautious commercialization trial.

📌 Key Takeaway

Doubao's paid launch marks China's AI industry pivot from land-grab to tiered monetization. Three bills trace a clear path: compute costs ended the free ride; pricing misalignment exposes product-capability ceilings; industry bifurcation reveals C-end subscriptions are just the front line—the real war is B-end. ByteDance didn't burn ¥200B to collect ¥68/month subscriptions. They're using C-end users as free beta testers to sharpen Agent capabilities, ultimately cashing out through Volcano Engine's B-end token economy. This isn't "settling accounts"—it's a B-end arms race with consumer users as the proving ground.
