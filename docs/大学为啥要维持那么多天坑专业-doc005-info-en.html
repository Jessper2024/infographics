Why Universities Keep So Many "Dead-End Majors"

Source: Jiu Bian · WeChat Official Account · June 16, 2026 | A university's primary function has never been employment — China's 40-year anomaly created a collective illusion that's now fading

Every application season, "dead-end majors" spark heated debate. This article argues from first principles: a university's primary function is to store knowledge, not train employees. China's 40-year economic boom created the illusion that good university = good job — a global anomaly, not the norm. Hot majors expire faster than a 4-year degree takes to complete. What actually determines your life trajectory is personal traits, not your diploma.

01The University's True Purpose – Knowledge Warehouse, Not Job Factory

A university's primary function is to store knowledge so humanity doesn't reinvent the wheel — not to deliver students to specific jobs.

Ancient military knowledge was lost between dynasties because it could only be passed down within families. Qi Jiguang wrote exhaustive military manuals covering everything from food logistics to battle formations — because all prior knowledge had been lost. Modern military academies finally gave knowledge a permanent home.

A Harvard admissions officer explained the "donate a building" policy: those donors don't need a Harvard degree, won't compete with you for jobs — but their money funds research, hires professors, and may be paying for your scholarship.

"A university's primary purpose is to store knowledge, second to let it grow, and only last to help with employment. Most majors were never easy to get jobs with — China's 40-year boom just created that illusion."

02China's Anomaly – The "Golden Age" Illusion

China's 40-year economic boom made "good university = good job" a collective hallucination — this was an anomaly, not the global norm.

In developed countries, people understand that university education correlates with high income — but weakly. A philosophy graduate from a top German university drives a taxi and plans to pursue a PhD — never expecting his degree to bring wealth. They've achieved complete "cognitive separation" — nobody thinks a prestigious degree guarantees a high salary.

China's university expansion coincided with one industry boom after another, creating a ladder of upward mobility. Now that the golden age is ending, the illusion is crumbling.

The career advisor who pushed civil engineering saw those graduates face a collapsed job market. His CS recommendation is also fading. It's not bad judgment — the window is shorter than a 4-year degree. CS enrollment now far exceeds market demand, producing graduates who either strike gold or can't find work at all.

04Cross-Discipline Filtering – The Real Winners Switched Majors

During CS's golden age, most original CS graduates dropped out within years. The real winners were cross-discipline converts.

The reason is simple: those who survived the cross-discipline learning process passed a real filter. They genuinely loved the work. Most original CS students chose it because their exam scores were high enough or their parents picked it — not because they loved coding. After a few years, the suffering "somatized" — chronic illness forced them out.

⚠️ What determines your career is personal traits (discipline, self-confidence, intelligence, drive, emotional resilience, conscientiousness) — not your diploma. If you're still flexing your university and major 5 years after graduation, you're probably a loser. No exceptions.

05Back to Basics – Universities and Employment Are Decoupling

The decoupling of university and employment isn't decline — it's a return to the global norm.

  • Professional knowledge depreciates, industries change, jobs disappear — but the mental training from your discipline persists in subtler ways
  • Fewer and fewer people work in their major field 10 years after graduation — this is universal, not just Chinese
  • University installs high-level mental models in your brain, building a foundation for future learning without fear
  • Information is now completely open — you can learn anything you want. Your degree and major are not decisive factors
  • The most important thing is to discover yourself and find what you truly want to do — through personal traits, not credentials
"If you're still flexing your university and major 5 years after graduation, you're probably a loser — no exceptions. What you can truly rely on are your traits: discipline, self-confidence, intelligence, drive, emotional resilience, conscientiousness."

Key Takeaways:
① A university's primary function is storing knowledge — employment is last; ② China's 40-year boom created the "good university = good job" illusion, now fading; ③ Hot majors expire faster than a degree takes — choose traits over trends; ④ Cross-discipline survivors are the real winners, not original majors; ⑤ What determines your life 5 years out is personal traits + luck, not your diploma.
