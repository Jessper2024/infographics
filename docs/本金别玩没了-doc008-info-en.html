01IC Investing – Only for "Thick-Skinned, Easygoing, Carefree" People

Rolling ICs (Index Certificates) is very personality-specific: suits disciplined, self-controlled investors. Impulsive traders using leverage will eventually destroy themselves.

Mao Bi Dao's IC approach: keep enough in the account, know the warning levels, then forget about it. Logs in 4-5 times a year, rolls positions quarterly, doesn't even look otherwise. During the bear market, he forgot to roll expiring contracts — remembered weeks later, and it actually saved him from losing hundreds of thousands.

"This is for thick-skinned, easygoing, carefree people. If you're the type who stares at K-lines and wants to act on every move, I advise you to stay away."
⚠️ Iron rule of leverage: if you're impulsive and tend to go big when heated up, leverage will eventually kill you. IC isn't forbidden — it's forbidden for the wrong people.

02Wealth Management Firms – Avoid Them All, 5-6% Isn't Worth Gambling Your Principal

Avoid all domestic "wealth management" firms. Risking your principal for 5-6% returns is a terrible deal.

Many high-net-worth families developed an autopilot habit: park money, collect interest, relax. Then one day the redemptions stop and you can't get out — everything collapses. CreditEase recently announced a "benign exit." A reader has ¥30 million trapped inside, can't sleep.

03"Considering" ≠ "Deciding" – Think Independently

Mao Bi Dao said he was "considering" buying a house in Beijing in Q3-Q4. Readers misread it as "deciding." "Considering" is just considering — who says considering means you must buy?

Same pattern: he said he'd "consider" Xiaomi if it fell below 40. It did — he considered, decided no. Said he'd "consider" at 30 — seriously considered, felt the trend was wrong, wait and see. No contradiction. But some people front-ran and tried to blame him.

04Underlying Risk – First Priority Above 8.5 Digits

Once wealth hits 8.5 digits (tens of millions), the only priority is underlying risk. Gains and losses don't matter — losing principal means instant class demotion.

This isn't about maximizing returns — it's about survival. If the underlying assets fail (wealth firm collapse, P2P fraud, trust default), you don't just lose a few years of interest. You go back to square one overnight.

"Once wealth hits 8.5 digits, the first priority is underlying risk. Gains and losses don't matter — lose your principal and you suffer instant class demotion."
