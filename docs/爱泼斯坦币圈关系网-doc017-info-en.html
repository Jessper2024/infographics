Methodology: Open-Source DB + Claude + Three-Round Keyword Convergence
With no coding background, the author systematically screened 260K+ relationship records using a GitHub database and Claude-assisted analysis.

As Epstein files were being declassified, the author wondered if anyone in crypto was implicated. Thousands of PDFs couldn't be manually searched. He discovered Epstein Doc Explorer on GitHub—a project that used AI to structure data from the House Oversight Committee's public Epstein files. The core data lives in rdf_triples table within document_analysis.db, with each record in the format: who-did-what-to-whom-when-where.

💰 Confirmed Investments
Decrypt's reporting on new DOJ files confirms: Epstein invested in Coinbase (opportunity brought by Pierce) and Blockstream (Adam Back's Bitcoin infrastructure company). Blockchain Capital stated Epstein ultimately invested independently of Pierce's firm.

A fundraising memorandum appearing in someone's personal files has one standard interpretation in finance: this person was approached as a potential LP. Given Pierce was both a Blockchain Capital co-founder and the person demoing Bitcoin at Epstein's dinner table, the two threads likely connect—demo Bitcoin to build interest, then hand over the fund's fundraising materials. A complete roadshow pipeline.

Core Takeaway

Epstein was not an investor in the crypto industry. He wasn't a technical participant either—he wrote no code, launched no token, built nothing on-chain. But he clearly wasn't a passive bystander. What he was doing looks more like an information broker: arranging for Pierce to demo Bitcoin to an ex-Treasury Secretary in his own dining room, holding Blockchain Capital's complete fundraising deck, discussing regulatory politics with a Bitcoin Core developer, funding MIT's digital currency research, and analyzing Facebook's Libra in his final two weeks of freedom.

The common thread across all these behaviors: positioning himself at the intersection of information flows—the connective node between crypto entrepreneurs, policymakers, and academic researchers. This mirrors his playbook in every other domain (funded scientists without doing science himself; arranged political meetings without holding office). Crypto was simply the latest field to which he applied the same methodology.

The possible differentiator: crypto was going through its critical window of transition from underground to mainstream between 2008 and 2019—a phase where the industry was uniquely dependent on informal networks for policy insight and capital access. And that, precisely, was what Epstein was best at providing.
