Those who broke and exited before the rally won't come back. The article closes with the author's candid breakdown of his reader-supported business model.

01
Earth-Shattering: A Rally Unseen in 34 Years
A-share performance over five trading days surpassed every precedent in 34 years of history—sheer buying power hoisted the entire market.

On September 30, combined volume hit ¥2.6 trillion, a new all-time record. Just one week earlier, on September 23, volume was only ¥550 billion—a world of difference in the span of days.

9/23: ¥550B
→
9/30: ¥2.6T

This rally overturned traditional technical indicators and patterns. Raw force overwhelmed nuance—overloaded liquidity broke the market, producing a wild rally no technical framework could constrain.

02
Spillover Effect: When Your Neighbor Asks How to Open an Account
Explosive profit-making began spreading to marginal populations—a neighbor with zero financial background opened an account in 5 minutes and immediately wanted to buy.

The author's neighbor messaged on WeChat asking about opening a brokerage account. She ran a traditional business and had never touched finance before. The author sent her a referral code with a caution about market risk. Five minutes later she said she'd opened the account and asked if she could buy immediately—she didn't even understand what "funding an account" meant but was desperate to get in. Turned out the clearing house was overloaded with new account applications that day; no new accounts could be opened.

This small episode signals something larger: A-shares had truly broken into mainstream consciousness. New money plus returning veteran investors → massive short-term inflows → a rally no technical framework could constrain.

03
Random Confidence Nuclear Explosion: Why This Time?
When the September 26 Politburo meeting content was released, the market suddenly bought in—but the author admits he still hasn't fully figured out the causality.

The surface trigger: the Fed's major rate cut followed by China's more aggressive easing policy. But over the preceding year and a half, the government had rolled out multiple rescue packages—for housing, for stocks, macro-level—each previous policy announcement met a poor market response, sometimes even accelerating declines.

❌ Previous Rescue Attempts
Housing, stock market, macro—serious policies with real intent. But the market didn't accept them, sometimes accelerating the decline.
✅ 9/26 Politburo Meeting
More substantial, but nothing qualitatively transformed vs. prior policy. Yet the market suddenly accepted it. Leaders didn't expect it, experts didn't expect it, retail investors were shocked too.

The author's conclusion: a fairly random confidence nuclear explosion, releasing three years of suppressed emotional potential energy all at once. Capital markets always harbor enormous uncertainty—they can't be planned, they don't obey administrative commands.

04
Too Narrow a Window: 3 Years of Decline → 5 Days of 33% Rebound
The window is too narrow: you can't catch it without enduring enough suffering. Worse, many couldn't withstand the prolonged torment and broke before the rebound arrived.

From an investment perspective, this market is deeply unfriendly to ordinary investors. Three years of grinding decline, halving portfolios—then five trading days of 33% rebound.

💔 Those who broke and exited before the rebound—how will they view these days of surging prices? Resentment, bitterness, despair, envy. The author's assessment: most of them won't choose to come back.

The author's long-awaited 3500-point level was within touching distance—one more push after the holiday. Three years of torment, finally about to be released. But this is not a market that is friendly to ordinary investors.

05
"Rather Than Getting Better, It's More Like a Different Illness Breaking Out"
When someone asked "is everything getting better?", the author gave an answer that neither rained on the celebratory parade nor said something he didn't believe.

This is the essay's core metaphor: "Rather than getting better, A-shares right now are more like a different kind of illness breaking out." Not recovery—a changed set of symptoms. The previous illness was three years of chronic wasting decline. The current illness is five days of irrational euphoric surge. Both stem from the same underlying condition: a market structure incapable of delivering stable returns to ordinary investors.

He's clear about his position—"We count the labor, not the glory." He treats his readership traffic with care, never casually monetizing it. Writing the blog is part of his life, genuinely enjoyable, which is why he's kept at it for years.

Core Takeaway

The late-September 2024 A-share rally was a mirror: it reflected the deepest irrationality of financial markets—three years of suppressed emotion can release in a single random confidence explosion, and technical patterns, policy expectations, and professional judgment all fail in the face of liquidity overload.

Mao Bidao didn't join the euphoria. His diagnosis—"a different kind of illness breaking out"—strips the bull market of its "recovery narrative." The illness didn't heal; it just switched symptoms. The prior phase was chronic wasting decline (slowly killing holders). The current phase is explosive euphoria (luring new entrants, punishing those who quit midway).

Same underlying condition: a market structure with an extremely narrow window, fundamentally unfriendly to ordinary investors. 33% in five days sounds like good news—but only if you survived three years of being halved first.
