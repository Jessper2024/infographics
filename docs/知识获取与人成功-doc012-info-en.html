Why Is Knowledge More Accessible, Yet Ordinary People Succeed Less?

Source: Li Wenzhuoyu · WeChat · June 1, 2026 | From Ming Dynasty merchant guides to Qi Jiguang's army — open information doesn't equal open opportunity. Between knowing and doing lies an abyss of fear and capital.

The author traces a counter-intuitive historical pattern: in Ming Dynasty Wanli era, cheap mass-printed merchant guides with national logistics routes and fraud prevention manuals were widely available — yet ordinary peddlers never broke through class barriers. Imperial exam model answer books (Chengmo) proliferated but poor scholars' success rates actually declined. Elite examiners invented a new standard: "workshop style" vs. "scholarly elegance." Qi Jiguang's military manual was read by every general — only his army could execute. The gap between knowing and doing isn't about information — it's about capital, power networks, and psychological steel.

01Ming Merchant Guide: Open Info ≠ Social Mobility

The Wanli-era book Keshang Yilan Xingmi recorded every water and land route across Ming China, locations of bandit inns, dozens of scam types, and exact toll prices at every checkpoint. Jianyang mass-produced editions were cheap — anyone literate could afford one.

A poor Huizhou youth memorized the entire book, borrowed 100 silver taels, and set out. At the first tax checkpoint, a minor clerk detained him. The book said the toll was 1 qian — but the clerk, seeing his shabby clothes, unfamiliar accent, and lack of servants, fabricated a smuggling charge and fined him 10 taels. The book covered scams, but not pure power abuse. He was beaten, his goods dumped in the mud. Nearby, a retired mandarin's family barge passed untouched — the clerk fawned and waved them through with a smile.

"Information is just a map. A map will never tell you where landmines are buried, where bridges have collapsed — nor will it give you an armored vehicle."

02Civil Exams: "Workshop Style" — Elites Change the Rules

When poor scholars mastered standard answers through cheap study guides, Jiangnan elite examiners invented a new gate: "Your prose reeks of workshop style, devoid of scholarly elegance."

Jianyang publishers drove printing costs to the floor. The bestseller wasn't the classics, but Chengmo — top-scoring exam essays with annotations by retired examiners and failed old scholars (equivalent to today's "past exam papers + top student notes"). Poor village children could all afford them. Yet southern elite families (Nanzhili, Zhejiang) maintained crushing dominance. Examiner Shen Defu coined the term fangke qi (workshop style): essays that followed the formula perfectly, indistinguishable from mass-produced templates. What examiners truly valued was shujuan qi (scholarly elegance): a subtle grace that came from growing up with thousands of volumes, exposure to historians, poets, obscure classics, and famous scholars — an incredibly expensive taste that no cheap study guide could replicate.

03Qi's Army: The Gap Between Knowing and Doing

Qi Jiguang's Jixiao Xinshu detailed every drill, formation, and weapon coordination with illustrations — every Ming general owned a copy. But only one army could execute it.

Qi himself explained: formations are dead, men are alive. When hundreds of screaming Japanese pirates charged from 50 paces away, soldiers' minds went blank. The rattan shield bearers dropped their shields and fled. Qi's army could hold formation not because they memorized the manual better — but because Qi had Hu Zongxian's financial backing to pay far higher wages (heavy reward), brutally harsh collective punishment (heavy penalty), and most importantly, had survived countless bloody battles until the muscle memory of resisting fear was carved into their bones.

"You think you're missing a military manual. Actually, you're missing Hu Zongxian's treasury, execution-enforcing discipline, and nerves that don't break on corpse-strewn battlefields."

04Three Non-Information Resources

The author identifies three resources the internet can never download: trial-error capital, hidden power networks, and execution ability with psychological resilience.

The internet erased knowledge access thresholds — it never erased, and can never erase, human weaknesses. Between knowing and doing lies a vast ocean of fear, anxiety, second-guessing, and self-doubt.

05Omniscient Paralysis — Knowing Too Much, Daring Too Little

A peasant centuries ago didn't know how big the world was. He had no choices, so he just worked — and slowly accumulated. Today, a university graduate opens his phone: left, AI replacing all knowledge workers; right, new energy sector brutally over-competitive; above, global macro headwinds; below, is it too late to start short video?

He sees countless paths, each marked with dense risk warnings and the corpses of pioneers. Brain overload. He starts weighing, hesitating, burning his most precious action capacity in endless analysis. When you understand everything, you often dare nothing.

Those who truly break through in this era are those who know how to block information. Like a half-blind wild boar, they fixate on one tiny point and barrel in relentlessly.

"The internet has indeed laid out the world's knowledge like a free, infinite library. But the library contains no power, no capital, no courage — and no grit to face the abyss. The real battlefield has always been outside the library doors. The wind there is fierce, there are no standard answers. And entry requires blood."

Core argument: ① Cheap Ming guidebooks didn't enable class mobility — open info ≠ open opportunity; ② When exam knowledge became accessible, elites invented "workshop style vs. scholarly elegance" as the new barrier; ③ Qi Jiguang's manual was read by all but executed by one — capital, discipline, and steel nerves fill the knowing-doing gap; ④ Three non-information resources are decisive: trial-error capital, power networks, psychological resilience; ⑤ Modern people suffer "omniscient paralysis" — knowing too much prevents action.
