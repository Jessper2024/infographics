Defending Bilibili

LatePost · January 2020 · How an "ordinary" man became the guardian of China's largest anime community
A man who couldn't remember his own home address, blew ¥10M in stock options down to ¥2.4M, and dreamed of working at the same company until retirement. How did this "ordinary person" become the controlling shareholder of China's largest ACG community? He didn't choose Bilibili — the brutal corporate warfare at 360 and Cheetah Mobile drove him into anime as an escape. Fate pushed the least aggressive man into a job that demands the most restraint of all. The counterintuitive truth: the best community CEO is rarely the hungriest wolf.
01
The Unambitious
How does a man who can't find his own house and wasted $1.5M in options end up running a $7B company?
Chen Rui grew up comfortably in Chengdu. He turned down Huawei's ¥6,300/month offer for Kingsoft because the company talked about "building national software." He forgot his new home's address. He sold stock and fat-fingered an extra zero. His boss Lei Jun had to email him about matching his socks. Zhang Yiming wanted to build the world's greatest company. Lei Jun wanted to be Steve Jobs. Chen Rui wanted to stay at Kingsoft forever. A VC described him: "His personality is round, like a ball. No sharp edges. No aggression toward anyone."

02
Driven to Refuge
He didn't choose Bilibili. The war with 360 and Fu Sheng pushed him into anime as a shelter.
Chen Rui's four years at Cheetah Mobile were his darkest — 90% of his time was spent fighting 360, not creating value. His only daily escape: watching anime alone after midnight. He discovered Bilibili, became an early user, invested as an angel, and was eventually invited to become chairman. Months before Cheetah Mobile's IPO, he walked away — forfeiting options worth over $15M. "You can only have one thing in life. And you have to give up before you can receive."

03
Counterintuitive Calls
No growth team. 100-question entrance exam. A permanent ban on pre-roll ads. Every call defied internet orthodoxy.
Bilibili went years without a user growth team, yet built the highest membership barrier in Chinese internet: answer 100 questions in 60 minutes, score 60+ to comment. When users panicked over pre-roll ads in 2016, Chen Rui publicly promised: never. "If Bilibili turned bad, it would break my heart. So I have to suppress my own hunger for growth and speed." In an industry where growth is god, every major decision was a deliberate reversal.
🏭 Standard Internet Playbook
· Growth team + paid user acquisition
· Lowered registration barriers, fast expansion
· Pre-roll ads → standard monetization
· Data-driven, KPI-oriented
🎯 The Bilibili Way
· Zero growth team, zero paid acquisition
· 100-question exam with 60-point cutoff
· Public promise: no pre-roll ads, ever
· "Only let those with初心 become creators"
04
The Silent Guardian
"Users should never be able to meet anywhere" — the counterintuitive design that kept community from collapsing
Chen Rui barely speaks in meetings. His most common response: "Got it, I'll think about it." But three design choices define his legacy: ① All social interaction must orbit around creator content — no open forums, no chat squares; ② Hypersensitive bullet-comment moderation, instant bans for fights; ③ Gave creators the power to delete comments against online mobs. Scale up 10x — as long as the content structure holds, the culture holds.

05
Forced Acceleration
The most restrained CEO finally said "grow or die" — because preservation isn't about locking the gates
In 2019, Chen Rui finally resolved to push Bilibili into high gear, targeting a $10B valuation within three years. This wasn't ambition — it was cold survival logic. Old users age out. If new users can't enter, the community dies. If traffic plateaus, creators lose motivation → less content → further user loss → death spiral. Douban is the cautionary tale: it preserved books/film/music categories but refused to expand, shrinking into a mere utility. "If you listen to your oldest users, the community's best state is the moment it was born."

06
One-Man Company
$10B is the survival line, but Bilibili's biggest risk isn't the competition — it's "if Chen Rui falls, the company falls."
Bilibili's revenue leans heavily on a single game. Its management layer is immature. Chen Rui, inheriting Lei Jun's obsessive attention to detail, does everything himself — an asset and the ultimate liability. He missed the livestreaming window. In late 2019, he began importing senior executives to break the one-man dependency. When asked if he worries Bilibili will end up like Kingsoft — never peaking but never dying — he said: "If I can make Bilibili like Kingsoft, I've already succeeded. Thirty years from now, how many of today's companies will still be alive?"
📈 Near-Term Engines
