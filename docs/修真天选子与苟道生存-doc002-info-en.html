The Chosen One & The Dog Tao of Trading

A Weibo essay that uses xianxia cultivation metaphors to explain trader psychology and survival

Core Insight

This essay uses the xianxia (cultivation novel) framework to expose survivorship bias and the limits of imitating star traders. Liangxi-style "Chosen Ones" succeed through a non-replicable script — they jump off cliffs and land in ancient treasure caves (blow up and bounce back), but ordinary people who jump just splatter. The real path to survival isn't trying to be a protagonist, but embracing the "Dog Tao" — know you're an ordinary cultivator, manage your risk, and outlast everyone else.

01

The Chosen vs The Mortal

A Chosen One's script is pre-written; a mortal's path is carved step by step. Never confuse a biography with a playbook. From birth, the universe allocates every resource to a Chosen One. They jump off cliffs and always land in ancient treasure caves. They're hunted to near-death and awaken a hidden bloodline or find a grandmaster in a ring. The more brutal their tribulation, the more insane the reward that follows.

The Chosen One

  • Jump off cliff → Ancient cave + inheritance
  • Near-death → Bloodline awakens
  • Worse tribulation → Bigger payoff
  • No risk control needed — fate has their back

The Ordinary Cultivator

  • Jump off cliff → Splatter on the ground
  • Near-death → Actually dies
  • Tribulation → Spiritual collapse, death
  • No risk control = no second chance
02

The Liangxi Trap: Hero ≠ Mentor

Admiring Liangxi is fine. Copying his trades? That's suicide. Liangxi is a Chosen One — born for this game. His risk appetite, psychology, and trading style are written into his script. When an ordinary trader copies him, it's the same as a random cultivator watching someone jump off a cliff for treasure and doing the same — ending up as a red smear on the rocks.

⚠️ Warning: "Meat and wine pass through the gut, but Buddha remains in the heart. If ordinary men try to imitate me, they walk the demon's path." The full quote includes the warning. Half-quotes are self-deception.

For an ordinary cultivator, even the smallest divine lightning tribulation requires a century of preparation — formations, spirit weapons, lightning-resistant pills. One mistake and you're gone. Trading is the same: before every trade, risk control is your formation, and a stop-loss is your lightning pill.

03

The Cost of Half-Quotes

The full version warns you to stop. The half-version encourages you to go all in. Everyone quotes "Fortune favors the bold." The full version: "Fortune favors the bold, but the bold lose it too. Seek ten, get one; risk ten, lose nine." The original intent was a warning — know when to pull back. Chasing everything will destroy you.

Traders make the same mistake: they hear "high risk, high reward" and stop listening before "high risk = high probability of losing everything." On the trading floor, the people who only remember the inspirational slogans are usually the first to go broke.

04

The Mortal's Tribulation Protocol

If you're not the Chosen One, make risk control your main job and profit the byproduct. A Chosen One doesn't need stop-losses because fate writes their comeback story. You're not them. Every trade without a stop-loss is a tribulation without a lightning-resistant pill.

05

The Dog Tao: Last One Standing Wins

In cultivation novels, the ones who ascend to immortality are rarely the blazing prodigies — they're the mediocre cultivators who mastered the "Dog Tao." The flashy geniuses become stepping stones for protagonists and ancient monsters. The "Dog Saints" — talentless, patient, unremarkable — outlive everyone and inherit their enemies' legacies.

Markets work the same way. There will always be star traders — 100x in a year, legendary comebacks after blowing up. But the people still trading after 10 or 20 years are the ones nobody's heard of. Join the Dog Tao. Accepting you're not the protagonist isn't surrender — it's knowing your niche, then using time and discipline to win.

📌 Takeaway

A Chosen One's success is not a replicable strategy. Liangxi is a cultivation novel protagonist — copying him leads to spiritual collapse. The trader's real edge: know you're not the main character, build your risk systems (formation + weapon + pill), and play the Dog Tao — outlast the market through discipline and time, not through one all-in gamble.
