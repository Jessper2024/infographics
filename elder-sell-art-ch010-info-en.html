The Art of Selling · Ch.10 "Exploring the Bottom"

When markets plunge into complete despair, how to systematically identify true bottoms and enter with precision using coordinated multi-tool signals

Overview

Bottoms aren't guessed — they're "scanned" out using systematic signal combinations

In autumn 2008, the S&P 500 weekly NH-NL indicator collapsed to -18,000 — a level never before seen in U.S. stock market history. Markets entered total despair: Bear Stearns bankrupt, Lehman liquidated, institutions refusing to trade with one another out of counterparty risk fear. Yet it was precisely in this extreme sentiment that the most reliable bottom signals began to emerge. Elder devotes an entire chapter to a complete bottom-trading system: from extreme indicator alerts to multi-timeframe confirmation, to false-breakout divergence pattern recognition, to value-zone channel execution — five layers of coordinated signals, none optional.

01
Extreme Indicator

NH-NL Extreme Oversold: The Contrarian Signal in Despair

When the NH-NL indicator drops below -4,000, it generates a powerful buy signal — in the entire history of the S&P 500, this indicator had never fallen below -6,000. In October 2008, that rule was shattered: the weekly NH-NL plunged to -18,000, a spike reflecting markets at the point of total despair. When the indicator returned above the zero line from severely oversold levels, it meant the market would not "shut down." The key question shifted from "Will it collapse?" to "When will the bottom appear?"

Confirmation System

The Double Helix: Multi-Timeframe Signal Confirmation

Signals appearing simultaneously across different timeframes are rare. Professional traders can handle this complexity, but beginners often cannot even identify the signals. The Triple Screen system provides a clear methodology: start with long-term charts for strategic decisions, then shift to daily charts for tactical timing. Because the daily NH-NL indicator is highly sensitive, you must wait for a "second crossover" to confirm a buy signal — what Spike group's Stephen Morris calls the "Double Helix" buy signal.

03
Core Pattern

False Breakout + Divergence: The Most Reliable Bottom Pattern

A false breakout accompanied by bullish divergence is one of the most important bottom patterns, especially reliable on weekly charts. The RIMM (BlackBerry) case perfectly illustrates the "missing right shoulder" formation: the stock broke below its prior low by just 4 cents ($35.09 → $35.05) then quickly recovered. The MACD histogram formed a divergence structure: Bottom 2 < 0 → Top 3 > 0 → Bottom 4 ≥ 0. The composite system turning from red to blue confirmed the buy. That break below the prior low surely stopped out those who placed stops "one cent below the prior low" — that's precisely the lethality of a false breakout.

✅ Correct Approach

Wait for price to break the prior low → confirm the false breakout with quick recovery → verify MACD bullish divergence → wait for composite system color change → enter in the value zone, with a stop below the new low

❌ Common Mistakes

Blindly buying the dip on a new low (without false-breakout confirmation); ignoring MACD or Force Index divergence signals; entering impulsively without a stop or without waiting for the system signal

04
Entry & Exit

Value Zone Channel: Precise Entry, Exit & Staged Profit-Taking

Buy in the value zone (near the lower channel band), sell near the upper band in overbought territory — this is the core rule of Elder's channel trading system. CEPH case study: bought at $54.62 in the value zone, sold at $57.06 near the upper band, capturing 53% of channel width — Grade A rating. A more refined strategy is staged profit-taking: sell half at the upper channel band to lock in profits, then sell the rest when the composite system changes color. The O case yielded 39% and 44% scores respectively. Accepting that you "sold too early" is a forgivable mistake, far wiser than the "pain of holding floating profits."

05
Psychology & Discipline

Trading Psychology: Walk With Your System

Bull markets do not rise in a straight line. Charles Dow and Robert Rhea described three classic bull-market phases — each transition between phases is interrupted by corrections. Professional traders repeatedly trade the same stock, enduring multiple stop-outs without giving up, eventually securing a good entry in the expected direction — like building a relationship rather than bar-hopping. Steve Alcorn's lesson is profound: "Opportunities that don't require much experience or skill to make money come rarely." Executing calmly and relaxedly beats chasing the perfect signal.

Key Takeaway

A bottom is not a single signal — it is the systematic coordination of multiple tools: extreme indicator alert → double-helix multi-timeframe confirmation → false-breakout divergence pattern recognition → value-zone channel execution → disciplined staged profit-taking. Trust your system and execute calmly and relaxedly. It is worth having a simple trading system, with the plan in your head and risk relatively small.
