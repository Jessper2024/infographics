Wenhua Kulv · Ch. 7 "Hidden Spring in the Desert"

The summit is just a narrow ledge—true warmth and beauty hide in deep valleys. Giving peace to the restless is the rhythm of heaven and earth

Core insight: Climbing Mingsha Sand Mountain is a bodily philosophy—the harder you push, the deeper you sink. At the summit, you find only a narrow ledge; Crescent Moon Spring hides in the deep valley. But the descent takes mere steps. Most of life’s anxieties are self-inflicted. ‘Give peace to the restless, give cool clarity to the impatient, give groundedness to the lofty, give beauty to the rough’—this is the rhythm of heaven and earth.

QuestionWhat can a hidden spring in the deep desert tell us about life?

AnswerClimbing Mingsha Sand Mountain serves as a metaphor for life—the more you struggle, the deeper you sink; you can only relax and ‘grind against’ it. At the summit, you find only a narrow ledge; true warmth and beauty hide in the deep valley, unreachable. The descent takes mere steps—those seemingly tragic fears were all self-inflicted. An old nun guards Crescent Moon Spring: ‘Give peace to the restless, give cool clarity to the impatient.’ Climbing up and sliding down—this is a complete life.

1
Body Philosophy

The Harder You Struggle, the Deeper You Sink: Sand Mountain’s Gentle Violence

Climbing Mingsha Sand Mountain, the harder you push, the deeper you sink—the soft sand ‘doesn’t scrape, doesn’t trip you, it just gently erases all your strength.’ The sand mountain teaches through tender dissolution: brute force is useless.

The author grew up in the mountains of eastern Zhejiang and could sprint to summits with raw power. But here, ‘the more you rage, the gentler it becomes—gentle to the point of infuriating.’ The only choice: ‘suppress the thunderous anger, relax the soles of your feet, and grind against it.’ The sand keeps no plank roads trodden by millions—only ‘your own footprints.’ Climbing is not for the summit but for ‘the curve already drawn’ and ‘the life already spent’—‘Not for the peak growing higher, but for this curve already drawn—climb. No matter where it leads, for the life already consumed—climb.’ (Yu Qiuyu)

2
Summit Truth

The Summit Is Just a Narrow Ledge Barely Wide Enough to Stand On

After immense struggle to reach the top, the author discovers the summit is ‘just a narrow ledge barely wide enough to stand—you can’t walk sideways, can’t walk forward, can only enjoy a moment’s bird’s-eye pleasure. How can one stay long?’ The height that overlooks all things turns out to be self-mockery.

At the summit, the author feels ‘an unprecedented loneliness and dread.’ ‘The truly warm beauties of this world all press close to the earth, hidden in deep valleys.’ Crescent Moon Spring lies at the mountain’s base, separated by a near-vertical slope—‘you can’t discover it without climbing high; having climbed high, you can’t get close.’ Such is life’s predicament. Yet the sunset dunes are breathtaking: ‘Light and shadow divided by the cleanest lines, gold and dark brown so pure they seem sifted through an enormous sieve.’ (Yu Qiuyu)

3
Subversive Experience

Hours to Climb Up, Mere Steps to Come Down

Clenching teeth, the author tries descending—and: ‘A miracle. Nothing happened. Two steps and I’d slid several meters down, standing perfectly steady.’ Hours of agonizing ascent took just a few steps to reverse. Kant said: the comical is the mismatch between expectation and outcome.

The descent was like slow-motion dance—‘each stride like dancing, just a dozen steps to the bottom.’ The author ‘laughed out loud’: the tragic determination and terror before stepping down had been entirely self-inflicted. ‘Hours of agonizing climb, and coming down took mere steps!’ The sand mountain’s ascent and descent form a perfect bodily metaphor—life’s seemingly impossible climbs are, in retrospect, just brief descents.

4
Heaven-Earth Rhythm

Crescent Moon Spring: A Lone Wanderer Creating a Charming World by Its Own Strength

Crescent Moon Spring—‘a crescent of clear water lying at the mountain’s base,’ clear, serene, slender and graceful—coexists impossibly with swirling sand and midnight gales. An old nun guards a rough hut beside it, her face lined with fine, tranquil wrinkles.

The spring stretches three to four hundred paces, dense with water plants, three black-feathered ducks floating on its surface. ‘In the vastness of the desert, entirely by its own strength, it has arranged a charming world.’ (Yu Qiuyu) The old nun answered only where her supplies came from, then fell silent—‘for a Buddhist, such questions are too clumsy.’ Swirling sands have never buried this spring; midnight gales have never drained it. ‘Give peace to the restless, give cool clarity to the impatient, give groundedness to the lofty, give beauty to the rough. Only thus does life show its vitality, the world its refinement, history its grace.’ (Yu Qiuyu) The mountain is Mingsha, the spring is Yueyaquan—both within Dunhuang.

‘Give peace to the restless, give cool clarity to the impatient, give groundedness to the lofty, give beauty to the rough. Only thus does life show its vitality, the world its refinement, history its grace.’
— Yu Qiuyu, ‘Hidden Spring in the Desert’
💡
Key Takeaway

Mingsha Sand Mountain and Crescent Moon Spring form an ultimate pair of metaphors: climbing upward is human instinct, but the summit is only a narrow ledge; true warmth and beauty lie hidden in the deep valley. Life is destined for endless climbing up and sliding down—only by preserving stillness amid clamor and clarity amid impatience do we touch the rhythm of heaven and earth.

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Wenhua Kulv · Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
