Cultural Bitter Journey · Ch.16 "Tianyi Pavilion in Storm"

How a private library defied book burnings, wars, floods, fire, and theft for over four centuries, becoming an indomitable symbol of Chinese civilization

CORE PROPOSITION

Tianyi Pavilion is not merely a library—it is the living symbol of Chinese civilization's struggle against destruction. Fan Qin built Asia's oldest surviving private library through astonishing willpower. His descendants guarded it for four centuries under quasi-religious family rules. When great scholars like Huang Zongxi were permitted entry, the library transcended one family. After surviving theft and war, it became a witness to modern cultural conscience.

01
Context

The Enemies of Books: A History of Cultural Destruction

A book's survival is extraordinarily fragile—the disasters that attack it are each powerful and merciless.

Imperial book burnings: from Qin Shi Huang to Qing literary inquisitions. War: Dong Zhuo's rebellion destroyed 6,000+ cartloads; Western Wei troops burned 140,000 scrolls in one day; the Sui rebellion burned 370,000. Water and fire: capsized boats, fires, pests, mold—two centuries of collection could vanish in a day.

02
Founder

Fan Qin: A Bibliophile Chosen by History

History wanted China to produce an outstanding bibliophile, so it placed Fan Qin in an official career that sent him across the entire country.

Fan Qin was born the same year Da Vinci completed the Mona Lisa. His official career spanned Hubei, Jiangxi, Guangxi, Fujian, Yunnan, Shaanxi—most of China. At every post he collected local gazetteers and contemporary documents. He once suffered 'court beating'—stripped and beaten 36 times on false charges—which forged his psychological steel.

“
For Fan Qin, book collecting was his life's main work; being an official was his side job. The busy 'official duties' you spend your life on may not be your primary contribution to the world.
—— Yu Qiuyu

Unlike other collectors who worshipped only Song editions, Fan Qin focused on contemporary documents and social materials—a choice that made him irreplaceable in Chinese history.

03
Legacy

Family Guardianship: Four Centuries of Hard Labor

The heroic history of Tianyi Pavilion began after Fan Qin's death—he bound his descendants to a crushing duty: 'The books must never be divided.'

Fan Qin compensated his second son with 10,000 taels of silver and bequeathed all books to his eldest son. The family rules were severe: unauthorized entry meant exclusion from three ancestral rites; bringing visitors meant one year; lending books meant three years. Qian Xiuyun, niece of the Ningbo prefect, married into the Fan family just to see the books—but the rule forbidding women from the tower drove her to die of despair.

⚠️
The Family Paradox: If no one could ever read the books, what was the library's purpose? The Fan family knew: open the smallest crack, and it would split wide. Keys were held by different branches—if even one was absent, no book could be accessed. Four centuries of maintaining this lonely cultural fortress.

'I can't even see a single herb. What's the point of living? If you love me, bury me near Tianyi Pavilion, so I can finally rest.' —Qian Xiuyun's dying words.

04
Turning Point

Opening to Great Scholars: The Library Finds Meaning

Huang Zongxi's visit in 1673 marked the turning point—Tianyi Pavilion became a sacred site that could open to truly great scholars.

Huang Zongxi's character, integrity, and scholarship so moved the Fan family that they permitted him to read all the books. Over the following two centuries, only about a dozen scholars were granted entry. When the Qianlong Emperor compiled the Siku Quanshu, Tianyi Pavilion contributed over 600 rare texts, 96 of which were included—a family collection became an imperial dissemination.

“
Tianyi Pavilion finally revealed its reason for existence, even though the opportunities for such revelation were so rare.
—— Yu Qiuyu

The Qianlong Emperor ordered major imperial libraries to be modeled on Tianyi Pavilion's architecture. Fan Qin had achieved the ultimate success—his library lived on in the national encyclopedia and imperially designated collections.

05
Ordeal

Modern Trials and Cultural Conscience

In modern times, Tianyi Pavilion suffered theft and war—yet through these trials, it became a witness to modern cultural conscience.

During the Taiping attack on Ningbo, thieves stole books to sell as scrap paper. In 1914, Xue Jiwei sneaked into the tower and stole nearly half its precious collection. Zhang Yuanji of Commercial Press urgently rescued the books—only for them to be entirely incinerated by Japanese bombs. Yet in the 1930s, 50s, 60s, and 80s, Tianyi Pavilion was repeatedly restored.

✅ Surviving strength

Like a worldly-wise elder, it gazes at posterity with philosophical calm—it can withstand the greatest disasters. It has become a witness to modern cultural conscience.

⚠️ Shadow of destruction

Date pits spat out by the thief littered the floorboards Huang Zongxi once tread. The burning of Hanfen Lou proved: civilization's trials never truly end.

The author climbed the stairs with agonizing slowness, asking himself: 'Have you come? Which generation of Chinese scholar are you?'

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

The story of Tianyi Pavilion answers the ultimate question: how does civilization survive against all odds? From Fan Qin's vision, the Fan family's sacrifice, to Huang Zongxi's entry and Zhang Yuanji's rescue—each generation infused this library with meaning beyond themselves. It reminds us: the preservation of civilization never happens naturally. It is a heroic undertaking completed by specific individuals, difficult choices, and generations of unwavering persistence.
