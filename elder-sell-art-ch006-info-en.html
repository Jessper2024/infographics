The New Sell & Short, 2nd Ed. · Ch.6 Selling on Trading Noise

Markets don't always follow your plan — how discretionary traders exit before the stop is hit

Core Question
When the market emits unusual "noise," should you stick to the plan or exit early?

Systematic traders follow signals; discretionary traders enjoy a luxury — the option to tighten stops and exit when they don't like what the market is doing. But this requires deep experience.

The Answer: Discretionary traders identify five forms of trading noise — weakening momentum, pre-earnings risk, market alarm bells, NH-NL divergence, and a structured decision tree — to exit before stops are triggered, protecting profits and reassessing from the sidelines.

01
Momentum Fade

Weakening Momentum — MACD Bearish Divergence Is the Earliest Exit Signal

When MACD histogram and price form a bearish divergence — the stock no longer moves in your favor — a savvy swing trader exits early rather than waiting for the target. The price may or may not reach the target; taking profits and reassessing from the sidelines is the safer play.

02
Info Risk

Selling Before Earnings — The Hidden Battlefield of Leaks and Insider Trading

Earnings reports rarely "surprise" the market: experts forecast them, and information often leaks in advance. Insider trading is far more widespread than most imagine — only the greedy and stupid get caught. If a company has a history of leaks, exit before the announcement.

⚠️ Information Leaked in Advance

Price already reflects expectations before the announcement. "Buy the rumor, sell the fact" dynamics create gap risk. CSCO case: price stood above the upper channel before earnings, then plunged post-announcement.

🔒 Honest Announcement

When a company keeps secrets well, the announcement can trigger violent moves. RIMM case: three positive surprises released simultaneously — shares gapped up to an all-time high, negating all bearish divergences.

⚠️
Safe strategy: No matter how confident you are in your system, closing trades before earnings always puts you on the safe side. Even if the stock moves favorably afterward, you only lose an opportunity — not your capital.
03
Extreme Sentiment

Market Alarm Bells — When Outsiders Start Making Consistent Money, the Top Is Near

Alarm bells ring rarely, but the signal is unmistakable: when amateurs consistently profit and the least sophisticated start recommending stocks, market laws appear suspended — but that's only the temporary illusion of a bubble. Hearing the bell and acting is what separates novices from disciplined traders.

A Japan Airlines steward earned more from stocks than his salary; months later the Nikkei crashed, losing half its value
1929

Leading Indicator

NH-NL Index — The Best Leading Indicator for the Stock Market

New Highs (NH) represent bullish power; New Lows (NL) represent bearish power. A positive NH−NL value signals bull dominance; divergence warns of reversal. When the S&P 500 hits new highs but NH-NL declines, a major top signal has formed.

Key Takeaway

Trading noise is the discretionary trader's most valuable early-warning system. Systematic traders rely on signals; discretionary traders rely on judgment — the two are not opposites. The best traders fuse both: hunt for opportunities and set stops like a system, while listening to market noise like a hunter. When MACD diverges, earnings loom, alarm bells ring, or NH-NL warns — exiting early is not cowardice, it is discipline.
