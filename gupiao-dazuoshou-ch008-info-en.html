Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Chapter 8

Livermore reveals the core principle of position-building: never average down, never add to a losing position. The correct method is to pyramid up—every new lot must build on the profit of the previous one.

Overview

How to Build Positions on the Way Up and Add Only When Right?

Buy on the way up, sell on the way down. Livermore completely rejected averaging down: test with 2,000 shares first; if it rises one point, buy another 2,000; keep adding only as the position proves correct. Every new lot must rest on the profit of the prior one. The Deacon White story perfectly illustrates: use test trades (selling) to verify buyer appetite, then commit once confirmed.

Core thesis: A good beginning is half the battle—trading success depends critically on choosing the right moment to begin.

01
Position Rule

Pyramiding Up: Why Must You Buy Higher, Not Lower?

Never wish to buy too cheap or too easily. Livermore’s system was explicit: buy 2,000 shares at 110. If it rises to 111—correct, buy another 2,000. At 114—still correct, buy another 2,000. Average cost 110¾ for 6,000 shares. Wait for a pullback. If it drops to 112¼ and rallies, buy 4,000 at 113¾. The key is watching how those 4,000 fill: if prices rise as you buy, you’re right; if fills are scattered, something’s wrong.

02
Classic Case

The Deacon White Story: Testing Information Through Action

Information alone proves nothing; only market reaction reveals truth. A tipster told Deacon White: “H.O. Havemeyer is buying Sugar heavily.” White immediately ordered to sell 10,000 shares. His logic: sell to test the market’s absorptive capacity. When it was absorbed smoothly, he sold another 10,000—also absorbed. Conclusion: real buying power existed. He covered and bought 30,000, making a fortune.

03
Big Money

The Art of Selling Large Positions: Sell When You Can, Not When You Want

You can’t expect the market to absorb 50,000 shares as easily as 100. Livermore highlighted the core dilemma of large-scale trading: easy to enter, hard to exit. The holder of large positions must patiently wait for sufficient buying power to absorb them. When the moment arrives, act decisively—“he must sell when he can, not when he wants to.”

❌ Amateur Mistake

Go all-in immediately without considering market conditions. Use the full 500-share limit at once, leaving no room for probing.

✅ Professional Approach

Buy 100 shares as a probe. Add only when profitable. Observe, test, wait. When selling, equally assess market absorptive capacity—patience for the right moment.

💡
Key Takeaway

Livermore’s position-building philosophy can be distilled into one sentence: never add to a loser, only pyramid on winners. Probe → Confirm → Add → Wait → Add again—this cycle strictly follows the iron rule that every step must rest on the profit of the previous one. Deacon White’s classic case further reveals the methodology of information verification: never trust any tip; test the market with real trades and let the market tell you the truth.
