Fooled by Randomness · Chapter 13 "Probability and Skepticism"

Nothing is certain — only conclusions of varying degrees of probability
13
Probability Is a Measure of Belief, Not a Computation

Situation: Probability found a home in the mathematics of gambling, but its roots lie much deeper — it began as a subjective measure of belief in the hands of ancient Greek rhetoricians.
Complication: Modern financial academia attempts to "scientifically" measure market risk — forgetting that nature's game has no written rules.
Question: Do we have the courage to embrace skepticism — to admit uncertainty, and overturn our most firmly held opinions at a moment's notice?
Answer: Scientists are more dangerous than science. True rationality lies not in precise calculation, but in refusing to defend your past self.

01
Classical Skepticism
Carneades in Rome: Two Truths, One Location

In 155 BC, the Greek skeptic Carneades arrived in Rome and delivered a dialectical performance at the Senate that would echo through two millennia. In the same place, over two days, he persuaded the same audience of two completely opposite arguments.

🏛️ Day 1 · Justice Supreme
Justice is the highest motive

With flawless logic and compelling rhetoric, he argued that exercising justice stands at the apex of human motivation. The Roman audience was deeply moved.

🔄 Day 2 · Everything Overturned
Demolished his own argument with equal force

Using equally persuasive reasoning, he argued that justice should rank at the bottom of human motives. No knowledge is absolutely certain.

"Carneades was not only a skeptic but a dialectician — he never clung to the premises behind his reasoning, nor to any conclusions derived from them. He spent his life opposing the dogma of a single truth."
— The core tenet of skepticism: nothing is absolutely certain

The elderly Censor Cato was outraged and persuaded the Senate to expel the three envoys from Rome — he could not tolerate such intellectual "uncertainty." Yet this event profoundly shaped generations of thinkers, cementing the skeptic's most rigorous stance: we can reach conclusions of varying probability, and use them as guides for action.

02
Historical Roots
The Birth of "the Most Probable": Probability as Argument

The earliest known probability thinking emerged in 6th-century BC Sicily. The rhetorician Corax first introduced the concept of "the most probable" into legal argumentation — when direct evidence is lacking, decide based on the most likely scenario.

"For instance, land ownership — in the absence of further information or physical evidence — belongs to the person most people know as the owner."
— Corax's "most probable" method, an example

Probability began as a tool of argument and judgment, not cold mathematical computation. It is inherently about "what we believe" — perfectly aligned with Taleb's definition in Chapter 3: probability concerns belief in alternative outcomes, causes, or motives, not the calculation of odds.

03
Intellectual Liberation
The Right to Contradict Oneself: Proust vs. Soros

In 1968, during the French student uprising, Paris protesters scrawled on walls: "We demand the right to contradict ourselves!" — the weight of always having to appear intellectually consistent had become suffocating. Yet our culture treats changing one's mind as a betrayal.

📖
Proust's Marquis de Norpois

A semi-retired diplomat who publicly contradicted himself. Proust pilloried him: "He wasn't lying. He simply forgot." Society framed inconsistency as shameful.

🎯
George Soros: Flipping in a Snap

Bearish and heavily short, then completely reversed within days — not only covering shorts but building a massive long position. Made a fortune, without embarrassment. His actions are never shackled by the past.

"One of Soros's strengths is his ability to revise his opinions at considerable speed, without the slightest embarrassment. What sets true speculators like him apart is their lack of path dependence — they are completely unshackled from past actions. Every day is a blank slate."
— Taleb on Soros's non-path-dependent trait

The trader Bebic was the same — he might impulsively buy a currency he had strongly predicted would fall just hours earlier, without feeling the least bit awkward. What changed his mind? He saw no need to answer that question.

04
Behavioral Trap
Path-Dependent Beliefs: Would You Keep the Painting?

Evolution has wired us to defend ideas into which we've sunk time — we treat them like our own children. But Taleb proposes a simple test that exposes this path dependence:

🖼️ You own a painting bought at $20,000, now worth $40,000.
If you didn't own it, would you buy it at the current market price?
✅
Yes, I Would Buy

Non-path-dependent. You decide based on true current value, unshackled by historical cost. Every day is a blank slate.

⚠️
No, I Would Not

Path-dependent. If you wouldn't buy at the market price, the price exceeds its true worth — holding it is emotional investment. You are clinging to the status quo.

"Many people cling to their ideas until the grave. When a chain of ideas takes its marching orders from the first one, we call that path-dependent belief."
— Rational investors view this as a genetic anomaly, perhaps a rare mutation

Politicians don't switch parties based on new evidence. Academics won't say anything that undermines their past achievements. Party-switchers are branded "turncoats" or "traitors." Soros-types may be products of a genetic defect — their amygdalae are flawed, yet this makes them rational decision-makers.

05
Sober Reflection
Science Is Great, Scientists Are Dangerous: LTCM & the "Measurement" Illusion

Nobel laureates Merton and Scholes believed they could "scientifically" measure market risk. In 1998, their Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) nearly brought down the global financial system. What did they do after the crisis?

💥
LTCM's Scientists

Denied any misunderstanding of markets. Refused to admit their method was flawed. Blamed "vultures" in the market for accelerating their collapse. Defended themselves with all their might instead of learning and profiting from the episode.

🪞
The Soros Style

Tells anyone patient enough to listen: "I am fallible." Learns from mistakes, revises quickly, feels no embarrassment. Every day is a blank slate.

"People often confuse science with scientists. Science is great, but individual scientists are dangerous. They are human and contaminated by human biases, perhaps even more so. Science evolves from funeral to funeral."
— Taleb: not through scientists changing their minds, but through old ones exiting and new ones entering
Chapter's Ultimate Insight: Probability is a measure of belief, not a tool of computation. Embrace skepticism, admit uncertainty, refuse to defend your past self — those unshackled from path dependence are the ones who survive and profit from randomness. Science advances not because scientists admit error, but because generations of old guards exit. So too does the market.
Source: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, Fooled by Randomness, Chapter 13 · Probability and Skepticism
