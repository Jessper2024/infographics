1
Life Upheaval

From Prime-Minister Material to Exiled Criminal

Huangzhou was a remote, bleak riverside town — Su Shi's official title was "Acting Vice Director of the Water Bureau, Huangzhou Military Vice Commander, locally confined, forbidden to sign official documents," a punishment akin to banishment with no end in sight.

"Life is truly an unpredictable circle." He recalled his meteoric rise in the capital — the emperor called him prime-minister material, senior officials welcomed him as an honored guest. Who could have imagined ending up in this desolate town? What courage, what perseverance, what extraordinary psychological resilience would it take to face such a boundless life upheaval?

As a criminal official, he had no official residence. He and his son lodged at Dinghui Temple, eating vegetarian meals with monks. His salary was paid in kind — mostly leftover wine sacks from the government storehouse. He mocked himself: "I laugh at myself: a lifetime busy for my mouth."

2
Psychological Trauma

Fear and Withdrawal: Sleeping by Day, Walking by Night

The calamity that fell from heaven gave Su Shi an inexplicable terror and trembling toward the outside world. In his early days at Huangzhou, he often stayed indoors all day, sleeping from morning till night, venturing out only under the quiet moonlight.

"Drowsy, I lie back down, tossing without satisfaction. Forcing myself to go out, my lonely dream still continues." Everywhere were traps, everywhere unfathomable dangers. Even when he occasionally bought a cup of rustic wine at a tavern by the city wall, he remained vigilant: "The true flavor of drink grows richer with age, but drunken wild words are terrifying when sober."

The people of Huangzhou grew accustomed to seeing a lonely stranger wandering aimlessly through the hills and waters — sometimes standing dazed before flowers, sometimes muttering to what seemed like the birds, the trees, the fish, the clouds. They vaguely sensed: he was a kind-hearted eccentric.

3
Spiritual Self-Portrait

The Lonely Wild Goose: An Immortal Lyric

One night by the Yangtze, Su Shi saw a solitary wild goose sweep past — among the cold forest's thousand branches, the lonely bird refused to fold its wings casually. With a mournful cry, it finally landed on the desolate sandbar in the river's heart. This lonely yet proud goose was Su Shi's very incarnation.

Thus an immortal masterpiece burst forth: "A fragment moon hangs on the sparse parasol tree; the water clock stills, all people quiet. Who sees the recluse coming and going alone — the shadow of a misty, solitary goose? Startled, it turns back — with regret no one understands. Choosing among cold branches, unwilling to perch alone — the chill on the lonely sandbar."

The upper stanza describes the recluse, the lower stanza the goose — the recluse is as lonely as the goose, the goose as startled as the recluse, each line echoing double meanings. "Choosing among cold branches, unwilling to perch" is a declaration of personality: even as a criminal official cast to the ends of the earth, he refused to drift with the current and abandon his integrity.

4
Life's Sorrow

The Crabapple Self-Portrait: Resonance with a Noble Flower

On a small hillside east of Dinghui Temple, Su Shi discovered a crabapple tree in full bloom — a precious flower native to his homeland of Sichuan, inexplicably growing in this backwater where no one recognized its value. Deeply moved, he wrote his immortal "Crabapple Song."

"A charming smile within the bamboo fence; among the hills, peach and plum seem utterly vulgar. The Creator must have had deep meaning, sending this beauty to an empty valley. Noble elegance comes from Heaven's endowment — no need for golden platters in ornate halls." The crabapple's innate nobility, needing no finery to enhance its worth, was exactly a portrait of Su Shi's own circumstances and character.

The poem uses personification to portray the crabapple as a noble, pure, uniquely elegant beauty, while simultaneously using it as a metaphor for Su Shi himself, bearing deep sorrow over life's vicissitudes. Critics hailed it as an immortal masterpiece. Su Shi loved it so much he personally copied it out as a gift dozens of times.

5
Spiritual Cleansing

Rebirth Through Fire: Bathing as Philosophical Ritual

Su Shi often visited Anguo Temple south of the city to bathe. During this daily ritual, he washed not only the dirt from his body but also cleansed his heart of attachments to glory and disgrace — each time emerging with a sense of total purity of body and mind.

"Not only forgetting clean and unclean, but also washing away glory and disgrace." Bathing had always been one of Su Shi's great pleasures, but now it took on philosophical depth. "How much dust and grime can there be? Suddenly freed from fetters. Dressed, I sit in a small pavilion, hair loose, facing slender bamboo. The mind weary, all attachments void; the body at ease, one bed enough."

As he gradually grew familiar with Huangzhou, Su Shi began to change his nocturnal habits. Though still speaking little and keeping to himself, he was slowly emerging from the initial terror and trembling — the hillside wanderings, the Anguo Temple baths, the moonlit solitary chanting — all helped repair a spirit shattered by political storms.

💡
Key Takeaway

Su Shi's early Huangzhou months trace a psychological arc from "Life is truly an unpredictable circle" to "Choosing among cold branches, unwilling to perch." He used the lonely wild goose to symbolize unyielding pride, the crabapple to express life's sorrows, and the bathing ritual to wash away glory and disgrace. These four months of retreat were not depression but accumulation before rebirth. As he wrote: "Clear poems I chant alone, harmonizing with myself; the white wine is gone — who could lend me more?" Poetry and nature became his most reliable salvation in the darkest hour.
