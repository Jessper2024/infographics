Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 25 "A Word of Advice to the Public"

In the tone of an elder, Livermore offers his most sincere advice to ordinary investors: don't blindly trust broker recommendations, recognize the "free gift" ruse from insiders, see through the essence of stock-split games — in a world without symmetrical regulation, your wallet must be guarded by you alone.

▎S — Situation
The public always hopes someone will tell them something. This is precisely what makes the giving and receiving of tips ubiquitous — brokers' market letters, friends' well-meaning recommendations, media's authoritative analysis — each one telling you what to buy and what to sell.
▎C — Conflict
Conflicts of interest pervade every link in the chain: brokers earn their living from commissions, yet simultaneously receive sell orders from insiders — for the same stock, they tell retail investors "buy" while executing "sell" orders for major shareholders. Insiders use "free red envelopes" — small favors — to have the public take over their positions. The law only punishes bearish rumors, not bullish lies — retail investors fight on an inherently asymmetric battlefield.
▎Q — Key Question
How can ordinary investors protect their hard-earned money in this information-asymmetric market?
▎A — Answer
Three iron rules: ① Never blindly trust any "free" recommendation — first ask whose side the recommender's interests are on; ② Never be tempted by "free gifts" — insider-gifted stocks are the most expensive presents, the price being that you help them open a distribution market; ③ See through packaging magic — stock splits and certificate color changes do not alter intrinsic value; when a stock needs price-cutting promotions to attract buyers, ask "why." Your most reliable ally is only your own independent thinking.
01
Advice One

Broker Recommendations: First Ask Who's Paying

It is entirely proper for brokers to provide trading advice to clients, whether through market letters or verbal suggestions. But Livermore exposes a fatal conflict of interest: the stocks that brokers induce the public to buy through market letters or verbal comments are often the very same stocks for which they have already received sell orders from insiders.

A more precise description: the insider approaches the head of a brokerage firm and says, "I'd like you to develop a market for my stock and help me sell 50,000 shares," with terms including put and call options. For the broker, as long as they have a large following, they can simultaneously earn commissions and option profits by mobilizing the crowd to buy — in other words, every time a retail investor "follows a recommendation," they are paying for the dual profits of both the broker and the insider.

Livermore's criterion is simple: the market's trajectory typically leads the current fundamental situation by 6 to 9 months. Today's profits do not constitute sufficient reason to buy — the broker cares about earning commissions now, while the trader must look far into the future.

02
Advice Two

Insider "Free Gifts": The Most Expensive Presents

Livermore portrays the standard operating procedure of a well-known Wall Street insider: he visits the customer manager of a large brokerage firm, expressing gratitude — "You've helped me many times in the past; now I want to show my appreciation. I'm going to give you a chance to really make some money." Then he "lets" them have a batch of stock at below market price — creating an instant "paper profit."

What do the customer managers who received these "red envelopes" do? Naturally, they recommend to every man and woman they can reach that they buy this stock — freely opening a market for that "generous insider." The insider uses a small portion of discounted stock as bait to have the entire following crowd take over his position. Livermore's warning cuts to the bone: free gifts are always the most expensive — what you receive is a "discount"; what you give away is the opportunity to count your own money.

03
Advice Three

The Stock-Split Game: Old Wine in New Bottles

Livermore exposes the most popular and completely legal sales gimmick of the era — the stock split: issuing 2, 4, or even 10 "new" shares to replace 1 "old" share. "The method frequently consisted of nothing more than changing the color of the stock certificate."

This is a pure marketing strategy: a 1-pound package of goods sold for $1 moves slowly; split it into quarter-pound mini-packages sold for 25 cents (or even 27 or 30 cents) — the total price is unchanged or even higher, but it "looks" cheaper. Livermore is pained by the public's refusal to think: "Why doesn't the public ask why the stock needs to be promoted through subdivision in the first place? This alone constitutes a complete warning signal — yet the public turns a blind eye, and as a result loses vast sums of money year after year."

A wise trader never blindly trusts the adversary. A stock split itself is not the problem — the problem is the motive. If the company is truly that good, why does it need "price-cut promotions" to attract buyers? A product that needs a packaging change to be sold is inherently suspect in its intrinsic value.

🎭
Recognition Signal: When a stock needs to be sold through splits, name changes, or certificate color changes, it often means its own value is no longer sufficient to move genuine investors. At that moment, what is needed is not "buy" but "ask why."
💡
Key Takeaway · BLUF

The most precious legacy Livermore leaves to ordinary investors is not trading techniques but this crystal-clear advice: in a market where the law only punishes bearish rumors but not bullish lies, your wallet can only be guarded by yourself. Three iron rules: never blindly trust any recommendation (first trace the interest relationship), never accept free gifts (what's given free is the most expensive), never worship packaging magic (splits and color changes do not equal value creation). Independent thinking is your only talisman on this asymmetric battlefield.
