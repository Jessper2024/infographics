Core QuestionWhat kind of place and family produced a literary genius like Su Dongpo?

Core AnswerMeishan, Sichuan — a comfortable small town at the foot of Mount Emei and by the Glass River, famous for its lotus flowers. The Su family, known as the "Three Sus," are among the "Eight Great Masters of Tang and Song." Grandfather Su Xu was illiterate yet extraordinarily wise and generous. Father Su Xun only began serious study at age 27 but achieved supreme literary fame. Mother, née Cheng, was well-educated and constantly encouraged her husband. The Sichuan people's argumentative spirit, confidence, and refusal to accept defeat were perfectly embodied in Su Dongpo.

1
Geography

Beneath Mount Emei: Meishan's Geography and Spirit

Meishan lies in western Sichuan, beneath the shadow of Mount Emei and by the Glass River — with a perfect water control and irrigation system that has worked for over a thousand years, making western Sichuan a fertile plain in all four seasons.

Traveling up the Yangtze, past Hankou, through the Three Gorges into far western Sichuan, you would see the Great Buddha of Leshan — a 360-foot Buddha carved from the cliff by the riverbank. Forty miles north of Leshan lies the town of Meishan in Meizhou prefecture, home to the most famous literary family in Chinese history — the Sus, known as the "Three Sus": father Su Xun and sons Su Shi (Dongpo) and Su Zhe (Ziyou), all three among the "Eight Great Masters of Tang and Song."

Meishan was not a large city but a very comfortable place to live. The streets were clean, famous for lotus flowers in May and June. Walking along the streets, you would see lotus ponds everywhere, their fragrance filling the air. The Su residence had a tall pear tree, a pond, and vegetable gardens. The small courtyard was planted with various flowers and fruit trees, with a dense bamboo grove beyond the wall.

2
Family Genes

The Wisdom of an Illiterate Old Man: Grandfather Su Xu

Grandfather Su Xu could not read, yet possessed extraordinary physical and mental vitality, a broad mind, and strong, decisive integrity — his wisdom lay dormant in his blood and later bloomed into extraordinary flowers and fruits in his grandson Su Dongpo.

In his youth, Su Xu was tall and handsome, healthy and strong, fond of drinking, generous and open-hearted. Unlike others who hoarded rice, he exchanged rice for unhusked grain and stored 4,000 measures. When famine came, he opened his granary and distributed grain — first to family and relatives, then to his wife's relatives, then to tenants and village poor. Only then did people understand: grain can be preserved for many years, while milled rice would spoil from damp.

On the day his second son passed the imperial exams, Su Xu was dead drunk with a piece of beef in his hand. He took the official notice, read it aloud to his friends, stuffed the beef, notice, official hat, and robe all into a bundle, had a village boy carry it, and rode into town on a donkey. The townspeople, seeing the drunk old man on the donkey with that strange bundle behind him, burst out laughing. Su Dongpo later said: "Only excellent scholars can appreciate that kind of innocence."

3
Father's Example

Late Bloomer: Father Su Xun's Comeback

Su Xun only began serious study at age 27, yet achieved supreme literary fame — his reputation was not entirely eclipsed by his outstanding sons, a rarity in Chinese literary history.

Su Xun was taciturn, highly intelligent, hot-tempered, independent-minded, and eccentric — not an easy person to get along with. He had ample opportunity to learn reading and writing as a child but wasted his youth. Only after his sons were born, seeing his elder brother, brothers-in-law, and others pass exams and serve as officials, did he feel ashamed and begin to apply himself seriously.

Su Xun's weakness was poetic rhyme — examinations required considerable verbal skill and wit, while Su Xun was only interested in concepts. Yet the monumental work he wrote on principles of politics, war, and peace was deeply original. A disciple of Su Dongpo once said: "Su Xun had better natural talent, but Su Dongpo had broader learning." Su Xun taught his sons to study the laws of historical rise and fall, to pursue a simple style, and to oppose the ornate fashion of the day.

4
Regional Spirit

The Argumentative Spirit and Confidence of Sichuan People

Sichuan people are hardy, argumentative, confident, and largely self-governing — Sichuan natives, ancient and modern, love to argue and produce powerful, lengthy essays. Su Dongpo shared this innate eloquence and refusal to accept defeat.

In Su Dongpo's era, many officials and successful scholars came from Sichuan, where the general academic standard was higher than in the provinces now assigned to Hebei and Shandong. Chengdu was the cultural center, famous nationwide for fine paper, Shu brocade, and beautiful temples. Scholars' works still adhered to the simple, classical style of early Han, entirely different from the decadent and flowery styles prevalent elsewhere.

Meizhou natives earned a reputation for being "hard to govern." Su Dongpo once explained: people here, unlike those from less cultured regions, would not be easily intimidated by officials. Every gentry household kept a law code and freely discussed legal provisions "without considering it improper." If a local official was fair and honest, at the end of his term people would hang his portrait in their homes and worship it for fifty years.

5
Cultural Soil

Home of Printing: Fertile Ground for Knowledge

Printing was invented in Sichuan a hundred years earlier, sparking a sudden enthusiasm for learning — even before Su Dongpo's birth, this land had already prepared the intellectual ground for a genius to emerge.

Even in that era, Sichuan preserved certain ancient customs and culture. Thanks especially to the invention of printing, Sichuan people suddenly developed a passion for learning. By the time of Su Dongpo's birth, Meizhou already had a considerably high cultural standard. Su Dongpo's mother, née Cheng, came from a landed aristocratic family and was well-educated. Su Xun noted in his wife's epitaph that she had constantly encouraged him to advance.

At Su Dongpo's birth, his grandfather was 63 and still alive. His father, 27, was undergoing the greatest spiritual crisis of his life — for seven years he had worshipped daily before a portrait of Zhang Xian, praying for a son, and his wish was finally granted. The family was comfortably off, owning farmland, with at least two maids and a wet nurse hired for Su Dongpo.

💡
Key Takeaway

Su Dongpo's birth was no accident. Four forces converged on that crying, kicking baby boy on December 19, 1036: the cultural spirit of Sichuan as an independent kingdom, his grandfather's wisdom and vitality, his father's late-blooming dedication, and his mother's fine education. As Lin Yutang put it: the illiterate old man's wisdom lay dormant in his blood, only to bloom into extraordinary flowers and fruits in his grandson.
