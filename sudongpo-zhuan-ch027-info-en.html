苏东坡传 · 第27章「Chao Yun」

Su Dongpo's Huizhou story is inseparable from Chao Yun. She followed him since Hangzhou, never leaving even after their infant son died. She was 31 in Huizhou; he was 57. Bright, cheerful, lively, and perceptive, she understood him better than any woman he had known. What is a love that transcends age and fate? When a young woman chooses to accompany an aging poet into life's twilight, what does that choice mean?

Su Dongpo compared Chao Yun to the 'Deva Vimalakirti' — the celestial maiden whose scattered flowers clung to nothing. Their love sublimated from worldly passion into spiritual communion: Chao Yun studied Buddhist sutras, Su practiced alchemy and health cultivation, together they built a life-release pond. In White Crane Residence, their bond was both the deepest human love and a shared spiritual quest.
01
Love Story

The Deva Vimalakirti

Su Dongpo likened Chao Yun to the flower-scattering celestial maiden — she had transcended all dualistic perception, understanding her husband's inner world with supra-mundane wisdom.

Buddhist scripture: a deva scattered flowers on bodhisattvas — petals clung to only one. She explained: 'Those whose petals fall away have eliminated distinctions. Just as fear cannot enter unless one first fears.' Unlike Bai Juyi's concubine who left her aging master, Chao Yun set aside her dancing sleeves for sutras and alchemy.

02
Poetic Witness

A Poem for Her Sash

'White hair, aging face — just the emptiness of Vimalakirti's realm… Tomorrow at Dragon Boat, I'll weave orchids for her sash and find a fine poem to write upon it.' — This lyric perfectly blends love and religion.

Written within two weeks of arriving in Huizhou. First stanza compares himself to Vimalakirti — scattered flowers cause no hindrance; second praises Chao Yun's 'vermilion lips, elegant hair,' looks forward to writing a poem for her sash at Dragon Boat. Love and religion intertwined — rare in classical Chinese poetry.

"
White hair, aging face — just the emptiness of Vimalakirti's realm; scattered flowers in this small room, what hindrance? Vermilion lips, elegant hairpins aglow. Through a thousand lives, only this good heart, these human graces — by the idle window, clouds gather, brows darken. Tomorrow at Dragon Boat, I'll learn to weave orchids for her sash, find a fine poem to write upon it.
— Su Dongpo, 'To Chao Yun'
03
Spiritual Practice

Shared Path of Practice

Chao Yun formally studied Buddhism under the nun Yichong; Su began sleeping alone for health cultivation — each on their own path, yet together pursuing spiritual elevation.

Together they built a life-release pond, practicing Buddhist compassion. Su wrote: 'Celibacy is difficult at first — try three months, then extend another three.' Chao Yun also explored Taoist longevity. Their practice merged Buddhist compassion, Taoist health cultivation, and Confucian rectitude of mind in daily life.

04
Eternal Memory

Chao Yun Hall

After Su Dongpo's death, White Crane Residence was renamed 'Chao Yun Hall' — the house passed down bearing her name, the most tender footnote to a love between white-haired poet and youthful companion.

Chao Yun was 26 years younger than Su. Not merely a concubine, she was his spiritual confidante in his twilight years. Qin Guan wrote she was 'beautiful as a spring garden, eyes like morning light.' Their story teaches: true love is not possession, but helping each other become better, walking together toward a higher realm.

KEY TAKEAWAY

Chao Yun's story teaches us: true love helps the other become a better person. In White Crane Residence, a 57-year-old poet and a 31-year-old woman, through sutra-reading, alchemy, and life-release ponds, elevated worldly love into shared spiritual seeking. Su called Chao Yun the 'Deva Vimalakirti' — not for her beauty, but for her wisdom transcending worldly distinctions. This may be love's highest form.
