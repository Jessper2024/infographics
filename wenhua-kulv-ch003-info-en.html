Mountains and rivers are the ultimate benchmark of all culture—the Yellow River, Yangtze, and Great Wall determined Chinese civilization’s genetic map

Core insight: The ultimate code of Chinese civilization lies not in books but in mountains and rivers. The Yellow River is the genetic foundation, the Yangtze the direction of migration, the Great Wall the civilizational boundary—three heaven-earth lines weave China’s survival framework. ‘Climate > Terrain > Human Harmony’ (reversing Mencius): climate has always been history’s prime mover at the millennial scale.

QuestionWhat determined the survival patterns and spiritual framework of Chinese culture?

1
Cognitive Shift

How Did the Tangshan Earthquake End the Cultural Revolution?

Yu Qiuyu’s core insight: what truly ended China’s Cultural Revolution was not politics but the Tangshan earthquake—a natural catastrophe jolted a nation from ideological madness back to the ‘survival baseline’ of being human.

‘The sudden extinction of hundreds of thousands of lives stunned a China mired in far-left obsession.’ (Yu Qiuyu) After the quake, most Chinese stopped caring about political rhetoric and returned to fundamental survival questions. Yu was studying Chinese classics in the mountains when the earthquake struck; it led him to ancient myths like ‘mending the sky’ and ‘filling the sea’—his first encounter with Chinese culture’s survival baseline. From this moment, he moved from textual culture to ecological culture.

2
Geographic Frame

A Sealed Land: China’s ‘Isolation Mechanism’

China is a land sealed on all sides—the impassable Pacific to the east, unnavigable deserts to the northwest, and the world’s highest plateau to the southwest. This ‘isolation mechanism’ fundamentally shaped Chinese civilization’s survival patterns.

In this enclosed environment, people ‘settled in one corner, lived by the water, men farmed and women wove.’ This agrarian ecology crystallized into a cultural psychology favoring stability, unity, conservatism, and centralization. Even with Zheng He’s maritime technology, China never pursued overseas expansion. The author argues: Earth holds no ‘universal culture’—China’s land-bound path was ecologically rational.

3
Civilizational DNA

Three Heaven-Earth Lines: Yellow River · Yangtze · Great Wall

Chinese culture has three fundamental axes—the Yellow River (cradle of agriculture), the Yangtze (direction of cultural migration), and the 400mm rainfall line / Great Wall (boundary between farming and nomadism). Understand these three lines and you understand China’s spatial DNA.

The Yellow River shaped Chinese culture’s character and intellectual heights. From the cold Western Jin period, the cultural center migrated south to the Yangtze, whose civilization asked more cosmic questions (Qu Yuan’s ‘Heavenly Questions’). Inside and outside the Great Wall: dry clashed with wet, cold with warm—yet conflict was also synthesis. The Xianbei Northern Wei preserved and elevated Han culture toward Tang Dynasty splendor; Emperor Kangxi’s Rehe Palace allowed diverse ecologies to coexist peacefully. Yu Qiuyu notes: born in the Yangtze basin, culturally rooted in the Yellow River, yet his ancestral home lies beyond the Wall in Wuwei, Gansu—all three lines converge in his biography.

4
Climate Determinism

‘Human Harmony < Terrain < Climate’

Mencius said ‘climate is less important than terrain, terrain less than human harmony.’ Yu Qiuyu reverses this hierarchy entirely: at the macro scale, climate is always history’s prime mover. The Yellow Emperor defeated Chiyou, Zhuge Liang ‘borrowed the east wind,’ the Mongols failed to invade Japan—in every case, climate was the decisive variable.

China’s 5,000-year temperature curve: warm (Shang) → cold (Western Jin, triggering southward migration) → warm (Sui-Tang, golden age) → cold (Southern Song) → cold (Ming-Qing). ‘Ecological factors are always history’s first finger.’ (Yu Qiuyu) Even individual battle outcomes depended less on troop numbers or strategy than on climate. The 400mm rainfall line, overlapping with the Great Wall, distinguished agriculture from nomadism—heaven and earth drew this civilizational boundary through the First Emperor’s hand.

5
Walking Philosophy

The Road Is the Book: From Textual to Ecological Culture

When asked about the relationship between ‘reading ten thousand books’ and ‘traveling ten thousand miles,’ Yu Qiuyu’s answer—‘There is no relationship. The road IS the book.’ Brushing one’s brief life against the rugged corners of this planet is the only pursuit worth undertaking.

Yu Qiuyu resigned all positions and wandered alone through the wilderness for over twenty years. He traced the Yellow River from source to sea, traversed the Yangtze basin, explored both sides of the Great Wall, and studied fourteen countries along the Silk Road. He describes his outlook as ‘grand and beautiful pessimism,’ but insists: ‘Only by walking, by setting everything in motion, can we find real details through mountains and rivers, truly feeling that we have lived once between heaven and earth—and that might not be so bad.’

‘Real footsteps through mountains and rivers turn all great enterprises into ordinary scenery… Humanity should put everything down, put it down among the mountains and rivers.’
— Yu Qiuyu, quoting Tao Yuanming: ‘Entrust the body to the mountain ridge.’
💡
Key Takeaway

Mountains and rivers are the ultimate benchmark of all culture. China’s three heaven-earth lines (Yellow River, Yangtze, Great Wall) determined its civilizational DNA, and climate has wielded more power over millennia than any dynastic change. Through twenty years of walking, Yu Qiuyu demonstrates that returning from text to ecology—understanding China within the framework of ‘Climate > Terrain > Human Harmony’—is the truest form of cultural self-awareness.

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Wenhua Kulv · Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
