The New Sell & Sell Short · Ch.7 "Shorting Stocks"

Stock markets rise over the long term, but declines are twice as fast as advances. Short sellers trade against the prevailing wind, seeking profit in faster moves while performing a public service by capping excesses and cushioning crashes.

Overview

How do you short safely and profitably in a market that rises over the long term?

Shorting stocks demands that traders first overcome the "long bias" and internalize a core asymmetry: tops are wide and prolonged (driven by greed), while bottoms are narrow and brief (driven by fear). Elder offers two distinct approaches—shorting at tops (higher difficulty, higher reward) and shorting in downtrends (safer, more systematic)—and emphasizes dual confirmation from fundamentals and technicals, small initial position sizing, and short interest metrics as essential risk-management filters.

Core thesis: Shorting is not fighting the market—it is capturing counter-trend profits within the market's own rhythm: enter and exit quickly, control risk rigorously, confirm every signal twice.

Mindset

Who Are Short Sellers and What Does the Market Need from Them?

Most traders are naturally biased toward going long—they believe profit only comes from rising prices. But Elder argues that trading is simply about betting on the direction of price movement, regardless of which direction. Short sellers not only profit for themselves but perform three public services: increasing supply at highs, buying first during crashes, and ultimately dampening excessive market volatility. Adam Smith's insight applies perfectly: in free markets, people help others by pursuing their own self-interest.

Market Structure

Why Are Tops Harder to Identify Than Bottoms?

Market bottoms are driven by fear—longs who can no longer bear the pain sell at any price, making bottoms narrow and brief. Market tops are driven by greed—the pleasure of profit persists, and bulls keep pouring in money, making tops wide, prolonged, and riddled with false breakouts. A sharp FI spike down signals exhaustion (bottom), while a sharp FI spike up often just marks trend continuation, not a top.

Strategy

Shorting at Tops vs. Shorting in Downtrends

Elder divides short-selling into two modes. Shorting at tops is like "running with the bulls"—fighting the crowd at market peaks; it requires smaller positions, wider stops, and readiness to re-enter. Shorting in established downtrends is safer: enter near value (between the two EMAs) and cover in oversold territory (below the lower channel). The Channel Rating Method targets capturing ≥30% of the channel width for an A-grade trade.

How to Find Short Candidates Using Fundamentals + Technicals?

Fundamentals act as the "signal generator"; technicals are the "aiming scope"—both must confirm; conflicting signals mean no trade. Two paths to find shorts: community picks (e.g., Spike group) and independent scanning (using TC2007 to scan industries → subsectors → constituents, sorted by price high-to-low, looking for "high and wobbly" stocks). Risk Control

What Risk Metrics Must Short Sellers Monitor?

Your first short must be small—position size amplifies emotions. Two essential filters: the Short Interest Ratio (shares short ÷ float; <10% is tolerable, >20% is dangerous) and Days-to-Cover (total short interest ÷ average daily volume; <10 days is safe, >20 days signals stampede risk). Kerry Lovvorn advises: use these as auxiliary tools for entry/exit, not as a standalone trading signal.

Key Takeaway

Successful short-selling hinges on acknowledging and exploiting market asymmetry—declines are twice as fast as advances (that's the edge), while the long-term uptrend is the headwind. Traders should choose the method that fits them (top-shorting or trend-shorting), insist on small initial positions, confirm every entry with dual signals, rigorously monitor short interest metrics, and keep a visual trading journal after every trade to keep evolving. Shorting is not fighting the market—it is finding the profit window that opens when the crowd heads in the opposite direction.
