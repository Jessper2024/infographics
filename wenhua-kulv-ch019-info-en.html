Cultural Bitter Journey · Ch.19 "The Distant Sea"

CORE PROPOSITION

Chinese culture has long watched the sea with fear and prohibition—but for countless living Chinese, this has never been true. They enter, embrace, and depend on the sea. The author experienced life at its limit on a boat above the Mariana Trench, and encountered various Chinese 'home wherever they roam' across Micronesian islands—from indigenous canoe navigators to a Chinese couple sailing for 25 years. The sea reveals a long-buried dimension of Chinese culture: freedom, challenge, symbiosis with nature.

01
Experience

Solo Boat on Raging Seas: Life as Living Sculpture

Above the Pacific's deepest Mariana Trench, when the largest waves strike, you cannot even flinch—you must become eternal rock sculpture.

The seawater was dark brown with a purple tint; the waves felt semi-solid rather than liquid. The boat oscillated between extreme heights and depths in absolute, suffocating silence. Humanity's artistic dream of being 'above nature' must be abandoned here—all adjectives and lyricism become pathetically laughable.

“
Even if cracked, even if broken—still eternal rock sculpture. Facing fury from all directions, any other posture, attitude, or strategy is utterly useless.
—— Yu Qiuyu

The author pressed down on the stern with his entire body—his whole form turned black, as if 'the dark-iron and black-jade energy from life's depths had been fully mobilized.' Ancient warriors were said to emit black energy when facing powerful foes.

02
Origins

From Hemudu to Canoes: The Buried Maritime Gene

Since Hemudu and Liangzhu—or even earlier—countless canoes departing from China have been obsessed with the sea.

The indigenous boatman said elders believed their ancestors also came from China. The author, researching the ultimate fate of Hemudu and Liangzhu peoples, was open to the possibility of distant sea voyages driven by catastrophic flooding. Even today, rare islanders can navigate by stars alone, paddling canoes from Micronesia to Hawaii.

🔑
Buried History: Rigid Chinese characters have no affinity with the sea. Where did the great navigator Zheng He die? No clear record exists. Half of China's history sank beneath the waves. Lazy armchair scholars only search dusty books—books that never contained the real ocean or the Chinese lives entwined with it.

'One person, paddling a canoe, can reach Hawaii?' 'Yes.' 'Then could they reach China?' 'Yes.'—The boatman's answers were calm and certain.

03
Characters

Home Wherever They Roam: Another Form of Chinese Home

The philosophical meaning of 'home' is the breakthrough of its ordinary meaning—the farther away, the more essential.

The author's wife fell ill on the island, with no doctor or medicine. Yang Gang—a former Beijing diplomat who quit his career, entranced by the sea—appeared with rice porridge with greens and Chinese herbal pills. 'Rice porridge with greens, plus huo xiang zheng qi wan—one taste brings you back. This is what it means to be Chinese.'

04
Legend

Twenty-Five Years at Sea: A Couple's Maritime Life

One couple continuously lived on a sailing boat for 25 years—anthropology, ethics, literature, and aesthetics have all been quietly rewritten by such a couple.

The husband, Luc, was Belgian; the wife, Jackie, was Chinese-American. Each voyage typically lasted under two weeks, with the two taking night watches. Any woman would find such a life unbearable, but this Chinese woman endured. Her face looked older than her years—the solemn imprint that ferocious space and time left on a Chinese woman.

“
Insignificant humans—one man and one woman—walking a path of tenacity, a water path, a sea path, a forever unknowable path, yet also an astonishing path of life, a path of faithful love, a path of human self-affirmation.
—— Yu Qiuyu

Diving couples around the world depend on each other for life and death. They generally have no children, no house. Their minds hold only distant dive sites they must visit.

05
Revelation

The Gap Between Culture and Life: Finally, Someone Disturbs the Sea and Sky

Chinese culture may have long feared and forbidden the sea, but countless living Chinese have always entered and embraced it—culture and life are, after all, very different.

From Hemudu canoes to Zheng He's treasure ships, from distant ancestors on Pacific islands to today's sailing couples—that long-buried life within Chinese culture has finally been awakened in an era when people can step beyond texts and beyond the small family.

✅ The Sea of Life

Entered, embraced, depended upon—from Hemudu canoes to today's diving couples. Finally, some Chinese have disturbed the sea and sky.

⚠️ The Sea of Culture

Watched, suspected, feared, forbidden—rigid characters with no affinity for the sea. Half of China's history sank beneath the waves.

The author finally said: 'It must be a sea-going boat.' His wife smiled: 'Of course.' Behind this simple exchange lies a solemn affirmation of the maritime dimension missing from Chinese culture.

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

'The Distant Sea' is the most embodied chapter in Cultural Bitter Journey—the author no longer observes as a scholar but confronts the raging sea with flesh and blood. This embodiment reveals the deeper meaning of the 'cultural bitter journey': Chinese culture needs not only to reflect in the study, but to rediscover its vital dimension at sea, at the limit. From Hemudu canoes to today's Chinese who disturb the sea and sky, the maritime gene suppressed by a sea-fearing culture has always flowed in the depths.
