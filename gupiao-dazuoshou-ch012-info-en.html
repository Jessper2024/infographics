Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Chapter 12

From escaping a corn corner by feinting with oats, to unloading July cotton using a newspaper article—Livermore demonstrates the core quality of a top trader: adaptability in desperate situations, turning every crisis into opportunity.

Overview

When Conventional Methods Fail, How Does a Trader Survive?

Trading isn’t brute force—it’s intelligence. Stratton cornered corn; Livermore’s 10-million-bushel short faced massive losses. He didn’t buy back directly—that would be cutting his own throat. Instead, he shorted 200,000 bushels of oats to trick traders into selling corn, bought back 6 million bushels in ten minutes, with net cost just 1.5 cents away. In July cotton, stuck with 140,000 bales he couldn’t unload, a newspaper article accidentally created the perfect exit window—he seized it and was completely out within ten minutes of the open.

Core thesis: A professional trader, like a billiards player, thinks several shots ahead, turning ‘position-first thinking’ into professional instinct. Feint east, strike west; adapt to every change.

Narrow Escape

Corn Corner: 200K Bushels of Oats to Unlock 10M Bushels of Corn

A $3,000 decoy that defused a massive crisis. Stratton had cornered corn; Livermore was short 10 million bushels. Buying back directly would drive prices even higher against him. He thought of a feint: had four brokerage houses simultaneously sell 50,000 bushels of oats each at market. Oats fell sharply. Traders assumed the Armour interests were attacking Stratton—corn would be next—and rushed to sell corn. Livermore bought back 6 million bushels in ten minutes.

Leveraging Events

The Cammack Story: Why Buy on Bearish Insider News?

Bear master Cammack was bearish on the whole market. A tipster told him: William Rockefeller is heavily selling St. Paul. Cammack didn’t sell—he ordered 15,000 shares bought. His logic: Rockefeller’s selling was pressuring the market, which would provide ammunition for his massive short campaign. Once Rockefeller stopped, Cammack had accumulated 60,000 shares of St. Paul long—then used that position as leverage to short the entire market savagely. St. Paul fell from the 90s to 44; Cammack cleaned up.

Perfect Exit

July Cotton: How a Newspaper Article Became the Perfect Cover

An unexpected article solved the unsolvable exit problem. Livermore had bought 140,000 bales of July cotton—virtually all the floating contracts. He knew unloading such a position without collapsing the price was nearly impossible. Then the New York World ran a front-page story: “July Cotton Cornered by Larry Livingston.” The market went wild. Ten minutes after the open next day, Livermore was out—every single bale, mostly at the day’s high.

Key Takeaway

Chapter 12’s core lesson is ‘adaptability.’ In the corn corner, Livermore resolved the crisis with a feint—not by head-on collision, but by exploiting others’ psychological expectations to create liquidity for himself. In July cotton, he demonstrated the ability to seize an unexpected opportunity—not creating it, but recognizing and exploiting it. The Cammack story further shows: professional traders think about the entire chessboard, not isolated moves. The common revelation: in speculation, flexibility matters more than correctness—only by adapting to every change can one find a way out of desperate straits.
