明朝那些事儿 · 朱元璋 · Chapter 24「Endpoint, Starting Point」

An aging Zhu Yuanzhang bid farewell to his last old friend—and to his own era. The Ming Empire thrived, but its founding generals were gone. He chose Zhu Di as his military heir—never imagining this choice would rewrite history.

In his later years, the Ming Empire flourished—the wounds of war had healed, the economy recovered, and the treasury filled. After sweeping away nearly all the founding generals, Zhu Yuanzhang felt at peace. But new problems emerged: Mongol cavalry still raided the borders, and no experienced generals remained to lead armies. Zhu turned to his fourth son, Zhu Di—a man of extraordinary military talent. He would never know that this choice would produce the greatest crisis of his dynasty: the Jingnan Campaign that would overthrow everything he had built.

Final Purges
Saying Goodbye to Old Friends
In 1392, Zhu Yuanzhang killed Zhou Dexing, the fortune-teller who had predicted his fate forty years earlier. In 1394, he executed Duke of Ying Fu Youde and Marquis of Dingyuan Wang Bi. In 1395, Duke of Song Feng Sheng—the last surviving of the six founding dukes—finally fell to the blade. Tang He was the wisest: he had long since relinquished his military command. But as Zhu's closest companion from the beginning, he posed the greatest threat simply by existing. When Zhu visited Tang He, he found the once-valiant general slumped in a chair, drooling, struggling to perform the kowtow.

The Lonely Emperor
Looking at Tang He in the chair, Zhu Yuanzhang realized: all his old companions were gone. Some had died for him, others he had killed. In Haozhou City forty years ago, a squad leader had followed a humble thousand-man commander. Now, the survivors were one man standing and one man lying."Tang He, stay alive. Those who rode into battle with me, those who shared life and death—now only you and I remain. Walk with me to the end. I am very lonely." This was Zhu's final farewell to an old friend—and the deepest meaning of "the solitary sovereign."

Empire's Risks
Who Will Fight the Mongols?
Burning the good bow and killing the loyal dog secured the throne—but created new problems. Mongol cavalry still raided the northern border, and all the experienced generals were dead. Young officers were unreliable, knowing only books. Li Jinglong, though young, was worth nothing beyond being Li Wenzhong's son. This seemed unsolvable—until Zhu Yuanzhang, as always, found a solution. He identified a man of extraordinary military talent whose loyalty could be absolutely trusted. That man was Zhu Di—the same Zhu Di who would later rewrite the history of the Ming Dynasty.

The Seed
Zhu Di's Birth and Fate
In April 1360, as Chen Youliang prepared to attack Yingtian, a low-status concubine gave Zhu Yuanzhang a son—Zhu Di. "Born in war, destined to die on campaign" seemed his fate. His mother's low status and his non-firstborn position meant Zhu Di never received the privileges of his older brother Zhu Biao. While Zhu Biao studied under the finest scholars, Zhu Di's father was just another rebel leader too busy surviving to notice this unremarkable son. "Why must I be born into an imperial family?" the young Zhu Di must have wondered.

End & Beginning
The End of Zhu Yuanzhang's Era
Through over two decades of bloodletting, Zhu Yuanzhang had eliminated all threats to his descendants. He believed that if his descendants strictly followed the institutions he created, the Ming Dynasty would last forever. He was right—but not in the way he imagined. Ironically, despite all the unforeseen events that followed (the Jingnan Campaign, the Tumu Crisis, eunuch domination, partisan strife), the Ming Dynasty survived 276 years—longer than Zhu Yuanzhang could have dreamed. His era had ended, but the Ming Dynasty was just beginning.

KEY TAKEAWAY
Zhu Yuanzhang spent a lifetime building the Ming Dynasty, and the second half of his life eliminating everyone who might threaten it. He chose Zhu Di as his military heir—only to unknowingly cultivate the empire's greatest enemy. By destroying one generation of loyalists, he made the Jingnan Campaign inevitable. The endpoint of Zhu Yuanzhang's story was also the starting point of a new chapter in Chinese history.
