Wenhua Kulv · Ch. 15 "Apologies to Shanxi"

In the 19th century and for a long time before, China's richest province was not where you'd imagine — it was Shanxi. A generation of "Going West" brothers created the empire's greatest commercial miracle.

While browsing historical records, Yu Qiuyu was stunned — 19th-century China's richest province was Shanxi. Well into the early 20th century, Shanxi remained China's financial hub. The "Rishengchang" exchange bank on Pingyao's West Street was the "country grandfather" of all Chinese banks. The Qiao Family Compound in Qi County astonished even Soong Ai-ling. Shanxi's "Going West" farmers, armed with commercial openness, far-sighted vision, unwavering integrity, and disciplined management, created a miracle of wealth. Yet internal conflicts (the destructive rivalry between Lei Lütai and Mao Honghui) and historical upheaval brought their ruin. Most painfully, China's vast scholarly canon left virtually no record of this monumental commercial achievement.

01
Discovery
China's Richest Province Wasn't Jiangsu-Zhejiang — It Was Shanxi
Over a decade ago, Yu Qiuyu was startled while browsing historical records — in the 19th century and long before, China's richest province was Shanxi. Even into the early 20th century, Shanxi remained China's financial and trade center. The headquarters of the most respectable financial institutions in Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Wuhan were mostly located on ordinary streets in Pingyao and Taigu County. These great cities were merely docks where wealthy Shanxi merchants tested their skills. In 1820, the scholar Gong Zizhen proposed mass westward migration, singling out only two exceptions — one being Shanxi, "reputed as the richest under heaven; its natives have no wish to move — no discussion needed."
02
Going West
Farmers Who Refused Poverty Built "The Richest Under Heaven"
The Pingyao-Qi County-Taigu region had poor natural conditions — "Pingyao's land is thin and barren, its people more farmers than anything else"; Taigu "has many people and little land; even a bumper harvest cannot supply two months." Facing poverty, Shanxi farmers made a choice unlike any in conventional historical narratives: they refused suffering but had no desire for political power; they felt crowded but wouldn't betray their neighbors; they rejected unearned wealth yet wouldn't pour a lifetime's sweat into a small patch of soil. So began the great "Going West" migration — "Elder brother, you're going west / Little sister can't bear to see you leave…" From the early Qing, the caravans grew. The poor young farmer Qiao Guifa from Qiaojiabao Village founded the Qiao family fortune; his descendants' "Fushenggong" firm laid the commercial foundation for the entire city of Baotou — "First came Fushenggong, then came Baotou City."

03
Character
Four Pillars of Shanxi Merchant Success
The all-around success of Shanxi merchants was tied to their exceptional character. Even the Yongzheng Emperor noted Shanxi's unique social order: first, commerce; second, farming; third, military; fourth, scholarship. In this environment, Shanxi merchants were at ease with trade — they made the merchant class pure. They had far-sighted vision — as perpetual travelers, the entire map of China lay within their horizon, and they eventually positioned themselves as masters of the nation's private wealth circulation through the exchange bank system. They valued trust and loyalty — operating in networked groups with interlocking partnerships, they would write off giant debts with a wave of the hand. They enforced rigorous management — in an era without government regulation, they created detailed internal rules with clear hierarchies and profit-sharing that would impress even modern management theorists.

04
Fracture
Lei Lütai vs. Mao Honghui: Two Geniuses Destroying Each Other
Lei Lütai, general manager of Rishengchang, and Mao Honghui, his deputy — two of the finest commercial minds in the land, co-founders of a new era in Chinese finance. Lei was magnanimous and extraordinarily capable; Mao was brilliant and fiercely ambitious. But after their success came the quintessential Chinese dilemma: who was the real architect? A power struggle sparked by Lei's sick leave escalated into a war: Lei forced the owner to kneel by threatening to recall all branch offices; Mao stormed off to a rival firm. What followed was a spiral of mutual sabotage — poaching talent, blocking each other's business, exposing lowly origins. Most heartbreaking: both men ultimately named their grandsons after each other as an insult — Lei's grandson became Lei Honghui, Mao's grandson became Mao Lütai. When commercial competition leaves the realm of business and destroys opponents on a personal level, it strays far from healthy practice.

05
Silence & Decline
Scholarly Silence and the Tide of History: Who Remembers the "Going West" Generation?
For all their decades of struggle, the Shanxi merchants never found a thinker to speak for them. Nearly all cultural scholars avoided them. Their actions lacked high-level rational support; their achievements were never given eloquent historical justification — "For a vast culture to so lightly dismiss a vast wealth connected to itself, and its vast community of creators, is truly incomprehensible." Add the extreme turmoil of modern China (the Taiping Rebellion, the Boxer era, the bank runs of the 1911 Revolution) and the arrival of British, American, Russian, and Japanese banks alongside the Qing government's own Imperial Bank, and the Shanxi exchange banks reached their end. In 1915, the Dagong Bao newspaper described the shuttered firms: "Those towering, splendid mansions — all now locked behind iron doors, dim and colorless. The stone lions at the gates, eyes glaring, seem to weep, as if wanting to roar on behalf of their masters."

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Shanxi merchants created China's greatest commercial wealth, yet China's vast scholarly literature left almost no record of them — a culture that so thoroughly dismissed a wealth and community connected to itself. Their internal failure was a lack of intellectual champions and high-level rational support; the external cause was historical upheaval and the onslaught of Western modern banking. Standing a little longer on Shanxi soil is an apology owed to this forgotten history.
