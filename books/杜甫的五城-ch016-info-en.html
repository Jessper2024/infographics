Five Cities of Du Fu · Ch.16 "The Wuliang Shrine"

Wuhan · Zhengzhou · Kaifeng · Jiaxiang

In spring 1992, Lai Ruihe realizes he has traveled ninety percent of China’s railways. Studying the map, he plots a route from Wuhan to Jiaxiang—not for the big cities, but for a Han Dynasty shrine buried under Yellow River silt for centuries in a remote Shandong village: the Wuliang Shrine. Passing through Wuhan for hot dry noodles and Wuchang fish, Zhengzhou for a five-thousand-year-old painted pottery vessel, and Kaifeng for night-market snacks and Song Dynasty nostalgia, he arrives at Jiaxiang—a tiny county town with only one main street. A young driver takes him to the shrine after hours, finding Professor Zhu Xilu, who has guarded these stones for thirty years—‘gaunt and kindly, chain-smoking.’ On the carved stones: the assassin Jing Ke’s dagger has pierced right through the pillar. On the way back, four men squat by the roadside to drink watermelon—the Shandong dialect’s unique verb for eating juicy fruit. This shrine has drawn scholars from Qing epigraphers to Rong Geng, Édouard Chavannes, Wilma Fairbank, and Wu Hung.

01Wuhan & Zhengzhou: Yangtze Fish, Yellow River Regret
Flying from Guangzhou to Wuhan, tasting Cailinji hot dry noodles, Sijimei soup dumplings, and Wuchang fish—‘I drank the Yangtze’s waters.’ An overnight train to Zhengzhou—‘the busiest train station in all of Asia.’ Visiting Dahe Village site, a five-thousand-year-old painted pottery double-linked pot—‘primitive yet romantic.’ At the Zhengzhou Yellow River, watching trains crawl across the bridge—‘an inexplicable stirring in my heart.’ But missing the Yellow River carp: the greedy wish to taste Yangtze fish then Yellow River carp went unfulfilled.
02Kaifeng: Night Markets & Song Dynasty Memories
In Kaifeng, missing the famous Tube Chicken but indulging at the night market—Ma’s spiced beef, various bowl-steamed dishes, made-to-order stir-fries. Walking the reconstructed Song Imperial Street: ‘I couldn’t help feeling this was another kind of postmodern irony.’ Remembering studying Song history under Professor Liu Zijian at Princeton—‘listening to his lectures was pure enjoyment.’ Only upon entering the Ming-Qing buildings on Bookshop Street did Kaifeng finally feel somewhat ancient.
03Jiaxiang & Wuliang Shrine: A Márquez-Style Quest
Jiaxiang has only one main street. A young driver solicits: ‘Why wait until tomorrow? We can go right now.’ He picks up Communist Youth League friends along the way—like a countryside outing. The road to the shrine was built in twenty-four hours by the entire village for a vice premier’s visit—reminding Lai of Márquez’s One Hundred Years of Solitude. Iron gates locked; the driver seeks Professor Zhu to open them: ‘Just like dropping by a friend’s house for a chat.’
04Zhu Xilu and Han Stone Carvings: The Pierced Pillar
Professor Zhu Xilu has guarded Wuliang Shrine for thirty years—‘gaunt and kindly, he chain-smoked, nearly one after another.’ He explains the Jing Ke assassination carving in detail: ‘The dagger has actually pierced right through the pillar’ ‘The King of Qin’s sleeve has been cut off, suspended in mid-air.’ Lai buys Zhu’s book on the Wuliang Shrine carvings and asks for his signature—‘I felt boundless respect for this senior scholar.’
05Drinking Watermelon: Shandong Dialect’s Local Flavor
On the way back, spotting a watermelon vendor, the young driver stops and declares generously: ‘Come, come, I’ll treat you to drink watermelon.’ ‘And so, the four of us in the car squatted by the roadside and drank two large watermelons. Summer watermelon, sweet and juicy—described with the Shandong dialect’s unique verb drink, it had an especially distinctive flavor.’ Nearby, several stone-carving workshops—‘Stone carvers have been working here for probably several thousand years.’
💡 Key Insight
A Tang institutional historian detours a thousand li to the remotest village in Shandong for a Han Dynasty shrine. The Wuliang Shrine lies far from any tourist route, yet for three centuries it has drawn the world’s finest scholars—from Qing epigraphers to Wilma Fairbank, from Rong Geng to Wu Hung. Professor Zhu Xilu’s thirty-year solitary vigil among stone images is more moving than any carving. The most valuable cultural sites often hide in the most unassuming places; the most worthwhile journeys often begin with the purest intellectual curiosity.
