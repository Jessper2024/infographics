Dongpo's Forest of Jottings · Chapter 6「Tales of the Netherworld and Human Legends」

SCQA · 情境 → 冲突 → 问题 → 回答 If Part 1 of Strange Events concerns immortals and Daoist arts, Part 2 is more about earthly wonders and inexplicable phenomena — an abandoned infant surviving by inhaling a toad's breath during a famine, a clerk returning from the dead after being mistakenly seized, a sow transforming into a spring with two carps still swimming in it, hundreds of fish inexplicably leaping from a deep pond — how does an exiled scholar make sense of these events that defy rational explanation? Su Shi neither blindly believed nor hastily dismissed; instead, as an observer and recorder, he treated these wonders as windows into a world with more possibilities than we assume.

This chapter collects seventeen essays on strange events (Vol. 3 of Dongpo's Forest of Jottings, "Strange Events, Part 2"): Zhu Yan Studies Chan, the Late Abbot Chongbian, Abandoned Infant Inhaling a Toad's Breath, Shi Pu Sees a Slave's Ghost, Chen Yu Mistakenly Seized by Underworld Clerks, Record of the Strange, the Sow-Buddha, Wang Yi's Dream Deer and Realgar, Xu Ze Refuses to Teach Prince Guang, Lady Cheng Refuses to Unearth Treasure, Mount Taibai's Ducal Title, Fan Zhen's Last Days, Zhang Hanzi the Madman, Female Immortal, Fish Leaping from the Pond, Sun Meets a Strange Man, and the Self-Cultivation Calendar. From Chan koans to famine legends, from karmic retribution to bizarre creatures — Su Shi captured the world's hidden rhythms with inexhaustible curiosity in his remote exiles.

01
Life & Death's Mysteries
Zhu Yan's Question & Chen Yu's Return——Philosophical Inquiry at Life's Edge
Military judge Zhu Yan studied Chan Buddhism and suddenly grasped something from the Surangama Sutra. He asked the monk Yijiang: "After this body dies, where does this mind dwell?" Yijiang, without answering, countered: "While this body is not yet dead, where does this mind dwell?" After a long pause, Zhu Yan replied in verse: "When the four elements are unbalanced, the five aggregates incomplete — where now is mind? Where now is body?" — This was a serious Chan inquiry from a government official, solemnly recorded by Su Shi. Another life-and-death tale: Chen Yu, a clerk, died suddenly and revived after three days, narrating in detail being led into the underworld by his deceased sister, crossing the Naihe Bridge, witnessing an underworld judge's tribunal, and finally being released when the judge said "mistaken seizure." Su Shi recorded this with a journalist's precision. And the underworld judge's advice in "Self-Cultivation Calendar" is a simple maxim: "Whatever you do by day, you must record by night — what you dare not record, you dare not do." No need to wait for posthumous judgment; nightly self-reflection is the best cultivation calendar.

02
Marvels of Nature
Abandoned Infant & Toad's Breath·Sow Spring·Leaping Fish——A Naturalist's Animate World
During Fu Bi's famine relief in Qingzhou, a starving couple abandoned their infant in an empty roadside tomb. When they returned a year later intending to collect the bones — "the child was still alive, plumper and healthier than before." Inside the tomb was a giant toad, "like a cartwheel, breathing heavily" — the infant, from birth, had learned to inhale the toad's breath to survive. "The earth's qi suffices to nurture all living things." Su Shi attributed this to terrestrial vitality — a proto-scientific naturalist explanation. In Qingshen County of Meizhou stood the "Sow-Buddha" shrine — a century ago, a sow had prostrated on the ground and transformed into a spring, with two carps swimming within. The Sichuanese built a Buddha hall above it — "this is a pig-dragon." And Ren Da of Meizhou told of something even more spectacular: hundreds of fish raised for thirty-plus years in a deep pond — one clear, thunderless day, the pond erupted with a roar like wind and rain, and all the fish leapt upward in spiraling columns. Su Shi did not force an explanation, simply recorded faithfully — this direct observation of wondrous natural phenomena made him one of the most naturalist-minded literati of the Song dynasty.

03
Karma & Retribution
Shi Pu's Murderous Haunting·Wang Yi's Dream Deer——Folk Narratives of Good and Evil
Shi Pu "loved killing, took killing as entertainment." Once, drunk, he bound a slave and ordered him thrown into the Bian River; the subordinate, unable to bear it, released the slave secretly. When Shi Pu sobered and regretted it, the subordinate, fearing his violence, dared not confess. Later, gravely ill, Shi Pu "saw the slave's ghost haunting him" — believing this a death omen, he was paradoxically saved: from then on he renounced killing and reformed. Su Shi recounts this without visible emotion, but the four characters "took killing as entertainment" already contain his condemnation. Wang Yi of Qiting in Huangzhou was the opposite — "wealthy and devoted to goodness," he dreamed of saving a severely beaten man by the waterside. The next day, happening by the water, he saw a deer captured by a hunter, already hit by several arrows. Wang Yi "was enlightened," and ransomed the deer for thousands of coins. The deer entered the water and submerged; Wang Yi pursued and found an enormous peach — "cutting it open, he obtained a piece of realgar, like a peach pit." Simple faith in karmic reward found vivid expression through the folk narrative motif of animal gratitude. Su Shi also recorded the late Abbot Chongbian: Master Qisong was ever angry and none had seen him smile; Master Haiyue Huibian was ever joyful and none had seen him angry — both passed away seated in meditation — extreme personality differences both resolved into the same serenity at life's end.

04
Gods & Mortal Order
Taibai Mountain's Title & Lady Cheng's Hidden Treasure——When Gods and Human Order Intersect
When Su Shi served as assistant in Fufeng, a great drought struck. He asked elders where to pray — all pointed to Mount Taibai. But recent prayers had gone unanswered; Su Shi investigated and concluded: the mountain god had previously held the title of "Duke," and when the prefect petitioned to demote the god to "Marquis Who Saves the People," the deity was displeased — so Su Shi petitioned to restore the ducal title. Behind this seemingly superstitious act lay deep insight into folk religious psychology. Another story came from Su Shi's mother, Lady Cheng. Renting a house in Meishan, two maids were ironing silk when their feet sank into the ground. Looking closer, several feet down, they found a large urn covered with dark wooden planks — clearly treasure hidden by previous occupants. From the urn came sounds like a human coughing; Lady Cheng urgently ordered it reburied with earth. Su Shi used this to illustrate his mother's virtue: no greed for unexpected wealth, reverence for the unknown. Zhang Hanzi presents yet another archetype — a holy fool. His behavior was that of a madman, cursing passersby as "fire-setting thieves," wearing the same hemp garment through thirty summers and winters, refusing money when offered. Su Shi neither judges nor explains — he simply records. This mysterious eccentric may have been another kind of cultivator.

05
Tales of Practitioners
Xu Ze Refuses Prince Guang·Record of the Strange·Sun Meets a Strange Man——Resolve and Character of Cultivators
Xu Ze of Donghai lived as a recluse on Mount Tiantai, abstaining from grain to cultivate his nature. The Perfected Lord Xu descended to tell him: "When you pass eighty, you shall be teacher to a king, and then attain the Dao." Prince Yang Guang of Jin, hearing his name, summoned him. Xu Ze told his disciples: "I am eighty, and now he summons me — Lord Xu's words are true." He went to Yangzhou — but died soon after. Su Shi poses the question without offering an answer. Perhaps true "attainment of the Dao" was death itself? "Record of the Strange" depicts a dramatic scene: a Daoist was lecturing at Maoshan with hundreds in the audience, when a huge, dark figure burst in, cursing: "The weather is hot — why gather crowds to fabricate demons?" The Daoist rose and apologized, citing insufficient mountain resources — then the intruder suddenly vanished. "Suspected to be a divine being" — this anti-formulaic narrative subverts the usual "Daoist miracle" pattern. Sun Bian, traveling through Huayin in heavy snow, saw an old woman sitting beneath a milestone, hair white as snow yet showing no sign of cold, with no one on the road before dawn — "Was she a monster? Was she an immortal?" Su Shi's open-ended question leaves an eternal mystery.

KEY INSIGHT
In Part 2 of his strange event records, Su Shi brings immortal legends back to the human world — abandoned infants, those returning from death, a sow transformed into a spring, leaping fish, a mis-titled mountain god, a mother refusing to unearth hidden treasure — each strange event touches on human life and death, good and evil, greed and faith. What Su Shi records is not "superstition" but the many impossibilities that become possible in the human world: beneath the apparently absurd surface lies his reverence for life, his affirmation of goodness, and his wonder at nature's mysteries.
