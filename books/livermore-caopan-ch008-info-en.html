How to Trade in Stocks · Ch.8 "The Livermore Market Analysis Method"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Stock price patterns repeat themselves constantly, but minor daily fluctuations lead traders astray. In his early years, Livermore spent all day trying to catch minor intraday swings — a critical mistake. How can one systematically record price movements, incorporating time, to anticipate major trends? The answer is a market recording system of Six-Column Tables + Three-Color Ink + 6-Point Criterion + Composite Verification.

Livermore distilled decades of work into one market recording system: specially designed six-column sheets (Secondary Rally, Natural Rally, Upward Trend, Downward Trend, Natural Reaction, Secondary Reaction), recording upward trends in black ink, downward trends in red ink, and the remaining four columns in pencil for adjustment phases. A 6-point movement criterion determines column transitions, while composite prices from two stocks verify trend changes. The classic 1939–1940 steel stock case proved an eternal truth: market action itself is always the only reason you ever need to know.
01
Core Tool
The Six-Column Table: A Trading Map for Anticipating Future Movements
Livermore designed specially printed sheets with six columns per stock — this is what he called his "map for anticipating future market movements." The six columns correspond to six types of price movement, with prices classified and recorded according to specific rules, forming a three-dimensional panorama of the market.

02
Operational Rule
Three-Color Ink Coding: Making the Record Speak
Ink color is far from arbitrary — it is the language of the entire recording system. If you are always recording in pencil, you realize you are merely noting natural oscillations. When it switches from black to red (or vice versa), the trend has changed — this is the clear signal demanding immediate action.

03
Judgment Criterion
The 6-Point Movement Criterion & Composite Price Verification
By measuring movement amplitudes, Livermore established two ironclad rules from which he never deviated: 6 points per stock confirms a natural movement; 12 points combined from two stocks verifies a trend change. These figures do not belong to you — if the condition is not met, do not bend the rules.

04
Classic Case Study
The Silent Signal of Steel Stocks — Market Action Is the Only Reason
In 1939, all stocks made new highs — except steel stocks, which conspicuously failed to follow. The market record had already made it clear: the upward movement in steel was over. Four months later the truth emerged: the British government had sold over 100,000 shares, Canada another 20,000 — but it was already too late. The deepest lesson: you never need to know the reason before acting.

05
Trading Discipline
Eliminate Minor Fluctuations, Forge Action Courage Through Discipline
Livermore frankly admitted this method was not perfect, but after years of testing he had approached a state where he could rely on it. The key lies in sustained discipline — he never deviated from those point values, never made exceptions, never made excuses to bend the rules. Whether you succeed depends on the courage with which you act when the record issues its signal.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The essence of the Livermore Market Analysis Method: through a six-column table, three-color ink coding, the 6-point movement criterion, and two-stock composite price verification, construct an objective, quantified "trading map." It filters out all minor fluctuations, capturing only the beginning and end of major trends; it relies on no external reasons, obeying only the signals the market itself emits. Keep recording, strictly adhere, never make exceptions — market action itself is always the only reason you ever need to know.
