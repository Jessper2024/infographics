Ming Dynasty Those Years · Ch.1 "Childhood"

Written from the peaceful years before the 1344 catastrophe—a cattle-herding boy loses everything in the late Yuan chaos, and in the space between survival and despair, fate quietly plants the seed that will rewrite Chinese history.

Situation Born 1328 to peasant Zhu Wusi, named Zhu Chongba; spent childhood herding cattle; his greatest dream was simply to survive, marry, and raise children.
Conflict The 1344 disasters shattered everything. Father, elder brother, and mother all starved within sixteen days. Landlord Liu De refused to help. The seventeen-year-old was left utterly alone.
Question How does a boy who loses everything transform from a survival-seeker into an armed-to-the-heart warrior?
Answer Three years of wandering as a begging monk—studying geography, meeting heroes, and discovering that true power grows from loss.

The Name "Zhu Chongba": Yuan-Era Numbering of Commoners
Yuan law decreed: commoners without education or office had no name—only numbers derived from parents' ages or birth dates. Zhu Chongba (Zhu Bamba) continued this tradition across four generations: ancestor Zhu Bailiu, Zengzu Zhu Sijiu, Grandfather Zhu Chuyi, Father Zhu Wusi. It was not mathematics but law. Zhu Yuanzhang's later imperial name contrasts sharply with the number he was born with—a silent rebuke to Yuan hierarchy.

02
The Broken Hut: A Cattle Boy's Sixteen Years
Zhu Chongba's childhood unfolded in a broken hut—"cool in winter, warm in summer, well-ventilated"—as he herded cattle for landlord Liu De for twelve uninterrupted years. He once dreamed of studying, but education was unaffordable. At sixteen, his happiest vision was a simple life: marry a hardworking girl, name a son Zhu San'er, and have him herd for the next Liu De. This was a poor man's dream—and the common destiny of Yuan-era peasants.

03
Late Yuan Tyranny: Four Classes and Systematic Exploitation
Yuan China was divided into four racial classes. A Mongol killing a Southern Chinese paid only one donkey—often nothing at all. Taxes covered every imaginable pretext: festival levies, labor fees, litigation fees, even random "scatter-money" extortions. Corruption was institutional: disaster relief grain was siphoned at every level from the central government down to the county—by the time it reached peasants, only chaff remained. Officials thanked the emperor; the emperor praised himself. Everyone was satisfied—except the people.

04
Sixteen Dark Days: The Total Destruction of a Family
From April 6 to April 22, 1344—sixteen days: father Zhu Wusi starved; elder brother starved; elder brother's son starved; mother Chen starved. Seventeen-year-old Zhu Chongba watched each death, powerless. This was "the greatest pain a human can endure." He begged landlord Liu De for burial land—refused cleanly. He and his elder brother carried the bodies searching everywhere. At last a kind soul gave them a plot. "Soul drifting, searching for parents in vain"—these were the words of a future emperor who remembered.

05
Three Years Begging: From Cattle Boy to Armed Warrior
Fifty days after entering Huangjue Temple, famine forced all monks to go begging. Zhu Chongba was dispatched to the poorest regions—Huai West and Henan, where famine was worst. "Who would give him anything?" Yet this wandering three-year journey remade him completely: walking across Huai West, studying geography and folkways, meeting local heroes, embracing Maitreya faith. He transformed from a boy who could only cry in the haystack into a warrior armed to the heart. When he returned, he was unrecognizable.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Zhu Yuanzhang's childhood is a history built entirely from loss—losing four family members in sixteen days forced him to abandon the simple wish to survive, and turn against a world of injustice. Three years of begging as a monk forged not just knowledge and networks, but his core character: true power is not what you possess, but what you become after losing everything. This is the personality code behind the future emperor.
