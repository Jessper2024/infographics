Dongpo Zhilin · Ch.11「The Rise and Fall of Dynasties—From the Duke of Zhou to Sima Qian」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer One of the greatest questions in Chinese history: why do dynasties rise and fall? Was King Wu's conquest of the Shang a righteous revolution or regicide? Was King Ping's eastward relocation to Luoyang a wise retreat or a fatal strategic error? Was Qin Shihuang's abolition of feudalism visionary or self-destructive? For two millennia, orthodox narratives turned evaluations of these events into unquestioned "common sense"—but Su Shi refuses. He delivers one shocking judgment after another: "King Wu was not a sage." "Zhou's greatest strategic mistake was nothing like the eastward relocation." "Qin's skill was merely inherited from Zhi Bo." "Sima Qian bears two great crimes." When historical verdicts collide with logical deduction, how does Su Shi duel with the sharpest minds across a thousand years? Answer: he drops moral judgment and picks up strategic rationality—using cold causal reasoning, he dismantles one "historical truth" after another, stripped of emotion and fine rhetoric.

This chapter collects the first seven essays of Su Shi's "On Antiquity" section: King Wu Was Not a Sage, The Eastward Relocation Was a Mistake, Qin's Clumsy Conquest of Chu, Qin Abolishes Feudalism, On Wu Zixu, Wen Zhong, and Fan Li, On the Three Huan of Lu, and Sima Qian's Two Great Crimes. These form the core of Volume 5, using strategic rationality and causal historical reasoning to deliver subversive verdicts on pivotal events and figures of the Zhou, Qin, and Han dynasties.

01
Revolutionary Legitimacy
"King Wu Was Not a Sage"—Overturning Three Millennia of Revolutionary Narrative
In "King Wu Was Not a Sage," Su Shi launches his most radical challenge to the Confucian orthodoxy. He traces Confucius' hidden meaning: Confucius actually "condemned King Tang and King Wu," but "considering himself a descendant of Yin and a subject of Zhou, dared not speak openly." Evidence: Confucius praised Boyi and Shuqi for "refusing to eat Zhou grain" and starving on Mount Shouyang; Confucius said King Wu's music was "perfectly beautiful but not perfectly good"—subtly distinguishing aesthetic achievement from moral purity; Confucius said "with two-thirds of the world, to still serve Yin—this was the utmost virtue"—by this logic, King Wen serving Yin with two-thirds of the world was virtue; King Wu's conquest was betrayal. Su Shi goes further: it was Mencius who "disrupted this"—by saying "I have heard of King Wu executing a tyrant, not regicide." From then, regicide gained legitimacy. Su Shi imagines an ideal history: had King Wen lived, he would not have attacked Zhou. King Zhou might have died naturally or in chaos—Yin could have enthroned a new ruler to serve Zhou. "The way of lord and subject—would not both be preserved?" As for King Wu's remedy—killing Zhou then enfeoffing his son Wugeng—Su Shi reasons coldly: "King Wu personally beheaded Zhou with the yellow axe. Had Wugeng accepted the fief without rebellion, would he still be human?" Rebellion was inevitable. The enfranchisement was mere strategic pacification, not sage virtue.

02
Strategic Decisions
The Eastward Relocation—Selling the Ancestral Home as Strategic Suicide
Su Shi declares in an unforgettable metaphor: "Zhou's greatest strategic mistake was nothing like the eastward relocation." He compares it to a rich family's prodigal son: "What the wealthy leave their descendants is land and houses. When misfortune strikes, borrowing to survive is acceptable—but one must never sell the land. King Ping took the enterprise of Wen, Wu, Cheng, and Kang and cast it all away—this is selling the family estate at the first defeat." His historical survey is brutal: every state that "relocated to escape enemies" perished—"if not immediately, never revived." Wei King Hui fled Qin to Daliang—fell. Chu King Zhao fled Wu to Ruo—fell. Dong Zhuo abducted the emperor to Chang'an—Han fell. The sole counterexample was Eastern Jin's Wang Dao: after Su Jun's rebellion nearly destroyed Jin, everyone wanted to relocate; Wang Dao alone refused: "A king does not move his capital for wealth or poverty." Had King Ping had one Wang Dao—refusing relocation, rallying the remnants at Feng and Hao—"Qi and Jin, however strong, would not have dared disloyalty. How could Qin have risen?"

03
Unification Strategy
Qin's Clumsy Conquest of Chu—A Lucky Victory and a Historical Gamble
Su Shi argues Qin's unification was "not by Heaven's way, merely by skill—and luck." The greatest puzzle: why was Qin so clumsy against Chu yet never defeated? Su Shi's answer: "luck." Qin first attacked Chu with Li Xin's 200,000 men and failed, then sent Wang Jian with 600,000—"emptying the entire state for war." Had Qi been awake at that moment, "sweeping its borders to attack Qin—with a long-peaceful Qi against a war-weary empty Qin—toppling Qin would be as easy as flipping a palm." But Qi was sedated by forty years of Qin not attacking—which Su Shi calls "cleverness in taking Qi": Qin didn't attack Qi precisely to make Qi break its alliance with the Three Jin. He deploys a dental metaphor: "Qin thought Chu was a loose baby tooth—just yank it out. One pull and the tooth comes, but the gum is injured and the finger bitten. That Qin did not perish was luck." Compare Fu Jian's million-man army routed at Feishui: "Shihuang was lucky; Fu Jian was not."

04
Institutional Design
Abolishing Feudalism & Defending Wu Zixu—Institutional History and Human Nature
In "Qin Abolishes Feudalism," Su Shi offers a historic defense of Qin Shihuang and Li Si: "Having unified the world, dividing it into commanderies with governors—this was natural order, like furs in winter and linen in summer." He cites Liu Zongyuan's "feudalism was not the sage's intent, but the force of circumstance" as eternal law. His most radical conclusion: "All that has blood will fight; fighting is for profit; nothing yields more profit than feudalism. Feudalism is the origin of conflict and the beginning of chaos." Feudalism is the root of all civil war. As for Wu Zixu, Su Shi mounts the most complete human defense: Yang Xiong had accused Zixu of three crimes—"staying after three admonitions, whipping a corpse, and the Ji Guan affair." Su Shi refutes all three. "A father wrongly executed—the son takes revenge. This is ritual. Alive, behead him; dead, whip his corpse. This is the extreme of pain—there is no choice." On "three admonitions then leave," Su Shi says it applies only to ministers of shallow bonds—Wu Zixu was a dynastic minister whose fate was tied to the state. "A hundred admonitions ignored—follow them with death." And he twists the knife: if Fan Li and Wen Zhong had fought and died persuading Goujian, Yang Xiong would have blamed them with the same charges as Zixu—"all children's views."

05
Confucian Politics & Historical Ethics
The Three Huan and Sima Qian—The Sage's Dilemma and Historiography's Original Sin
In "On the Three Huan," Su Shi examines Confucius with rare political realism. In 497 BCE, Confucius asked Duke Ding of Lu to "demolish the three cities" (the private fortresses of the Three Huan families). Critics said "Confucius' governance was precarious and difficult." Su Shi's reading is profound: the Three Huan's power equalled Cao Cao's—"their territory was less, but mutual suspicion was no less"—yet Confucius managed to implement demolition without the families suspecting hostile intent. "There must have been a credibility that worked without words, an authority that prevailed without anger." And Confucius, in old age, still sought to attack Qi after Chen Heng assassinated his duke: "I know Confucius wanted to rectify all the feudal lords... until old and dying, he never forgot." The capstone essay indicts Sima Qian for "two great crimes"—first, praising Shang Yang and Sang Hongyang. Qin's prosperity, Su Shi argues, "was the effect of Duke Xiao encouraging agriculture, not Shang Yang's blood-soaked merit." Sang's "supplying the throne without raising taxes" was "merely devising ways to seize the people's wealth—worse harm than raising taxes." Su Shi's most devastating metaphor: taking "cold-food powder" (an ancient hallucinogenic drug) for longevity—"ulcerous backs and vomiting blood follow one after another, yet they never awaken, beguiled by the beauty of the words, forgetting the cruelty of the calamity." That is the allure of all dangerous ideas.

TAKEAWAY · Core Insight
Su Shi's "On Antiquity" is an unprecedented intellectual jailbreak. He proves King Wu was not a sage—not as a moralist's pretense, but as a serious inquiry into the legitimacy of violent revolution. He denounces the eastward relocation as a mistake—not as hindsight, but using the common-sense principle of "don't sell the ancestral home" against all arguments of strategic necessity. He defends Qin Shihuang's abolition of feudalism—not to flatter a tyrant, but recognizing the historical necessity of the commandery system and the inherent violence in feudalism. He condemns Sima Qian—not from literary rivalry, but from vigilance over the power of historical writing: "Beguiled by the beauty of words, forgetting the cruelty of calamity"—this line, even today, remains the ultimate warning against all seductive ideas. Seven essays, each a demolition of a thousand-year verdict. After the demolition, what Su Shi leaves is a world of historical reason—dependent on causal logic, not moral slogans.
