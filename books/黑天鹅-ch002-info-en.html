The Black Swan · Ch2 "Black Swans in Publishing"

A book predicted to sell 10 copies sold millions, translated into 40 languages Yevgenia Krasnova was an unknown neuroscientist and novelist. Every publisher rejected her manuscript. One editor carefully added: "My dear friend, this book will sell only ten copies." Five years later, her "A Story of Recursion" became one of the greatest, most astonishing successes in literary history — a Black Swan in publishing.

Through the fictional character Yevgenia Krasnova's publishing saga, this chapter reveals Black Swan phenomena in winner-take-all environments. Publishers rejected her manuscript because it was "neither fiction nor nonfiction," fit no genre, and had no clear audience. An obscure small press accepted it for a tiny royalty — and it became a phenomenon selling millions of copies in 40 languages. Afterwards, every editor who had rejected her claimed they "saw the value all along." This story perfectly illustrates: in Extremistan, success cannot be predicted, and post-hoc attribution is pure illusion.

01
Systemic Rejection
Every publisher rejected a goose that would lay golden eggs
The publishing industry has a systemic blind spot for anything that doesn't fit its classification templates. Yevgenia was both neuroscientist and novelist, writing foreign dialogue in original languages with subtitles. Publishers couldn't answer the first question: "Is this fiction or nonfiction?" They demanded she "fit into a genre" because "bookstores need to know which shelf to place it on." The industry's classification system is itself a Black Swan generator — it systematically excludes everything new that cannot be categorized.

02
Institutional Exclusion
The writing workshop dogma: using past templates to deny future possibility
The standard of "good writing" was compliance with rules that had become absolute truths. Yevgenia attended a famous writing workshop and left nauseated. Every writer she met was trying to imitate stories published in The New Yorker, failing to recognize that, by definition, most new things cannot find templates in back issues. The workshop instructor, very gentlemanly but firm, told her she was hopeless. This is Platonicity in action — using known categories to negate unknown possibilities.
📋

✗ Dogmatic Standards

Imitate New Yorker templates, obey genre rules, "amateurs write for themselves, pros write for others" — judging all work by known frameworks.

💡

✓ Conditions for a Black Swan

Refuse categorization, persist with originality, find a small readership online — then a shrewd small-press owner discovers it.

03
Path to Success
From small press to empire: Black Swans need time to ignite
Positive Black Swan events take time to manifest their impact. Posting her manuscript online changed Yevgenia's fate. She attracted a small readership, including a shrewd small-press owner with pink-rimmed glasses and a gruff Russian accent. He offered to publish without altering a word — because of her insistence, he paid only a fraction of standard royalties, so he had little to lose. It took five years for the book to catch fire. The small press became a major company. This is a classic positive Black Swan: unnoticed at first, earth-shattering in cumulative effect.

04
Post-Hoc Attribution
After success, everyone claims they "saw it all along" — the greatest illusion
The smarter the explainer, the more hollow the explanation. After her success, many editors who had dealt with her blamed her for not coming to them — they were certain they would have immediately recognized her work's value. One literary scholar wrote a paper titled "From Kundera to Yevgenia," tracing her work's lineage to Kundera — but Yevgenia had never read Kundera, only seen a film adaptation. Another famous scholar claimed to see Gregory Bateson's influence on every page — Yevgenia had never heard of Bateson. The absurdity of these post-hoc attributions perfectly demonstrates the impossibility of pre-hoc prediction.

05
Industry Essence
Publishing is Extremistan embodied: classification systems manufacture Black Swans
Categorization always reduces complexity — this is the generator of Black Swan events. Publishers now have a theory: "truck drivers who read don't read books written for truck drivers," and "readers despise authors who flatter them." The fiction-nonfiction divide is too ancient to withstand modern challenges. In hindsight, her genius is obvious — but beforehand, "hopeless" was the verdict. The entire publishing classification system is a Black Swan manufacturing machine: it systematically excludes everything unclassifiable, then claims "this is exactly what we were looking for" when the new thing succeeds.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Publishing is the quintessential Extremistan domain. The industry's classification system (fiction/nonfiction, genre, target audience) systematically manufactures Black Swan events: it excludes all innovative works that defy categorization, then claims to have "seen the value all along" when innovation succeeds. Yevgenia's story teaches us: pre-hoc prediction is futile, post-hoc attribution is fraudulent. True Black Swans can only be discovered — they cannot be prophesied.
