"Can You Think Ahead?"

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → AnswerAn intern wants to quit and apply to grad school — she believes stronger professional knowledge requires a master's degree. But has she really thought through whether a degree equals competitiveness? How can forward-looking thinking guide major life decisions? Professor Sorenson's VT (Validity Test) and ST (Soundness Test) two-dimensional model provides a clear path.

Forward-looking thinking requires two-dimensional validation: VT tests whether "the result necessarily follows when the assumption holds," while ST tests whether "the assumption itself is valid." Both tests must stand before a decision merits further consideration. The author applied this model to the intern's graduate-school decision — revealing that companies need ability, not knowledge.

01
Thinking Framework
VT Validity Test: Does A Necessarily Lead to B?
The core question of the VT test: When assumption A holds, does result B necessarily follow? Take the semiconductor case — does production capacity increase (A) necessarily improve gross margins (B)? The answer is "yes and no": when existing facilities can accommodate new equipment, yes; but when a new factory is needed, doubling capacity doubles costs, so margins may not improve. This "Yes and No" captures the essence of VT.
💡 The essence of VT: "Yes and No" — context determines whether the causal chain holds; there is no universal cause-and-effect.
02
Thinking Validation
ST Soundness Test: Is Assumption A Actually True?
The ST test examines whether the assumption itself is true. Returning to the capacity investment example — "Can capacity really be increased as planned?" The answer: not necessarily. Your competitors are making the same upgrades. The game-theory result: either nobody invests, or everyone invests. Everyone investing means a price war, where margins fall instead of rise. ST demands we look beyond our own plans and examine the external competitive landscape.
💡 The essence of ST: Don't just see what you want to see — game theory teaches us that a decision everyone makes can be the worst decision.
03
Case Study
The Semiconductor Case: Two-Dimensional Testing of Capacity Investment
Professor Sorenson's classic case demonstrates how VT+ST test a strategic decision in parallel. Investing in capacity to improve margins seems self-evident, but dual testing reveals: from the VT perspective, economies of scale only work within existing facility limits; from the ST perspective, competitor behavior severely undercuts the true effect of capacity expansion. Both tests are indispensable — only combined can they produce a reliable forward-looking assessment.

Capacity ↑ → Margin ↑? YES within existing facilities; NO when building new plants. The boundary conditions of scale determine the outcome.

ST: Soundness Test

04
Career Analysis
Deconstructing the Grad-School Decision with VT+ST
The author applied the same model to analyze the intern's career decision. VT — Does grad school (A) lead to competitiveness (B)? Companies need comprehensive abilities (communication, adaptability, analytical skills), not textbook knowledge. Since the market demands practical capability, further isolating oneself in school fails the VT test. ST — Does grad school truly improve professional knowledge? Universities teach structural dead knowledge; companies need living knowledge connected to legal, accounting and business operations. Application outweighs concepts.

05
Core Insight
Companies Need Ability, Not Knowledge
English Proficiency Level 8 doesn't mean you can represent a department at a multinational conference — behind that lies communication skills and adaptability. Universities still teach structural dead knowledge; companies need living knowledge connected to real practice: legal, accounting, and business operations. Studying internal control theory endlessly is less useful than understanding how your company configures USB permissions in online banking. The real tragedy: most university graduates cannot use forward-looking thinking to navigate their own life direction.
  • 📖English Level 8 ≠ Leading multinational meetings (needs communication + adaptability)
  • 🎓University structural knowledge → Company needs legal + accounting + business living knowledge
  • 💻Internal control theory ≠ Understanding USB permission settings in online banking
  • 🏢The company is the best learning environment — building comprehensive abilities beats the classroom
🔑 Key Takeaway
Forward-looking thinking = VT (Validity) + ST (Soundness), neither test can be skipped. VT verifies if the causation is necessary; ST tests if the assumption holds. All major life decisions — grad school, job changes, investments — can be tested with this two-dimensional model. Remember: companies need ability, not knowledge; application outweighs concepts.
