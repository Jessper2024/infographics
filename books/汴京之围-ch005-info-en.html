The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.4 "Each Harboring Their Own Schemes"

Xuanhe Years 2–4 · The Rise of the Jurchens & Song's Internal and External Crises On the surface, the Song-Jin alliance appeared harmonious, but beneath the smiles, undercurrents surged: Ma Kuo earned the Jurchens' respect on the hunting grounds but failed to secure the Western Capital; the rebellions of Fang La and Song Jiang derailed the northern expedition; Jin envoys were left stranded in Bianjing for months, and Aguda no longer waited for the Song.

In the second year of Xuanhe (1120), the Ma Zheng diplomatic mission arrived at the camp of Jin Emperor Aguda. Ma Kuo, trained in military arts, won the Jurchens' respect with his superb archery and closely observed their hunting-and-fishing way of life — from battue hunts to diet, from tents to battle formations. Yet beneath the diplomatic toasts, Aguda denied any promises regarding the Western Capital and the prefectures north of the mountains, acknowledging only Yanjing and the prefectures south of the mountains. Meanwhile, Fang La's rebellion erupted in the southeast and Song Jiang's uprising in the north, forcing the Song to divert its main northern expedition forces southward for suppression. Jin envoys were stranded in Dengzhou for months. Aguda finally stopped waiting and launched a fatal strike against the Liao Central Capital, suddenly accelerating the race to destroy Liao.

01
Envoy Observations
Ma Kuo conquered the Jurchens with archery, but his diplomatic mission came up empty-handed
Ma Kuo earned the title "Yelü Mali" (Master Archer) by hitting two snow-covered targets with perfect shots and killing a yellow roe deer with one arrow, yet behind the meat and wine he witnessed the cutthroat reality of international relations. He observed: Jurchen battue hunts were military drills — linear formations contracting into spiral circles, with battlefield tactics all derived from hunting; Aguda remained frugal, but his sons had already acquired a taste for luxury; most critically, Aguda admitted to promising the Song the Yanjing prefectures, but flatly denied any promise about the Western Capital and prefectures north of the mountains — oral promises count for nothing in international relations.

The Jurchen Hunting Law

Riders spaced five to seven paces apart, lines connected head to tail forming a spiral — twenty to thirty dense rings. The formations evolved from hunting were the secret of Jin's invincibility.

⚠️

The Western Capital Dispute

Zhao Liangsi's memoirs claim Aguda promised the Western Capital, but the official documents record nothing. Aguda privately told his subordinates: without the prefectures north of the mountains, how do we contain the Song?

02
Internal Crisis
Fang La and Song Jiang revolt in the south and north, completely derailing the northern expedition
The consequences of the Flower and Rock levy exploded at the worst possible moment — in the tenth month of Xuanhe Year 2, Fang La raised an army in Muzhou, declared himself "Sage Duke," and established his own reign era. Zhu Mian's "Southeastern Mini-Court" had plundered the wealthy southeast into a powder keg. In just over a month, the rebel army seized Hangzhou and set it ablaze for six days; within half a year, they overran six prefectures and fifty-two counties, with civilian deaths exceeding two million. Simultaneously, Song Jiang rose in the north at Haizhou. Tong Guan diverted the elite northwestern troops — originally meant for the northern expedition — southward to suppress the rebellion. The Song-Jin joint attack on Liao was completely sidelined in favor of domestic pacification. Han Shizhong captured Fang La, but the biggest beneficiary was the eunuch Tong Guan.

03
Diplomatic Stalemate
Jin envoys stranded for months; Emperor Huizong's ambiguous letter sowed the seeds of disaster
In the second month of Xuanhe Year 3, Jin envoys landed at Dengzhou, but the garrison commander forcibly detained them — because Tong Guan, who was responsible for the negotiations, had gone south to suppress the rebellion. The Jin envoys were so enraged they nearly set out on foot for the capital. They only reached Bianjing on the first day of the fifth month, and were left waiting for another three months. In the eighth month, Emperor Huizong dismissed them with a vague letter that "said almost nothing" — neither repudiating the treaty nor making clear commitments. The Jurchens interpreted this as the Song emperor accepting Aguda's terms: only Yanjing and the prefectures south of the mountains would be returned. Even more fatally, the Song sent no envoy to accompany them back — leading the Jurchens to feel the Song were "evasive and not honest people."

04
Tipping the Scales
Aguda stops waiting: Jin forces take the Central and Western Capitals in succession, doomsday arrives for Liao
Once the Jin envoys returned with Emperor Huizong's ambiguous letter, Aguda immediately ordered the attack on the Liao Central Capital. Guided by Yelü Yudu (a Liao defector), Wuqimai and Nianhan led their armies and captured the Central Capital in the first month of Xuanhe Year 4. Emperor Tianzuo fled at the first report — from Yuanyang Lake → Baishui Lake → Yunzhong → Jiashan, fleeing further and further west. In the fourth month, the Jin captured the Western Capital. In Yanjing, the regent Li Chuwen and others enthroned Yelü Chun as Emperor Tianxi, splitting Liao in two. Emperor Tianxi tried to make peace with the Song, but Huizong refused to recognize him on the grounds that Emperor Tianzuo was still alive — he was busy completing his "Record of Genyue," immersed in celebrating the completion of his imperial garden.

05
Military Failure
Tong Guan leads 150,000 troops north against Yanjing; the Baigou defeat exposes Song's military impotence
In the fourth month of Xuanhe Year 4, Tong Guan marched north with 150,000 troops, armed with the emperor's three-tier strategy — from a smooth takeover to nominal submission. Cai You even asked the emperor for palace maids as rewards for victory. Tong Guan first tried to persuade the Liao to surrender, but every envoy he sent was beheaded by Emperor Tianxi. Ma Kuo risked his life entering Yanjing as an envoy and obtained intelligence from Liu Zongji that the Liao had no more than three hundred crack troops. Yet Zhong Shidao was ambushed and routed by Yelü Dashi at Baigou, and the western army was also defeated at Fancun. Tong Guan panicked — he blamed the failure on faulty intelligence. Zhong Shidao was forced into retirement, and He Shen was punished. In the sixth month, the army retreated, and the first northern expedition ended in a chaotic rout. The only person with a clear conscience was Ma Kuo, who had nearly lost his life as an envoy.

Tong Guan's "Three-Tier Strategy"

Top: Yanjing lines the streets in welcome, smooth takeover. Middle: Emperor Tianxi submits as a vassal and pays tribute. Bottom: a stalemate. Not one tier covered "what if we lose."

🔵

After-Action: Find a Scapegoat

Tong Guan reported that "the Khitans were too strong, hence the defeat." Zhong Shidao was forced to retire, He Shen was punished. The real problems — Song military incompetence and command chaos — were never discussed.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The iron law of international diplomacy: only promises written into formal documents count; oral commitments can be disowned at any time. The Song-Jin alliance was built from the start on divergent understandings of its terms — the Song believed they would recover all Sixteen Prefectures; the Jin intended to return only Yanjing and six prefectures south of the mountains. The Fang La and Song Jiang rebellions tied down Song forces at the most critical moment, causing Aguda to stop waiting for Song to join the attack. Once Tong Guan's 150,000 troops exposed Song's utter weakness in the northern expedition, the balance of power in the Song-Jin game tipped irreversibly toward the Jin.
