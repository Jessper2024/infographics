Scattered throughout the book, the translators' and editors' notes are supplementary explanations, miniature models of cross-linguistic bridges, precious samples of “how analogy operates at linguistic boundaries.” Here are the most representative selections.

The 23 annotations (translators' and editors' notes) scattered throughout the book are “marginalia” beyond the main text, yet they constitute a miniature laboratory for understanding “how analogy operates at linguistic and cultural boundaries.” These notes fall into several categories: cross-linguistic category differences, analogical traps from superficial interpretation, cultural allusions, and polysemous analogical extensions. These annotations are not redundant — they are living examples of “how categories are divided differently across languages.”

01
Cross-Linguistic Categories
How Languages Carve Up Categories — Same World, Different Slicing
The most fascinating notes are those revealing “how different languages divide the same conceptual domain in different ways.” Note (4) points out: in English, both “the moon” and “satellite” are moon — in English's categorical system, Earth's natural satellite and all artificial satellites share a single word. Note (21) reveals: the English verb multiply simultaneously means “reproduce” (biological category), “grow exponentially” (quantitative category), and “enlarge” (scale category) — three concepts requiring different words in Chinese are covered by one word in English. Note (22) is equally striking: “partitioning” and “division” are the same word in English — division — meaning English speakers conceptually treat “physical cutting” and “mathematical operation” as the same category. Note (23) adds: English surgery means both “medical operation” and “mathematical partitioning.” Note (19) points out the Chinese-English directional difference: “Chinese and English habits are opposite, going from large to small” — address, date, and name writing orders all reflect this deep categorical organization difference.
1
moon = the moon AND satellite — English treats “natural satellite” and “artificial satellite” as the same category
2
multiply = reproduce AND grow exponentially AND enlarge — three Chinese concepts share one English word
3
division = partitioning AND division — “physical cutting” and “mathematical operation” categorized together
4
surgery = medical operation AND mathematical partitioning — surprising overlap of “medical procedure” and “mathematical concept”
02
False Etymology
Analogical Traps of False Etymology — When Word Surface Structures Mislead Our Category Judgments
Note (18) provides a brilliant “false etymology” case: in English, titmice is the plural of titmouse, and “Janet, judging by surface appearance, took titmice to be little mice: tit, small; mice, the plural of mouse. In reality, titmice are small birds: chickadees.” This example perfectly illustrates the recurring theme in Surfaces and Essences — how “naive analogies” mislead our cognition. Janet decomposed “titmice” into known categories (“small” + “mice”), mapping via analogy to a wrong conclusion (“these are little mice”), when the word actually refers to an entirely different category (a bird). “Similar Chinese examples abound, such as eyu (crocodile) and hema (hippopotamus)” — “eyu” is not a fish, “hema” is not a horse, but Chinese word structure induces us to first categorize them under “fish” and “horse.” This is precisely the phenomenon discussed in chapter 7 (“Naive Analogies”): naive analogies lodge permanently in our minds, leading us into dead ends.

03
Cultural Allusions
Bridging Cultural Allusions — From Achilles' Heel to Wang Guowei's Poetic Remarks
Note (2) explains “Achilles' heel”: “In Homer's epic, Achilles, son of the mortal Peleus and the nymph Thetis, had a heel that was the only part of his body not dipped in divine water, his sole weakness. It illustrates this principle: even the mightiest hero has a fatal vulnerability or soft spot.” Note (13) adds “the Sword of Damocles”: “symbolizing danger that could strike at any moment.” Note (12) cites “Wang Guowei's Poetic Remarks on the Human World.” These cultural allusion notes form bridges connecting Chinese and Western cultures — they help Chinese readers understand Western cultural concepts cited in the book, while also reminding us: allusions themselves are products of cultural categorization. Citing “Achilles' heel” to illustrate “fatal weakness” is precisely using a concrete Greek mythological story (source category) to map onto an abstract concept (target category) — this is analogy. What the notes do is make such analogies understandable in cross-cultural contexts.

Achilles' heel: Greek mythology's “fatal weakness” — using concrete stories to map abstract concepts, an embodiment of analogical thinking
2

3

04
Etymology
The Archaeology of Word Roots and Etymology — Tracing Category Family Trees
Note (1) traces the etymology of French “happiness” (bonheur): “In French, the root of ‘happiness’ is heur, with the masculine suffix -eux and feminine suffix -euse” — this note reveals the family tree of the word “happiness” within its own language. Note (20) explains the origin of “braindump”: “modeled on coredump (kernel dump), meaning to pour out everything in one's mind all at once” — a vivid case of a new word born through analogy from an old word. Note (16) traces the history of “engine”: “a tribute to Charles Babbage. A 19th-century British computing pioneer, he invented an important prototype of today's computer, which he called the ‘Analytical Engine’” — a 19th-century mechanical computing device's name, extended through analogy to today's “search engine,” “game engine,” and other concepts. These etymological notes all tell the same story: every word is the result of a long chain of analogies — just as Surfaces and Essences argues.

05
Annotations as Meta-Analogy
Annotations as “Meta-Analogy” — How Translators Build Scaffolding for Cross-Linguistic Analogy
Viewing all 23 notes as a whole reveals a deeper pattern: annotations themselves are “meta-analogy.” When translators annotate “in French, the root of ‘happiness’ is heur,” they are helping Chinese readers complete a cross-linguistic category recognition — mapping the French category of “happiness” onto the Chinese category of “happiness.” When translators annotate “in English, both the moon and satellites are moon,” they are alerting readers to the difference between English and Chinese category divisions — a form of “category comparison.” When translators annotate the cultural background of “Achilles' heel,” they are providing necessary “scaffolding” for a cross-cultural analogy. Note (8) is especially interesting: “These bumps, in English, are a string of rhyming humps, bumps, and lumps. And this dialogue was originally recorded in French, where all of these are the same word ‘bosse’” — the same concept (bumps), expressed in English through three different rhyming words, yet in French through just one word. This phenomenon of “the same concept being covered by different numbers and types of categories in different languages” is the most exquisite annotation of the book's entire thesis.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The 23 translators' and editors' notes scattered throughout the book, seemingly mere appendages to the main text, are in fact 23 miniature experiments in “how analogy operates at linguistic boundaries.” They show how different languages carve up the same conceptual world differently, how a word's surface structure can induce wrong categorical judgments, how cultural allusions serve as intermediaries for cross-cultural analogy, and how etymology reveals a category's family tree. Each note is a small window through which we see: without these cross-linguistic “analogical scaffolds” built by translators, Chinese readers would struggle to access the subtle analogies rooted in English, French, and cultural references. In this sense, the annotations are not mere supplements — they are the practice of the book's central thesis: to understand the unknown new through the known old, we need analogy; and in cross-linguistic reading, translators are the ones building analogical bridges for readers.
