The World of Yesterday · Ch.9 "The First Days of War, 1914"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer The summer of 1914 was idyllic — people enjoyed peace without a care. The shot in Sarajevo barely registered: the Archduke was unloved, life went on. Yet within weeks, all of Europe plunged from festivity into the abyss of war as intellectuals collectively lost their minds and reason was drowned. How did Zweig stay sane amid national frenzy? His international life and deep political skepticism gave him immunity.

This chapter records Zweig's firsthand experience of the outbreak of World War I. From the serene summer in Baden to the Sarajevo assassination's ripple effects, from the last peace on Belgian beaches to the war frenzy on Vienna's streets, Zweig reveals with cool precision how an era descended into collective madness: when intellectuals abandoned reason and crowds surrendered to hatred, war became an unstoppable stampede. He chose silence in a military archive, with Rilke as his last kindred spirit.

01
SITUATION
The Last Summer of Peace: Baden's Calm and Undercurrents
In the summer of 1914, Zweig was writing in the small town of Baden near Vienna, surrounded by carefree crowds. On June 29, a park concert stopped mid-melody — Archduke Franz Ferdinand had been assassinated. Yet the crowd showed no shock or grief. Two hours later, life resumed. The unloved Archduke's death barely registered. Newspaper tones rose against Serbia, but "banks, shops, and individuals all went about their business as usual."
☀️

Peace on the Surface

Sunlit Baden with open-air cafés everywhere. An old farmer sighed, "The grape harvest will be the best in years" — no one knew it was the last summer.

⚡

Undercurrents

Newspaper rhetoric grew more heated by the day. Serbia was blamed. But nobody imagined war — not banks, not shops, not individuals.

02
TURNING POINT
From Fear to Frenzy: The Dramatic Reversal of Mass Emotion
Vienna transformed overnight from fear of war to war fever. Strangers spoke to each other on the streets; those who had avoided one another for years now shook hands. Postal clerks, copyists, shoemakers suddenly found they could become "heroes." Freud's "disgust with culture" was unleashed — buried primal desires surfaced like bubbles.

03
CORE CONFLICT
The Intellectuals' Collective Betrayal: Poets as "War Drummers"
Nearly all German-language writers — led by Hauptmann and Dehmel — glorified war. Philosophers called war a "baptism," doctors boasted false limbs were better than real ones, clergy joined the chorus. The most emblematic case was Ernst Lissauer, who wrote "Hatred of England," was decorated by the Emperor, and read by all 70 million Germans. After the war, he was discarded by everyone, expelled by Hitler, and died in obscurity.
📢

Lissauer's Tragedy

Wrote "Hatred of England," decorated by the Emperor, recited by 70 million. Postwar, branded "the sole culprit" — everyone who praised him abandoned him. Died in exile.

🤫

Zweig's Choice

Retreated into inner silence. Found refuge in a military archive — "far more fitting than having a Russian peasant's bayonet thrust into my belly."

04
HISTORICAL COMPARISON
1914 vs. 1939: Two Generations, Two Attitudes Toward War
In 1914, after half a century of peace, people saw war as romantic adventure — recruits shouted "We'll be home by Christmas!" In 1939, people knew war was brutal; they obeyed but did not cheer. The fundamental difference: 1914 trusted authority; 1939 trusted no politician. The betrayal at Versailles turned everyone into skeptics.

05
PERSONAL CHOICE
Rilke and the Last Kindred Spirit: Alliance in Solitude
Amid national frenzy, Zweig's old friends became patriots and annexationists overnight. Long-time companions accused him of "no longer being Austrian." Only Rilke could still share his heart — appearing in uniform, looking "docile and clumsy," and parting with: "If one must go abroad, then go abroad! War is a prison." Zweig finally left Vienna to begin his own battle: fighting the betrayal of reason.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
The most terrifying aspect of war is not artillery but the collective collapse of reason. When intellectuals abandon independent thought and crowds surrender to hatred, war acquires its deadliest weapon. Zweig's firsthand account reveals that 1914's frenzy stemmed from naive faith — and that the shattering of that faith, through the betrayal at Versailles, became the prelude to an even more devastating 1939. Staying sane amid national madness requires not courage but the immunity that life experience grants.
