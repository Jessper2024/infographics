Chapter 6 "Selling on Trading Noise"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Systematic traders follow signals — discretionary traders have the luxury of choice. When the market emits unusual noise, should you stick to the plan or exit early? The answer lies in identifying five forms of trading noise and tightening stops before they're triggered.

01
Momentum Fade
Weakening Momentum — MACD Bearish Divergence Is the Earliest Exit Signal
When MACD histogram and price form a bearish divergence — the stock no longer moves in your favor — a savvy swing trader exits early rather than waiting for the target. The price may or may not reach the target; taking profits and reassessing from the sidelines is the safer play. Long-term traders may sell part and keep the core position.

02
Info Risk
Selling Before Earnings — The Hidden Battlefield of Leaks and Insider Trading
Earnings reports rarely "surprise" the market: experts forecast them, and information often leaks in advance. Insider trading is far more widespread than most imagine — only the greedy and stupid get caught. If a company has a history of leaks, exit before the announcement. No matter how confident you are, closing before earnings puts you on the safe side.
⚠️

Information Leaked in Advance

Price already reflects expectations before the announcement. "Buy the rumor, sell the fact" dynamics create gap risk. CSCO case: price above the upper channel before earnings, then plunged.

🔒

Honest Announcement

When a company keeps secrets well, the announcement can trigger violent moves. RIMM case: three positive surprises simultaneously — shares gapped up to an all-time high.

03
Extreme Sentiment
Market Alarm Bells — When Outsiders Start Making Consistent Money, the Top Is Near
Alarm bells ring rarely, but the signal is unmistakable: when amateurs consistently profit and the least sophisticated start recommending stocks, market laws appear suspended — but that's only the temporary illusion of a bubble. Hearing the bell and acting is what separates novices from disciplined traders.

04
Leading Indicator
NH-NL Index — The Best Leading Indicator for the Stock Market
New Highs (NH) represent bullish power; New Lows (NL) represent bearish power. A positive NH−NL value signals bull dominance; divergence warns of reversal. When the S&P 500 hits new highs but NH-NL declines, a major top signal has formed. Classic case: February 2007 — prices at new highs, NH-NL declining.

05
Execution Framework
The Sell Decision Tree — Turning Noise into Structured Trading Discipline
An expert's decision tree lives in their bones — it's a set of "if… then…" rules for what to do under specific conditions. The sell decision tree starts with trade type and works through stop placement, noise sensitivity, and response strategy. Short-term traders use tight stops; long-term traders ignore daily noise and focus on weekly signals.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Trading noise is the discretionary trader's most valuable early-warning system. Systematic traders rely on signals; discretionary traders rely on judgment — the two are not opposites. The best traders fuse both: hunt for opportunities and set stops like a system, while listening to market noise like a hunter. When MACD diverges, earnings loom, alarm bells ring, or NH-NL warns — exiting early is not cowardice, it is discipline.
