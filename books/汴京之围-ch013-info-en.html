The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.11 "War Resumes"

Part III: The Fall of Bianjing · Taiyuan Falls → Jin Army Sweeps South → Prince Kang Goes Solo → Second Siege In the 8th month of Jingkang Year 1 (1126), the Jin expedition issued a declaration of war and resumed their southern campaign. Taiyuan held out for 260 days before falling; Zhending was lost; the Jin army swept down the Fen River valley. Emperor Qinzong vacillated between war and peace as the Song military system collapsed — not for lack of good generals, but because the chain of command killed every chance of victory.

This chapter serves as the prelude to Bianjing's fall. The two Jin commanders (Nianhan and Wolibu) marched south again. Taiyuan, after a 260-day siege, finally fell — General Wang Bing drowned himself in the Fen River holding the portrait of Emperor Taizong. Zhending Prefecture fell soon after, and the Jin forces opened the Jingxing Pass. Emperor Qinzong attempted a last-ditch military reform by appointing four Supreme Commanders, but it was too late. The Jin crossed the Yellow River as if it were a creek. Prince Kang Zhao Gou, stranded in Xiangzhou after a mob killed the negotiator Wang Yun, accidentally became the last ember of the Song dynasty. On the 24th day of the 11th month, Wolibu's eastern army once again reached the walls of Bianjing — the second siege had begun.

01
Fall of Taiyuan
260 Days of Lonely Resistance: The Bravest City Fell While Reinforcements Watched Helplessly
Taiyuan was the most tenacious city of the Jingkang War — and the most tragic. Its forty-li walls were defended by every male aged 15 to 60 under Commander Zhang Xiaochun. The Jin deployed thousands of siege engines — stone throwers, tunnel carts, goose chariots, bridge towers, cloud ladders, fire ladders — all countered by General Wang Bing. But starvation broke the defense: first livestock, then leather goods, grass roots and tree bark, finally cannibalism. The population shrank by 80%. On the 3rd day of the 9th month, the city fell. Wang Bing drowned himself holding the portrait of Emperor Taizong. More damning: over 100,000 Song troops were stationed nearby — none could break through to help.

02
System Failure
Not the Generals' Fault: The Song Command System Killed Any Chance of Victory
The Song had no shortage of good generals — Zhong Shidao, Zhong Shizhong, Zhang Xiaochun, Liu Ge, Zhe Keqiu — but the command system made effective warfare impossible. Jin commanders had full authority to mobilize all resources. Song generals needed imperial approval → filtered through grand councilors → factional battles between hawks and doves → multiple layers of oversight. Lose a battle: censors impeach you to death. Win a battle: they promote you to strip your command. The checks-and-balances system designed by Emperor Taizu had worked for 100+ years of peace — until it met the Jin war machine. On the 27th of the 9th month, the court established four Supreme Commanders, essentially creating four military provinces — a radical departure from Song orthodoxy. But it came too late.

03
Unstoppable Advance
Zhending Lost → Yellow River Crossed → Jin Army Marched Unopposed
Zhending was the critical fortress of Hebei — with strong walls and ample provisions, it could pin down the Jin eastern army. But after Liu Ge was transferred away, Zhending fell under a new commander. Nianhan's western army swept down the Fen River: Fenzhou fell, Prefect Zhang Kejian and 18 family members died; the Huiniu Ridge garrison, given only two sheng of peas per day, scattered at the first sight of the enemy; Pingyang was lost. On the 12th of the 11th month, Nianhan reached the north bank of the Yellow River. Zhe Yanzhi had 120,000 troops across the river — after one night of Jin drumming, all 130,000 Song soldiers vanished from the south bank. On the 13th, the crossing was complete and Luoyang fell. Wolibu's eastern army crossed from Ligu Ford — with courtesans playing music under the moon.

04
Prince Kang Goes Solo
A Failed Diplomatic Mission Accidentally Created the Southern Song's Founding Emperor
Prince Kang Zhao Gou was supposed to be a hostage negotiator at Wolibu's camp — a mob changed his fate. Negotiator Wang Yun had ordered "clear the fields and fortify the walls" along the route; locals saw him as a traitor and killed him outside the Cui Fujun Temple in Cizhou. Advised by General Zong Ze, Prince Kang realized going north meant certain capture. He slipped back to Xiangzhou, where he met a young soldier named Yue Fei and received Emperor Qinzong's secret wax-pill edict — appointing him Supreme Marshal of All Forces, commanding him to raise a relief army. On the 1st of the 12th month, Prince Kang established his headquarters in Xiangzhou, becoming the center of national resistance. The Jin lost an ordinary hostage prince; the Southern Song gained its founding emperor.

05
Second Siege
The Absurdity of 500 Cannons: Song Bureaucracy Laid Bare in Crisis
On the 24th of the 11th month, Wolibu arrived at Bianjing and camped at Liujia Temple — where 500 cannons had been staged for an imperial military review. The cannons' ownership triggered an absurd bureaucratic relay: Ministry of War said they belonged to the Bureau of Military Affairs → Bureau said they belonged to the Armaments Directorate → Armaments Directorate's chief had been dismissed → Capital Defense Office pushed to the Cavalry Section → Cavalry Section pushed to the Arsenal Section → before the Arsenal Section could pass the buck, the Jin arrived and turned all 500 cannons around toward the city. Bianjing had fewer than 70,000 defenders, mostly archers. The emperor appointed layer upon layer of commanders — the southern wall alone had over a dozen — with hardly any troops to command.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The fall of Taiyuan was more than the loss of a city — it was the death certificate of the Song military system. 260 days of resistance proved Song troops could fight; what they lacked was a command structure that could coordinate and decide. The Jin crossed the Yellow River as if it were a stream — 130,000 Song soldiers scattered by one night of drumming, 500 cannons handed to the enemy by bureaucratic hot potato. Prince Kang's accidental survival became the sole ember of hope. When the second siege began, Bianjing's fate was already sealed — the question was never whether it could hold, but whether the system had ever been capable of holding.
