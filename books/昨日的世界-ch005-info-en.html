The World of Yesterday · Ch.5 "Paris — The Ever-Young City"

Zweig's years of freedom in Paris — encounters with Rilke, Rodin, and Verhaeren, and his discovery of the secret of art

This chapter is one of the most poetic in the entire book. Zweig paints with delicate strokes the spirit of freedom, inclusive temperament, and cultural depth of turn-of-the-century Paris. There he befriended Rilke, Rodin, Verhaeren, and Bazalgette, learning the true meaning of "total concentration" in art. Paris was not merely his spiritual home — it was the key to understanding European civilization. This city taught him that true art springs from simple living, and true greatness lies in selfless devotion.

1 Paris's Spirit of Freedom and Democracy

Paris was the only city in Europe where all classes felt at ease — no rigid German hierarchy, only the equality born of the French Revolution's legacy.

In Paris, Zweig experienced an unprecedented spiritual liberation. An officer's wife could befriend a teacher's wife; a café waiter could shake hands with a general; Norman farmers in heavy boots could enter fine restaurants without being disdained. Paris only knew that opposites could coexist — it knew nothing of "upper" and "lower" classes.

In this atmosphere of freedom, Zweig found his spiritual home at the Palais-Royal, where the morning breeze carried the aroma of the nearby market. Here was the heart of Paris, where he could feel France's pulse.

2 The Literary Circle: Greatness Born of Simplicity

Paris's poets and writers lived the simplest lives yet created the most immortal works — their integrity completely corrected Zweig's prejudices about French intellectuals.

Through Verhaeren and Bazalgette, Zweig entered the true Parisian literary circle. Impressionist painters lived in small houses doubling as studios. Poets worked as librarians in government offices, writing in their spare time. Claudel, Péguy, Romain Rolland, and Valéry remained unknown to all but a handful of literary devotees for decades.

Bazalgette spent ten years translating all of Whitman's poetry, guiding French eyes beyond national borders. He bluntly rejected Zweig's work — yet offered the most sincere friendship. This candor was itself a microcosm of the Parisian spirit.

3 Rilke: The Pure Poet of Caution and Mystery

Rilke was the purest poet Zweig ever knew — no home, no address, no fixed dwelling — yet he devoted his entire life to guarding the sacred art of poetry.

Every detail of Rilke revealed extreme aesthetic sensitivity and self-restraint. His manuscripts were written in mature calligraphy on the finest paper, with line spacings as if measured with a ruler. Even for ordinary letters, he chose good paper and never allowed a single correction. His deliberate slowness and earnestness charmed everyone who came near him.

"People who express their feelings as if spitting blood exhaust me. That is why I rarely get close to Russians — like sipping liqueur, I only taste a little." — Rilke

Walking with Zweig in Paris, this normally imperturbable man suddenly became impatient — upon hearing of André Chénier's grave and a convent. They went together; Rilke stood before the lonely cemetery and called it "the most poetic place in Paris." They waited twenty minutes at the convent gate, just for a glimpse inside.

4 Rodin's Studio: The Eternal Secret of All Art

In Rodin's studio, Zweig learned the lesson he carried for life — the eternal secret of all great art: total concentration, forgetting self, forgetting the entire world.

Zweig debated Rodin's greatness at Verhaeren's home; Verhaeren took him to meet the master. In the Meudon studio, Rodin donned a white work coat, picked up a scraper, and smoothed the shoulder of a statue. Then he was lost in concentration — eyes blazing, floor creaking, completely unaware of the young man standing behind him.

"Great figures are always the kindest." "Great figures are almost always the most modest in daily life." — Zweig's two great lessons from Rodin

Rodin entirely forgot Zweig's presence. When closing the door, he spotted the young man and almost glared in anger — then remembered, and apologized with embarrassment. In that moment, Zweig saw the complete secret of artistic creation: concentrate every ounce of energy, and achieve perfection through self-forgetfulness.

5 London's Chill and the Discovery of Blake

From Paris's warmth to London's coolness, Zweig experienced two utterly different cultural atmospheres — yet in solitude he discovered the forgotten genius William Blake.

In London, Zweig could not penetrate British social circles. Gentlemen spoke only of court, sports, and social gatherings — he had nothing to contribute. Like a rudderless ship drifting through cold streets, he spent nine-tenths of his time writing in his room or at the British Museum.

Yet it was in the Museum's print room that he found Blake — a lonely, controversial genius. The pencil drawing of King John became his companion for thirty years. Of all the things Zweig lost, this painting was the one he could never forget.

"I had searched the streets and cities for England's genius without finding it. And now that genius suddenly appeared before me in the form of Blake, a true star." — Zweig

💡 Key Takeaway

Paris taught Zweig three things: true freedom means equal movement between classes; true art springs from total concentration within simple living; truly great people are the most humble. The contrast between Paris and London showed him that a city's soul lies not in surface splendor, but in whether it offers shelter to solitary spirits. Paris is "the ever-young city" because it forever opens its arms to every sincere seeker.
