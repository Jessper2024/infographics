The Elements of Style · Ch7「Exercises on Chapters II and III」

Rules are not to be known, but to be practiced · 25 exercises covering every dimension of punctuation, syntax, diction, and style Strunk transforms the 7 grammar rules of Chapter II and 11 writing principles of Chapter III into 25 practical exercises. From punctuating a long passage on the French Revolution and Byron's travels, to analyzing how tiny textual changes between the 1798 and 1800 editions of Coleridge shifted meaning, to correcting 17 error-riddled sentences — this is the book's proving ground, designed to turn rules into instinct.

This chapter is the practical testing ground of The Elements of Style. The 25 exercises are organized into four tiers: ① Punctuation mastery (Ex. 1-2) — add correct punctuation to two unpunctuated historical passages, testing Rules 2-7 directly; ② Meaning analysis (Ex. 3) — compare Coleridge's 1798 and 1800 editions of The Rime of the Ancient Mariner, observing how punctuation and word choice shift meaning; ③ Punctuation correction (Ex. 4-8) — identify and fix erroneous punctuation in 5 passages; ④ Sentence correction (Ex. 9-25) — identify and fix grammar, structure, or style errors in 17 sentences. These exercises cover every one of the 18 rules from the preceding two chapters.

01
Punctuation Mastery
Punctuating historical documents: the French Revolution and Byron
Exercises 1 and 2 present two long unpunctuated passages requiring correct punctuation — a comprehensive test of Chapter II Rules 2-7. Exercise 1 is a historical account of the French Revolution — the King's advisers warning of bankruptcy in 1788, the summoning of the States-General, the storming of the Bastille. Exercise 2 covers Byron's first continental tour and the creation of Childe Harold's Pilgrimage. Both demand simultaneous application of comma-separation of series (Rule 2), comma-enclosure of parenthetic expressions (Rule 3), comma before conjunction introducing an independent clause (Rule 4), avoidance of comma-joined independent clauses (Rule 5), and avoidance of broken sentences (Rule 6).

02
Meaning Analysis
Coleridge's two versions: how punctuation and word choice transform poetic meaning
Exercise 3 is the sole meaning-analysis question. Strunk gives Coleridge's Rime of the Ancient Mariner in two editions — 1798: "God save thee, ancyent Marinere! From the fiends that plague thee thus—" vs. 1800: "God save thee, ancient Mariner! From the fiends, that plague thee thus!" Four tiny differences — ancyent → ancient, Marinere → Mariner, insertion of a comma, dash → exclamation mark — yet these four shifts fundamentally alter the poem's tone, rhythm, and emotional register: from colloquial archaic English to more formal modern pronunciation, from one connected utterance to two independent cries. This exercise demonstrates the entire philosophy behind Rule 12 (definite, specific language) and Rule 17 (consistent tense/style): tiny linguistic choices produce massive differences in effect.

03
Punctuation Fixes
Five punctuation correction exercises: non-restrictive clauses, comma splices, sentence fragments
Exercises 4-8 require identifying, explaining, and correcting erroneous punctuation — covering the most persistent classes of punctuation error. Exercise 4: "This course is intended for Freshmen, who in the opinion of the Department are not qualified for military drill" — the who-clause is restrictive (only those freshmen not qualified), so the comma is erroneous. Exercise 5: advertising punctuation abuse. Exercise 6: The Nation poets passage — a comma joins independent clauses, violating Rule 5. Exercise 7: a sentence fragment — "A long and narrow passage…" is wrongly split from its preceding sentence, violating Rule 6. Exercise 8: a misleading comma distorts the causal attribution of "largely because."

04
Correction: Grammar
Dangling participles, misplaced modifiers, subject drift — the first 8 corrections target grammar structure
Exercises 9-16 focus on Chapter II's core grammar rules, especially Rule 7 (participle subject agreement), Rule 16 (keep related words together), and Rules 4-6 (clause joining). Exercise 9: "During childhood his mother had died" — "during childhood" modifies "had died" not "his mother," but the placement creates logical confusion. Exercise 11: "My farm consisted of… having given a hundred pounds…" — the participle's logical subject should be "I," not "My farm" (Rule 7, dangling participle). Exercises 12-13: the same participle-subject mismatch problem. Exercise 14: "had several long and intimate conversations during the tedious weeks of the conference with his British colleague" — "with his British colleague" is misplaced, should be adjacent to "conversations" (Rule 16, keep related words together).

05
Correction: Style
Wordiness, vagueness, passive voice — the last 9 corrections target Chapter III's writing principles
Exercises 17-25 drill into Chapter III's writing principles, especially Rule 10 (active voice), Rule 12 (specific language), Rule 13 (omit needless words), Rule 14 (avoid loose sentences), and Rule 18 (emphatic words at end). Exercise 17 is a series of three loose sentences about building a fire — "Fire, therefore… Happily… Of these…" — a textbook example of consecutive loose sentences violating Rule 14. Exercise 22: "Not only had the writer… but by royalty also" — the but-clause structure is not parallel (Rule 15). Exercise 24: "Earnings from other sources are of such a favorable character as to enable a splendid showing to be made by the company" — this is Rule 13's ultimate anti-example: "character" is a filler, "splendid showing" is vague, and the passive voice bloats the whole. Exercise 25: "the status of affairs is such as to make it impossible to predict…" — similarly, a dual offense of vagueness + wordiness.
❌

Loose Sentences & Parallel Imbalance

Ex.17: three loose sentences chained — per Rule 14, restructure into periodic form. Ex.22: not only… but by royalty also — not parallel (Rule 15).

✅

Filler Words & Passive Voice

Ex.24/25 are anti-examples for Rules 13+10: character, favorable, splendid showing, status of affairs — delete these, switch to active voice, and the sentence clears instantly.

🔑 Core Takeaway
Twenty-five exercises prove one truth: good English writing is not about "knowing the rules" but about "executing them under fire." Chapter II's 7 grammar rules and Chapter III's 11 style principles are all compressed into these exercises. Every comma, semicolon, and dash is a miniature logical judgment. Every correction of a dangling participle is an act of loyalty to the "clear subject" principle. Every deletion of a filler word demonstrates the power of Rule 13. Strunk's exercises are not designed for the reader to "get them right" — they are designed so that, in the process of getting them wrong, analyzing why, and correcting them, the rules become muscle memory.
