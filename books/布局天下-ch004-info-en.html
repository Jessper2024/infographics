Layout of the World · Chapter 4
「Military Geography of the Central Plains」

Where all forces converge — why the Central Plains became the ultimate arena of imperial contention

The Central Plains, situated at the heart of all directions, can gather the full momentum of the realm. 'To conquer the realm, the Central Plains are the indispensable prize; to govern the realm, the Central Plains are the controlling hub.' This chapter first reveals the strategic logic — unity concentrates power here; fragmentation disperses it. It then dissects four sub-regions: the Sanchuan Valley (home of Luoyang), the Nanyang Basin (a revolving door in all directions), the upper Huai River (a fan-shaped water network), and the Henei region (the key pass between Shanxi and Henan). Finally, through the twin historical threads of east-west and north-south conflict, it illuminates the Central Plains' ultimate strategic position.

1
Momentum
Gathering the Full Momentum: The Strategic Logic

The Central Plains connect all directions. When the realm is unified, power concentrates here; when fragmented, it disperses. "Though the deer is hunted in the Central Plains, the hunters mostly arise not from the center but from the corners." In times of chaos, holding the exposed center is harder than holding the corners. Only after the accumulation phase should one advance on the Central Plains — the true arena for contesting the empire. Qin, Han, and Tang entered from Guanzhong; Eastern Han from Hebei; Ming from the Southeast — all followed this logic.

2
Sanchuan Valley
Sanchuan Valley: Luoyang, the Central Star of the Board

The Sanchuan Valley, formed by the Yellow, Yi, and Luo rivers, cradles Luoyang — blocked by mountains on three sides with the Yellow River barring the north. Tong Pass guards the west, Hulao the east, Yique the south, and Mengjin the north. Since King Wu of Zhou established Luoyi, the city was considered "the center of the realm." King Wu of Qin "wished to drive a chariot through Sanchuan to glimpse the Zhou court." The valley was pivotal in east-west relations — regimes based in Guanzhong relied on it to control the east; those based in Henan used it to bridge east and west.

3
Nanyang Basin
Nanyang Basin: The Revolving Door of Four Directions

The Nanyang Basin functions like a revolving door — accessible from and able to project toward all four directions. Westward up the Han River leads to Hanzhong; northwest through Wu Pass enters Guanzhong; south via Xiangyang reaches the Two Hu region; eastward gaps penetrate the Central Plains. Li Gang described it: "West to Guanshaan for troops, east to Jianghuai for grain, south to Jinghu and Bashu for wealth, north to the three capitals for reinforcement." In east-west conflicts it was contested between Guanzhong and the east; in north-south standoffs, Xiangyang and Nanyang at opposite ends embodied the clash of interests.

4
Upper Huai
Upper Huai River: The Fan-Shaped Water Network

Southeast Henan is structured by Huai River tributaries fanning outward — Bian, Guo, Ying, Ru rivers — whose headwaters reach deep into the Central Plains before joining the Huai and, via the Hangou Canal, the Yangtze. Northern forces typically advanced via the Guo and Ying rivers (downstream surges); southern forces favored the Si River (to contest Shandong and alter the strategic balance). Kaifeng, Shangqiu, Bozhou, Xiangcheng, Runan, and Xinyang formed a chain of strongholds. "To hold the Yangtze, first hold the Huai" — the upper Huai was both a lifeline for southeastern regimes and a corridor for northern invasion.

5
E-W & N-S Conflict
The Central Plains: Crossroads of East-West and North-South Conflict

East-west and north-south conflicts were two defining patterns of Chinese history. The east-west front ran along the eastern edge of China's second topographic tier — from Youyan southward along the Taihang, through Songshan and Fangcheng to western Hubei. The north-south axis followed the Huai River to the upper Han River. The Central Plains, centered on Luoyang, sits precisely at the intersection of these two axes — making it the unavoidable convergence point. Historically, western powers prevailed more often (topographic advantage), while in north-south contests, the north relied on Shanxi and the south on Nanyang.

📌 Key Takeaways

The Central Plains is the central star of the chessboard — the convergence point of all forces. Its four sub-regions each play distinct roles: Sanchuan Valley as the east-west hinge, Nanyang Basin as the four-direction revolving door, the upper Huai as the north-south corridor, and Henei as the Shanxi-Henan pass. But as exposed terrain, the center favors offense over defense. True strategists understood: from corners through sides, finally converging on the Central Plains — this is the geographic trajectory followed by nearly every unifying dynasty in Chinese history.
