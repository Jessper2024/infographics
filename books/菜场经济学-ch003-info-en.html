The End of Land Finance — Who Pays for the Massive Government?
SCQA Analysis
S (Situation): Public finance is the foundation of state governance. Over half of local government revenue comes from land sales and related economic activity.
C (Complication): The public payroll is bloated, local debt snowballs, the social security gap widens — yet all expenditures are rigid and untouchable. Tax cuts have been promised for years; enterprise burdens only grow heavier.
Q (Question): How is the fiscal deficit "eliminated"? Is local government debt really risk-free? Who will resolve the social security trap?
A (Answer): The trick to "eliminating deficits": classify government bond issuance as fiscal revenue. The local-debt game: "roll over old debt with new, dilute with printing." The fiscal binary choice: keep selling land to prop up high housing prices, or slim down the government. Buying a house is serving the nation.

🔴 The "Eliminated" Deficit: Statistical Magic

Where does government money come from? Earn, print, monopolize. "Earn" is taxation; "print" is spend directly; "monopolize" is selling exclusive goods at inflated prices (oil, land, utilities). The core secret of fiscal science: the statistics bureau simply classifies government bond issuance as fiscal revenue. Expenditure exceeds revenue, creating a deficit? Just broaden the definition of "revenue" — instant surplus. This has been practice for decades; all textbooks write around it vaguely. Central government revenue relies mainly on real-economy taxes; local government relies on real estate — hence one slows while the other surges. The PBOC faces three contradictory mandates: grow the economy, stabilize the currency, stabilize the exchange rate. The first and third demand more printing; the second demands less. The Hong Kong dollar is backed by USD — issue HK$7.80 only after depositing $1. The RMB? The 2013 deficit was ¥1T, 1.9% of GDP, below the international 3% safety line — but local balance sheets show zero deficit. Reality: city investment companies borrow from banks to fill the gap. Self-deception.

🟠 Slimming Government: The Impossible Mission

Two biggest fiscal burdens: too many people on the public payroll, and local debt too large. 500 million workers must support 610 million non-workers plus 40 million on the fiscal payroll. In a single subdistrict office of 20,000 people, the publically-supported headcount has reached levels the newspapers won't even report. Shift the fiscal base to SMEs? Either gouge them with heavy fines and fees, or nurture them while enduring austerity. Premier Zhu Rongji once pledged to halve the government headcount — when he tried to deliver, he discovered the ferocity of vested-interest pushback. Before 2002, many localities couldn't pay civil servants and teachers. Land and housing account for 65–70% of local revenue. What happens if that changes? Would civil servants lower housing prices if it meant their own paychecks disappearing? The binary choice: keep high land prices propping up high housing, or cut headcount. Without shedding the burden, ¥20/catty for bok choy is entirely plausible.

🟡 The Tax-Cut Dilemma: A Turnover Tax Trap

China's tax system centers on turnover tax, not income tax. The 17% VAT is essentially a wage tax — buy ¥100 of wood, process it into a ¥1,000 table; the ¥900 value-add is mostly labor cost. Staggering enterprise burden: for ¥10,000 in take-home pay, the company spends roughly ¥19,000 (17% VAT + 48% social insurance + 20% individual income tax). The biggest secret of tax collection: "What's this year's growth target? Miss it and lose the year-end bonus." Tax is collected by administrative directive; the tax law is hollowed out in practice. VAT reform as "tax cut"? The compliance costs of issuing invoices often exceed the deductible amount. When fiscal revenue shrinks, which public-payroll recipient volunteers for a pay cut? Massive tax reduction is extremely difficult. The only way out: expand the deficit — i.e., print money. The trilemma: austerity (the rich oppose), tax hikes (SMEs oppose), printing (white-collar workers oppose). The finance ministry expert says "prepare for a decade of tight living" — missing a subject. Tight for whom?

🟣 Risk-Free Local Debt: The Perpetual Rollover Machine

China's monetary aggregate is astronomical. Where did the money go? Local debt swallowed it — skyscrapers, tunnels, viaducts, high-speed rail, expressways. The defining feature of local debt: officials who borrow never worry about repayment, because when the debt matures, their term is already over. Successors won't bother with predecessors' principal; at most they'll service the interest. Banks can't foreclose on a highway, so they keep extending. The key: localities don't plan to repay, banks don't demand repayment — mutual bliss. But the money never entered the real economy; where does the profit come from to service high interest? Simple: borrow new debt to repay old debt plus interest. Compound snowball. The cover-up: print new money to dilute. Three options for resolving local debt: keep selling land, write it all off, or roll it over. Greek-style austerity repayment vs. 1998-style printing dilution — you decide. Local debt has massive inertia; it can't stop on a dime. Issuing local bonds? Buy freely, zero risk — this game has many years to run and absolutely no problems at the start.

🩷 The Social Security Trap: Reverse Mortgages as Ultimate Extraction

A universal social security system is a beautiful ideal — but who pays is the problem. The Human Resources Bureau raises the "average wage" annually for one purpose: to collect more social insurance contributions. The 48% rate — nominally shared between enterprise and employee — is entirely paid by the company finance department. The pension gap isn't an immediate crisis; pay-as-you-go can sustain a dynamic balance for many years. But in 15 years, seniors over 60 will hit 25% of the population (the actual claiming age is lower). Who will create those pensions? A mystery. "House-for-pension": seniors mortgage their homes to the bank, receive ¥10K/month, and the bank gets the house at the end — the state wants to extract every last drop from a family. Countless Beijing/Shanghai people plan to sacrifice their own generation to leave a home for their children. "Free benefits in a market economy and free benefits in a planned economy are not the same thing" — agricultural tax abolished, healthcare provided, cheap housing available. Then, when your son buys a home, multiply the price by five. That's the lesson. The post-70s generation had it easier (multiple siblings, low housing prices). The post-80s are destined to be history's toughest generation.

💡 Key Takeaway

Fiscal policy is the baton that conducts everything. Monetary policy, financial policy, industrial policy — all dance to the fiscal tune. Local government relies on land and housing for 65–70% of revenue — a ratio so extreme that imagining its disruption is staggering. Government revenue has three sources: earn (taxation), print (spend directly), monopolize (sell exclusive goods at inflated prices). The first two are nearing their limits; the third is unsustainable. The fiscal deficit's truth is "eliminated" through statistical magic. Local debt has no risk — not because it's repayable, but because the printing press will keep spinning. The ordinary person's survival rule: understand the fiscal structure and you understand all policy direction. "Buying a house is serving the nation" isn't a joke — half of local fiscal revenue comes from your home purchase. Social insurance squeezes the young to subsidize retirees; retirees' main consumption battleground is the hospital. Behind every "free benefit," your son's home purchase price multiplies by five. See this card clearly, and you see the entire deck of China's economy.
