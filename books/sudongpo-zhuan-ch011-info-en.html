Su Dongpo Biography · Ch. 11 "Hangzhou and Mizhou"

Through brotherly farewell, Ziyou's counsel, and Mencius's "flood-like qi," Su Dongpo's most liberated verse was born

In the summer of 1071, Su Dongpo left the capital with his family for Hangzhou. Over the next eight or nine years, he served with distinction in Hangzhou, Mizhou, and Xuzhou — his most prolific period of poetry. En route, he stayed seventy days with Ziyou in Chenzhou, celebrating the Mid-Autumn Festival together. Ziyou, knowing his brother's dangerous candor, placed a hand over his mouth and counseled silence. But Dongpo replied: "When I feel something is wrong, it's like finding a fly in my food — I have to spit it out." This "flood-like qi" — Mencius's "vast and unyielding" spirit — sustained Dongpo throughout his life.

01
Brotherly Bond
Seventy Days at Chenzhou: Their Longest Reunion
En route to Hangzhou, Su Dongpo stopped in Chenzhou to visit Ziyou and stayed over seventy days — the longest reunion the brothers would ever share. Ziyou, impoverished, lived with his large family in a low cottage. Dongpo teased him about his height: "Often he bows his head to chant the classics; suddenly he stretches — and bumps the roof!" For over two months the family lived together, the brothers rowing on Willow Lake or strolling the suburbs, discussing politics, household affairs, and their respective futures.

02
Character Contrast
Two Brothers: China's Henry and William James
The difference between the brothers was like that between Henry and William James: Dongpo, like William, infused wit and humor into political reality; Ziyou, like Henry, brought depth of human thought to his quiet prose. Ziyou lacked half his brother's brilliance, but his work carried substance that made him a major literary figure in his own right.

Philosophical Roots
The Flood-like Qi: Mencius's Legacy to Su Dongpo
"Qi" — vapor, spirit, force, suppressed fury — is the key concept for understanding Su Dongpo's character. Mencius's "flood-like qi" (haoran zhi qi), akin to Bergson's élan vital, represents the vivid, compelling force within human character — a noble spirit for right action and justice, innate, growing stronger through the course of a life.

04
Political Awareness
"If the Emperor Wants to Kill Me, I Die Without Regret"
Su Dongpo confessed to his brother that when he submitted memorials to the throne, he genuinely feared for his life. He told his classmate Chao Duanyan: "If the emperor wants to kill me, I die without regret. But one thing — sacrificing myself to advance you? I won't do that." Ziyou offered his philosophy of time: "One day of leisure equals two ordinary days. If a man lives seventy years entirely at ease, he has effectively lived one hundred and forty."

05
Literary Manifesto
"The Gibbon Cries, the Crane Calls — Unaware of Travelers Below"
Su Dongpo's poetic declaration: "Fancy phrases from past lives can never be ground away, a poet's lingering sentiment still winds its way. The gibbon cries, the crane calls, without intent — unaware of travelers on the path below." On the farewell night, Ziyou accompanied his brother down the Ying River. The two brothers rowed through the night, debating politics and exchanging poems. For six years thereafter, Dongpo would never again spend the Mid-Autumn Festival with his brother.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Hangzhou and Mizhou period was a pivotal turning point in Su Dongpo's life. He left the capital bearing political wounds, yet found warmth in brotherly love, strength in Mencius's "flood-like qi" ("vast and unyielding"), and poetic inspiration. Ziyou counseled silence — placing a hand over his mouth — but Dongpo could not comply. As he wrote: "The gibbon cries, the crane calls, without intent — unaware of travelers on the path below." His expression was like nature's own sounds, instinctive and free. This flood-like qi — preferring death to silence — would later produce both his greatest poetry and his greatest danger. But Su Dongpo chose to be himself: heroically, consistently, to the very end.
