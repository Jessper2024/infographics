弹性生长 · Ch.4
「Too Exhausted to Hustle—Is Civil Service the Answer?」

The Rise and Fall of Civil Service as a Career, and the Perils of Societal Safetyism

📖 Chapter Overview

A decade ago, civil service was last on graduates’ lists; now it’s a hot ticket. Through a classmate who walked all three paths—abroad, big tech, civil service—the author analyzes the appeal: stability compounds astonishingly over 40 years, while no market sector has sustained boom beyond 10 years. Yet he warns: if the best talents stop taking risks, society falls into a Ming-Qing style stagnant stability.

🔍 Deep Dive
One Classmate’s Three Lives
Born into a civil servant family, the author’s classmate dreamed of being nothing like his parents. He mastered English, worked in America, found it boring. Returned to a telecom giant, saw the industry plateauing. Algorithm-fed midlife anxiety made him crave stability—studied a year, passed the exam, now feels great.
The Temperature Gap: Inside vs. Outside the System
In big tech, colleagues are passing strangers—your department might vanish. In the system, you instantly realize you’ll deal with these people for years, competition isn’t fierce, and warmth abounds. More importantly: income isn’t just salary; benefits can double or triple the base pay.
The Cyclical Rhythm of Civil Service Popularity
1980s: system jobs hot → 1990s: Shenzhen wealth myths cooled it → 2000-2010: pay raises revived it → 2010-2020: internet boom cooled it again → recent years: internet plateau reheats it. In 40 years, no industry sustained a boom beyond 10 years. Only civil service maintained continuity.
The Logic Parents Can’t Articulate: Better with Age
Young people don’t get it: why do parents prefer 3K/month civil servants over 30K/month coders? Medical school people know: 1.1^40 ≈ 45. If pay rises 10% yearly from 20 to 60, income multiplies 45x. Parents lived it: every industry gets disrupted. Only civil service compounds continuously.
The Decline Trap of Stable Systems
Beware society-wide ‘safetyism.’ If the best talents stop taking risks and challenging the status quo, society sinks into Ming-Qing style stagnant stability. History shows: stable systems eventually decline. Civil service pay should be modestly above median—too high distorts values. Who would innovate then?
💡 Key Takeaway

Pursuing stability is natural, but don’t mock those who take entrepreneurial risks. The former is our instinct; the latter is society’s hope. Civil service is a good path, but society needs risk-takers to break boundaries. The key is balance—stability provides security, risk-taking provides vitality.
