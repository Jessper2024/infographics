Dongpo's Forest of Jottings · Chapter 5「Immortals and Transcendent Encounters」

SCQA · 情境 → 冲突 → 问题 → 回答 During his exile in Huangzhou, Huizhou, and Dan'er, Su Shi, far from the clamor of court politics, had ample time to collect folk tales of the strange and supernatural. From Wang Lie discovering stone marrow that he could not consume, to Daoist adepts vanishing on Luofu Mountain, from rumors that Dongpo himself had "ascended to immortality," to a ghost woman luring a wedding guest with poetry — how did a banished scholar construct a worldview that transcended life and death in these remote frontiers? His answer: he approached the strange with "let's say what can be said, listen to what can be heard" — replacing fear with curiosity, superstition with documentation, and glimpsing human truth at the edges of the uncanny.

This chapter collects fifteen essays on strange occurrences recorded during Su Shi's exile (Vol. 3 of Dongpo's Forest of Jottings, "Strange Events, Part 1"): Wang Lie's Stone Marrow, Daoist Xu Wenzhen, Liu Mengde's Poem on Luofu, Strange Realms of Luofu, Dongpo's "Ascension," Huang Pushe, Hermit Chongtui, Immortal's Calligraphy, Record of a Ghost, The Li Family's Daughter Returns from Death, Daoist Zhang Yijian, On Spirit Possession, The Three Old Men, Peach Blossom Enlightenment, and Erzhu Daoist's Cinnabar Elixir. These tales — some from friends, some from village lore, some from personal experience — are recorded by Su Shi with a historian's brush, hiding deep reflections on worldly fame, religious practice, and mortality within seemingly absurd accounts.

01
Immortal Encounters
Wang Lie's Stone Marrow and Luofu's Strange Realms——The Immortal Is Just a Step Away
Wang Lie found stone marrow as sweet as syrup in the Taihang Mountains and brought it to his friend Ji Kang — but Ji Kang saw only stone. Su Shi sighed: "Immortality depends on destiny, not on striving." Luofu Mountain was an even stranger realm where immortals and mortals mingled — a government clerk entering the mountains saw dozens of Daoist chambers with a Daoist seated at the threshold, but when he approached, both chambers and man vanished. "Thus we know Luofu mixes the mortal and the sacred; such strange realms, lifelong practitioners may never see, yet a mere clerk alone witnessed them — truly, as the saying goes: one place missed, one place mysterious; immortals can only be encountered, never sought." Su Shi also wrote of "seeing the sun at night" on Luofu — a phenomenon that opened his mind to the world's greater possibilities.
The mountain is not tall, yet one sees the sun at night — this is strange indeed.
— Su Shi, "On Liu Mengde's Poem about Luofu"
02
Myth of Ascension
The "Dongpo Has Ascended" Rumor——The Absurd Fame of an Exile
When Su Shi was exiled to Huangzhou, his friend Zeng Gong died in Linchuan. Folk rumor wildly claimed that "Dongpo and Zeng Zigu died on the same day, like Li He, summoned by the Emperor of Heaven." The news reached the capital; even the Emperor inquired of Pu Zongmeng about it, and sighed. Later, exiled to Hainan, Su Shi heard the rumor again: "Now in Hainan, there are those who say I attained the Dao and sailed into the sea, never to return." Capital folk claimed his son had written to confirm this — but from where would his son have gotten a letter? Su Shi laughed: "The capital says my son wrote to tell them — from where would my son have gotten a letter?" Twice rumored to have "ascended," twice actually exiled. Fame here, life and death here — the exiled scholar became a folk myth. Also recorded: Huang Sun, who vanished one day and returned 32 years later, "his descendants painting his portrait and serving it" — the legend of one who disappeared and returned, reflecting mortals' undying fantasy of transcending life and death.

03
Ghosts and the Netherworld
Poetry-Luring Ghosts and Return from Death——Literary Imagination of Two Worlds
Su Shi recorded multiple tales of ghosts and the afterlife. Qin Guan told of a strange event at a Baoying wedding: a guest suddenly left the feast, walking toward the water, claiming "a woman summoned me with poetry" — the verse being "Beneath the long bridge a magnolia boat, through moonbeams and mist, freely I float. What use are halls of gold and jade, better to come and go in youth's parade." A ghost used poetry as lure, beauty as bait, to pull the living toward the water. Su Shi remarked: "This must have been a young lady of literary talent." A fuller netherworld narrative came from Dan'er: a Li family maiden died of illness and revived after two days, describing in detail being mistakenly seized by underworld clerks, crossing the Naihe Bridge, witnessing an underworld judge's tribunal — finally the judge said "you may go" and released her. Su Shi personally visited her father to inquire about the death and revival, recording in exhaustive detail like a tale of the strange. These stories are less superstition than the collective literary imagination of Song-dynasty commoners about the world after death.

04
Daoist & Buddhist Thought
From Sima Xiangru to Peach Blossoms——Su Shi's Daoist Reflections
Su Shi's Daoist cultivation extended beyond elixir recipes. In "Immortal's Calligraphy," he criticized Sima Xiangru for "fawning upon Emperor Wu, opening fissures in the southwestern frontier" — and even on his deathbed, still drafting the "Fengshan Treatise" — "is this not what is called dying without ceasing?" Xiangru despised reclusive immortals dwelling in mountains and marshes with emaciated forms; Su Shi retorted: you pursued fame to your dying breath — how could you possibly understand the realm of the recluse? In "Daoist Zhang Yijian," he fondly recalled his Daoist teacher from age eight — "among hundreds of children, the master praised only me and Chen Taichu." Taichu later sank to become a minor clerk, finally achieving "corpse-liberation." In "Peach Blossom Enlightenment," Su Shi sharply mocked those who blindly imitate ancient sages' paths to awakening: "People see that an ancient worthy attained enlightenment seeing peach blossoms, so they vie to praise peach blossoms, even make meals of peach blossoms — for fifty years utterly missing the point. Just as Zhang the Senior Scribe attained the spirit of cursive script from watching a porter and a princess contest the road — if one wishes to learn his calligraphy, one goes daily to seek instruction from porters — is that possible?" True insight comes from inner realization, not outward mimicry.

05
Skepticism & Reason
Spirit Possession Debunked & The Three Old Men's Boasts——Su Shi's Rationalist Core
Amidst numerous records of the strange, Su Shi always maintained a lucid rationalist core. "On Spirit Possession" is a superb empirical investigation: "Those who claim spirit possession are mostly servant girls and lowly concubines, or else the sick and dying." He noticed a crucial detail — "A man about to journey far, wanting to test his wife's devotion, hid a golden hairpin in the wall without telling her, then departed. Soon after, the wife suddenly saw her husband's spirit possessing someone, saying: 'I have died; the hairpin is in such-and-such place, go fetch it.' She retrieved the pin, held the funeral. Later the couple reunited, and the wife turned hostile." Su Shi analyzed: it was not a ghost knowing secrets, but the wife having already found the pin, resenting her husband's long absence — the "possession" was merely the outward performance of her subconscious. Such psychological deconstruction is extraordinarily rare in Song-dynasty miscellanies. And "The Three Old Men" is a piece of humor — three elders meet and compete over who is oldest; one said "I was friends with Pangu in my youth," another said "each time the sea turned to mulberry fields, I dropped a tally — my tallies now fill ten rooms." The third was silent — in truth, all were absurd boasts. Su Shi recorded this both to capture folk wit and to satirize the human tendency toward self-aggrandizement.

KEY INSIGHT
Su Shi's "records of the strange" are not superstition, but a unique mode of cognition. He embraces the unknown with a writer's imagination, dissects rumors with a historian's sobriety, and sees through absurdity with a philosopher's clarity — at the boundary of immortals and ghosts, what he found was not fear, but a deeper understanding of human nature. As he wrote in "Peach Blossom Enlightenment": true insight comes not from imitation, but from inner realization. Recording these strange tales was, for him, a way of keeping his curiosity alive through exile.
