This chapter focuses on Zhu's most terrifying rival: Chen Youliang. Ruthless and calculating, he killed his patron Ni Wenjun, general Zhao Pongsheng, and finally Xu Shouhui at Wutong Temple on June 16, 1360—declaring himself emperor mid-rainstorm with the era name "Dayi" (Righteousness), a brazen mockery of all moral conventions. Leading 100,000 naval forces downstream in three-story ironclad warships—veritable aircraft carriers—he crushed Taiping's 3,000 defenders in one strike. Zhu's advisors unanimously urged flight; only Liu Ji stood up, berating cowards: "Those saying flee should be killed on the spot!" Zhu decided on Longwan ambush. Double agent Kang Maocai misled Chen to a stone bridge instead of wood—"I've been tricked!"—Chen landed at Longwan where five Ming forces waited in silence deadlier than death. The Han army left 20,000 dead, 7,000 captured; Zhu's casualties: nearly zero. The great rivalry had truly begun.

01
Rise of a Tyrant
Chen Youliang: Ruthless Rise to Emperor
Chen Youliang—the only rebel who resisted Yuan from start to finish, unyielding and unapologetic. Born a fisherman (lowest caste), enduring lifelong discrimination. Became a county scribe through sheer study—still disrespected. Joined Xu Shouhui's rebellion, murdered his patron Ni Wenjun, then general Zhao Pongsheng. On June 16, 1360, he bludgeoned Xu Shouhui to death at Wutong Temple and declared himself emperor in a rainstorm—era name "Dayi" (Righteousness)—a mockery so bold it stunned the age.

02
The Salt Merchant
Zhang Shicheng: Small-Minded but Good
Salt smuggler Zhang Shicheng—"small-minded" but a genuinely good man. At Gaoyou, he led a handful of men resisting a million-strong Yuan army for three months—"Where else can we go? Better die here." This courage made him famous across the land. But wealth changed him; Zhu's verdict: "Qi Xiao" (small capacity). He exempted Jiangsu-Zhejiang from taxes, beloved by the people—yet lacked the ambition of a true hegemon.

03
The Fierce General
Chang Yuchun: Born Vanguard, Fatal Flaw
"I came here to be the vanguard—give me the vanguard seal." Brash on arrival—Zhu called him a refugee seeking handouts. Chang smiled: "Just watch." At Caishi, he single-handedly pole-vaulted onto Yuan's shore, killing several and opening the beachhead. Born cavalry leader, martial arts master. But his fatal flaw: he loved killing prisoners—burying 3,000 alive at Jiuhua Mountain just to send Chen Youliang a message: "I am Chang Yuchun. I defeated you."

04
Decisive Battle
Longwan Ambush: Strategy Over Power
Kang Maocai's double-deception: the bridge was replaced with stone, not wood. Chen Youliang called out "Old Kang!" into the moonlight—no answer. Looking up, he saw stone, not wood. "I've been tricked!" His forces landed at Longwan into a perfect trap. Zhu's five forces waited in silence deadlier than death—soldiers could see the reflection on enemy armor. When the yellow flag fell, the five-pronged pincer crushed 100,000 men; Chen's ships were stranded at low tide, his army jumped into the Yangtze to escape.

05
Hero's Spirit
Purple Mountain Inscription: Hero Unbound
After Longwan victory, Zhu visited Purple Mountain temple in disguise—and left a legendary poem. The abbot mistook him for a bandit, advising "cultivate yourself, study Buddhism." Zhu drew his sword, placed it at the abbot's throat: "The realm is chaos because people starve—you eat your fill, marry and breed, yet spout emptiness and steal time. Shameful!" Sheathed his sword, left in red vermilion on the temple wall: 「杀尽江南百万兵，腰间宝剑血犹腥！老僧不识英雄汉，只管哓哓问姓名。」Heroic spirit, beyond words.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Chen Youliang was Zhu's most terrifying rival—ruthless, lawless, and devastatingly powerful with his three-story ironclad fleet. But Zhu shattered him with strategy, not force: a double agent's deception, five-pronged ambush, and near-zero casualties against 100,000 men. True rivalry is never just a test of power—it is a contest of will, strategy, and character.
