Fupan · Chapter 10 — Why Zeng Guofan?

SCQA · Core Insight In intelligence, Hu Linyi and Zuo Zongtang matched him. In commanding troops, nearly every battle he led ended in defeat. How did this mediocre-talent, unremarkable Zeng Guofan become the "sage within, king without" — the foremost figure of the Qing restoration? The answer lies in his powerful post-action fupan habit: one year of self-review during mourning in 1857 completely transformed his life's trajectory.

Zeng Guofan was hailed as attaining "three immortalities — virtue, words, and deeds — the complete man as teacher, general, and minister." Chiang Kai-shek called him "the spiritual model of the Chinese people." Mao Zedong went further: "Among modern figures, I admire only Zeng Wenzheng." Yet this "complete man" had ordinary gifts — inferior in intellect to Hu Linyi and Zuo Zongtang, and losing nearly every battle he personally commanded. The turning point came in 1857: his father died, and Zeng went home for the mourning period. Freed from battlefield troubles, he painfully replayed every detail of the previous three to five years. At last, he understood: the direct Confucian and forceful Legalist methods seemed satisfying but planted hidden dangers. After returning to service, he practiced "softness overcoming hardness" — visiting every Changsha office, generously recommending subordinates, strict with himself, lenient with others. A new Zeng Guofan was born.

02
Turning Point
1857 Mourning Leave: A Painful but Necessary Self-Fupan
In 1857, Zeng Guofan's father died. Feuding with Jiangxi officialdom, he seized the chance to request mourning leave and returned home. Away from the battlefield, he painfully replayed three to five years of events countless times: Why couldn't his integrity win acceptance in officialdom? Why did following every national law consistently fail? The accumulated torment eventually broke him into serious illness.

Self-Discovery
Fupan the Flaw: From "Too Rigid" to "Soft Overcomes Hard"
Zeng Guofan finally saw his own problem clearly: his feuds with the Green Standard Army in Changsha, his forceful confrontations in Jiangxi — apparent victories that planted deeper dangers. Impeaching Qingde and Chen Qimai overstepped bounds; his sharp edges and excessive rigidity silently erected countless obstacles. "The greatest square has no corners, the greatest sound is barely heard, the greatest skill appears clumsy" — words once half-understood now opened with full clarity.

04
Behavioral Proof
After Return: The New Zeng — Strict With Self, Lenient With Others
After returning to service, Zeng fully embodied his fupan insights: riding a green-canopied sedan, he visited every Changsha government office, even paying calls on the lowliest county magistrates. Where he had once been too cautious with promotions, he now "enlightened subordinates with great righteousness while secretly promising generous recommendations" — after victories, promotion lists flowed freely. Core principles unchanged, but now lenient with others, strict with himself — no more moral pedantry.

05
Universal Lesson
Fupan Is Not for Great Men Alone: Ordinary People Can Grow Too
Zeng Guofan's fupan reveals the most important truth: fupan is not a method exclusive to successful or great figures. Every ordinary person, through fupan, can harvest their own growth and elevation. Zeng was mediocre in talent, lost most battles he led, started from an ordinary position — his path to becoming the "complete man" is the best proof of fupan's power.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Zeng Guofan's path to becoming the "complete man" proves: a person of ordinary talent can achieve self-transformation through fupan. The key is the courage to painfully reflect, to negate oneself, and to thoroughly change in action — strict with self, lenient with others. This is precisely the perfect demonstration of fupan's four stages: Review → Reflect → Explore → Elevate.
