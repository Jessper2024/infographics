Wenhua Kulv · Ch.13 "Shadow of the Mountain Resort"

A summer resort that held an entire dynasty—from Kangxi's vigorous personality to Wang Guowei's final leap

Chengde Mountain Resort, twice the size of the Summer Palace, encapsulates the soul of the Qing dynasty. Kangxi displayed extraordinary vitality here—crushing Oboi, pacifying Wu Sangui, refusing to repair the Great Wall while building "an invisible wall," hunting at Mulan, and studying Euclidean geometry. Han intellectuals went from armed resistance to cultural recognition through a long spiritual struggle. But Qianlong rejected the British trade mission, closed customs ports, and the resort began to carry an ominous chill. Jiaqing, Daoguang, Xianfeng—each weaker than the last—until Cixi ordered all construction halted. Finally, Wang Guowei drowned himself at the Summer Palace: "A dynasty that thundered through centuries always begins with the heroic postures of the strong, but its final period is always marked by the melancholic souls of the refined."

01
Historical Question
Why do we harbor such complicated feelings toward the Qing?
"People like us always have a complex emotional resistance toward the Qing dynasty." From childhood history lessons recounting the Yangzhou Massacre and Jiading Slaughter to the burning of Yuanmingyuan—"the Qing dynasty was the only elementary-school course steeped in tears." But Yu Qiuyu warns: we cannot equate Han with China. "The Han are of course magnificent... the problem is, we cannot therefore equate Han with China, piling all of Chinese history's justice, light, and hope onto the Han side." The Mountain Resort offers a fresh lens—"far away, quietly, purely, stealthily, it escaped the Central Plains' royal aura and preserved an unadulterated Qing dynasty."
02
Kangxi's Vigor
Kangxi: One of the most complete personalities among Chinese emperors
Kangxi ascended at eight, took power at fourteen—crushed Oboi at sixteen, pacified Wu Sangui over eight years starting at twenty. "He had re-cultivated the empire he inherited, transforming from successor into founder." He refused to repair the Great Wall: "The way to guard a realm lies only in cultivating virtue and pacifying the people. When the people's hearts are content, the foundation of the state is secure." He established Mulan hunting grounds beyond the Wall as military exercises, while building the Mountain Resort for diplomatic engagement. He participated in 48 autumn hunts, personally killing 135 tigers and 20 bears. More astonishingly, he studied Euclidean geometry, astronomy, physics, and medicine—"Chinese learning" and "Western learning" not conflicting at all. A stark contrast to the Wanli Emperor of the Ming who hid in his palace for 25 years without seeing a single minister.

03
Cultural Recognition
From life-or-death resistance to cultural recognition
At the dynasty's start, Liu Zongzhou starved himself for 20 days to death, Huang Zongxi took up arms, and Gu Yanwu became a lifetime wanderer. But Kangxi displayed genuine passion for Han culture—personally presiding over the "Erudite Scholars" examinations, treating with courtesy those who refused to serve like Fu Shan and Li Yong, repeatedly inviting Huang Zongxi to court and, when refused, ordering his works copied into the palace. Kangxi appointed Han historians to compile an honest "History of the Ming." Yongzheng personally wrote "Awakening to Great Righteousness" to debate Han intellectuals. By Qianlong's era, first-rate scholars no longer thought of restoring the Ming—Han intelligentsia even regarded Qianlong as a sage-ruler of China.

04
Turning Point
Qianlong's hubris: shutting the door on the world
Qianlong, resting on the foundations laid by Kangxi and Yongzheng, became the most fortunate emperor in Chinese history. He carved his "Ten Perfect Military Campaigns" into the resort's steles, savoring them with self-satisfaction. In 1793, a British mission arrived seeking trade ports. Qianlong rejected everything—"Every inch of the Celestial Empire's territory is mapped and bounded." Yet Kangxi had opened maritime customs in 1684. Qianlong closed all ports except Guangzhou, forbade foreigners from learning Chinese or buying Chinese books, and banned Catholicism. "Qianlong's sonorous words scolding foreign monarchs at the Mountain Resort—to the ears of the old man of history, they did not sound quite right."

05
Final Leap
Wang Guowei's suicide: dying for a classical cultural era
On June 2, 1927, Wang Guowei drowned himself at the Summer Palace. His testament: "At fifty, I owe only death. Having lived through this transformation of the age, righteousness demands no further humiliation." Chen Yinke explained: "When a culture is in decline, those who have been transformed by that culture must feel pain... only through suicide can one find peace of conscience and fulfill one's duty." Wang Guowei could not separate culture from the Qing—on his shelves, the imperial encyclopedia, Kangxi Dictionary, Complete Library of the Four Treasuries, and Qian-Jia scholarship were all indelibly present. He was visiting the Summer Palace for the first time, having borrowed five yuan for the trip—the ticket cost sixty cents, and four yuan and forty cents remained in his pocket when he died. "A dynasty that thundered through centuries always begins with the heroic postures of the strong, but its final period is always marked by the melancholic souls of the refined."
🔑 Key Takeaway
The true shadow of a dynasty lies not in Beijing's Forbidden City but in the Mountain Resort beyond the Great Wall. Kangxi's vigorous personality infused the Qing with moral stature, but Qianlong's arrogance slammed shut the window of dialogue with the world. Han intellectuals moved from desperate resistance to cultural recognition to dying for culture itself—the extreme fragility and extreme power of culture are both revealed in the splash of Wang Guowei's plunge into the water. Historical judgment ultimately rests not on surname or ethnicity, but on the force of personality and the burden of culture.
