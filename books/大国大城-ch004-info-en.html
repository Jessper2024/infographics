Great State Needs Big CityCh.3 · Breaking the "Impossible Triangle" of Great Nation Development

Urbanization · Agglomeration · Administrative Allocation · Efficiency Loss Can a country simultaneously pursue unity, efficiency, and regional balance? If "balance" means uniform distribution of resources and population — impossible. Enforcing uniform distribution through administrative means inevitably damages efficiency, and ultimately, even per capita balance becomes unattainable. The solution: redefine "balance" — from uniform resource distribution to per capita GDP and quality of life.

This chapter presents the book's core theoretical framework — the "impossible triangle" of great nation development: a unified market, economic efficiency, and spatial uniform distribution of resources cannot all be achieved simultaneously. If a government pursues "balance" defined as uniform resource distribution, it must intervene against market forces, damaging efficiency and even undermining market unity through local protectionism. The solution is not to abandon unity and efficiency, but to redefine "balance" — shifting from spatial uniform resource distribution to balance measured by per capita GDP and quality of life. The chapter develops this argument across four dimensions: the government-market boundary, international comparisons, the divergence between administrative allocation and population flows, and the cost of trading efficiency for uniformity.

01
Impossible Triangle
Unity, Efficiency, Uniform Distribution — You Cannot Have All Three
Every class when the author asks students about these three goals, half support and half oppose administrative intervention. The author reveals: if you pursue all three simultaneously, understanding "balance" as uniform resource distribution and intervening through administrative means — it's impossible. Suppressing market forces to enforce uniform distribution inevitably sacrifices efficiency; local governments protecting their own interests will then fragment the market, damaging unity. There is only one way out: redefine "balance" — from "uniform resource distribution" to "balance measured by per capita GDP and quality of life." This type of per capita balance can be achieved precisely through free population mobility, without requiring administrative controls.

02
Government vs Market
"Legislating" Urbanization: Where Is the Boundary Between Government and Market?
The market's role: Individuals and firms weigh the benefits (higher income, diverse consumption, knowledge spillovers) and costs (congestion, pollution, high housing prices) of urban expansion. Profits and wages serve as price signals guiding optimal resource allocation. The government's role: Correct market "externalities" — amplify positive externalities through infrastructure investment (e.g., subways relieve congestion), reduce negative externalities through technology and management (e.g., surveillance cameras reduce crime). The result of improving "urban diseases" is not to reduce city populations but to increase them. There is no theoretical basis for direct administrative population control, and certainly none for dividing urban populations into "wanted" and "unwanted" categories.
📊

What Markets Do

Determine optimal locations for people and firms: profits and wages serve as price signals guiding resource allocation. Land and housing prices naturally become entry barriers for inefficient firms.

🏛️

What Governments Do

Correct externalities: more transit reduces congestion (+externality); cameras reduce crime (−externality). These interventions increase, not decrease, urban population capacity.

03
International Comparison
The World Is Not Flat: Spatial Agglomeration Is a Universal Law
Look at the world's economic maps: every country shows high spatial agglomeration. US economic activity is concentrated on the East Coast, West Coast, and a few southern areas; Japan's three major metropolitan areas hold over 50% of the population, with the Tokyo metro area housing one-third of the nation's people on just 3.5% of the land (equivalent to Shanghai plus Suzhou); France's economy resembles the Eiffel Tower, concentrated around Paris. Germany is the exception — but its unification is only a little over a century old, and it still bears the imprint of long-term division. China's real problem lies east of the Hu Line. Compared with the US, Europe, and Japan, China's population agglomeration process still has a very long way to go.

04
Divergence Cost
Administrative Allocation vs. Population Flow: The Cost of Trading Efficiency for Uniformity
Market forces guide people East — administrative forces guide resources West. The two directions are completely opposite. After 2003, the government heavily tilted construction land quotas toward central-western provinces. These provinces' land supply share exceeded 50% of national total while their GDP share was only about 40%. Result: massive industrial parks in the central-west at zero land prices with no firms; land-backed local government bonds deeply intertwined with land mortgages; strict land supply controls in the east causing housing price rises far outpacing wage increases — effectively voluntarily abandoning the comparative advantage of labor-intensive industries. Most seriously, enterprise total factor productivity (TFP) growth has declined continuously since 2003, efficiency gaps between firms widened, and resource allocation efficiency deteriorated — perfectly coinciding with the land policy turning point.

05
The Way Out
Breaking the Impossible Triangle: Redefine Balance, Unleash Market Forces
The key to breaking the "impossible triangle" is abandoning "uniform distribution" balance and pursuing "per capita" balance. This is not a word game but a fundamental policy paradigm shift. From Europe to America, from Japan to China, economic laws never change: people flow toward high-productivity areas, achieving per capita income equalization through movement. Governments should not restrict big city development, but should: improve infrastructure and public services to correct negative externalities of urban development; provide necessary public service transfer payments to less developed regions (rather than guiding productive investments); and eliminate institutional barriers like the hukou system to guarantee the right to free labor mobility — this is both a basic human right and a requirement of economic law.
❌

Old Approach (Failed)

Pursue uniform distribution → administrative allocation → efficiency loss, market fragmentation, per capita balance impossible → lose-lose

✅

New Approach (The Way Out)

Pursue per capita balance → remove mobility barriers → market guides people to high-efficiency areas → per capita income converges + national efficiency rises → win-win

🔑 Key Conclusion
Great nation development faces an "impossible triangle": unity, efficiency, and uniform resource distribution cannot be achieved simultaneously. For the past decade-plus, China has walked precisely this dead end — tilting resources westward through administrative means while restricting big-city development in the east. The result: TFP growth has continuously declined, efficiency gaps among firms have widened, industries have been forced to relocate to Southeast Asia, and massive industrial parks in the central-west sit empty. The solution lies in redefining "balance" — from "uniform resource distribution" to "balance measured by per capita GDP and quality of life." This requires governments to stop intervening administratively in spatial resource allocation and instead remove institutional barriers to population mobility, letting market forces guide resources to the most productive locations. Only then can a unified great nation achieve both efficiency and balance.
