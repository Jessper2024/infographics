Su Shi: A Life · Ch.3
"Benevolence and Grace Fill Hangzhou"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Escaping the political storms of Kaifeng, Su Shi rediscovered his poetic heart and life's joy amid West Lake's beauty, leaving the immortal line: "Plain or adorned, she's always just right." How did a disillusioned official, sidelined by political struggle, achieve spiritual healing and reach the pinnacle of poetic art in Hangzhou's landscape?

In 1071, Su Shi arrived in Hangzhou as Vice Prefect. West Lake's four-season beauty, the meditative serenity of Solitary Hill Temple, and the joy of literary gatherings rekindled his vivacious nature. From visiting monks in misty isolation, to watching rain transform in an instant, to the culminating metaphor comparing West Lake to Xi Shi — Hangzhou healed Su Shi's poetic soul, and Su Shi reshaped Hangzhou's cultural DNA.

01
Landscape Healing
Lake and Mountain Beauty Heals Political Wounds
Upon arrival, Su Shi "couldn't help but lose himself in the lake and mountain scenery." Those stubborn sorrows and frustrations melted away imperceptibly among the hills and waters. Hangzhou was "bright mountains and clear waters, prosperous and thriving" — West Lake glittered like "a dazzling pearl," while the Qiantang tidal bore surged "like ten thousand galloping horses." Su Shi even moved his desk to the lakeside, handling official business while enjoying the view, "his brush swift as wind, resolving matters clearly amid laughter."

02
Solitary Hill Visit
Mist-Shrouded Temple: A Meeting of Kindred Spirits
Soon after arriving, Su Shi visited the poet-monks Huiqin and Huisi, recommended by Ouyang Xiu. In the mist-shrouded Solitary Hill Temple — "paper windows, bamboo hut, ancient cypresses reaching skyward" — the three "talked with palms striking, endlessly, feeling they had met too late." Returning home "as if waking from a beautiful dream," he wrote "Winter Day Visit to Solitary Hill," capturing the scene in lines like "Water so clear, rocks exposed, fish can be counted; Forest so deep, no one there, birds call to each other."

03
Poetic Pinnacle
West Lake in Verse: "Comparing West Lake to Xi Shi"
The pictorial beauty of West Lake found its most perfect, most vivid depiction in Su Shi's brush. "Drinking at the Lake, First Sunny Then Rainy" — with deceptively simple language and a startlingly fresh metaphor — became an immortal masterpiece. From then on, "Xi Shi Lake" became West Lake's poetic alias. His West Lake captured all seasons: summer's "deep blue allure," autumn's "lotuses blooming one by one," winter's "cloud and snow merging water with sky," spring's "new reeds emerging, willows reflected."

04
Literary Gatherings
Fine Scenes, Joyful Hearts: Hangzhou's Social Circle
With "warmth, wit, and an expansive heart," Su Shi quickly integrated into Hangzhou's literary circles. "At every kind of banquet and gathering, everyone delighted in his presence." He formed deep friendships with Prefect Chen Xiang and his successor Yang Yuansu. At Youmei Hall, amid toasting, a sudden thunderclap sent the Qiantang River spraying upright in the wind. He watched the new moon: "The new moon like a beauty, just emerging from the sea"; boated by moonlight: "Released fish and turtles follow people near, ownerless lotuses bloom everywhere."

05
Parting Sorrow
A Reluctant Farewell: From Paradise Hangzhou to Desolate Mizhou
After three years, Su Shi reluctantly bid farewell to this landscape. Liu Shu and Zhang Xian escorted him to the Rainbrow Pavilion. The 85-year-old Zhang Xian sang "Pacifying the Waves" himself — "counting on his fingers in silence, life and death in one parting, tears streaming as he grasped Su Shi's sleeve" — knowing his remaining days were few. Su Shi had planned to visit his brother in Jinan, but frozen canals forced him to "brave the cold wind and head straight for Mizhou" — from paradise to barren wilderness.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The three Hangzhou years were Su Shi's "healing period": West Lake's landscape not only soothed the wounds of political marginalization, but ignited his most brilliant poetic creativity. The line "Comparing West Lake to Xi Shi" achieved mutual transformation — West Lake gained its poetic alias "Xi Shi Lake," while Su Shi, in profound communion with nature, Zen, and friends, completed his spiritual metamorphosis from disillusioned official to transcendent poet.
