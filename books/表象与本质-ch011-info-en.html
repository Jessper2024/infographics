Surfaces and Essences · Ch7 "Naive Analogies"

Three anecdotes spanning a lifetime—from a four-year-old to a retired professor, naive analogy as a spontaneous, unconscious, automatic cognitive act remains identical across the entire human lifespan. It quietly links unfamiliar concepts to existing knowledge; though often wrong, it is the default way humans understand the world.

This chapter reveals the nature of naive analogy through three anecdotes. Young Tim watches Dad shave with shaving cream and a razor—drawing on his experience of "sugar dissolving in hot water," he concludes that shaving cream dissolves whiskers. Jeanette receives a neighbor's message about seeing "titmice" in a tree—unaware that titmice are small birds rather than rodents, she pictures mice running in the branches. Professor Alexander tells a young colleague departing for Germany to "send me your email address"—naively mapping "email address" to "physical mailing address." A four-year-old, a young adult, and an octogenarian professor—across enormous age gaps, the cognitive phenomenon is identical: they all fall prey to analogy's temptation, deceived without awareness.

Anecdote 1
Young Tim's Shaving Cream—Dissolving vs. Scraping
Four-year-old Tim watches his father shave. He observes Dad wetting his face, applying shaving cream, using a razor, then washing. The razor looks nothing like scissors or a small knife—it never occurs to him that it could be used for scraping. On the other hand, Tim knows very well that certain substances dissolve in others—sugar dissolves in hot water. So he is entirely convinced that the shaving cream dissolves Dad's whiskers, and the razor's sole purpose is to scrape off the cream that has done its job. Here a classic naive analogy occurs: shaving cream → dissolving agent (from the sugar-in-hot-water experience), razor → scraping tool. Neither analogy is conscious—they spontaneously and unconsciously complete the understanding of the unfamiliar situation.

Anecdote 2
Jeanette's Titmice—Mice in the Trees?
Jeanette receives a message from her neighbor: "This morning I was delighted to see a flock of titmice feasting on insects in the tulip tree." Jeanette is utterly baffled. Her mind fills with images of small rodents scampering among branches—something she's never seen. Reading on makes it even more bewildering: how strange that mice and small birds coexist peacefully on branches! Then it strikes her: titmice are not rodents but tiny birds, like chickadees. Jeanette's naive analogy maps the unfamiliar English word "titmice" to the most familiar category "mice"—tit suggesting small size, mice suggesting rodents. The analogy is so natural and unnoticed that it takes several minutes to realize she's been fooled.

Anecdote 3
Professor Alexander's "Email Address"—An Octogenarian's Naive Analogy
Professor Alexander is bidding farewell to a young colleague heading to Germany for a month. The elderly professor says, "Once you're there, send me your email address, okay?" Seeing his colleague's puzzled expression, the professor slaps his forehead: "What am I saying? Your email address doesn't change at all." Here, the professor naively maps "email address" to "physical address"—in his most familiar category system, "address" means a physical location, and physical locations change when people move. This naive analogy is so deeply ingrained that it springs from his mouth without any conscious thought. An octogenarian and a four-year-old—identical cognitive phenomena.
The Age-Spanning Truth

The age gap between Tim (4) and Jeanette (ca. 30) is roughly the same as between Jeanette and Professor Alexander (ca. 80). Yet despite these enormous age differences, the cognitive phenomenon experienced by child, young adult, and elder is the same—all are tempted by analogy, all fall for it. Naive analogy is a fundamental cognitive mechanism that spans the entire human life.

04
Deep Mechanism
The Essence of Naive Analogy—Spontaneous, Unconscious, Default Cognition
Naive analogy means that an unfamiliar concept (shaving cream, titmice, email address) is linked to existing knowledge through a seemingly natural analogy, making the unfamiliar concept plausible—though inaccurate. This analogical process is spontaneous, unconscious, and automatic. You don't think of yourself as making an analogy. This contrasts sharply with the common understanding of analogy: a standard analogy involves constructing mappings between situations through deliberate thought. Naive analogy points directly to a conclusion without considering alternatives, producing no uncertainty or doubt. In cognitive psychology literature, this phenomenon carries various labels—preconceptions, natural reasoning, naive reasoning, naive theories, naive conceptions, habitual models, conceptual metaphors, misconceptions, alternative conceptions—but their common core is naive analogy.

Book Connection
Naive Analogy Between Chapters 5 and 6—The Spontaneous vs. the Deliberate
Chapter 5 discussed "category blindness"—unwittingly allowing inappropriate categories to influence cognition. Chapter 7's naive analogies are the microcosmic version of Chapter 5's phenomenon: the protagonists in all three anecdotes fall for analogy's temptation, making errors without awareness. Chapter 6 showed how people actively deploy caricature analogies to share intense feelings. Naive analogy sits between Chapter 5 (being manipulated by analogy) and Chapter 6 (actively manipulating analogy): it is neither "manipulation" (nobody is actively misleading) nor "active creation" (no deliberate crafting), but rather the default state of human cognition—the brain automatically mapping novel things to the most familiar categories in the absence of expertise. Naive analogy is the foundation layer of cognition, the ground upon which all more complex analogical operations are built.

🔑 Core Conclusion
Naive analogy is the default mode of human cognition. Across an enormous age span from four to eighty, three individuals experience the same cognitive phenomenon—all tempted by analogy, all err without awareness. Naive analogy forms a sharp contrast with standard analogy: the former is spontaneous, unconscious, and automatic, pointing directly to a conclusion without producing any doubt; the latter involves deliberate thought and fine-grained mapping between situations. The universality of naive analogy reveals a profound truth: most of the time, humans are not "doing" analogy—analogy is "doing" us. This is key to understanding the book's central thesis that "categorization is analogy-making": the brain's most basic cognitive operation is naive analogy, and all more complex forms of thinking are built upon this foundation.
