Wenhua Kulv · Ch.21 "Ba Jin's Century" — Infographic

Ba Jin's gaze surveyed a century; a century's gaze surveyed Ba Jin—he lived longer than any other first-rate writer, ancient or modern, Chinese or foreign

Ba Jin (1904–2005), lived exactly one hundred years—longer than any other first-rate writer in world history. But more importantly, his gaze surveyed an entire century. In his first half, he used fiction to participate in 'anti-feudalism' and 'humanism,' building a bridge between depth and popularity. In his second half, he used nonfiction to cry out 'denounce the stick' and 'tell the truth'—leading alone, shaking the land. On May 9, 1962, he foretold the 'stick' essence of the Cultural Revolution. In his later years, Random Thoughts and 'telling the truth' became the most important three-character cultural maxim of late twentieth-century China. Through a lifetime of melancholy, he recited Dante's Divine Comedy in Italian with a Sichuan accent, guarding an intellectual's dignity in the darkest hours.

01
Century Writer
Among all writers past and present, Ba Jin stands first
Yu Qiuyu listed the lifespans of first-rate writers: Cao Xueqin and Liu Zongyuan in their 40s; Sima Qian and Han Yu in their 50s; Qu Yuan, Li Bai, Su Shi in their 60s; Pu Songling and Li Qingzhao in their 70s; Lu You in his 80s. In the West: Dante, Shakespeare, Balzac in their 50s; Hemingway, Zola in their 60s; Kawabata, Sartre in their 70s; Goethe, Hugo, Tolstoy in their 80s; Shaw in his 90s. And Ba Jin—a full century. “If Sima Qian could have witnessed the Han collapse, Cao Xueqin the 1911 Revolution, Lu Xun the Cultural Revolution—what immense intellectual collisions would have occurred!”
02
Gentle Gaze
Not Lu Xun, not Hu Shi, not a revolutionary—Ba Jin was a gentle humanist
Ba Jin's gaze was the gentlest legacy of the May Fourth New Culture Movement. What made him unique: he was no revolutionary (despite youthful anarchist fascination, his essence was humanism); he was not Lu Xun (no 'daggers and spears', just sincere confession in a low tone); he was no Westernized intellectual like Hu Shi or Lin Yutang (no worship of the foreign, purely indigenous). Precisely because of this, Ba Jin built a bridge between 'depth' and 'popularity'—turning May Fourth enlightenment ideals into popular appeal through family conflicts and fated struggles, without vulgarization or compromise.
03
Denouncing the Stick
May 9, 1962: a speech that foretold the Cultural Revolution
In his speech at the Second Congress of Shanghai Literary Artists, Ba Jin precisely described seven characteristics of 'the stick': ① a special force that makes writers tremble and lose ambition; ② a five-step method—create a frame→trap people→grab pigtails→put on hats→beat with sticks; ③ dormant, hidden, ambiguous—'normally you never see them'; ④ sharp-nosed and quick-striking; ⑤ few in number but massive in noise—'a wolf pack capable of anything'; ⑥ driven by pure envious destruction; ⑦ they display 'rage', 'raised arms', 'fierce beating'—appearing to represent justice. After the speech, Associated Press broadcast it from Hong Kong; Yao Wenyuan and Zhang Chunqiao labeled Ba Jin 'the one providing ammunition for imperialist attacks on China.'

04
Two Lives
First half: Anti-feudalism · Humanism / Second half: Anti-stick · Truth-telling
Ba Jin's life can be summarized in twelve characters: first half—'Anti-feudalism' and 'Humanism' through fiction, joining a broad tide; second half—'Denouncing the stick' and 'Truth-telling' through nonfiction, leading alone, shaking the land. During the Cultural Revolution, Ba Jin suffered deeply; his wife Xiao Shan died of illness. He walked home daily from the Writers' Association, finding only denunciation articles in his mailbox. After the disaster, Ba Jin issued three appeals: build a 'Cultural Revolution museum', write Random Thoughts for self-reflection, and advocate 'telling the truth'. Yu Qiuyu commented: 'In our lives, how many "truths" have we heard, and how many true words? I would rather have none of the "truth" that opposes true words!'

05
A Century of Melancholy
Dante in Italian, recited in Sichuan-accented Chinese—melancholy was his lifelong spiritual tone
Ba Jin maintained melancholy throughout his life. From writing Family in youth, to discovering 'the stick' in middle age, to advocating truth-telling in old age—melancholy accompanied every step. Bing Xin once asked: 'Brother Ba Jin, why so melancholy?' She only understood much later that Ba Jin was enjoying life through melancholy. His melancholy revealed social scrutiny, distance from crowds, grief over the gap between ideal and reality, interrogation of human nature. 'His heart was always warm; but his eyes were always cool, disappointed. He was naive yet never deceived; he was shrewd yet never conspired. Thus he never won, never lost—only melancholy remained.' At 100, bedridden, Ba Jin was attacked as a 'turncoat' and 'hypocrite'—'No gangster anywhere would attack a hundred-year-old man. How can today's Chinese cultural media do this?'
