Su Shi: A Life · Ch.4
"Only Compassion for Farmers Remains"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer From paradise Hangzhou to barren Mizhou, in the desperate grip of locusts and drought, Su Shi led disaster relief with "compassion for farmers," embodying the Confucian scholar-official's noblest people-first spirit. When a disillusioned literatus was transferred to a desolate land ravaged by disaster, how did he choose between personal despair and the people's survival?

In winter 1074, Su Shi arrived in Mizhou. He set aside personal sorrow and led locust eradication and drought relief with "shrewd competence and diligent dedication." Confronted with corpses strewn everywhere and abandoned children filling the city, he "picked up abandoned children through the city in tears," establishing a grain-subsidy adoption system that saved thousands. He proved through action: the greatest poetry is not always written on paper — it can be written on the land.

01
Stark Contrast
From Paradise to Wasteland: Mizhou's Harsh Reality
From warm, prosperous Hangzhou to remote, desolate Mizhou — "cold winds howling, a bleak landscape." Su Shi's mood was somber. Yet entering Mizhou, a strange sight caught his eye: though it was the farming off-season, men, women, young and old worked in groups everywhere, turning soil deep and scraping up grass — for over 200 li along the road. Su Shi immediately realized: this year's locust invasion would be ferocious. In that moment, "a heavy burden settled on his shoulders, and personal sorrows were temporarily set aside."

02
Locust Eradication
Leading from the Front: "My servants calloused, my horses exhausted"
Su Shi immediately investigated: farmers had already reported 30,000 hu of captured locusts! Yet local officials were "indifferent," some even flattering superiors by claiming "locusts flying in can weed for the people." Su Shi sharply refuted this, devising a meticulous plan: before spring warmth, mobilize the masses to burn and bury larvae, allocating grain as rewards. He himself "busied from dawn to dusk in the fields, inspecting and supervising" — "My servants' hands grew calloused, my horses collapsed in exhaustion."

03
Humanitarian Light
"Picking Up Abandoned Children in Tears": Saving Thousands
Years of locusts and drought left famine victims everywhere — "corpses strewn across the wilds, abandoned children visible at every turn." With a heavy heart, Su Shi "picked up abandoned children through the city in tears," and after much struggle, allocated hundreds of dan of grain specifically for their care. He posted official notices: families willing to adopt would receive six dou of grain monthly. Over time, "adopters developed deep bonds with their children — no matter how hard life became, they could not bear to abandon them."

04
Justice & Mercy
Benevolent Governance: "Risking Death as Bandits" Is "Only Natural"
In the great famine, bandits swarmed. But Su Shi understood the deeper truth: those who "risk death as bandits" might die; those who "fear the law and do not steal" might also starve. Many "turning to banditry together" was only natural. He formulated a thorough bandit-suppression plan while fundamentally practicing benevolent governance — reporting the disaster truthfully to the court and calling for tax relief. For Su Shi, the root of public order lay in letting the people survive.

05
Poetic Compassion
"Elders Ask After Me": Official and People as One Family
In his two Mizhou years, Su Shi "longed for timely snow and rain every moment, just like a farmer": winter snow falling, he thought "buried locusts should reach a thousand chi deep, overwintering wheat may cloud the hills for some households"; spring rain clearing, he regretted "heaven's intent truly hard to fathom, now it makes a brilliant spring clear again." He often consulted old farmers and learned that Mount Chang prayers were most effective. At the crisis's peak, he bathed, burned incense, and observed a vegetarian fast to pray earnestly at Mount Chang.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The two Mizhou years marked Su Shi's transformation from "literary talent" to "people-first official." Plunged from paradise Hangzhou into a locust-ravaged wasteland, he did not wallow in personal hardship but poured all his energy into disaster relief: burning and burying locusts, fasting to pray for rain, picking up abandoned children in tears, establishing a grain-subsidy adoption system. He proved through action: a truly great scholar-official writes immortal poetry not only on paper, but also on the land — compassion for the people is the highest poem of the Confucian spirit.
