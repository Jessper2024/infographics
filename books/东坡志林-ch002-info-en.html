Dongpo's Miscellany · Chapter 2「Self-Cultivation and Reflections on Destiny」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Su Shi was plagued by illness and hardship throughout his life — eye disease, malaria, political storms, and relentless slander. Yet his insights into self-cultivation and fate were remarkably ahead of his time. From breathing exercises to dietary discipline, from meditative dreams to scholarly practice, he wove care for body and soul into a seamless whole. How could a mere scholar forge an indestructible heart amid suffering? The answer: he treated cultivation, learning, and destiny as one — nourishing the body is nourishing the mind, and nourishing the mind is accepting fate.

This chapter gathers Su Shi's writings on self-cultivation (\"On Nourishing Life,\" \"On Abstaining from Grain,\" \"Letter to Ziyou on Cultivation\"), illness (\"Zizhan Suffers from Conjunctivitis,\" \"Treating Eyes and Teeth\"), dreams (\"Dreaming of Canliao's Tea Poem,\" \"Dreaming of Ziyou's Pagoda\"), scholarship (\"Recording Ouyang Xiu's Words\"), and fate (\"Han Yu's Life of Slander,\" \"Life Has a Fixed Portion\"). Su Shi fused Confucian, Daoist, and Buddhist cultivation into one: Daoist breathing exercises, Buddhist meditation, Confucian self-discipline — practiced in daily life, tested in exile. He did not theorize about health from a study; he enacted it with his body at life's most difficult moments.

01
Body & Soul
"Eat only when hungry, stop before full" — Dongpo's Operating System for Life
Su Shi's \"On Nourishing Life\" constructed a complete daily cultivation system: eat only when hungry, stop before full; walk after meals until the stomach empties; then enter a room — day or night, sitting or lying — and make the body still like a wooden puppet. Add breath-counting meditation: \"Counting to several hundred, the mind becomes silent, the body immovable, equal to the void.\" Advanced practitioners use \"following the breath\": let consciousness follow the breath in and out — \"from eighty-four thousand pores, like clouds steaming away, all illnesses naturally depart.\" This is not mysticism but a fully operational mind-body management system.

02
Hardest of All
"The difficulty of cultivation lies in eliminating desire" — Su Shi's Sober Recognition of Human Weakness
Su Shi frankly observed: Su Wu, imprisoned by the Xiongnu, ate snow and gnawed felt without a single word of surrender. When life itself was at stake, what cultivation could there be? Yet when peace returned, Su Wu's later years were tarnished by lust. The conclusion is brutal and true: \"I then understood that captivating things can move a person — not only beauties like Wu Shu and Xi Shi, but even a single woman can bring down a state.\" The difficulty of self-cultivation is not knowing enough methods, but truly eliminating desire — especially in times of comfort, not hardship.
⚠️

Su Wu's Lesson: Easy in Hardship, Hard in Comfort

Imprisoned, he ate snow without surrender. Returned to honor, he fell to lust. Su Shi's warning: cultivating oneself is hardest when life is easy.

🪷

Nourishing the Yellow Center

A Daoist concept: keep the heart like an unopened lotus bud, preserving inner vitality. Rainwater and well water are \"good medicine\" — the simplest things hold the deepest truths.

03
Daily Practice
Dongpo's \"Three Nourishments\" — Turning Philosophy into Daily Habits
In \"Recording the Three Nourishments,\" Su Shi proposed a minimalist system of daily self-discipline: first, accept your lot to nourish fortune — accept fate's arrangements without resentment or grasping; second, relax the stomach to nourish vital energy — restrain eating and drinking; third, cut expenses to nourish wealth — live frugally. These three seemingly plain principles fuse Confucian contentment with poverty, Daoist conservation of vital energy, and practical financial wisdom. He even wrote: use rainwater to brew tea and prepare medicine — both delicious and beneficial; consume it without interruption and you will prolong life.

04
Dreams & Fate
Scorpio-born: Su Shi's Self-Understanding Through Astrology
Su Shi repeatedly recorded his dreams: discussing tea poems with the monk Canliao, composing verse at Emperor Minghuang's command, dreaming of his brother's pagoda dream... These were not absurd reveries but projections of his spiritual world — even in dreams he composed poetry, debated scholarship, and pursued enlightenment. In his writings on fate, he quoted Han Yu: \"The hour of my birth, the moon lodged in the Southern Dipper\" — both were Scorpio-born, both faced a lifetime of slander and praise. But he met this with humor rather than resentment: fellow sufferers share a bond. \"Life has a fixed portion — even a full meal is as hard to obtain as fame and wealth\" — this sentence contains no bitterness, only hard-won acceptance.
\"Han Yu's poem says: 'The hour of my birth, the moon lodged in the Southern Dipper.' Thus I know Han Yu had Scorpio as his body-palace, while I have Scorpio as my fate-palace. A lifetime of slander and praise — we likely share the same affliction.\"
— \"Han Yu's Life of Slander and Praise\"
05
True Learning
Ouyang Xiu's Writing Secret: \"Just read diligently and write more — mastery comes naturally\"
\"Recording Ouyang Xiu's Words\" preserves the advice given to Sun Jue: \"There is no other technique — only diligent reading and abundant writing; mastery comes naturally. People suffer from writing too little and reading too lazily. Every piece they produce, they expect to surpass others — few ever arrive. Flaws need not await others' criticism; write more and you will see them yourself.\" This is the source of Su Shi's scholarly spirit. Throughout his life he never stopped writing — even during exile, his output was staggering. The so-called genius is simply someone who \"read more and wrote even more.\"
📖

Diligent Reading: The Foundation

Even in exile, \"his manuscripts of the Documents, Changes, and Analects were always with him.\" His learning came not from innate genius but accumulated effort.

✍️

Abundant Writing: Self-Correction

\"Flaws need not await others' criticism\" — through massive practice, one self-corrects. This is the fundamental method behind Su Shi's literary greatness.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Su Shi's cultivation was not passive life-preservation but an active philosophy of living. He treated body, mind, and destiny as an indivisible whole: dietary discipline respects the body, breath meditation trains the mind, accepting a Scorpio fate is wisdom about destiny. \"The difficulty lies in eliminating desire\" is his soberest insight into human nature; \"Life has a fixed portion\" is his calmest acceptance of fate. And Ouyang Xiu's teaching — \"just read diligently and write more\" — ran through Su Shi's entire creative life: no shortcuts, only daily practice. This four-in-one system of body, mind, fate, and learning is Su Shi's most precious guide to living well.
