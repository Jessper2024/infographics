Su Shi Biography · Ch.7
"Three Odes to the Red Cliff Became Ultimate Masterpieces"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer 1082, the third year in Huangzhou — "Hell and Heaven are already equal to me." A brand-new self emerged from suffering. Tilling the Eastern Slope, gathering at Snow Hall, chanting at Red Cliff — the most brilliant chapter in Chinese literary history was born. With external circumstances unchanged, how did Su Shi complete his metamorphosis from criminal official to immortal literary giant?

After Snow Hall was built, Su Shi tilled by day and read by night, mingling with fishermen and woodcutters, drinking and climbing walls with friends — completely shedding the burden of reputation. On March 7, caught in rain, he wrote "Calming Wind and Waves." On July 16, drifting beneath Red Cliff, came the First Rhapsody. On October 15, revisiting Red Cliff, came the Second Rhapsody, along with "Charm of a Maiden Singer: Reminiscing at Red Cliff." Transcendent equanimity had become his dominant character; thought and art had been elevated to a state of supreme beauty.

01
Identity Shift
Tilling Dongpo: From Scholar-Official to Farmer
Su Shi spent most of his time on the Eastern Slope — tilling by day, reading and writing at Snow Hall by night. A wine flask always at his side, he would drink a few sips when tired, then sprawl on the grass, head pillowed on a broken brick. "Mornings playing on Yellow Mud's white clouds, evenings lodging in Snow Hall's green mist" — he mingled with fishermen and woodcutters like an ordinary man. He left at dawn and returned deep into the night; the city gate guards came to know him well. "Since ancient times, which worthy has not walked this road?" — is suffering not a necessary path to eternity?

02
Life's Pleasures
Snow Hall Life: Forbidden Liquor, Roast Beef, and Climbing Walls
Snow Hall became a gathering place — they secretly drank contraband homebrew, and a western neighbor slaughtered his ailing ox for a barbecue. Belly full and happily drunk, they staggered through the wilderness singing and strolling, scaling the city wall after the third watch. Another legendary night: after repeatedly getting drunk and sobering up at Snow Hall, Su Shi returned to find his servant snoring. He simply stood by the river leaning on his staff, listening to the waves, and wrote: "Long have I resented that this body is not my own — when shall I forget the hustle? A little boat, from now on I'll drift away, entrusting my remaining years to rivers and seas."

03
Declaration of Character
Caught in Rain: An Unfettered Declaration
On March 7, heading to Sand Lake to inspect farmland, Su Shi was caught in a sudden downpour. While all his companions were flustered, he remained utterly unperturbed — straw sandals, bamboo staff, chanting and strolling slowly. This fleeting storm spawned his eternal declaration: "Pay no heed to the sound of rain beating through the forest — why not chant aloud and stroll at ease? Bamboo staff and straw sandals, lighter than a horse — who's afraid? One straw rain-cloak, a lifetime of mist and rain." When the storm raged, he had the courage of one unmoved by Mount Tai's collapse; when the sunset smiled, he didn't exult — he had transcended all external influences.

04
Immortal Masterpieces
Three Red Cliff Odes: The Pinnacle of Literature
On the night of July 16, Su Shi and guests drifted in a boat beneath Red Cliff — a cool breeze came, waves were still. Amid wine and poetry, one of the supreme works of Chinese literature emerged: the First Red Cliff Rhapsody. Through host-guest dialogue, it moves from lamenting "like mayflies between heaven and earth, a single grain in the vast sea" to finding aesthetic salvation in "the cool breeze on the river and the bright moon among the mountains." The Second Rhapsody depicts a moonlit mountain climb. "Charm of a Maiden Singer" opens with "The Great River flows eastward, its waves washing away a thousand ages of heroes" — the heroic passion of Zhou Yu's Red Cliff and the awakening that "life is but a dream" shine in mutual reflection.

05
Spiritual Elevation
Moonlit Dongpo: Crystal Joy in the Tapping of a Staff
Only in the deep quiet of night would Su Shi walk back to Lingao Pavilion by the pale moonlight — listening all the way to the crisp sound of his staff striking the uneven road, his heart brimming with crystalline joy. Suffering had been fully transmuted into aesthetic experience; resentment and fury had become things of the past. "Rain-washed, Dongpo's moonlight is clear; the city folk are gone, the rustic walks alone. Don't despise this rough and rocky hillside road — I love the tink-tink of my trailing staff." Wasn't that bumpy hillside road the very image of his life's rocky path? Yet what he heard in it was crisp, pleasing music. He had become brighter, warmer, kinder, more tolerant, filled with a mature humor gleaming with wisdom.

🔑 Key Takeaway
In his third Huangzhou year, Su Shi completed the most astonishing transformation in Chinese literati history: from the lonely indignation of "choosing among cold branches, unwilling to perch" to the unfettered equanimity of "one straw rain-cloak, a lifetime of mist and rain," to the cosmic vision of "The Great River flows eastward." The three Red Cliff odes are not merely a literary peak but the construction of an entire philosophical system. Those two moonlit nights — July 16 and October 15 — left an inextinguishable radiance in the sky of Chinese literature. As he said: "Rain-washed, Dongpo's moonlight is clear" — suffering had been cleansed into moonlit beauty.
