The Gay Genius · Ch.23 「Governor of Hangzhou」

In 1089, the 52-year-old poet arrived in Hangzhou — an ancient city of half a million, in ruins. Eighteen months later, he had built China's first public hospital, dredged the canals, rebuilt the water supply, and raised the immortal Su Causeway. How did a poet transform ruins into paradise on earth through sheer action?

When Su Tungpo arrived as governor, the city was in shambles: government buildings had collapsed and killed people, canals were choked with silt, there was not a single public hospital, and residents drank only salty water. For a year and a half he never opened a book. He tackled four major projects simultaneously: water supply, a public hospital, canal dredging, and the West Lake restoration. He judged cases like poetry, settled a debt by painting fans, and proved that governance could combine efficiency with warmth.

01
Public Service
China's First Public Hospital: Anlefang
Su Tungpo allocated 2,000 strings of cash from government funds and donated 50 taels of his own gold to build China's first public hospital, "Anlefang" (Hall of Peace and Joy), in the center of Hangzhou. With a population of 500,000 at the mouth of the Qiantang River, epidemics were common. The hospital treated over a thousand patients in three years. Earlier, in Mizhou, he had copied medical prescriptions in large characters and posted them throughout the city. The hospital later moved to the lakeside, renamed "Anjifang," and continued serving the people long after Su left.

02
Urban Engineering
Canal Dredging & Water Supply Reconstruction
Su designed an ingenious water diversion system: the sand-laden sea tide was directed through the suburban Maoshan Canal, keeping the city's Yanqiao River clear. Meanwhile, West Lake spring water was piped into the city through a newly built ceramic pipe network. He consulted a 70-year-old monk and replaced the rotting bamboo pipes with ceramic ones protected by stone slabs above and below, laying 300 yards of pipeline. Canal dredging began three months into his term and was completed the following April — at a depth of eight feet, "unprecedented."

03
Poetic Justice
The Warmth of a Judge's Brush: Fans for a Debt
Su Tungpo used his judge's brush to create cursive calligraphy, withered trees, and rocks on twenty white silk fans, enabling a young debtor to sell them and pay off his obligation — the wisdom of a scholar proving warmer than cold statutes. A young man whose father had died could not repay a debt due to continuous rain. Su pondered for a moment, then took twenty folding fans and painted them on the spot. Word spread; people scrambled to buy each for a thousand cash. They sold out instantly. He even once changed the address label and wrote a recommendation letter for a scholar falsely using the Su family name — showing humanity beyond the letter of the law.

"Bring me a pile of fans. I'll sell them for you." — Su Tungpo to the indebted young man

04
Engineering Legacy
Su Causeway & West Lake: An Eternal Landmark
Su's restoration of West Lake truly earned Hangzhou its reputation as "paradise on earth." The lake was choked with weeds, its bed raised by silt. He petitioned the court for funds to dredge the lake bottom, using the excavated silt and weeds to build a long causeway (later called "Su Causeway"), planted with peach and willow trees along its length. He also leased portions of the lake to farmers for water chestnut cultivation, preventing weed growth while generating tax revenue for maintenance — a perfect marriage of human design and nature.

05
Spirit of Action
A Year and a Half Without Opening a Book
Su Tungpo became a completely different person in Hangzhou — "he did not open a book for a year and a half." He was not a bureaucrat sitting in his office judging cases; he personally supervised construction sites. He knew to consult an old monk for technical details, to design ceramic pipes to replace bamboo ones, to use dredged silt to build a causeway. As Lin Yutang observed: he proved that a poet could simultaneously be an engineer, a doctor, a judge, and an urban planner. This is the complete Su Tungpo — the unity of thought and action.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Eighteen months, one public hospital, one water supply system, one dredged canal, one immortal Su Causeway. Su Tungpo proved that a poet can also be a man of action — discovering problems with an artist's sensitivity, solving them with a doer's decisiveness. The highest art of governance is not power but making a city more beautiful because you were there. A thousand years later, what people see in Hangzhou is not just West Lake, but the eternal footprints of a poet.
