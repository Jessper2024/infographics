Ming Dynasty Those Stories · Chapter 19 "Purging All Corruption"

Zhu Yuanzhang hated corruption to his core, issuing the harshest anti-graft law in history — flaying officials, allowing commoners to arrest corrupt magistrates, and creating an era where being a government official was the most dangerous profession.

📖 Chapter Overview · SCQA

Situation (S): In 1369 (Hongwu Year 2), Zhu Yuanzhang emotionally told his ministers about witnessing corrupt officials ignoring people's suffering during his peasant days, vowing to strictly prohibit corruption by law. The young Ming Dynasty needed to curb graft urgently.

Complication (C): Zhu prescribed only five legal punishments, yet ignored his own rules — flaying, slow slicing, intestine-pulling, scalding with hot water. Official salaries were pitifully low, corruption persisted despite mass executions, and officials fell like dominoes.

Question (Q): Why did corruption persist despite Zhu's ruthless purge? Between starvation salaries and sadistic punishments, how could the empire break the cycle of graft?

Answer (A): Zhu over-relied on draconian punishment, neglecting systemic reform. Low salaries, brutal holiday policies, and the vast gap between imperial relatives' wealth and officials' poverty were the root causes. Execution alone could never stop corruption.

01
Anti-Corruption Resolve — From Parents' Starvation to Flaying Officials Alive

Zhu Yuanzhang's burning hatred of corrupt officials traced back to his parents' death — starvation caused by corrupt magistrates diverting disaster relief grain. He would use the most brutal methods imaginable to create a corruption-free dynasty.

In 1369, Zhu emotionally addressed his court: "When I was a commoner, I saw corrupt officials completely ignoring people's suffering, and I hated them from the bottom of my heart. Henceforth I will strictly prohibit it by law, and will show no mercy to any official who dares harm the people!" He issued the harshest anti-graft decree in Chinese history: embezzle 60 taels of silver or above, and be executed on the spot!

To amplify the terror, he created a chilling new institution: a "Earth God Shrine" between the main gate and inner gate of every county office, used exclusively for flaying corrupt officials alive, stuffing the skin with straw, and displaying the effigy beside the magistrate's seat — a warning to all successors.

Root of the Hatred

Parents starved due to corrupt officials diverting disaster relief grain. This childhood trauma became a lifelong obsession with punishing graft — at any cost.

60 Taels of Silver

At dynasty founding, 60 taels was not a large sum. Zhu used this threshold to signal zero tolerance — better to execute an innocent than miss a guilty party.

02
The Zhu Liangzuo Case — The First Minister Beaten to Death in Court

Founding general Zhu Liangzuo sheltered local tyrants and had honest county magistrate Dao Tong assassinated on Zhu Yuanzhang's orders. When the truth emerged, Zhu personally flogged Zhu Liangzuo to death in the imperial court — the first minister executed in open court in Ming history.

Dao Tong was an incorruptible magistrate who enforced law strictly, earning the hatred of local gentry. They bribed Zhu Liangzuo, who repeatedly interfered with Dao's enforcement and even deployed troops to surround the county office to rescue captured bullies. Zhu Liangzuo then memorialized Dao Tong with trumped-up charges. Zhu, relying on Zhu Liangzuo's faster military horse, ordered Dao Tong's execution before the honest magistrate's own appeal arrived.

When Dao Tong's appeal arrived and Zhu discovered the injustice, it was too late. Dao Tong died courageously, his body carried through crowds of weeping citizens. Zhu was furious — he personally flogged Zhu Liangzuo and his son Zhu Xia to death in the imperial audience hall, establishing a precedent that would echo throughout the entire Ming Dynasty.

03
Flaying and Stuffing — The Earth God Shrine Terror

Zhu Yuanzhang added an Earth God Shrine between the main and inner gates of every county office, reserved exclusively for flaying corrupt officials alive and displaying their straw-stuffed skins. This made Ming officials tremble with fear — China's legal history knows no parallel.

Beyond flaying, Zhu created an arsenal of terror: slow slicing (lingchi, record: 3,000+ cuts, victim still alive), intestine-pulling, scalding with boiling water then brushing with iron, hanging with iron hooks to air-dry, castration, knee-cap removal. Officials continued to corrupt, rushing to the execution instruments like suicide bombers.

Most revolutionary: Zhu decreed that any commoner who discovered a corrupt official could bind and deliver them to the capital. All checkpoints were required to pass them through, and anyone obstructing would be executed with nine clans exterminated. Unprecedented in Chinese legal history, though rarely implemented in practice.

04
Jianjiao and the Embroidered Uniform Guard — The Omnipresent Spy Network

Zhu Yuanzhang built a vast spy network — the Jianjiao (spies) and later the Embroidered Uniform Guard (Jinyiwei). These agents penetrated everywhere, even into officials' private homes, providing the most efficient and terrifying anti-corruption intelligence source in history.

When National Academy rector Song Nei was angry at home one night, Zhu asked him the next morning why he was upset, and produced a portrait — Song Nei's angry expression from the night before. In the era before photography, someone had been in Song Nei's home at midnight, watching and sketching him. The stuff of horror films.

Jianjiao agents came from the lowest social strata, did not know each other, and reported only to Zhu. Some were even his old acquaintances — monks. An official who took a bribe today might find anti-corruption officers at his door by morning. Zhu got out of bed at midnight for urgent intelligence. Anti-corruption efficiency was unprecedented.

05
Falling One After Another — Institutional Failure and Officials' Despair

Zhu killed and killed, but officials kept corrupting like suicide bombers. By 1386 (Hongwu Year 19), officials across all 13 provinces rarely completed their full terms. Going to court was like attending a funeral; coming home alive was a cause for celebration.

Statistics tell a grotesque story: 364 new officials appointed in one year. After one year, 6 were executed, and 358 were "serving while wearing death or exile charges" — condemned officials continued doing office work, even sentencing fellow prisoners, before returning to face their own sentences. Officials had become so scarce that Zhu created this world-class absurdity.

The officials' ultimate strategy was feigned insanity — dementia, wild hair, calling everyone "father," greeting people with kitchen knives. Success story: Censor Yuan Kai, when Zhu forced him to take sides between the emperor and crown prince, feigned madness so convincingly that he ate a dog-dung-like substance (actually flour and sauce dough) to fool Zhu's investigators. Corruption continued, but at least some survived to tell the tale.

💡 Core Conclusion

Zhu Yuanzhang's anti-corruption campaign was launched from an elevated motive — his parents' tragic death at the hands of corrupt magistrates burned into his soul. His methods were of unprecedented cruelty — flaying, slow slicing, nine-clan extermination. Yet the results were bitterly ironic: corruption persisted, officials fell one after another. The root cause lay in systemic failures: salaries too low to support families, holiday policies so brutal they crushed all morale, and the vast wealth gap between imperial relatives (all first-rank from birth) and scholar-officials (lifelong struggle for third or fourth rank). Zhu over-relied on the power of punishment, utterly neglecting institutional reform. Cruelty unprecedented in scale; anti-corruption failure unprecedented in scope. This pair of contradictions forms the deepest lesson of Hongwu-era governance for all posterity.
