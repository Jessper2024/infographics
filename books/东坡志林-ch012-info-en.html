Dongpo Zhilin · Ch.12「The Abyss of Power—Political Wisdom from Fan Zeng to An Lushan」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer The hardest thing in the arena of power is not doing things—it is making judgments. When should Fan Zeng have left Xiang Yu? Was Qin Shihuang's employment of Zhao Gao arrogant folly or irresistible fate? When a ruler dies and no heir has been born, who should rightfully govern? Li Si's one wrong decision, faced with Zhao Gao's conspiracy, determined the Qin dynasty's fate. Duke Yin, refusing to execute the conspirator Gongzi Hui, was instead killed by him. Zheng Xiaotong was poisoned for saying he "didn't see" a secret memorial. To kill or not to kill, to trust or not to trust, to advance or retreat—every choice for a political actor is a walk on a tightrope. Su Shi travels across millennia, searching for "correct judgment" in history's narrow crevices. He does not deliver moral answers—he provides a framework for causal analysis. Who should have been killed, who should not? Which decisions were "taking medicine before falling ill"? Ultimately, he gives us a maxim worthy of inscription on every statesman's desk: "Governing the world is like nurturing health; worrying about the state is like taking medicine"—wrongly timed medication is more deadly than the disease itself.

This chapter collects the final six essays of Su Shi's "On Antiquity" section: On Fan Zeng, The Disaster of Unemployed Strategists, Zhao Gao and Li Si, The Regent, Duke Yin's Misfortune, and Seven Virtues & Eight Warnings. From Fan Zeng's timing, to the role of the strategist class in national stability, from the Zhao Gao-Li Si conspiracy to the history of regency ritual, from Duke Yin's fatal hesitation to the "health cultivation" metaphor of governance—Su Shi completes a comprehensive system of political judgment.

01
The Adviser's Exit
The Tragedy of Fan Zeng—When Is the Right Time to Leave?
Fan Zeng, suspected by Xiang Yu, resigned and died of an ulcer on his back before reaching Pengcheng. Su Shi's evaluation is rich with tension—"Fan Zeng's departure was good. Had he not left, Xiang Yu would have killed him. I only regret it was not sooner." But when should he have left? At the Hongmen Banquet, when Xiang Yu refused to kill Liu Bang? "No"—Fan Zeng wanting Liu Bang dead was ministerial duty; Xiang Yu's refusal was a ruler's magnanimity. The true dividing line was when Xiang Yu executed Song Yi (the "Qingzi Champion"). "Xiang Yu's killing Song Yi was the omen of killing the Righteous Emperor. Killing the Righteous Emperor was the true origin of his suspicion of Fan Zeng." The Righteous Emperor had been Fan Zeng's creation—by killing him, Xiang Yu declared fundamental distrust. Su Shi's classic formulation: "A thing must first rot within before worms grow; a man must first harbor suspicion before slander enters." Chen Ping's stratagem succeeded only because Xiang Yu already doubted Fan Zeng. Aged seventy, "if you can work together, stay; if not, leave"—failing to decide at the critical moment, hoping to achieve fame through Xiang Yu—"shallow." Yet the final tribute: "Fan Zeng was feared by Emperor Gaozu. Had Fan Zeng not left, Xiang Yu would not have fallen. Alas—Fan Zeng too was a hero among men!"

02
Institutional History
Strategists as Social Safety Valve & The Regency Ritual—Elite Management and Power Transition
In "The Disaster of Unemployed Strategists," Su Shi offers a penetrating historical thesis: during the Spring and Autumn and Warring States periods, lords competed to retain strategists—some with three thousand retainers. The six kingdoms oppressed their people "no less than Qin's First and Second Emperors," yet not one commoner rebelled—because society's "outstanding ones" (the four types of elite: the wise, the brave, the disputatious, the strong) were all maintained as retainers, not unemployed. When Qin Shihuang unified the realm, considering retainers useless, he "razed famous cities, slaughtered heroes, dispersing the realm's outstanding talents back to the fields." It was like releasing millions of tigers and wolves into the mountains, hungry and thirsty. Qin's swift fall was caused by the total unemployment of the elite class. The conclusion: elite placement is the key to long-term stability. In "The Regent," Su Shi debates Ouyang Xiu on Duke Yin's legitimacy as regent. Ouyang argued the Spring and Autumn Annals titling Yin as "Duke" proves he wasn't a regent. Su Shi, citing Confucius' own words on ritual, proves the regency system was "the canonical code of former kings, the lawful words of Confucius"—when the rightful heir is unborn or too young, a brother temporarily governs; if the child is a girl, the regent ascends; if a boy, the regent steps down. Su Shi criticizes Qin and Han for abolishing this system in favor of empress-dowager regency: "When the hen crows the dawn, the household faces ruin"—leading to the disasters of Empress Lü, Empress Wu, Wang Mang, and Yang Jian, who nearly changed dynasties.

03
Conspiracy of Power
Zhao Gao and Li Si—How One Wrong Decision Destroyed an Empire
The longest essay in "On Antiquity." Su Shi begins with Qin Shihuang's institutional design: Meng Tian commanding 300,000 troops "awing the north," Fusu as overseer, Meng Yi as strategic adviser—"what great villain would dare cast a sidelong glance?" Yet Shihuang fell ill on tour and sent Meng Yi away to pray at mountains—"thus Zhao Gao and Li Si could execute their plot." Su Shi pinpoints the root cause: "Shihuang's path to chaos was employing Zhao Gao." He boldly exposes a pattern: peerless rulers like Shihuang and Emperor Xuan of Han both fell to eunuch disasters—"they thought themselves brilliant heroes; what could castrated slaves accomplish? Yet when their states fell and courts descended into chaos, they were no different from mediocre rulers." The deeper analysis traces the institutional poison left by Shang Yang's reforms: "execution treated as light punishment, extermination of three clans as routine law"—Qin's laws were so harsh that ministers "considered dying a stroke of luck," and even pleading for the crown prince required courage they no longer had. Fusu and Meng Tian, receiving a forged edict ordering suicide, obeyed instantly—not from stupidity, but from decades of conditioning by absolute terror: "Knowing Shihuang's ferocity was irreversible, how could they suspect forgery?" Su Shi prescribes Confucian "reciprocity" (shu) as the antidote: "With loyalty and reciprocity in the heart and fairness in governance, those above are easily understood and those below easily reached." And the warning spans millennia: "Those who poison the world with law—never fail to have it rebound upon themselves and their descendants."
With loyalty and reciprocity in the heart and fairness in governance, those above are easily understood and those below easily reached. Even traitors would find no gap to exploit.
—Su Shi · Confucian "reciprocity" as the only antidote to autocratic poison
04
Crisis Judgment
Duke Yin's Misfortune—Five Lives, Five Lessons
Duke Yin of Lu elicits Su Shi's deepest lament: "Duke Yin's wisdom was less than a passerby on the road—alas!" Gongzi Hui requested permission to kill Duke Huan and become Grand Steward. Duke Yin not only refused to punish Hui but said "I was going to yield the throne anyway, I will retire"—Hui, fearing future retribution, went to Duke Huan and slandered Yin, then assassinated him. Su Shi's judgment is both pained and rational: "Had Duke Yin executed Hui and then yielded the throne, even Boyi and Shuqi could not surpass him." From this case, Su Shi connects five historical figures' fates: Li Ke executed by Duke Hui, Li Si dismembered in the marketplace, Zheng Xiaotong poisoned by Sima Shi (for saying he "didn't see" a secret memorial—Sima Shi's principle: "Better I wrong you than you wrong me"), and Wang Yunzhi who survived by pretending to be drunk-vomiting after overhearing Wang Dun's conspiracy. "Traitors and rebels are like vipers—even the grass and trees they bite can kill a man, let alone their actual victims!"

05
The Philosophy of Governance
Seven Virtues & Eight Warnings—"Taking Medicine Before Illness" as Ultimate Political Wisdom
This may be the most theoretically elevated essay in Dongpo Zhilin. Su Shi opens with Guan Zhong's supreme virtue—persuading Duke Huan of Qi to reject Prince Hua of Zheng's treacherous request while honoring the Cao Mo covenant—then presents seven "virtuous acts that can be eternal law" and eight "acts that should be eternal warnings." The Seven Virtues: Duke Huan of Qi not killing Tian Jingzhong despite divination, King Cheng of Chu not killing Chong'er, Emperor Gaozu of Han not killing Wu Wang Bi, Emperor Wu of Jin not killing Liu Yuanhai, Fu Jian not killing Murong Chui, Emperor Ming of Tang not killing An Lushan. The world says these seven "erred in not killing, thereby planting chaos." Su Shi offers a completely opposite causal analysis: "These seven all had their own causes of downfall—it was not the fault of not killing." If Duke Jing of Qi had not imposed heavy punishments and taxes, the Tian clan could not have seized Qi. If Emperor Jing of Han had not harmed the Wu crown prince or employed Chao Cuo, there would have been no Seven Kingdoms' Rebellion. If Emperor Wu of Jin had not installed the idiot Emperor Hui, Liu Yuanhai could not have caused chaos. If Emperor Ming of Tang had not employed Li Linfu and Yang Guozhong, what could An Lushan have done? The Eight Warnings: Emperor Jing killing Zhou Yafu, Cao Cao killing Kong Rong, Emperor Wen of Jin killing Ji Kang, Emperor Jing of Jin killing Xiahou Xuan, Emperor Ming of Song killing Wang Yu, the Last Ruler of Qi killing Hulü Guang, Emperor Taizong of Tang killing Li Junxian, Empress Wu killing Pei Yan—all because of "worrying about the state, preparing against chaos." Su Shi's master metaphor unifies everything: "Governing the world is like nurturing health; worrying about the state and preparing against chaos is like taking medicine. Nurturing health is merely being careful with diet and rest, moderating sensual pleasures—caution before illness, medicine after illness. Now, fearing a cold, one first takes monkshood poison; fearing a fever, one first takes kansui root—the illness hasn't arrived, but the medicine has already killed." Those eight were all "taking medicine before they were sick."
Governing the world is like nurturing health; worrying about the state is like taking medicine... the illness hasn't arrived, but the medicine has already killed. Those eight were all taking medicine before they were sick.
—Su Shi · The culminating metaphor of Dongpo Zhilin's "On Antiquity" section; the highest expression of political wisdom in the entire collection

TAKEAWAY · Core Insight
The six essays of "On Antiquity's" second half weave through Fan Zeng's "timing problem," the strategist class as "social safety valve," the Zhao Gao-Li Si conspiracy as "institutional poison," Duke Yin's "crisis judgment" lesson—all converging in the "Seven Virtues and Eight Warnings" and its peerless health-cultivation metaphor. "Governing the world is like nurturing health; worrying about the state and preparing against chaos is like taking medicine. Caution before illness, medicine after. Fearing a cold, one takes monkshood first—the illness hasn't come, but the medicine has already killed." This passage is the spiritual summit of Dongpo Zhilin's entire "On Antiquity" section. Prevention matters more than cure—but wrong prevention (preemptive violence) is deadlier than the disease itself. With this, Su Shi closes the book: a thousand-year miscellany, ultimately presenting how an independent thinker dismantled every historical proposition from Yao and Shun to An Lushan, then handed the reader a key—not moral judgment, but causal rationality.
