Wenhua Kulv · Ch.11 "Declaration of Hangzhou"

Hangzhou's beauty was not heaven-sent—four titans penned a thousand-year manifesto of ecological engineering

Marco Polo called Hangzhou "the noblest and most beautiful city in the world." Yet Hangzhou began as a shallow bay—a saltwater lake that by all natural laws should have degenerated into swamp and saline wasteland. Its beauty was entirely man-made. From the Tang to the Song dynasty, Bai Juyi, Qian Liu, Su Dongpo, and modern builders invested their lives in dredging and protecting West Lake. Hangzhou proved one thing beyond dispute: ecology comes before culture. Even the folk tale of the White Snake has been woven into Hangzhou's landscape as an eternal stage reminding us why the human world is worth cherishing.

01
World Renown
Marco Polo: Hangzhou, the world's noblest and most beautiful city
The Travels of Marco Polo launched Hangzhou onto the world stage—Columbus kept the book on his helm as he sailed toward the great unknown, completing the Age of Discovery. Mariners never reached Hangzhou, but the city flickered perpetually on the compass of their hearts. Marco Polo came from Venice, itself Europe's most beautiful city, and thus had a discerning eye. More remarkably, Hangzhou had just suffered the Mongol conquest of the Southern Song capital. Even diminished, it was still the most beautiful place on earth—"one can only imagine what it was like before."
02
Poet-Engineer
Bai Juyi: A great poet writing verse with mud and stone
In the 820s, Bai Juyi—already past fifty and author of "Song of Everlasting Regret" and "Song of the Pipa," an undisputed titan of Chinese literature—became governor of Hangzhou. He set aside his literary identity and transformed into a hydraulic engineer and urban planner. He studied the relationship between storage and drainage in West Lake: dredging the lakebed, building dikes to increase capacity, releasing water for irrigation by precise measure, and clearing the civilian "Six Wells." His own poems "hid among the water-weeds and stone crevices, covering their mouths, lest lesser poets hear them and abandon their brushes in despair."

03
Master Builder
Qian Liu: "Protect the realm, pacify the people"
In the 10th century, King Qian Liu of Wuyue—a rare urban master builder in Chinese history—rose from the margins: a salt smuggler and martial enthusiast. He governed by conquering water first: weaving giant bamboo cages filled with boulders to build sea-walls, driving six layers of massive wooden piles as foundation, and constructing sluice gates on the Qiantang River to prevent saltwater intrusion—people called him "Dragon King of the Sea." He deployed military "lake-dredging troops" to clear weeds and algae, opened Ciyun Ridge to link Qiantang River and West Lake. His strategy was "serve the great with humility"—his grandson Qian Chu peacefully surrendered to the Song, preserving Hangzhou intact.

04
Ecological Rescue
Su Dongpo: He'd rather save a lake than write a poem
Su Dongpo served twice in Hangzhou and, like Bai Juyi, became an engineer rather than a poet. He discovered West Lake was already half choked by weeds; within twenty years it would vanish entirely—salt tides would invade the canals, wells would fail, citizens would flee, and Hangzhou would become a dead city. He petitioned the throne, raised funds, and launched six simultaneous rescue projects. "Everyone in Hangzhou knew the director was Su Dongpo; no one remembered that this Su Dongpo was the same Su Dongpo who stunned the world with his poetry." Su Causeway remains today a pilgrimage route paying homage to natural ecology.

05
Cultural Manifesto
The White Snake and Hangzhou's thousand-year manifesto
Hangzhou has honored the White Snake legend with a lake of crystalline water, a rain-swept Broken Bridge, and a Leifeng Pagoda that rises each time it falls. Standing at the edge between human and non-human, Lady White solemnly tells the world what it means to be human—a profound challenge to Chinese history, which has always had "two excesses and one shortage": too many demons, too many immortals, too few humans. Hangzhou knew the greatness of this tale from the start and built for it an eternal physical stage. At its core, Hangzhou pursues the beauty of human life—and human beauty rests on ecological beauty. Modern builders have continued this legacy, dredging the lake, restoring Yanggong Causeway, and developing Xixi Wetland. The thousand-year manifesto—drafted by Qian Liu, co-authored by Bai Juyi and Su Dongpo, polished by Lady White, and now continued by today's builders—is both complex and simple: about nature, about ecology, about beauty, about humanity.

Ecology First

🐍

White Snake's Meaning

Lady White wanted only to be an ordinary human. The story asks: why is the human world worth cherishing? Hangzhou built it an eternal stage.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Hangzhou proved incontrovertibly that "ecology precedes culture"—even the greatest poets, upon arriving, set aside their brushes to become ecological guardians. The entire history of Hangzhou is a history of ecological protection: Bai Juyi, Qian Liu, Su Dongpo, and modern builders fought in relay to preserve West Lake, transforming an artificial landscape into the world's most beautiful city. The manifesto is both complex and simple: it is about nature, ecology, beauty, and the human world.
