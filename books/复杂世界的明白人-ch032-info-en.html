Insider's Guide to a Complex World · Ch.32 "How Should We View Manufacturing Moving to Southeast Asia"

From low-end OEM to the smile curve — industrial relocation isn't a crisis, it's the necessary choice of upgrading

Manufacturing moving to Southeast Asia is an established fact — like an aging population. Debating "can we keep it?" is pointless. The real question is "how do we respond?" Post-90s and post-95s are voting with their feet and fleeing assembly lines. The state is canceling subsidies for low-end factories. 300 km of road transport costs as much as 10,000 km of sea freight... Behind this is a grand chess game: China is evolving from Iron Man's Mark I (OEM factory) to Mark III (DJI / BOE). The service sector already exceeds 47% of employment and keeps climbing — this isn't deindustrialization, it's a deep fusion of industry and services.

01
COST GRAVITY
Cost gravity: Why low-end manufacturing must leave
Cost pulls production capacity to low ground like gravity. Manufacturing margins are razor-thin to begin with — the lion's share of profit goes to R&D and sales, leaving crumbs for manufacturing. 300 km of road freight costs as much as 10,000 km of sea freight. If you can find a buyer (Vietnam, Philippines) within 10,000 km, it's better to go offshore. Relocating inland only works along the Yangtze with barges. China's working-age population peaked in 2012 and has been declining; workers' wages are rising noticeably. More fatal: post-90s and post-95s would rather not work than set foot on an assembly line — factories have become "city pit stops": young people work a few days and quit.

02
INDUSTRY TYPES
Iron Man's three suits — not all industry is the same
Industry ≠ Industry. Chinese firms are evolving like Iron Man's three generations of armor. Mark I: no technology, no supply chain, no upward mobility — these are the ones that have been moving out in recent years. Mark II: appliance companies — massive output, patents to sales all domestic, the engine of internal circulation. Mark III: BOE / DJI / Huawei — high investment, high output, constant evolution, training battle-hardened engineers. The hardest to relocate.

03
DEINDUSTRIALIZATION
"Deindustrialization" isn't abandoning industry — it's upgrading it
The term sounds scary, but it really means no longer unconditionally welcoming low-end factories. In 2013, the tertiary sector's GDP share (46.1%) surpassed the secondary sector (43.9%) for the first time. R&D is a service. Scientific research is a service. Financial support is a service. If you want to upgrade industry and raise technological levels, the service sector inevitably flourishes. The US service sector accounts for 80% of GDP — tech, finance, law, and healthcare contribute 80%. High-end capacity (chips, GPUs) is in short supply; low-end capacity is severely overproduced — companies undercut each other to the point of losing money, surviving on export subsidies.

04
SMILE CURVE
Low marginal cost means explosive potential — the chasm between labor and creation
A factory worker earns one piece's worth of money per piece — with a daily cap of five or six hundred, earnings have a hard limit. A software developer's product can be replicated a million times at zero cost. A 5-person team makes a game that sells 5 million copies — each person pockets ~¥50 million. Same with scientific research — one small improvement can benefit tens of millions. Consumption power = income minus basic living expenses. Someone earning ¥3,000 a month has nothing left after rent and food. Someone earning ¥10,000, after ¥3,000 of basic expenses, has ¥7,000 of real spending power. Low-end manufacturing workers can't sustain domestic demand.

05
INEVITABLE
Industrial upgrading is both forced and riding the tide
Market forces are stronger than human will — once the trend is set, accept it or not, it's coming. Young people are voting with their feet and fleeing factories. The government canceled subsidies and left only market forces. If you can't hire workers, it means they have somewhere else to go. China's industrial upgrading is the inevitable choice at this stage of social development: part shifts to high-end production + services upgrade, the rest either automates or moves out. The purpose of hard work isn't to stay hard forever. The purpose of low-end manufacturing isn't to stay low-end. All "dividends" must eventually be repaid.
"I was born in 1994. Those three months on an assembly line in Dongguan were the most miserable of my life — exhausted to death, zero freedom. Now I fix phones. It doesn't pay much, but I control my own time and can talk with people about what else I could do. No way I'm ever going back to a factory."
🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
Manufacturing moving to Southeast Asia isn't a crisis — it's the inevitable growing pain of China's industry evolving from "Mark I" to "Mark III." Low-end factories can't be kept and don't need to be kept — young people vote with their feet, the government cancels subsidies, and market forces take the wheel. The real direction: raise manufacturing's value-add, push toward both ends of the smile curve (R&D + sales), and deeply fuse services with industry. The purpose of hard work isn't to stay hard forever. The purpose of low-end isn't to stay low-end.
