Sensible in a Complex World · Ch.28 "Why People Rush to Buy Negative-Rate Bonds"

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer In 2020, China issued bonds to Europe at -0.152% interest — and they were snapped up like crazy. Paying to lend money? Unthinkable. Europe and Japan are already deep in negative-rate territory: banks charge you to hold deposits, governments beg people to spend but nobody responds, the economy enters a kind of "zen mode." Why? The answer: aging → fewer children → weak consumption → no entrepreneurial drive → rates keep falling → capital idles in circles. This is a pitfall facing all of humanity — can China sidestep it?

This chapter uses the counter-intuitive phenomenon of negative-rate bonds as a springboard, unfolding into a systematic analysis of the "negative-rate era" and the "micro-growth era." The core logical chain: bonds that lose money still get bought → because European rates are even lower + bet on RMB appreciation + discount arbitrage → why are European rates negative? → aging + tech stagnation → loss of consumption drive → economy enters "zen mode" → negative rates aren't just Europe and Japan's problem, they're humanity's shared future → can China avoid this? Three keys: a population that still strives, poverty alleviation expanding the internal market, and hope for technological breakthroughs. The author's final message: knowing how brutal the world is, yet staying optimistic.

01
Counter-Intuitive
A losing deal everyone rushes for? Three layers of arbitrage decoded
Europeans buying China's negative-rate bonds aren't irrational — three layers of arbitrage stack together. ① Rate comparison arbitrage: Europe's negative rates are deeper than China's — whichever bonds you buy, you lose, so pick the one that loses less. ② FX appreciation arbitrage: the world prints money like crazy, China is relatively restrained; with RMB appreciation expected, Europeans are essentially shorting the euro. ③ Discount arbitrage: bonds can be resold before maturity — if they appreciate, you profit on the spread. Stack all three and China's bonds may actually turn a net gain.

02
Root Cause Analysis
From aging to "zen mode": why Japan and Europe are stuck
The surface symptom of negative rates is banks begging you to borrow, but the root cause is society losing both the will to consume and the drive to start businesses. Japan's path: hyper-competitive growth era → scared off the next generation → young people avoid marriage and kids, embrace minimalism → no kids = no consumption → nobody buys products → starting a business is suicide → rates go negative and still nobody borrows. Europe's path: generous welfare → no reason to strive → same destination of zero desire. Neither side's people have children, and the biggest expense of a family with kids simply vanishes. An economy ultimately runs on consumption, and consumption is driven by: children, young women, and tech iteration. All three engines dead = zero growth.

03
Drivers of Demand
The three engines of consumption: children, young women, tech iteration
The economy ultimately moves on consumption, and consumption has only three core drivers — all three dead means economic stagnation. ① Children: parents never pinch pennies on kids — school district housing, extracurricular classes, travel; even wealthy families always want more. Without children, adult spending is minimal. ② Young women: peak consumption power in youth, sharp decline after mid-thirties. ③ Tech iteration: when smartphones emerged, everyone ditched Nokias for smart devices → consumption frenzy → mobile internet birthed countless new companies → but now the phone market is stable, people use one device for 5-6 years. Three engines dead = the fundamental logic of the negative-rate era.

04
Global Trend
The M-shaped society arrives: negative rates are a trap humanity can't dodge
All of humanity is sliding toward negative rates — just at different speeds. An M-shaped society is taking form: the middle dissolves, wealth concentrates at both extremes. US: massive money printing → flows into China for RMB → China's M2 artificially expands. Rich people get the printed money → buy houses, stocks → "rich inflation, poor deflation." Investment opportunities shrink → big tech companies even compete with vegetable vendors → 9 out of 10 startups lose money → nobody dares borrow → rates forced down. In China, Yu'ebao rates fell from 6% in 2014 to 2% in 2021 — visibly sliding toward negative territory. Prime assets get bid to the sky: Tier-1 city housing, Moutai stock, Bitcoin — money idles in financial markets, never reaches the real economy, never benefits the grassroots.

05
Breaking Through
How can China sidestep? Striving genes + domestic depth + tech breakthrough
It's hard for Chinese people to become as desireless as the Japanese — countless people still work tirelessly to pursue a better life. That's the first line of defense. The second is a massive internal market: Pinduoduo's rise is about tapping the "lower-tier market's" enormous potential — "the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all." The third line is tech breakthroughs: they have to happen fast, or the whole society falls into the pit. The past decade of mobile internet created nearly 200 million jobs, but the dividend is exhausted — the next tech wave (like EVs) is desperately needed. The author's stance: avoid both Europe's welfare that breeds lethargy, and Japan/Korea's grind that makes people stop having children. As long as we don't bottom out into total apathy, things won't get too bad.
"Limited by our lifespans, we can't see the bigger picture. From the long arc of history, the world has always moved in cycles — decline, recovery, rise. I hope that after understanding the world's cruelty, we still choose an optimistic attitude."
— The author's final stance on the "negative-rate era"
🔑 Key Takeaway
The frenzy for negative-rate bonds isn't irrational — it's a triple layer of rate comparison + FX expectations + discount arbitrage. The root cause of negative rates isn't banks going crazy but society losing both consumption and entrepreneurial drive — aging, fewer children, and tech stagnation converging into a cold front where money can neither be lent nor spent. This is humanity's shared future, and China is sliding toward it at visible speed. The only way out: keep the will to strive, expand domestic demand through poverty alleviation, and await the next tech breakthrough. Knowing the world's cruelty, yet choosing optimism — in the long run, both pessimism and optimism are right.
