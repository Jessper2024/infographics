02
Military Campaign
Capturing Jiqing: Two Failures, Then Triumph
Inferior boats, excessive underestimation—two assaults on Jiqing in 1355 both failed. But failure brought opportunity: both rival commanders Guo Tianxu and Zhang Tianyou died in battle, and Zhu smoothly became marshal. In 1356, personally leading a three-pronged assault, he took Jiqing in ten days and renamed it Yingtian. History works this way: those who stood before him—Yuan or "his own people"—all became stepping stones.

03
Geopolitics
The Neighbors: A "Worker" Surrounded by Giants
Zhu was surrounded by powerful enemies, yet exploited the strategic gap perfectly. Yuan forces wanted to attack but couldn't reach him (Liu Futong blocking north); Zhang Shicheng was rich but "small-minded"—only acted when the knife was at his throat; Chen Youliang wanted to fight but hadn't yet consolidated power. Zhu exploited each: the Yuan were revenge targets but not yet the priority—Chen and Zhang were the real threats.

04
Geopolitical Wisdom
Strategic Window: Liu Futong as Shield
Liu Futong's Red Turban Army blocked Yuan main forces to the north—Zhu's strategic shield. The Yuan court was deeply concerned about Zhu but "couldn't reach him." Liu Futong and Zhu shared the Red Turban banner, creating a brother-unit relationship. This gave Zhu the critical breathing room to grow. Zhu understood: Yuan was the revenge target but the time was not yet right—the real threats were his two neighbors, Chen Youliang and Zhang Shicheng.

05
Empire Begins
Renaming Yingtian: The Turning Point
"Poor Zhu had finally escaped Fengyang, Bozhou, Chuzhou—arrived at wealthy Nanjing. But the real empire was just beginning." The 1356 capture and renaming of Jiqing to Yingtian marked Zhu's transformation from peasant rebel to southeastern hegemon. His nominal rank was low (vice marshal, Guo Tianxu was marshal), but actual military authority was unchallenged—the real challenger was approaching from the west: Xu Shouhui and Chen Youliang.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Zhu's empire began with precise exploitation of the strategic window. Surrounded by powerful enemies, he used the principle: "those who could fight didn't want to, those who wanted to fight couldn't." Liu Futong's Red Turbans blocked Yuan forces north, giving him breathing room. Capturing Jiqing and renaming it Yingtian marked the transformation from peasant rebel to southeastern hegemon. The real threats: Zhang Shicheng's small-mindedness was not feared; Chen Youliang's ambition was.
