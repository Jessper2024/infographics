Chapter 11「The Miracle of Hongdu」

A dissolute nephew's last-stand miracle — Zhu Wenzheng defies five hundred thousand besiegers with barely ten thousand defenders

Situation: In 1363, Chen Youliang led four hundred to six hundred thousand troops to storm Hongdu (modern Nanchang). The city's defender was Zhu Yuanzhang's own nephew — Zhu Wenzheng, previously dismissed as a dissolute playboy who ignored military affairs. Complication: The garrison held barely ten thousand men against a force six times larger. City walls were thin and crumbling. All observers expected the city to fall. Question: How could an undermanned garrison hold against overwhelming force? Answer: Zhu Wenzheng revealed himself as a military genius. He deployed precision defense across nine gates, introduced matchlock volley fire, and inspired his men to fight for eighty-five days — buying Zhu Yuanzhang the decisive time needed for the Poyang Lake showdown.

Character Contrast
Zhu Wenzheng: Playboy by Day, Military Genius by Night

Before Chen Youliang arrived, Zhu Wenzheng spent his days drinking and composing music, earning universal contempt. When the enemy came, he revealed himself as one of the finest military minds of his age.

Zhu Yuanzhang had entrusted Hongdu — a critical supply hub — to his clean-shaven nephew. The appointment baffled everyone. Zhu Wenzheng had indeed indulged in pleasure houses, drinking heavily and commissioning songs for his maids. He left all military planning to subordinates. But the moment he learned that Chen Youliang's massive army was approaching, he dropped his careless mask and convened the most serious war council of his life. He told his generals plainly: those who wished to surrender could leave; the rest would fight to the last man. He was a bad man, yes — but he was a useful one.

Military Genius
Nine-Gate Defense: Force Multiplication at Its Limit

Zhu Wenzheng was a once-in-a-generation military talent — with only ten thousand men to guard nine gates, he deployed forces with accountant-like precision and still had two thousand troops left as a strategic reserve.

A city with many gates is a military nightmare when facing six hundred thousand attackers. The enemy can strike everywhere at once; the defender is stretched thin. Zhu Wenzheng matched each general to the gate that suited his character: Deng Yu held the critical Fuzhou Gate; Zhao Desheng covered three gates with the heaviest load; Xue Xian held two gates; Niu Hailong and Zhao Guowang covered the remaining two. After all assignments, he still had two thousand fresh reserves ready for emergencies. This meticulous accounting made Chen Youliang's massive army unable to find any weakness.

Willpower Test
Matchlock Fire: Deng Yu's Revolutionary Defense at Fuzhou Gate

Chen Youliang's soldiers hacked a breach over thirty zhang wide in the wall and poured through — Deng Yu met them with matchlock fire, delivering gunpowder's stunning debut in large-scale siege warfare.

On the twenty-seventh day of the siege, Chen launched his fiercest assault on Fuzhou Gate. Arrows and boulders from above were too fierce to allow siege towers. Enraged soldiers hacked at the brick wall with blades until they created a gap more than thirty zhang across — a shoddy wall, Zhu Wenzheng would later grumble. As Han troops surged through the breach, Deng Yu's matchlock companies opened fire. The volley stunned the attackers. Deng Yu seized the moment and built a wooden barricade right in front of the breach. Fighting and construction continued from dawn until dusk. Generals Li Ji-xian, Niu Hailong, and Zhao Guowang all fell in battle. Zhu Wenzheng himself was wounded. Chen Youliang realized for the first time: Hongdu was far harder to take than he imagined.

04
Heroism
Zhang Ziming: The Scholar Who Chose Death Over Betrayal

Zhang Ziming crossed enemy territory for half a month to bring Zhu Yuanzhang a request for aid, then turned back immediately — only to be captured at Hukou, where he chose to die rather than betray Hongdu's defenders.

Zhu Wenzheng sent Zhang Ziming to Yingtian (Nanjing) to beg for reinforcements. Zhang traveled by night and slept by day, eluding Chen's forces for fifteen days. He arrived exhausted but wise — he did not say "we cannot hold." Instead he told Zhu Yuanzhang: "Chen's army has suffered heavily. Their supplies are nearly gone. Send troops and victory is certain." Zhu, overjoyed, immediately recalled Xu Da from besieging Luzhou and prepared for battle. He told Zhang: "Tell Zhu Wenzheng to hold one more month. I will be there." Zhang rushed back, but was caught near Hukou. Chen Youliang personally interrogated him, then demanded he call on Hongdu to surrender. Zhang appeared to agree. But when brought before the city walls, he shouted at the top of his lungs: "Hold fast! Our great army is almost here!" Enraged, Chen had him executed on the spot.

Historical Turning Point
The Eighty-Five Days That Reshaped Chinese History

With a crumbling wall and barely ten thousand men, Hongdu blocked Chen Youliang's six hundred thousand-strong army for eighty-five days — a feat that directly determined the fate of the Ming Dynasty.

From April through July, Chen tried every siege method imaginable: frontal assaults on city gates, massive siege towers, water-pass infiltration, and fire attacks — all failed. Xue Xian led a cavalry charge that routed the attackers outside a city gate. Zhao Desheng was killed by a stray arrow, yet his men held the position without missing a beat. Matchlock units repelled wave after wave. The garrison proved that willpower could overcome numerical superiority. Hongdu gave Zhu Yuanzhang vital time to prepare his decisive fleet. Chen's army consumed itself in the long standoff — morale and food supplies both running dry. The siege's failure directly led to the Battle of Poyang Lake, where the fate of China would be decided.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Defense of Hongdu stands as a classic miracle of asymmetrical warfare. Zhu Wenzheng used a playboy's disguise to mask a military genius, achieving a one-to-sixty defensive legend through flawless force allocation. Zhang Ziming completed his mission with his life. Deng Yu's matchlock tactics opened the era of gunpowder in large-scale siege defense. Eighty-five days of resistance drained Chen Youliang's morale and supplies, buying Zhu Yuanzhang the precious time needed for the Poyang Lake naval showdown. History proves: numbers never decide wars — will and wisdom do.
