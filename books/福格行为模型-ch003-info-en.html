01
Core Principle
Simplicity Wins: Humans Are Wired to Choose Easy — Simplicity > Motivation
Why did Instagram defeat Facebook and Flickr despite their superior funding and technology? Because it was dead simple — just 3 taps to upload a photo. Mike Krieger and Kevin Systrom learned from their failed product Burbn that users loved sharing photos but the interface was too complex. They stripped every unnecessary feature, keeping only: snap, filter, share. This wasn't corner-cutting — it was deep understanding of human behavior: making a behavior simple is 100× more effective than strengthening motivation. You're not lazy — evolution wired you to choose the path of least resistance. Good behavior design aligns with human nature instead of fighting it.
📱

Burbn (Failed Product)

Too many features: check-in + sharing + social. Users didn't know where to start.

📷

Instagram ($100B+ Product)

3 steps: Snap → Filter → Share. Ultimate simplicity. Perfect behavior design.

02
Three Paths
Three Ways to Make Behavior Easy: Improve Skills → Get Tools → Shrink Behavior
Three paths to making behavior easier, from long-term to instant. ① Improve skills (long-term strategy) — learn proper running form to reduce injury risk. ② Acquire tools and resources (mid-term) — buy a good juicer to make "drink juice daily" easy. ③ Shrink the behavior (instant results) — the most powerful and most overlooked strategy. The Starter Step is most effective: begin with 2 push-ups, not a 1-hour gym session. Shrinking behavior isn't "lowering standards" — it's lowering the activation threshold. Once you start, you often naturally do more.

03
Tiny Habits Strategy
Tiny Habits: Behavioral Units So Small You Can't Possibly Fail
Sarika's story illustrates the power of tiny habits. She lives with bipolar disorder — her mood and energy fluctuate wildly. Traditional big habits (cook daily, exercise regularly) all collapsed. When she switched to Tiny Habits, everything changed: water the plants on the porch every morning — that's it. This tiny action became her "emotional barometer" — if even watering plants felt impossible, she knew her state needed attention. Tiny Habits aren't "meditate 30 minutes daily" but "take 3 deep breaths daily"; not "work out for an hour" but "do 2 push-ups." So tiny you can't fail — that's the secret to sustainable change.

04
Ability Diagnosis
The Ability Chain: Diagnose Which Link Is Stuck
Every behavior can be broken into five ability dimensions — the weakest link is your breakthrough point. Time — Do you have it? Money — Is it affordable? Physical Effort — Enough energy? Mental Effort — Manageable cognitive load? Routine — Does it fit your daily flow? Sarika's ability chain analysis: Time and Mental Effort were weakest during low-mood periods, so she chose the simplest possible behavior (watering plants) as her entry point. Your struggle is the same — you're not "incapable"; one specific link is just too tight. Find it. Loosen it.

05
Mindset Shift
If It's Simple Enough, You Don't Need Willpower
Human nature dictates we can't sustain painful things long-term. Many people approach change with a "go big or go home" mentality, equating change with suffering and grit. This is a massive misconception. Simple behaviors = sustainable behaviors. Change isn't about beating yourself with willpower — it's about designing behaviors so simple you can do them anytime. Sarika's life went from "out of control" to "manageable" not because her willpower suddenly strengthened, but because she found a simple enough behavior — watering plants. It takes 30 seconds, but it's the starting line for all change.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Simplicity > Motivation. Humans are wired to choose the path of least resistance — this isn't a flaw, it's an evolutionary advantage. Instagram defeated every feature-rich competitor with a 3-step flow, not because it had more features, but because it was simpler. Similarly, changing your own behavior doesn't require "more willpower" — you just need to shrink the target behavior until you can't possibly fail. Start with 2 push-ups a day. Start with 3 deep breaths. Tiny starts create massive compounding — because starting is the hardest step. Once you start, momentum carries you further.
