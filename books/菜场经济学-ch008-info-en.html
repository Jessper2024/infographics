Focus for Ten Years, Outlast the Suffering — Cai's Twenty Survival Rules for the Underdog
SCQA Analysis
S (Situation): Graduates from ordinary universities face sky-high housing prices, low income, no girlfriend, and suffocating frustration everywhere.
C (Complication): 99% of private companies survive ≤5 years; male underdogs do sales, females do admin — past 35, economic pressure hits: men sink into despair, women stray. Individual effort can't beat the power of capital: lose the parent lottery, and even a Harvard grad can't afford a decent Shanghai home before 40.
Q (Question): What can ordinary people do? Is there any viable path?
A (Answer): Choose one industry you can stick with for ten years, dive in deep, don't fear losses and don't fear hardship — in ten years, you'll be the pillar of that industry in your region. Use three generations of connections to get into a state-owned enterprise; spend three generations of savings to buy property in a big city. Master the paperwork and take 80% of the profit — stick to that ratio and you're wealthy for life. Location determines destiny: finding a demolition-compensated girl in Jiading beats being a financial dog in Lujiazui.

🔴 Focus Is Your Only Weapon

Find one industry you can commit to for ten years. Dive in deep. Don't fear losses, don't fear hardship. In ten years you'll be the pillar of that industry in your region. A home, a wife — they will come. Small success comes from focus; big success is luck. Mega-rich tycoons get jailed or executed all the time — don't envy them too much. Don't study too many subjects, don't stick your nose into everything — it won't add to your life, it just scatters your energy. When dating, date wholeheartedly. When studying, study hard. No grandstanding — keep your feet on the ground. Set clear goals and strive for them — there will always be reachable targets, like paying rent on time. If you surpass your father's level, your life is a victory. Don't compare yourself to princelings and rich kids. Generation by generation, accumulation brings hope. Overnight success is pure luck.

🟠 Pragmatic Survival: Skills Beat Diplomas

Outside of engineering second-tier degrees, humanities and social sciences are worthless — employers won't scrutinize your diploma, only whether you work diligently. Having memorized Keynes and Samuelson is less lucrative than knowing how to install a water heater. Learn one truly mastered skill, save money, buy a home. Underdogs should learn practical trades first: excavator operation, coding, drafting, mortar hauling, bricklaying — earn your first ¥10M, then think about real estate. The prerequisite for studying management is first owning a company big enough to pay a hundred salaries. Young people must understand: what makes you cry isn't Cai's mockery, but your failure in the parent lottery. Even if you graduate from Harvard, you can't afford a decent Shanghai home before 40 — unless a demolition-compensated girl in Jiading or Nanhui accidentally chooses you. Location determines destiny.

🟡 Workplace Iron Rules: Humility and Loyalty

Rule one for underdogs in the workplace: humility. Frequently say things people like to hear — \"hometown specialties,\" \"my hometown is nothing compared to Shanghai.\" Never, ever say \"a relative made millions last year\" — that's asking for lightning. Your enemies are those peers who call you brother — similar seniority, never enough opportunities. Distance yourself from them. Stay vigilant. No matter how close, never confide your true thoughts — those words will quickly reach the boss as tribute. The rule of being human: loyalty. Once you've chosen the right person, never waver. Lü Bu was the mightiest warrior, yet constant betrayal left him abandoned by all. Better to make a mistake than to betray. 99% of new grad resumes are just award collections — completely meaningless. Every organization only needs two kinds of people: oxen who work tirelessly and rainmakers who bring in business.

🟣 The Wealth Formula: Paperwork Takes 80%

Remember one rule and you'll get rich: master the paperwork, take 80% of the profit; the doers get 20%. Stick to this ratio and you're wealthy for life. A ¥10M mega-software contract with a state enterprise — brainpower is worthless, landing the deal is everything. Giving away 80% of the profit is the baseline. First collaboration, 50/50 split on paper — proactively concede a bit, you take 60%, I take 40%. Do your best to make that first collaboration succeed — that protects your external reputation. After it's done, observe your partner's attitude. If they think taking 60% is their natural right, stop there. If they think next time should be split by contribution, their character is solid — worth keeping. Treat people generously — they'll know. Take 30% each time and you may get 100 opportunities. Take 70% and there's no next time. Never train yourself from childhood in cunning and taking advantage — integrity means eating small losses while waiting for big opportunities. Taking advantage is like stealing; it's addictive.

🩷 Life Roadmap: Two Paths Forward

If you want an easier life, go work in a midwestern provincial capital, find a wife with a decent salary, work hard for 5–8 years and save a down payment — but your children will have to retrace your path into Beijing/Shanghai. If you're willing to sacrifice yourself, two paths: first, go to BJ/SH and work like crazy your whole life, rent forever, buy a tiny remote place before retirement and wait for hukou policy to crack open; second, learn from Sister Feng. The male underdog's poverty-escape formula: state-unit job + demolition-compensated girl — everything else is illusion. Going to BJ/SH requires caution — as soon as you arrive, speed-date to find a demolition-compensated girl, and everything is solved. Financial dogs working in Lujiazui and the Bund seem glamorous but get nothing real — after work, you squeeze onto the bus back to your shared rental. Ten years of grinding, still can't afford a down payment for anything within a one-hour commute. It's definitely a life tragedy. Underdogs, shamelessness is the path — when you see the powerful, cling to them relentlessly. After a few years, you'll get money, opportunities, or experience. Too embarrassed? Then suffer.

💡 Key Takeaway

Cai went from being scorned at a Pudong Avenue job fair, penniless and alone, to \"having gained far too much\" twenty years later. Temporary poverty and hardship are not frightening — as long as you don't lose faith, society will reward you. But the prerequisite is getting the direction right: use three generations of connections to enter a state enterprise, spend three generations of savings to buy property in a major city. Most private companies aren't great — state enterprises and public institutions are good. Strive to pass the exams. Cherish your university years, cherish your loved ones — once you leave those gates, you'll face brutal reality and a bunch of bastards like Cai. Life will inevitably throw obstacles at you — don't despair, don't grieve. People are great because of their dreams. Small apartment, nagging wife, girlfriend who dumped you, single dog, no hukou — all temporary. Cai is old now, and hopes the young can withstand life's crushing torment and pass a better land to the next generation.
