In the winter of 1368, Yuan general Wang Baobao led 100,000 cavalry out of Juyong Pass to retake Dadu. Xu Da saw through the ruse—ignoring Dadu, he struck at the undefended Taiyuan. On the night of December 4, Chang Yuchun led a surprise cavalry raid on Wang Baobao's camp. Wang Baobao fled with one bare foot; his army collapsed. This was the first major Han cavalry victory over Mongol forces. Chang Yuchun then pursued the Yuan emperor to Shangdu, but died of sudden illness on the return journey at age 40. Meanwhile, Wang Baobao set a "besiege to draw reinforcements" trap at Dingxiang, nearly destroying a Ming relief force. A decisive desert battle loomed.

Military Action
Xu Da Seizes Taiyuan: Wang Baobao Trapped
Xu Da chose the third road—one that left Wang Baobao speechless. Wang Baobao attacked Dadu; Xu Da did not reinforce. He judged Dadu was well-defended, but Wang Baobao had left Taiyuan undefended by taking all forces north. Xu Da led cavalry straight for Taiyuan that same night—his target was not the city but Wang Baobao himself. Wang Baobao, as expected, immediately turned his 100,000 cavalry around to rescue Taiyuan—walking right into Xu Da's trap.

Battles
Chang Yuchun's Night Raid: First Han Victory
On the night of December 4, Chang Yuchun proposed a night raid—"Our cavalry is assembled but infantry has not arrived; attacking now will cause heavy casualties. A night raid will succeed." Wang Baobao was reading military texts by lamplight when shouts erupted outside. He fled with one bare foot. His army collapsed: 40,000 killed, the rest scattered. Chang Yuchun pursued to Xinzhou; Wang Baobao was left with only 18 bodyguards. This was the first major Han cavalry victory over Mongol forces.

03
Key Figures
Chang Yuchun's Fall: A General Lost
Chang Yuchun pursued the fleeing Yuan emperor to Shangdu, capturing over 10,000 Mongol soldiers and seizing 3,000 horses, 50,000 cattle—his final victory. On the return march through Liuhechuan, he died of sudden illness at age 40. Ming's number one vanguard, whose style mirrored Bai Qi—shock cavalry, irresistible momentum. "Drunk on the battlefield, who laughs? Since ancient times, how many return from war!"

04
Strategic Offensive
Wang Baobao's Ambush: Ming Nearly Trapped
After defeat at Taiyuan, Wang Baobao learned patience—masters avoid rash moves. When he learned Zhang Wen had advanced deep into Lanzhou, he mobilized 120,000 troops: tens of thousands besieged Lanzhou as bait, while 100,000 main force lay in ambush at strategic Dingxiang. The "besiege to draw reinforcements" stratagem worked—Ming general Yu Guang led his relief force straight into the trap and was completely destroyed.

05
Aftermath
Dingxiang Approaches: Desert War's Turning Point
With Shaanxi pacified, most of the North fell to Ming—but Wang Baobao's 100,000 cavalry remained a mortal threat. New dynasties always eliminated the old, and Zhu ordered the Northern Yuan's complete destruction. After the Taiyuan victory, Ming forces turned toward Gansu. Wang Baobao prepared his ambush at Dingxiang—the second major Han-Mongol cavalry confrontation loomed, one that would decide the Northern Yuan's fate.

🔑 核心结论
Chang Yuchun's surprise night raid at Taiyuan was the first major Han cavalry victory over Mongol forces—40,000 of Wang Baobao's 100,000 were annihilated. This battle proved the Ming army could defeat the Mongols in open field combat. However, Chang Yuchun died suddenly at age 40 on the return march. Wang Baobao licked his wounds at Dingxiang, setting a "besiege to draw reinforcements" trap that nearly destroyed a Ming relief army. The war against the Northern Yuan was far from over—a decisive desert battle was approaching.
