Layout of the World · Chapter 5
「Unification and Division: Politics & Geography」

Order unites, chaos divides — the logic of dynastic rhythm and geography's centripetal-centrifugal power

As dynasties rise and fall, the realm alternates between unity and division. 'Unification must first be grounded in social-political cohesion and centripetal force; geographic management comes second. Division begins with political centrifugal forces, from which geographic centrifugal forces then emerge.' This chapter explores the dual logic of unification and division from both political and geographic perspectives: China's geographic environment possesses both overall unity and local independence. Whether geography promotes connection or centrifugal drift depends on the degree of political integration and the astuteness of central governance. The Qin-Han transition serves as a case study revealing how the 'center of gravity' of the problem fundamentally shifts — overthrowing the old order requires intensifying contradictions, while building a new order demands dissolving them. Only those who grasp this transformation can achieve unification.

1
Logic
Political Cohesion vs. Geographic Centrifugal Force: The Dual Drive

The rhythm of unification and division has its own historical logic; geographic conditions are but one factor. Unification requires social-political cohesion and centripetal force as its foundation; geographic management is secondary. Division, conversely, begins with political centrifugal tendencies before geographic forces manifest. China's geographic character exhibits both overall unity and local independence: rivers like the Yangtze and Yellow River link east and west, while the Taihang, Qinling, and Dabie ranges act as natural barriers. Whether geography fosters connection or promotes fragmentation depends on the degree of political integration and the wisdom of central governance.

2
Rise & Fall
Dynastic Cycles: From Centripetal Gathering to Centrifugal Drift

When a dynasty is strong, its rulers tend to embrace a broad vision and implement proactive political, economic, military, ethnic, and cultural policies, maximizing geography's unifying potential while suppressing latent centrifugal forces. When a dynasty weakens, accumulated ills brew widespread contradictions, centrifugal forces grow, and geographic fragmentation emerges. Once centrifugal momentum reaches a threshold, unity may shatter. In the alternation of order and chaos, geographic factors operate only after more fundamental social and political factors have set the stage.

3
Two Phases
The Pivot: From Dismantling the Old Order to Building the New

Between the collapse of the old ruling order and the establishment of a new unified order, turmoil divides into two phases. Phase one intensifies contradictions; phase two dissolves them. To overthrow the old dynasty, first movers must exploit widespread grievances — even amplify them — to mobilize support. But once the old dynasty falls and contradictions lose their focal point, the core question shifts: what resource can reintegrate the realm and dissolve centrifugal forces? Liu Bang's case reveals this pivot: upon entering Guanzhong, he issued the Three Articles rather than taking revenge on the Qin people, preventing further East-West antagonism. By mourning Emperor Yi and framing Xiang Yu as the realm's 'common enemy,' he simplified complex, scattered contradictions — accelerating unification.

4
Chu-Han
Liu Bang's Three Masterstrokes: Building Legitimacy for Reunification

Liu Bang executed three pivotal masterstrokes on the path to unification. First, the timing of retaking the Three Qin territories — striking east before fragmentation hardened into permanence. Second, mourning Emperor Yi at Luoyang — using a dead 'common sovereign' to legitimize his cause, casting Xiang Yu as the realm's common enemy, and transforming his campaign into a righteous crusade that united a 560,000-strong coalition. Third, adopting Li Yiji's geographic strategy — 'seize Xingyang, secure Aocang's granaries, block Chenggao, seal the Taihang passes, hold Feihu, guard Baima Ford' — creating a clear east-west confrontation that made 'the realm know where to rally.' Critically, earlier advisors had urged Chen Sheng to reinstall the Six States' heirs to expand the anti-Qin front, which was sound in phase one. When Li Yiji proposed the same in the unification phase, Zhang Liang immediately rejected it — the purpose had fundamentally changed.

5
Rhythm
Three Great Divisions and Three Great Unifications: History's Rhythm

Chinese history witnessed three prolonged periods of division: the Spring and Autumn / Warring States, the Wei-Jin-Northern and Southern Dynasties, and the Five Dynasties / Song-Xia-Liao-Jin coexistence. After each, a greater unification followed: Qin-Han, Sui-Tang, and Yuan-Ming-Qing. Each post-division unification was deeper and broader than the last, with higher social integration. 'The collapse of the old order is typically the product of intensified social conflicts. Rebuilding a new order requires first integrating diverse contradictions, dissolving political centrifugal forces — only then can geographic centrifugal forces be overcome.' Those who first rose against the old order — Chen Sheng, the Green Woods, the Yellow Turbans, Liu Yuan, Wang Bo, Han Shantong — rarely achieved unification. They were precursors, not finishers.

📌 Key Takeaways

The fundamental driver of unification and division is social-political cohesion and dispersion; geography operates on this foundation. Strong dynasties concentrate centripetal force and suppress centrifugal tendencies; weak dynasties allow centrifugal drift, which geographic conditions then amplify. The transition from an old order's collapse to a new unification requires a critical pivot — from intensifying contradictions to dissolving them. Only those who grasp and navigate this shift can achieve unification. Liu Bang's success lay in his earlier recognition of this transformation: from the Three Articles that defused Qin animosity, to mourning Emperor Yi to construct legitimacy, to geographic strategy that clarified the situation. China's historical rhythm — three great divisions, each resolved by a deeper unification — reveals a political integrative power that transcends geographic constraints.
