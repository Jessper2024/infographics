Review, Reflect, Explore, Improve — None Can Be Omitted

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer After completing a project or delivering results, we assume the work is done—the greatest illusion. Review tells us: we've only done half; the other half holds equal value but is often discarded. How do we truly "finish" something? Review, Reflect, Explore, Improve—all four are indispensable.

When is work truly finished? The answer: delivering results is only half the journey. Reviewing the process, reflecting on causes, exploring patterns, and improving capability form the complete closed loop. This chapter illustrates with a sales campaign—target ¥1M vs. actual ¥100K—showing 5-level review, 3-layer reflection, 4 deep patterns, and 4 "if we could do it again" improvements. Review → Reflect → Explore are the actions; Improve is the outcome—all four form an indivisible chain.

Review Process
Review: Five-Level Reconstruction of the Sales Reality
Review is a panoramic reconstruction of completed events, answering four core questions. What was the goal? What was the result? What was done? What unexpected results emerged? In the ¥1M→¥100K gap, review isn't just describing facts—it's discovering hidden assets.
✅

Reflect on Causes
Reflect: Three-Layer Root-Cause Digging
Reflection is not surface-level cause listing but deep root-cause tracing. Why the 10× gap? Why did execution deviate from plan? What did tight timing cause? Three dimensions of deep questioning.
💰

Why the 10× Sales Gap

Wrong market perception·Online education looked hot but willingness to pay was low·Consumers preferred income-tied products (professional certification)

📊

Why Execution Deviated

Print media cannot reach campuses·Students are "always online"·Shifted budget to online marketing

⏰

Timing Factor

Only 2 months before summer break·Missed critical mass-contact window·Students left campus

🔑 Key Takeaway
Review is not simple post-mortem summary—it is the complete cycle of Review→Reflect→Explore→Improve. A ¥1M target that only achieved ¥100K failed not because of poor execution but because of missing market understanding, missing decision mechanisms, and missing self-protection before committing. Every review builds a "don't-repeat checklist" for the next decision.
