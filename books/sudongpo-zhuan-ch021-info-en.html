The Gay Genius · Ch.21 「Chinese Painting」

Ink bamboo, scholar's painting, and dance on paper — how Su Tungpo changed Chinese art with a single brush

Su Tungpo's genius and sensibility gave birth to a new kind of Chinese painting — the scholar's painting (wénrénhuà). The new style he explored with Mi Fei would become the most distinctive and representative style of Chinese painting. The core theory: "beauty is movement." Chinese calligraphy and painting are the same discipline — technique, method, spirit, and critical principles are identical. Su Tungpo invented the "ink bamboo," emphasizing rapid, rhythmic brushstrokes to express a unified concept — "create new meaning; do not tread in the footsteps of the ancients."

01
Art Revolution
Scholar's Painting: Expressing the Heart's Rhythm
Su Tungpo created scholar's painting — a style that does not pursue meticulous representation but seeks to express the artist's inner spirit through rhythmic brushstrokes. Before the Song Dynasty, Chinese painting had two schools: the Northern school of Li Sixun with fine golden outlines and bright colors, and the Southern school of Wang Wei emphasizing rapid, rhythmic strokes. Scholar's painting truly crystallized in the Song Dynasty — it valued vibrant vitality and the artist's conceptual unity.

02
Aesthetic Theory
Beauty is Movement: From Calligraphy to Painting
The fundamental concept of rhythm in Chinese art was established by calligraphy — a critic secretly traces the artist's movements from the first stroke to the last. Chinese characters are composed of lines in infinite variation. Chinese artists are not satisfied with static arrangements; they emphasize "living lines." Natural lines always evoke movement: a fawn's grace suggests leaping, a lion's paw suggests pouncing. All questions of art are ultimately questions of rhythm.

03
Historic Gathering
The West Garden Gathering: Sixteen Scholars
At the most famous gathering in Chinese art history — the "West Garden Gathering" — sixteen top scholars assembled at Prince Wang Shen's mansion. Su Tungpo, Mi Fei, and Li Gonglin were present together; Li painted the scene as a memento. Stone tables stood beneath tall firs and giant bamboos, a waterfall cascading into the river. Su Tungpo, in yellow robe and black cap, wrote at a table; Li Gonglin inscribed Tao Qian's poems at another; Ziyou, Huang Tingjian, Zhang Lei, and Chao Buzhi stood around the table; Mi Fei carved characters into a rock; Qin Guan sat on tree roots listening to lute music.

04
Technical Philosophy
The Art of Omission: Maximum Rhythm, Minimum Ink
For Su Tungpo, a few clumps of bamboo and several large rocks made a complete painting. The essence of impressionistic Chinese painting is "omission": using the fewest lines and least ink to express the greatest rhythmic vitality. The fewer the details, the easier to convey rhythm. Omission produces two effects: removing unnecessary objects stimulates the viewer's imagination; and it makes the bamboo leaves feel as if they "contain infinite vitality whether resting in moonlight or shaking in a storm."

05
Artist's Manifesto
New Ideas, Not Footsteps of the Ancients
"My calligraphy may not be excellent, but it springs from new ideas and does not follow the ancients — that is a joy." This is a watershed declaration in Chinese art history: the ultimate value of art lies not in technical refinement or imitation of the ancients, but in the independent spirit of the creator. Huang Tingjian recorded that Su "was extremely generous with his writing — except when asked. If someone begged for writing, he sternly rebuked them." But when inspiration struck on its own, "he would seize any paper on the table, regardless of quality, and cover it entirely."
🎯

Su Tungpo's Attitude

Never painted on demand. A patron once brought fine paper with detailed instructions. Su joked: "Do you think I'm selling vegetables?" Art is not a made-to-order commodity.

🛐

Traditional Artisan

Takes commissions. Copies the ancients. Meticulous representation. "Does it look alike?" as the standard. Lacks a personal artistic manifesto or independent spirit.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Su Tungpo's contribution to Chinese art goes beyond creating "ink bamboo" and "scholar's painting" — he also established a complete aesthetic theory: all questions of art are questions of rhythm; beauty lies not in static proportion but in dynamic vitality; the best painting does not depict what the eye sees but records the rhythm the heart feels. "Create new meaning; do not tread in the footsteps of the ancients" — these seven characters remain Chinese art's most resounding aesthetic manifesto.
