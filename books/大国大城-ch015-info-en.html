Great Country, Big City · Ch.7「The Urbanization Debate」

Urbanization Scale · Population Density · Migrants · Farmland · Environment This chapter debunks five popular myths about urbanization — excessive concentration, carrying capacity limits, prohibitive citizenization costs, the farmland red line, and the big-city pollution paradox — countering each with facts and data.

This is Book Chapter 7, systematically refuting five common urbanization myths. Myth 1 — "China is too concentrated": data show China's inter-city population size gap is among the world's lowest (spatial Gini coefficient 0.4234), and most Chinese cities are below their optimal size. Myth 2 — "Carrying capacity limits": cities consistently shatter population forecasts, proving that technology and management can continually expand capacity (Qingcaosha Reservoir solved water supply; west-to-east power transmission solved electricity; subways solved transportation). Myth 3 — "Citizenization costs too much": five systematic biases inflate estimates; actual annual incremental cost is only ~640.9 billion yuan. Myth 4 — "Urbanization vs. farmland": rural construction land is 4× urban land; if migrant homesteads are re-cultivated, farmland increases. Myth 5 — "Bigger cities = more pollution": dense cities are actually greener (Vermont uses 6× more gasoline per capita than NYC). The right prescription: "live smaller, live closer, drive less."

Myth 1
Is China Over-Concentrated? — No, Quite the Opposite
A popular view: 30 years of resource concentration in eastern big cities means China is too agglomerated. Data say otherwise. Henderson's study of 2,684 cities across 142 countries: global spatial Gini coefficient = 0.5619; China = 0.4234, the lowest among 7 major population countries. Japan is highest (0.6579). Most Chinese cities are below their optimal size — international research confirms that labor mobility restrictions mean Chinese cities need expansion, not contraction. While inter-city GDP gaps have widened, gaps in population and built-up area have not — resembling Central Asian former planned economies. China's problem is not too many big cities, but that its big cities are not big enough and there are too few of them.
02
Myth 2
Carrying Capacity or Vitality?
Beijing's population plans have been shattered years ahead for decades: 1983 plan for 10M by 2000 → 10M already in 1986; 1991 plan for 12.5M by 2010 → 13.82M in 2000; 2004 plan for 18M by 2020 → 19.61M in 2010. Shanghai mirrors this pattern. Repeated breaches are cited as reasons to control population — but few ask: are today's "urban ills" precisely because planned populations lagged far behind reality? "Carrying capacity" factors are all mutable through technology and management: Qingcaosha Reservoir (7.19M m³/day supply, serving 10M people); west-to-east power transmission ending Shanghai's electricity shortages; Tokyo's rail transit handling 80% of commuters. Shanghai's 2013 plan shifted language from "control" to "forecast" (~26.5M) — this is progress. Population repeatedly exceeding "carrying capacity" is precisely the manifestation of a city's vitality.

Myth 3
Citizenization Costs Severely Overestimated — Five Systematic Errors
Local governments oppose hukou reform citing "fiscal burdens," with studies claiming costs of 100,000-140,000 yuan per person, totaling 20-50 trillion yuan. The author identifies five major errors: ① Ignoring scale economies — marginal cost is far below average cost (adding 5 students to a classroom of 30 just needs more seats). ② Double-counting — cost = urban-rural gap in per-capita public service, not total urban cost (difference in per-student education funding for middle school: only 742 yuan/year). ③ Total expenditure vs. annual flow — actual annual incremental cost is ~640.9 billion yuan, not 20-50 trillion. ④ Confusing individual and public costs — "five social insurances and one housing fund" are mostly paid by individuals and firms; government subsidies are minimal. ⑤ Ignoring benefits — citizenization brings consumption gains, reduced public security costs, human capital accumulation. True citizenization cost is far lower than claimed.
04
Myth 4
Does Urbanization Threaten Farmland? — The Opposite Is True
Myth: Urbanization → urban expansion → threatens farmland. Fact: in 2008, rural settlements occupied 165,300 km² vs. urban construction land of 40,600 km² — rural is 4× urban! If farmers moving to cities recultivate their homesteads, total farmland increases. The 2001-2006 farmland decline was primarily due to the "Grain for Green" program (post-1998 floods), not urbanization. UK's farmland barely decreased during rapid urbanization; Japan's slightly increased. Another myth: "Eastern cities have no land left" — open Baidu Maps satellite view: Shanghai still has 1,990 km² of farmland, 1/3 of its total area! Even in the densest city, low-density industrial parks can convert to residential. At 5,000 people/km², converting half of Shanghai's farmland to construction land could accommodate ~5 million people, generating ~200,000 yuan per new resident in fiscal space. Urbanization doesn't threaten farmland; with proper institutions, it protects it.

Myth 5
Big City = Pollution? Density Is Greener
Intuition says big cities are more polluted. David Owen's "Green Metropolis" reveals a paradox: 79% of NYC's greenhouse gas emissions come from buildings (US average: 32%). NYC's deputy mayor used this to argue for change — Owen countered: this proves NYC is green! GHGs come mainly from vehicle exhaust; New Yorkers drive less, so building emissions appear proportionally higher. Compare Vermont (density 26/km²) with NYC (density 10,000/km²): Vermont uses 4× more electricity per capita and 6× more gasoline per capita! The green solution: higher density — "live smaller, live closer, drive less." Zheng Siqi (Tsinghua) found: each 1,000 people/km² increase in density reduces annual household taxi and bus CO₂ by 0.424 and 0.837 tons respectively. Shanghai's Suzhou Creek was unbearable 20 years ago; Shanghai now has better air quality than most East China cities. Environmental degradation is not the result of urban development — it's a passing phase of industrialization.
06
Livability
What Makes a Livable City? — Human Nature First
EIU's Global Livability Index: Melbourne, Vienna, Vancouver top 3; 4 Australian + 3 Canadian cities in top 10 — but these countries have extremely low population density (Canada <35M, Australia <23M). This ranking essentially proxies national density. NUS's livability index is more "human-centered": five categories — economic vitality, environmental sustainability, safety & stability, quality of life & diversity, good governance & effective leadership. Top 10: 7 European, 2 Asian (Singapore #3, Hong Kong #9). China's "Beishangguang" rank 46, 39, 47 respectively. Key insight: today's livable cities (London, Tokyo) were severely polluted half a century ago. Environmental improvement comes through industrial restructuring — manufacturing out, technology up, green lifestyles, vehicle-use controls. The right path is not regression to pre-industrial poverty but further development, structural adjustment, and lifestyle transformation. Livable = development + human nature, not anti-urbanization.
07
Pensions
Who Will Support the Elderly? — Migrants Are Key
A taxi driver asked: "Aren't there too many migrants?" The author replied: "Have you thought about who will support you in old age?" Shanghai case: by end of 2011, 3.48M registered elderly (60+) = 24.5% of registered population; by 2015, exceeded 30%. Life expectancy reached 82.29 years. Pension fund deficit of -10.35 billion yuan in 2010 turned into +21.16 billion in 2011 — mainly because a 2011 policy shifted migrant comprehensive insurance to urban employee pension insurance, swelling contributors from 5.43M to 9.27M (+70%). Regional solutions to pension shortfalls: increase labor productivity and rejuvenate the age structure through population inflow. If Shanghai kept its population frozen, pension insolvency would be imminent. Because the pension system lacks national pooling, inflow cities (Shanghai, Beijing) actually have better age structures thanks to young migrant workers — half of Shanghai's working-age population is now non-local.

08
Funding
Where Does the Money Come From?
A northern Jiangsu city government document: turning farmers into urban residents costs 150,000-200,000 yuan/person; transferring 1.6M farmers over 10 years would require 240-320 billion yuan — deemed unaffordable. This confusion comes from narrowly interpreting "urbanization" as "local urbanization." If farmers can urbanize elsewhere and bring re-cultivated homestead construction land quotas to high-value cities, the problem dissolves. Government spending efficiency also needs review: only 1.6% of land transfer net revenue goes to low-rent housing (requirement: ≥10%); central-western infrastructure is already overbuilt; large subsidies for farmer housing construction (yet 1/3 of rural housing in the central-west is vacant). Solutions: grow the pie + cross-regional construction land quota trading. Chongqing's land ticket auctions reached over 300,000 yuan/mu — a farmer selling land could receive ~250,000 yuan. If cross-provincial trading were allowed, compensation would be higher. What China needs is policy breakthroughs and institutional building, not avoiding the issue by banning cross-regional land quota trading.
09
Land Quotas
The Quota Dilemma: Land Reclamation and Hollow Villages
The ban on cross-provincial construction land quotas creates absurd outcomes: coastal cities with plenty of land but no quotas resort to large-scale sea reclamation — China's island area is shrinking dramatically, destroying ecological value. Meanwhile, inland provinces build industrial parks and new towns everywhere with no enterprises or people — factories sit idle. Population-outflow rural areas have massive vacant housing, but policy only allows within-village homestead trading — in outflow areas, there's no within-village demand. Why not allow farmers settled in cities to bring their homestead use rights (construction land quotas) to their host cities? The author calls for bold reform. Through inter-regional farmland offset-balancing and construction land quota reallocation, a virtuous cycle of homestead recultivation and urban expansion can be achieved — and total national farmland can increase. This is already being piloted in the Central Plains Economic Zone.

Conclusion
Beyond Urbanization Myths: Conclusions and Direction
After clearing all five myths, the correct direction for China's urbanization becomes clear: ① Re-concentrate — China's population is far from sufficiently concentrated; big cities aren't big enough and are too few; continued agglomeration boosts efficiency. ② Expand capacity — technology and management continually push back supposed limits; investment, not demand control, is the answer. ③ Citizenization is affordable — annual incremental cost ~640.9 billion yuan vs. 13 trillion in fiscal revenue — entirely manageable. ④ Farmland is not the problem — with the right institutions, urbanization actually protects farmland. ⑤ Green = density — high-density cities have lower per-capita carbon emissions; "crowded" is precisely the feature of green cities. Jane Jacobs said it in 1961: championing population density is not rebellion but a return to human nature. Tao Yuanming said "when the heart is distant, the place naturally secludes itself" — when you tire of big-city life, find peace within, don't try to make the city as low-density as the countryside.
"The vitality of great cities comes down to human nature. Most people, most of the time, are afraid of being alone."
— Lu Ming

🔑 Core Conclusion
Five myths about urbanization — over-concentration, carrying capacity limits, prohibitive citizenization costs, the farmland red line, and big-city pollution — are each refuted by facts and data. China's inter-city population gap is the world's lowest; most cities still need expansion. Carrying capacity expands with technology and management. Real citizenization cost is 640.9 billion yuan annually, not 20-50 trillion total. Urbanization doesn't threaten farmland — rural land is 4× urban land. Dense cities are greener. Livability is about development and human nature, not retreat to low-density pastoral fantasies. The right path for urbanization is agglomeration, openness, and integration.
