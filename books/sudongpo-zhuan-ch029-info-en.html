Su Dongpo · Ch29「Return to the North」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer In the third year of the Yuanfu era (1100), Emperor Zhezong died at just twenty-four. Huizong succeeded him, and a new Empress Dowager took the regency — all Yuanyou ministers were pardoned. Su Dongpo could finally leave Hainan, but the summer heat and river damp were silently consuming his life. How much time did the returning exile have left? Why was this triumphant journey also his final farewell? Su Dongpo answered with the last journey of his life: death is not to be feared — regret is.

Su Dongpo's journey north was both a triumph and a farewell to the world. Everywhere people welcomed him, accompanied him to scenic spots and temples, begged him to inscribe poems and write calligraphy. But in the June heat he contracted dysentery, and his body rapidly failed. His final regret was not seeing his brother — "I resent that Heaven will not let us reunite — what can I do against Heaven?" On the twenty-eighth day of the seventh month, 1101, the great poet died in Changzhou at sixty-four. He did not die at the hands of an emperor or Zhang Chun, but from a microscopic amoeba — an anticlimax that somehow makes his life even more legendary. Su Dongpo proved with his whole life: a man can be defeated but not destroyed.

01
Turn of Fate
The Death of Zhezong and Rise of Huizong: Fortune Reverses
Emperor Zhezong died at twenty-four, leaving a lifeless collection of scholars. Huizong succeeded him and pardoned the Yuanyou ministers — Su Dongpo could finally leave Hainan. Huizong would later leave behind thirty-one sons, several fine paintings, and a kingdom in chaos — but in this brief window, the Empress Dowager protected the Yuanyou partisans, and all exiled scholars were pardoned.

02
Journey Home
Triumph and Farewell: Joy and Sorrow on the Road North
Su Dongpo's journey north was a triumph — everywhere people welcomed him, begged for his poems and calligraphy. Arriving in Guangzhou to reunite with his children and grandchildren, he wrote it was "as if waking from a great dream." During a seventy-day wait for a boat at Gan County, he busied himself treating the townspeople's illnesses. But the journey also brought his densest farewells: six servants died of plague, and his old friend Wu Fugu closed his eyes before him. When someone joked they'd thought he was dead, Su Dongpo replied: "That's right — I died and ran into Zhang Chun halfway through the underworld, so I decided to turn back."

03
Final Journey
The Amoeba: It Wasn't Exile That Killed the Poet
Su Dongpo did not die at the hands of an emperor or Zhang Chun, but from a microscopic amoeba. On June 3rd he likely contracted amoebic dysentery, self-diagnosing it as "overindulgence in cold things." He had someone buy astragalus root — Chinese medicine holds it replenishes blood and internal organs. The great painter Mi Fu visited repeatedly, bringing "ophiopogon soup." "Last night I didn't close my eyes till dawn, sat upright feeding the mosquitoes" — his final letter to Mi Fu. His sixty-four-year-old body could bear no more.

04
Final Regret
"Heaven Will Not Let Us Reunite": The Last Words
Su Dongpo's final regret was not seeing his brother — "I resent that Heaven will not let us reunite — what can I do against Heaven?" Ziyou had already returned to the old farm in Yingchang and wrote urging Dongpo to join him. Dongpo wanted to go but was embarrassed to impose his household of thirty — family, children, grandchildren, servants — on his brother. He decided to settle first in Changzhou and reunite later. But on July 28, 1101, he died there. Huizong would later lose his throne and be captured by the Jurchens — every amassing emperor must pay a price. But Su Dongpo's name lives forever in the hearts of the Chinese people.

05
Immortal Name
The Poet Dies, the Name Lives: Defeated but Not Destroyed
Su Dongpo died from a microscopic amoeba — not political persecution, not the hardships of exile. This seems anticlimactic, yet it reveals life's deepest truth: the greatest enemy has never been external suffering but finite life itself. Yet in sixty-four years, Su Dongpo lived a life longer than most emperors — for his name still lives in every Chinese heart. A man can be defeated but not destroyed.
