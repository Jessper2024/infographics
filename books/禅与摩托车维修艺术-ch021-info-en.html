Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance · Ch.21 'The Pioneer's Chaos and Quality's Connection'

Chris says "You're not very brave, are you?" — descending, the Chautauqua enters the everyday world

Some distance from the summit, Chris says: "You're not very brave, are you?" "No," says father, "but you have great admiration for my intelligence." Chris has accepted not climbing to the top. Descending, father shifts the Chautauqua from abstract philosophy to the practical world of daily life: he knows the summit discovery was "very exciting and very important," but if that were all the Chautauqua had to say, it should end. Yet there is more: "the endless, monotonous work and years ahead of us." Sylvia, on the first day, called everyone heading the same direction a "funeral procession" — now his work is to enter that procession from a broader angle. He admits Phaedrus's claim that "Quality is the Tao" may not be true — any description of Quality is worse than no description. The real beneficiary is reason: "he advanced a method of expanding reason's scope to encompass the irrational." The chaos and spiritual incoherence of the twentieth century stem from "an extreme lack of recognition of these irrational elements." He proposes: Quality IS the Buddha — if true, it can provide a rational foundation for the unification of humanity's three divided realms of experience: religion, art, and science.

01 Brave 01 You're Not Very Brave, Are You?
Descending, Chris suddenly says: "You're not very brave, are you?" "No," father replies, tearing the skin off a stick of Italian salami with his teeth, "but you have great admiration for my intelligence." Chris's expression shows he's accepted the decision not to reach the summit. Mountain spring water is "the most delicious drink in the world." Chris volunteers to carry heavier items — father gratefully transfers some luggage: "He's always very considerate when he's in a good mood." The brush grows so dense they must use a machete to cut through, two or three branches with every step. Zigzagging down steep slopes, crossing a creek with a rope, finally hitching a ride back to Bozeman — arriving after dark, collapsing on the bed in a hotel room.

02
02 Chaos 02 The Pioneer's Chaos: Others Clean Up Behind Them
Father pivots the Chautauqua from abstraction to the everyday world. He introduces a rarely noted pioneer trait: "They see only their own far-off goals and completely ignore all the chaos they create. Others must follow behind them cleaning up." This work is inglorious and tedious. You may feel depressed before doing it, but "once your emotions get used to a low profile, it won't feel so bad." The summit discovery of the metaphysical relationship between Quality and the Tao is thrilling, but the greater task is: "the relationship between this discovery and the endless, monotonous work and years ahead of us."

03
03 Humility 03 Self-Reflection: Any Description Is Worse Than None
Father calmly admits: "I have no idea whether Phaedrus's belief that Quality is the Tao is true or not. I don't know any way to prove it." All he did was "compare his understanding of one mysterious entity with another" — he certainly thought them identical, but he may not fully understand Quality, nor fully understand the Tao. The book itself contains advice for the wise he should have heeded more. "All his metaphysical mountain climbing is completely useless to our understanding of Quality, and completely useless to our understanding of the Tao." This sounds like a rejection, but it isn't: any description of Quality is a form of definition, and therefore inevitably flawed. "His description of Quality is even worse than no description, because description is likely to obstruct others' understanding of Quality." The real beneficiary is reason: he advanced a method of expanding its scope to include the irrational.

04
04 Unity 04 Three into One: A Common Center for Religion, Art & Science
Phaedrus said "Quality is the Buddha" — if true, this assertion can "provide a rational basis for uniting the three divided domains of human experience: religion, art, and science." On art: Phaedrus has already demonstrated through rhetoric that "art is a high-quality endeavor — from human works one can see that art is god." The Old English root of Quality and Buddha is the same word: "Good and god." Now the most urgent domain to construct is science: "The view that science and technology are free from value judgment must be stopped." Tomorrow he will begin from this point.

05
05 Practice 05 The Funeral Procession: Entering the Crowd from a Broader Angle
Sylvia, on the first day, saw everyone heading in the same direction and called it a "funeral procession." Now father's work is to "enter this procession from a broader angle." He acknowledges that the chaos and spiritual incoherence of the twentieth century "comes precisely from an extreme lack of recognition of these irrational elements." His goal is to bring the summit vision into daily life: in the Bozeman hotel, in old uniforms, two days unshaven, dumping creek stones from boots — this is where philosophy meets life. Philosophy's value lies in whether it can change "the endless, monotonous work and years ahead."

💡 Key Takeaway

Pioneers see only far-off goals, ignoring the chaos they create. Phaedrus was such a pioneer — he discovered the identity of Quality and the Tao, but this discovery must be tested at the everyday, monotonous level. Any "description" of Quality is more dangerous than "no description," because it may obstruct others from experiencing Quality directly. The real beneficiary is not Quality itself, but the expansion of reason's boundary to encompass the irrational. Religion, art, and science can find a common center of integration in Quality. The remaining work is to bring all this back to daily life — back into the "funeral procession." If philosophy is only summit ecstasy, it stays on the summit. If it can change "the years ahead," then it is worthwhile.

Source: Robert M. Pirsig, Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance
