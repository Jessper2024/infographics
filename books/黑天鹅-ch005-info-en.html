The Black Swan · Ch5「You Can't Judge By Past Experience Alone」

Negative Empiricism · Falsification Over Verification A turkey is fed every day for 1,000 days. It believes humans are kind — until Day 1,001, Thanksgiving. We are the same: no past disaster does not mean no future disaster. The modern world is Extremistan, ruled by rare events. This chapter reveals: reliable reasoning comes not from finding proof, but from finding counterexamples.

This chapter is the logical foundation of the entire book. Taleb demonstrates that we are wired to predict the future from past experience — a fatal cognitive habit in the Extremistan of the modern world. The core error is the "round-trip fallacy": confusing "no evidence of Black Swans" with "evidence of no Black Swans." Moving from cognitive biases (confirmation bias, domain specificity) toward the solution: Popper's "negative empiricism" — approaching truth by seeking refutation rather than confirmation. Knowledge is fundamentally asymmetric: 1,000 days of observation cannot prove you right, but one day can prove you wrong.

01
Logical Fallacy
The Round-Trip Fallacy: "No Evidence" ≠ "Evidence of No"
The most universal and dangerous logical error — the two statements have a huge logical gap, yet appear almost identical in our minds.If I say "I had breakfast with Simpson and he didn't kill anyone," that doesn't prove his innocence. If I say "I napped on train tracks and survived," that doesn't prove tracks are safe. Yet your brain slides toward the latter. In medicine, NED (No Evidence of Disease) is routinely treated as END (Evidence of No Disease). Breast milk's value was denied for decades by doctors committing the same fallacy: confusing "no evidence of breast milk's advantages" with "evidence that breast milk has no advantages."

02
Cognitive Trap
Domain Specificity: Classroom Knowledge Doesn't Transfer to Life
Our behavioral responses, thinking patterns, and intuitions depend on context, not logic.Statistics professors who ace the law of large numbers in class will, on the street, answer that "the larger hospital is more likely" for 60% male births — forgetting statistics in real life. At a luxury gym, people take the elevator up, then head straight for the stair-climber. The brain lacks a central computer that applies the same logical rules to all situations. Some problems we solve in social contexts but fail at in abstract form — and vice versa.

03
Cognitive Bias
Confirmation Bias: We Naturally Seek Proof, Not Disproof
With tools and a fool, anything is easy to find. You can find "evidence" for anything.Wason's 2-4-6 experiment revealed a shocking fact: subjects were asked to discover the rule behind a number sequence ("ascending numbers"), yet almost none found it — because they only proposed examples confirming their guesses, never trying to falsify. Diplomats showcase achievements, not failures. Mathematicians cite successful applications, not wasted time. Chess grandmasters look for where a speculative move creates weakness (falsification); novices seek confirmatory moves. Soros constantly looks for evidence that he's wrong — that is true confidence.

04
Scientific Method
Negative Empiricism: Approach Truth Through Counterexamples
Popper proposed falsification: we can approach truth through negative examples.Seeing white swans cannot prove "all swans are white," but one black swan proves otherwise. Finding a malignant tumor proves cancer; not finding one does not prove its absence. Knowledge has fundamental asymmetry: 1,000 days of observation cannot prove you right, but one day can prove you wrong. You need not be a complete skeptic — just a "semi-skeptic": be certain about what you can be certain of, and skeptical about the rest. This is the foundation of Popper's "open society."

05
Evolutionary Mismatch
Ancient Instincts Meet Extremistan: We Need Longer "Suspension" Periods
Our reasoning instincts were designed for ancestral environments, but the modern world is Extremistan — ruled by rare events.In the ancestral world, rapid inference worked: run from a tiger. Events repeated frequently. But book sales, market crashes, and wars are not constrained by such limits. Concluding that "a writer has no talent" takes far longer than 1,000 days. We inherited instincts fit for our origin environment, maladaptive to today's hyper-complex, hyper-information world. The sources of Black Swans have multiplied beyond prediction — network effects among economic entities amplify once-minor events into catastrophic shocks.
🦁

Ancestral World (Mediocristan)

Fast inference works: run from tigers. Events repeat, experience is reliable. Inference errors have small costs.

🌐

Modern World (Extremistan)

Fast inference is fatal: Black Swans don't repeat. Rare events dominate everything. Inference errors have enormous costs.

🔑 Key Takeaway
You cannot judge the future by past experience alone. Reliable reasoning comes not from finding proof you're right (verification), but from finding proof you're wrong (falsification). One black swan is worth a thousand white ones. Remember the three asymmetries: evidence asymmetry (counterexamples outweigh confirmations), knowledge asymmetry (knowing what is wrong is easier than knowing what is right), and environmental asymmetry (inference risk in Extremistan dwarfs that of Mediocristan). Be a semi-skeptic: on important decisions, seeking counterexamples protects you far better than seeking confirmation.
