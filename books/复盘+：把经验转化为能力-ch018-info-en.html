PITFALLS & FACTORS
Five Review Pitfalls and Seven Key Success Factors
Lenovo's years of practice reveal five common review pitfalls: trying to prove oneself right instead of learning; going through the motions; fault-finding and blaming instead of learning; deflecting responsibility with excuses; and jumping to conclusions without deep analysis. To counter these, effective review requires seven key factors: an open mindset (learning is a door that only opens from within); candor and mutual trust; truth-seeking grounded in facts; harnessing collective wisdom (leader-led); deep self-reflection and owning subjective causes; digging to the root without "engraving the boat to find the sword"; and driving improvement rather than assigning blame.

TRAP GUIDE
The 25 "Traps" of Review and Their Countermeasures
Throughout the review process, 25 common challenges or traps are distributed across six phases. Goal review traps: no goals, vague goals, lack of consensus, lack of planning. Result evaluation traps: reporting only successes, reciting chronologies, blind-men-and-elephant, Rashomon effect, jumping ahead. Root-cause analysis traps: scratching the surface, tangled mess, narrow thinking, blaming external factors, chasing too many issues, fixating only on failures. Lesson extraction traps: case-by-case thinking, hollow abstractions, externalizing, mistaking coincidence for pattern. Application traps: no follow-up action, shirking responsibility, nobody tracking progress. Facilitation traps: poor design, insufficient preparation, low engagement, absent facilitation, lack of follow-through.

CORE COMPETENCIES
Three Core Competencies for Effective Review
To navigate the many challenges in the review process, three core competencies are essential. Review Facilitation — a qualified facilitator is the key to success, requiring mastery of review principles, facilitation methods, industry experience, and the practical ability to handle traps. Systems Thinking — the indispensable skill for tackling complexity: overcome the Rashomon effect, avoid the blind-men-and-elephant trap (the US Army uses observers + video + laser sensors to reconstruct "iron facts"). Knowledge Extraction — practitioners, guided by the facilitator, distill lessons through structured dialogue, distinct from the traditional expert-interview approach to knowledge capture.

TOOLS & METHODS
Mastering Methods and Tools: The Arsenal of the 3P9S Method™
Confucius said, "A craftsman who wishes to do his work well must first sharpen his tools." As a structured team learning method, review can leverage many facilitation tools (brainstorming, World Café). Under the 3P9S Method™ framework, every step has corresponding methods and tools: pre-meeting preparation clarifies topic/purpose/scope/format; meeting facilitation spans review opening, goal recall, result evaluation, root-cause analysis, lesson extraction, and action planning; post-meeting follow-through focuses on tracking actions and evaluating impact.

Doing review well is far from easy — it demands resonance among participants, facilitators, and the organizational environment.
