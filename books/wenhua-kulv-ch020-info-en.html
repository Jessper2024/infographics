Wenhua Kulv · Ch.20 "The Peephole at the Xie Family Home"

The life journey of director Xie Jin—how art, suffering, family, and humanity converged magnificently in one man

This chapter opens Part Three—"The Journey of Life"—of Wenhua Kulv. Here Yu Qiuyu clarifies the true meaning of "bitter journey": it is not the bitterness of travel, but the bitterness of creation. The suffering of creation connecting into a life's journey—this is culture's destiny. Xie Jin's life is the most profound human interpretation of this: he only "matured" at sixty, capturing humanity in the cracks of political discourse, and ultimately interrogating the era by the standard of humanity. With three disabled children at home, he transformed a disordered mental vortex into a shrine of humanism. In 2008, he returned to his ancestral Dongshan and passed away quietly amid 1,600 years of family spirit—as if having caught the last glimmer of light through the peephole.

01
Peephole
A-San's peephole: the metaphor of a cultural creator
Xie Jin's intellectually disabled son A-San spent every day pressed against the peephole waiting for his father—his eyebrows worn away from constant rubbing, never retreating except to sleep or eat. Yu Qiuyu told Xie Jin: "You ARE A-San! Like your son, you found a hole in a closed door and fixed your gaze there relentlessly, watching for light, waiting for human connection." Xie Jin adding glimmers of humanity to films in a closed era was exactly this—"Your peephole became the peephole of the entire nation's audience." A-San never knew what death was. After Xie Jin died, only A-Si and his 83-year-old mother remained. A-Si's decades-long task was handing his father his bag and shoes—when white flowers covered the slippers, he bent down, retrieved them, and carefully placed them by the door.

02
Artistic Path
Maturing at sixty: from the cracks of an era to the interrogation of humanity
At sixty, Xie Jin said: "Now, I've finally matured along with the entire nation!" His artistic journey divides into an exploratory period and a mature period. During the exploratory period, Stage Sisters moved textile factory women workers to tears—the verdict on "humanism" vs "class struggle" was decided by those tears. In his mature period, films like Hibiscus Town turned the tables, interrogating the era by the standard of humanity, leading the entire nation into a difficult yet beautiful awakening. Xie Jin wasn't a pretty scene scattered on the shore—he was a complete river, allowing everything along the way to find its place relative to him. His greatest achievement: using his own life to connect the entire tortuous logic of Chinese cinema after 1949.

03
Creative Hunger
"I'm just hungry": the creator refuses to be branded
Xie Jin's deepest pain in old age was being treated as a "brand" to be commercialized—someone called his name "something to be developed" for cultural industry. He wanted to make films, to hold the megaphone himself, to crouch behind the camera orchestrating everything. He wanted to remake The Peach Blossom Fan, to film the Chinese laborers on the Transcontinental Railroad, to make The Diaries of John Rabe—"You all say I'm too old and should step back, but I told you long ago—I only matured at sixty. So do the math..." For Xie Jin, this unstoppable creative passion was "the dignity of a powerful life demanding self-completion." Yet he increasingly performed vitality—speaking louder, challenging everyone to drinking contests. Until one day he stumbled on a train platform, face ashen—the age wrapped inside the leather jacket and red scarf could no longer hide.

04
Family
A shrine of humanism: greatness built within chaos
Of Xie Jin's four children with wife Xu Dawen, only Xie Yan was mentally normal. The family was poor and he was impossibly busy. Yet before Hollywood stars and French directors, he would ceremoniously present his disabled sons with eyes full of appreciation. Taking A-Si on trips, he'd turn back every few minutes from the front seat to ask "A-Si, are you tired?"—each glance's tenderness could melt snow-capped mountains. But fate's cruelty came in waves: the family's only normal child—Xie Yan, an elegant gentleman returned from overseas study—died before his father in 2008. He hid his illness, arranged everything, put on clean clothes, and went to the hospital, never to emerge. Xie Jin stood before the hospital bed like a suddenly desiccated statue, for a very long time. The day after Xie Yan's burial, Xie Jin embraced a friend who had also lost a son and wailed—a cry "like a tiger's roar, a wolf's howl, a dragon's cry, a lion's roar." That day, even West Lake sobbed.
05
Dongshan Xie
1,600 years of spirit: from Xie An to Xie Jin
Xie Jin's distant ancestor was Xie An, the Eastern Jin prime minister who won the Battle of Feishui—the Chinese idiom "Dongshan Comeback" originates from Xie An's story of re-emerging from seclusion at Dongshan. Xie An's grandnephew Xie Lingyun was the father of Chinese landscape poetry; his niece Xie Daoyun's talent far exceeded that of her husband (Wang Xizhi's son). The Dongshan Xie clan accumulated 1,600 years of spirit. Xie Jin cared deeply about this lineage but never spoke of it publicly—what he cherished was the mountain, the village, the house, the name, the spirit. On October 18, 2008, Xie Jin returned to his hometown Chunhui Middle School for an event. That night he ate a cold supper, drank no wine, and lay down to sleep—on his left side, never to wake. He was one month and three days short of his 85th birthday. Yu Qiuyu writes: "With this inexpressible deep belonging, he would return alone, to draw the final period of his life before that great assembly of ancestors."
🔑 Key Takeaway
Xie Jin's life portrait is the most ultimate human interpretation of Wenhua Kulv. Yu Qiuyu clarifies at the chapter's opening: the "bitter journey" is not about the bitterness of travel, but the bitterness of creation—the suffering of creation connecting into a life's journey, this is culture's destiny. Xie Jin proved with his life: the true cultural creator is one who finds the peephole of humanity in the closed door of an era and watches relentlessly, one who builds a shrine of humanism from a disordered mental vortex, one who maintains creative hunger until death and refuses to be branded. From Xie An's 1,600-year lineage to A-Si placing slippers by the door—cultural dignity ultimately rests in the tender backward glance that asks: "A-Si, are you tired?"
