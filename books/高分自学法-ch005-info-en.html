01
Real Case
Three Months: From Third-from-Last to Third-in-Class — A Learning Miracle
A ninth-grader who couldn't even recite personal pronouns turned his academic life around in three months with just two principles and three review cycles. First tutoring session: his pencil only traced circles on math problems, his English reading came out in mumbles, and all his subjects scored in the 30s — ranked third-from-last. Instead of assigning homework, the author taught only the method: Principle 1 — listen attentively in every class. Principle 2 — review what was taught. Three-tier review: immediately after class (like skimming a novel), every evening (all subjects from that day), and every weekend (the full week's content). Three months later, final exams: third in class — counting from the top. The student discovered in amazement: "When I opened my textbook before the exam, I remembered everything the teacher had said."

03
Scientific Evidence
Debunking the "Talent" Myth: Anders Ericsson's Berlin Experiment
Even in music — the field most associated with innate talent — skill differences are explained entirely by practice volume. In the 1990s, Ericsson's team at the Berlin Academy of Music divided 3,600 students into three tiers: elite, excellent, and average. Superficially identical: all started instruments around age 8, all spent ~51 hours/week on music activities. But the secret lay in one number: accumulated solo practice by age 18 — elite: 7,410 hrs; excellent: 5,310 hrs; average: 3,420 hrs. The conclusion: "Apart from differences in practice volume, we cannot find any trace of talent anywhere. If talent does exist, its role is far less significant than people believe."

04
Quantity Builds Quality
The 10,000-Hour Rule + 10-Year Rule: Geniuses Are Practice Geniuses
No field contains geniuses who skipped extensive practice — every person labeled a "genius" accumulated massive solo practice behind the scenes. Daniel Levitin's "10,000-Hour Rule": expertise in any domain requires ~3 hours/day, 20 hours/week, sustained for 10 years. John Hayes's "10-Year Rule": composers invariably wrote their first successful piece only after 10 years of composing. People called geniuses have IQs merely between 115-130 — 14% of the global population falls in that range. The difference isn't hardware specs but focused investment in practice.

05
Correct Method
Deliberate Practice: Identify Weaknesses → Repeat → Obtain Feedback
Time alone won't produce improvement — deliberate practice is essential. Three steps: ① Identify weaknesses (find what you don't understand), ② Repeat (confront it head-on), ③ Obtain feedback (close the book and test yourself). Noel Tichy divided human activities into three zones: Comfort Zone, Growth Zone, and Panic Zone. Real progress happens in the Growth Zone — things that aren't easy but can be achieved with effort. Learning follows the same pattern: find concepts you couldn't fully grasp, drill them repeatedly, and use the simplest feedback method — "close the book and say it out loud." As violinist Nathan Milstein's teacher said: "Practice with your fingers takes all day; focused practice takes just an hour and a half."

⚠️ Critical: "If I just listen to lectures repeatedly, I'll understand eventually" or "If I just reread the textbook enough times, I'll memorize everything" — this is staying in the Comfort Zone. Only by pushing your learning into the Growth Zone — gripping your weak points and drilling them repeatedly — will your ability truly improve.
🔑 Key Takeaway
Self-study is something everyone can do well — no special talent is required. Differences in ability come from differences in the volume and method of self-study. Quantity builds quality: the 10,000-Hour Rule shows that world-class performance in any domain requires sustained accumulation; quality breakthroughs come from "deliberate practice" — identify weaknesses → repeat → obtain feedback — the core learning formula (Read → Memorize → Verify). Tutoring centers, courses, and reference books are merely tools that lead the horse to water — drinking is something only you can do. Believe you can do it, increase your solo study time, use the right method — that's all there is to it.
