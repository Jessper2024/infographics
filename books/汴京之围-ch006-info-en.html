The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.5 "A Victory Bought with Money"

Xuanhe Years 4–5 · The Price of Recovering Yanjing Guo Yaoshi, full of fighting spirit, launched a surprise assault on Yanjing — only to fail when Liu Guangshi's reinforcements never arrived. Zhao Liangsi struggled tenaciously at the negotiating table, only to face Jin's escalating demands for annual tribute and taxes. In the end, the Song "bought" back Yanjing — an empty city.

Facing the Liao Empress Dowager Xiao's distrust, the Han general Guo Yaoshi led 8,000 crack troops to surrender to the Song, bringing the two prefectures of Zhuozhou and Yizhou. Emperor Huizong was overjoyed, yet once again wavered between "fight ourselves or let the Jin fight." Guo Yaoshi's surprise assault on Yanjing succeeded — but Liu Guangshi's reinforcements never arrived, and the victory slipped away; Liu Yanqing's army of 500,000 was routed when Xiao Gan set their camp ablaze. The Jin swept in and took Yanjing. Zhao Liangsi and Ma Kuo argued tenaciously at the negotiating table, but without military strength as backing, they retreated step by step — from the original vision of recovering all Sixteen Prefectures to gaining only six prefectures around Yanjing, and having to pay the Jin an additional one million strings of cash in annual taxes. Before withdrawing, the Jin abducted 30,000 wealthy Yanjing households, leaving the Song an empty city and a population that had lost all faith in their new rulers.

01
Defector's Pledge
Guo Yaoshi brings the Ever-Victorious Army to the Song: two prefectures gained without a fight, but an even bigger peril is sown
After Empress Dowager Xiao seized power, her distrust of the Han Chinese deepened, and Guo Yaoshi, stationed at Zhuozhou, sensed the danger of a purge. When Xiao Gan arrived at Zhuozhou, Guo Yaoshi — at a banquet — actually tried to persuade Xiao Gan to defect face to face. Xiao Gan fled in panic. Guo Yaoshi then led 8,000 crack troops and 500 armored cavalry, together with Yizhou's garrison commander Gao Feng (5,000 troops), to surrender to the Song. For Emperor Huizong, who had suffered defeat after defeat, this was tremendous news. He immediately declared that the Song could recover Yanjing without Jin help — ordering his envoys to change route, while also keeping a backup plan: if the Song had already taken Yanjing, the Jin would not be invited in; otherwise, they would request Jin troops for a joint assault.
⚔️

The Ever-Victorious Army's Strength

Guo Yaoshi's unit was the only Song force that could actually fight. After surrendering, they achieved repeated victories: recovering Anci and Gu'an, defeating Xiao Gan. They stayed out of Song political infighting and thought only in military terms.

⚠️

The Emperor's Two-Pronged Plan

He ordered envoys to change route (from sea back to land), while instructing: if Yanjing is already taken, forbid Jin troops from entering; if not, invite Jin troops for a joint attack. Perpetually wavering.

02
Victory Slips Away
Guo Yaoshi's surprise assault on Yanjing succeeds, but Liu Guangshi's failure to reinforce leads to total annihilation
Guo Yaoshi saw the entire Liao army of 10,000 marching out to confront the Song forces and concluded that Yanjing must be undefended. He proposed personally leading 5,000 crack troops in a surprise assault; Liu Yanqing agreed. Guo Yaoshi, Gao Shixuan, and Yang Keshi crossed the Lugou River under cover of night, reached Yanjing by dawn, and Zhen Wuchen led 5,000 cavalry to storm the Yingchun Gate — Yanjing was within their grasp. But Empress Dowager Xiao refused to surrender. The fatal variable: Liu Guangshi's follow-up force never arrived. Xiao Gan dispatched 3,000 crack troops from Liangxiang to reinforce the city; Gao Shixuan died in battle, and Guo Yaoshi, having lost most of his men, escaped with Yang Keshi by rappelling from the city walls. Had Liu Guangshi arrived on time, Yanjing would already have been in Song hands.

03
Diplomatic Tug-of-War
Zhao Liangsi, deft but retreating step by step: diplomacy without military backing is destined for tragedy
Zhao Liangsi and Ma Kuo formed two sides of the same mission: Zhao Liangsi argued diligently every time, trying to secure every inch of territory for the Song; Ma Kuo transcended specific negotiations, convinced that only a strong military backing could guarantee interests. The Jin held greater respect for Ma Kuo (he was a warrior), but harbored deep frustration toward Zhao Liangsi — which ironically proved Zhao Liangsi's "success." Yet both men met the same fate: without military power as backing, envoys could only concede. Zhao Liangsi went from fighting for all sixteen prefectures, to settling for six, to accepting exorbitant taxes. Aguda spoke one cold but honest sentence: "I have taken nine-tenths of the Khitan lands, leaving only one-tenth at Yanjing, surrounded on three sides with only one side open for the Song army — yet how is it that you still cannot take it?"

04
Ritual Signals
From tents to Han rituals: the Jin's rapid sinicization signals they would never return to the northeast
In the third month of Xuanhe Year 5, Song envoys in Yanjing witnessed the Jin's astonishing transformation from a nomadic tribe to a ceremonial dynasty. Envoys holding tablets knelt, presented state documents, and performed five obeisances of gratitude — the entire complex set of Han-style rituals was replicated in full by the Jin. Liao defectors helped the Jin establish a complete ceremonial system. This carried a dangerous signal: the Song court had always imagined the Jin would return to the northeast to continue hunting after destroying Liao, but the Jin had already fallen in love with Han rituals and palaces. Though Aguda remained frugal, he now listened to musicians outside the city walls, and his sons pursued luxury. "They may never go back" — and that meant the Song was next.

05
Heavy Price
An empty city and hearts thoroughly lost: the brutal truth of the Yanjing handover
On the seventeenth day of the fourth month of Xuanhe Year 5, Tong Guan and Cai You finally led their army into Yanjing — but were greeted by "devastation everywhere, ruins as far as the eye could see, foxes visible among the rubble." Before the handover, the Jin had already looted the city clean — this was an army without regular pay, reliant on plunder for morale. Even more fatal: Song officials traded 30,000 wealthy Yanjing households in exchange for Guo Yaoshi's Ever-Victorious Army. The Jin told the abducted people: "You are leaving your homeland because the Song government has abandoned you." The Song gained territory but utterly lost the people's hearts. When Tong Guan faced the Jin envoy and was ordered to kneel to receive an imperial decree, it was only Ma Kuo's deft maneuvering that saved him from immediate humiliation.
🏚️

The Yanjing Handed to the Song

The city was looted clean; 30,000 wealthy households were abducted; social order was shattered. The Song army received an empty city with only refugees.

💔

The Price of Exchanging for the Ever-Victorious Army

Handing Yanjing's elders to the Jin in exchange for Guo Yaoshi's troops — after the Jin told the abducted the truth, the Song completely lost the hearts of the Yanjing people.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Song's recovery of Yanjing was a diplomatic "Pyrrhic victory": without military strength as backing, envoys like Zhao Liangsi relied on personal skill to secure six Yanjing prefectures for the Song against Jin's relentless pressure. But the price was devastating — annual tribute nearly tripled, the Western Capital and the three Ying-Ping-Luan prefectures were permanently lost, Yanjing's wealthy were abducted, and popular support was utterly destroyed. Aguda did largely honor the Maritime Alliance (returning Yanjing), but he left behind one fatal remark: "Once I am dead, do as you please." With Aguda's death in the fifth month of Xuanhe Year 5, Jin's hawks rose to power, and the last pillar of Song-Jin peace came crashing down.
