杜甫的五城 · 第3章「The Immortal's Pastry」

Meixian · Chaozhou · Shantou

Lai took a 14-hour bus from Guangzhou to his mother's ancestral home in Meixian, then a bumpy minivan ride into the mountains to find the century-old 'Three-Hall House.' Here he drank Nanyang-style coffee and savored Hakka stuffed tofu and braised meatballs just like childhood—but what truly conquered him was Meixian's xianrenban (herbal jelly), so silky it melted on the tongue, compelling him to return three more times specifically for it. He then traced his mother's 1948 migration route south: Meixian→Chaozhou→Shantou, feasting on Chaozhou braised goose and Shantou's bouncy fish balls, before boarding a ship 'bigger than a football field' at Shantou port—drinking alone on deck at sunset, finally fulfilling his boyhood dream of sailing the high seas.

01Homecoming Recon: 14 Hours to Meixian
Lai's mother left Meixian at 16 in 1948 to marry in Southeast Asia—she hadn't returned in over 40 years. His mission: recon the route for her. At 6 AM, he boarded a dilapidated long-distance bus at Guangzhou's Yuexiu South station. All passengers were Hakka; the shirtless driver shouted in Hakka dialect—the flavor of home already filled the bus. Fourteen hours later, arriving at Meixian bus station, the hotel clerk spoke Hakka—'for the first time I truly felt like I'd come home.'
02The Three-Hall House: A Period-Film Set
A private minivan from Meixian to Shekeng market town, then 35 yuan extra to drive into Xinhua Village. The rutted dirt road bounced violently; the engine overheated, needing water scooped from a stream. The Three-Hall House—upper, middle, lower halls under three roofs—was the village's largest. Stepping inside felt 'like walking onto a period-film set': dark brown hues, cobwebs, even morning sunlight tinged with ancient shadows. The square table and benches at the right door looked 'like something from a wuxia film where heroes drink.' But after 1949, five or six unrelated families moved in; the ancestral hall became a threshing floor, with red 'Cultural Revolution' slogans still on the walls. Lai thought of Doctor Zhivago.
03Xianrenban: The Immortal's Herb Jelly
When his nephew brought back xianrenban in a five-liter water bucket, Lai was dismissive—this stuff was everywhere in Southeast Asia! But the first mouthful: 'My heart nearly melted'—that silky, instant-dissolving sensation, like many 'first-time' experiences, unforgettable. In under half an hour, six people finished five liters. Thereafter on Meixian streets, he drank it like water. After leaving, he returned twice more, almost 'entirely because I missed Meixian's xianrenban too much.' He even researched the character: it should be written as 粄 (bǎn)—the Hakka term for gelatinous sweets—making xianrenban truly 'the Immortal's pastry,' food fit for celestial beings.
04Chaozhou: Feast & Han Yu's Horseshoe-Crab Banquet
On the Meixian-Chaozhou bus, he witnessed 'code-switching' in real time: everyone spoke Hakka until Jieyang, then naturally switched to Teochew. Renting a bicycle (0.5 yuan/hour), he pedaled to Kaiyuan Temple and the Song-dynasty stone bridge. Dinner: half a braised goose filling a 10-inch plate, fish-dumpling soup for ten—cost just 14 yuan. 'The eating is in Chaozhou!' He recalled Han Yu, exiled here a millennium ago, hosting a seafood banquet for a Daoist priest and writing a poem listing horseshoe crab, oysters, stingray, clams, octopus—then complaining it was all 'rank and foul.' Why serve food you despise? A thousand-year mystery.
05Shantou: The Boyhood Ship Dream, Realized
Shantou's fishy-sea-salt scent was even stronger than Chaozhou's. Lunch: squid with water spinach, 'exquisitely fresh'; fish-ball soup with 'thirty or forty fish balls,' springily 'bouncing off the teeth.' Boarding the S.S. Nanhu that afternoon—'truly a great ship, carrying hundreds, absolutely bigger than a football field.' He recalled his mother's tales of emigrating: no dining hall, families cooking on deck in circles. At sunset, alone on the highest deck, he opened a bottle of fine liquor, drank facing the golden sun and surging waves—'the most exhilarating drink of my life.' Tipsy, he slept on the same sea his mother had crossed southward over 40 years before.
💡 KEY INSIGHT
'Going home' is more than a geographic displacement. From five liters of herbal jelly to the Song-dynasty bridge over the Han River, from cobwebs in the ancestral hall to a solitary sunset drink on deck—every taste, every frame, is a geological survey of one's own bloodline.
