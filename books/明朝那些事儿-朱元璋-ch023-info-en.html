明朝那些事儿 · 朱元璋 · Chapter 23「The Secret Behind the System」

From the Great Ming Code to the Da Gao, from the three-class occupational system to rigid funeral nomenclature: Zhu Yuanzhang built a comprehensive control system to lock down the empire—and the secret behind it all was ensuring his dynasty ruled forever.

Zhu Yuanzhang was one of the most legalistic emperors in Chinese history. He created the Great Ming Code with five punishments, compiled the Da Gao with over 10,000 criminal cases, established a hereditary occupational system dividing people into civil, military, and artisan households, and even regulated how people referred to death—different words for emperors, nobles, officials, scholars, and commoners. These seemingly absurd rules hid a deeper purpose: total control, prevention of rebellion, and the perpetual rule of the Zhu family.

Legal System
The Great Ming Code & Da Gao
Zhu Yuanzhang established the Great Ming Code with five punishments: flogging, beating, imprisonment, exile, and death. But his true genius lay in the Da Gao—a compilation of over 10,000 criminal cases with vivid descriptions of crimes and brutal punishments, designed to be read by common people like crime fiction. He also created an extraordinary enforcement mechanism: households that owned a copy of the Da Gao could receive reduced sentences. Those without it faced harsher penalties. This was China's first large-scale legal literacy campaign.

Strict Rules
Funeral Titles & Ritual Regulations
In the Hongwu era, people could not simply say someone "died." Emperors "passed away" (崩), nobles and relatives "expired" (薨), officials "completed their service" (卒), scholars "ended their employment" (不禄), and only commoners could be said to have "died" (死). This created the phenomenon of "crashing funerals"—visitors who didn't know the deceased had to ask for their official rank before deciding which term to use. Getting it wrong could cause serious trouble. Officials' clothing colors, belt materials, and even chest patches were strictly regulated. Violations could be punishable by death.

03
Occupational System
The Three-Class Hereditary Lock
Zhu Yuanzhang divided all subjects into three hereditary occupational classes: civil households (scholars, doctors), military households (soldiers, archers, guards), and artisan households (craftsmen, cooks, tailors). Your occupation was determined at birth and could never change. If you were a military household with no son, any male relative would be drafted. If you were an artisan who couldn't cook, you'd still be assigned as a kitchen worker. The system was designed for administrative convenience, but its flaws were obvious: armies filled with untrained conscripts, workshops staffed by unskilled labor.

04
True Purpose
Control Everything, Forever
Zhu Yuanzhang assigned specific duties to nearly every government office, specifying even how to conduct population surveys and how to use brooms for street cleaning. He banned farmers from migrating to cities and restricted all subjects to their native regions, setting up checkpoints on major roads. The purpose was to prevent people from gathering, prevent rebellion, and ensure the Zhu family's rule lasted for eternity. Zhu built an invisible prison with laws and regulations, making every subject a prisoner of the system.

05
Legacy
A System That Lasted 276 Years
Zhu Yuanzhang's institutions shaped the entire Ming Dynasty. The three-class hereditary system, though gradually relaxed, persisted in basic form until the dynasty's end. The Great Ming Code became the foundation of Ming law. The Da Gao was mandated reading for the imperial examination—without mastering it, one could not become an official. These institutions reveal Zhu's deepest secret: an obsessive need for control born from his humble origins and the trauma of witnessing the Yuan collapse. He would control every aspect of his subjects' lives to prevent any future uprising.

KEY TAKEAWAY
Zhu Yuanzhang's system was, on the surface, good governance. In reality, it was a carefully engineered control project. Through law, ritual, hereditary occupation, and household registration, he built an invisible prison that locked every subject in place. The system secured 276 years of Zhu family rule—but also made the Ming Dynasty one of the least innovative dynasties in Chinese history.
