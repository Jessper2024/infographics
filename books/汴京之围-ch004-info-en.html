The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.3 "The Most Dangerous Treaty"

Three-Way Diplomacy · The Alliance on the Sea's Fatal Ambiguities In 1118, the Northern Song envoy Ma Zheng crossed the Bohai Sea and arrived at Aguda's camp — the first official Song-Jin contact. Simultaneously, the Liao was engaged in a three-year investiture negotiation with the Jin. Three-way diplomacy entangled like three tightening nooses: Liao-Jin talks collapsed over the investiture title; Song-Jin negotiations culminated in the Alliance on the Sea. Zhao Liangsi, beneath Mount Qingniu, agreed with Aguda on the terms of a joint attack on Liao — but the two sides understood the treaty in fundamentally opposite ways from the very beginning. Emperor Huizong tried to clarify the ambiguities with a "memorandum of items," but the issues he listed would become the entire basis for Jin's later attack on the Song.

This chapter details the Alliance on the Sea from conception to signature. In 1116, the Bohai rebel Gao Yongchang seized Liao's Eastern Capital — Jin exploited the chaos, capturing both Shenzhou and Liaoyang. Northern Song border commanders repeatedly reported false intelligence about Liao military movements; Tong Guan nearly launched a premature northern expedition, stopped only by the returning envoy Tao Yue's eyewitness testimony. In 1118, Ma Zheng's mission first reached Jin, establishing contact. What followed was a labyrinthine three-way diplomatic game: Liao-Jin negotiations collapsed when Aguda rejected the humiliating "Eastern Cherishing State" investiture title; Song-Jin negotiations progressed through Zhao Liangsi's skilled diplomacy, culminating in the Alliance — Song would pay 500,000 in annual indemnity for the Yan-Yun territories, with both sides attacking Liao in a pincer. But the treaty's fatal ambiguities — Did "Yan territory" include the Western Capital? Who got the Ying-Ping-Luan prefectures? Was the deal void if Song forces failed to arrive? — planted every seed for the coming catastrophe.

01
Opportunist's Gain
Gao Yongchang's rebellion: how eleven drunk conspirators opened Liaodong's gates for Jin
On the first night of Chinese New Year, 1116, the Bohai man Gao Yongchang and a dozen companions got drunk and scaled the walls of Liaoyang's government compound with knives. Claiming to report a rebellion outside, they asked a servant where the Prefect slept, found Xiao Baoxian, assassinated him, and fled. After Xiao's death, the Revenue Commissioner and Deputy Prefect launched a dragnet for Bohai suspects — dozens were killed, many innocent. Panic spread; more people joined the revolt. By the fifth day of the new year, Liaoyang fell. Gao Yongchang declared himself "Great Bohai Emperor," seizing over fifty prefectures in Liaodong. Liao Chancellor Zhang Lin held Shenzhou, but on the 21st day of the fifth month, Jin troops — summoned by Gao himself — arrived, routed Zhang Lin, then turned and destroyed Gao Yongchang. Jin played the fisherman, taking all of Liaodong.

02
First Contact
Ma Zheng's mission: a thousand-li sea voyage, three hundred li under escort, the opening of Song-Jin diplomacy
In the intercalary ninth month of 1118, Ma Zheng, accompanied by his son Ma Kuo, interpreter Huqing, and eighty soldiers, set sail from Dengzhou — inaugurating the first formal Song-Jin exchange. Their ship was seized upon landing; their goods confiscated; they were nearly killed multiple times. Ma Zheng and Huqing kept explaining they were envoys before the soldiers finally bound them and escorted them across more than a dozen prefectures — three thousand li — to Aguda's camp beside the Lailiu River. Aguda's assistants — his nephew Nianhan (Wanyan Zonghan) and Wushi — received them. The mission's only goal was establishing contact; Aguda agreed in principle to a joint attack on Liao. But Emperor Huizong, hearing rumors of Liao-Jin peace talks, recalled the second mission. Aguda was furious: it was Song that first sought alliance; now Song was backing out and had sent an imperial "edict" rather than a state letter — "If it's just an edict, there's nothing more to discuss."

03
Three-Line Game
Three-way grand diplomacy: Liao-Jin talks collapse, Song-Jin secret contacts, and a Liao envoy watching Jin storm the Supreme Capital
Between 1117 and 1120, three-way diplomacy played out like a three-board Go match. The Liao-Jin track began in 1117 — Aguda, on advisor Yang Pu's suggestion, requested Liao investiture — but his terms included the Liao emperor calling Aguda "elder brother," ceding the Supreme, Central, and Xingzhong capitals, and sending imperial princes and princesses as hostages. When Liao finally conceded investiture with the title "Emperor of the Eastern Cherishing State," Aguda found "Eastern Cherishing" insulting — "a remote eastern petty state cherishing the great state's virtue" — rejected the title, and nearly executed Yang Pu who had suggested seeking investiture. On the Song-Jin track, in 1120, Zhao Liangsi watched in awe from beneath Mount Qingniu as Jin forces captured Liao's Supreme Capital in a single morning, then immediately sat down to negotiate. Remarkably, the Liao envoy Xiao Xieni was also present — three nations' envoys, same place, each negotiating for their country's fate.

04
Treaty Terms
What Zhao Liangsi secured beneath Mount Qingniu: the Alliance on the Sea, clause by clause
In the fifth month of 1120, Zhao Liangsi and Aguda, beneath Mount Qingniu outside the freshly captured Liao Supreme Capital, concluded negotiations that would reshape East Asia. Core terms: ① After jointly destroying Liao, Yanjing (Southern Capital) and the prefectures south of the Yan Mountains go to Song; the Supreme, Central, and Eastern Capitals go to Jin. ② The Western Capital (Yunzhou/Datong), Aguda orally agreed to transfer after capturing the Liao emperor. ③ Song transfers its annual Liao indemnity to Jin — 200,000 taels silver, 300,000 bolts of silk (500,000 total). ④ Neither side may negotiate separately with Liao. ⑤ Song forces must arrive on time for the joint attack; otherwise, the treaty is void. Zhao Liangsi also sought the Western Capital in writing and Song's right to attack Wei, Ying, and Shuo prefectures — but the Ying-Ping-Luan prefectures were never resolved. Aguda dispatched 200 cavalry to escort the Song envoy back, only to recall them mid-journey due to a cattle plague — rescheduling to the following year. Such meticulous care was rendered in vain.

05
Fissures Emerge
Huizong's three-item memorandum — intended to cement verbal promises, became Jin's perfect pretext for repudiation
Emperor Huizong composed a separate memorandum (shimu) beyond the state letter, painstakingly itemizing every ambiguity from Aguda's verbal promises. Item one: "Yanjing" must include the Western Capital and its attached prefectures, since Aguda orally said he had no interest in the Western Capital. Item two: The 500,000 indemnity is premised on receiving the Western Capital and all prefectures north of the mountains — otherwise, the sum would not be so high. Item three: "Joint attack" means both sides must act simultaneously; if Jin fails to appear, Song has no obligation under the treaty. This memorandum exposed the fatal misalignment: Jin understood "whoever takes the territory keeps it" (随得者取地); Song believed oral promises equaled written commitments. When Ma Zheng departed with this memorandum, both sides already sensed the agreement could not hold.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Alliance on the Sea was one of the most dangerous treaties in diplomatic history — not because the terms were inherently malicious, but because an unbridgeable chasm separated the two signatories' understanding of what they had agreed. Zhao Liangsi sealed the Song-Jin alliance with extraordinary skill, but between Aguda's "joint attack: whoever takes the territory keeps it" and Huizong's "oral promises equal written commitments" lay an entire world of difference. Huizong's memorandum attempted to close these gaps, but instead solidified them into the complete textual basis for Jin's later accusations of Song "treaty violations." The cruelest irony: while the Northern Song was still debating the status of the Western Capital and the Ying-Ping-Luan prefectures, the Jurchen had already demonstrated with action — only those who could capture cities deserved to own land. This brutally simple truth would not be understood by the Northern Song until the very day of the Jingkang Catastrophe.
