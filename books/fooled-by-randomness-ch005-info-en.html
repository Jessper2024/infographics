Fooled by Randomness · Ch.5
"Survival of the Least Fit — Can Evolution Be Fooled by Randomness?"

The rise and fall of two traders, and a question that pierces Darwinism with randomness

Carlos made $80 million in emerging markets using economic analysis — and lost $300 million in a single summer. John accumulated $16 million through statistical models — wiped to zero within days. They are not exceptions: at any point in time, the traders making the most money are often the worst traders. Through two real stories, Taleb reveals an unsettling truth: randomness can fool not only individuals but evolution itself. Survival of the fittest? Perhaps it's just the luckiest who happened to miss the rare event.

01
CARLOS CASE
Carlos: A Golden Road Built on Economic Intuition, Crushed Overnight by a Rare Event
Harvard economics graduate Carlos entered emerging-market bond trading in 1992. He firmly believed economic analysis could explain everything — buy more on dips, hold when fundamentals were good. Seven years of cumulative profits approached $80 million. In summer 1998, the Russian debt crisis erupted. He kept averaging down, shouting "That's just position squaring!" at anyone who questioned him. By the end, he had lost $300 million in one summer and was escorted out by security.

JOHN CASE
John: A "Ten-Sigma" Event — Once Every 100 Trillion Years — Happens in Days with Leverage
John relied on quant expert Henry's risk models, running global high-yield bond spread trades. He leveraged a $50 million portfolio fivefold. The model said: the probability of this kind of loss is once every 100 trillion trillion years. In summer 1998, the high-yield bond market crashed, and all his hedging strategies failed. In just a few days, $14 million in personal wealth evaporated, and he lost his job.

03
COMMON TRAITS
Traders Fooled by Randomness Share These Six Fatal Traits
Carlos and John are not exceptions — nearly every "most successful" trader in the 1992–1998 bull market displayed these traits. They have no formal definition, but you recognize them the moment you see them.
1
Overestimating the precision of their beliefs. Carlos believed in economics, John in statistics. Neither considered that their past success could be coincidence.
2
Refusing to close positions. There's a saying: getting a bad trader to close a position is harder than getting him to divorce. They'd rather die than take a loss.
3
Changing the narrative. When losing, they call themselves "long-term investors" — blurring trader and investor is just an excuse to avoid admitting defeat.
4
Denying reality. They don't believe the price on the screen when losing. "It's just position squaring, disappointment selling" — ignoring the market's message.
5
No pre-planned response. Buying more after a crash, but driven by emotion, not any predetermined plan.
6
Lacking critical self-examination. Refusing to accept that their valuation methods may be flawed. Soros, by contrast, re-examines his framework when faced with adverse results.
04
CORE PARADOX
The Cross-Sectional Problem: The Most Profitable May Be the Worst
How could Carlos and John make so much money after committing every error? The answer lies in a simple principle of randomness. They made money not just because they picked the right markets, but because their "buy-on-dips" style happened to match the character of the 1992–1998 historical period. During that stretch, people with that particular trait dominated the market, crowding out possibly superior traders.
"At any point in time, the traders making the most money are often the worst traders."
—— Taleb · The Cross-Sectional Problem
🔍

Reverse Causality Fallacy

We think traders make money because they're good. Perhaps it's the reverse: we think they're good only because they made money. One can profit in financial markets purely through random phenomena.

🎯

The Sample Path Trap

Both Carlos and John benefited from market cycles. Their success was over-adaptation to one particular "sample path" — one that happened to have no rare event. The longer a rare event is absent, the more vulnerable they become.

05
EVOLUTION FOOLED
Evolution Can Be Fooled by Randomness Too: Adapting to One Sample ≠ Adapting to All Possibilities
Darwin has been misused. Evolution is about reproductive fitness, not short-term survival. Gould discovered "negative mutations" — worse traits can temporarily survive. When a regime shift occurs, the previously adapted species may be the first to go extinct. Animals can only adapt to the one time sequence they have experienced, not the average of all possible environments. The longer a species goes without encountering a rare event, the more vulnerable it becomes to that event.

"If we extend time infinitely, according to the principle of ergodicity, that event will surely happen — and those species will surely be destroyed!"

"Whatever John and Carlos go on to do, they will remain fools of randomness."

—— Taleb · Chapter 5

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
The least fit may survive — as long as they happen to land on the one sample path with no rare event. At any point in time, the most profitable traders are often the worst, because they are merely over-adapted to one special slice of history. The truly fit are those who can withstand all possible environments — and that takes the long, slow proof of ergodic time.
