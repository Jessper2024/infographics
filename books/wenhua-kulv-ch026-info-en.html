Cultural Perplexity · Ch.26 "In Memory of the Pen"

The life of a single pen traces one man's complete journey through catastrophe, slander, and wilderness

Core insight: Through the journey of his pens, Yu Qiuyu narrates the entirety of a Chinese intellectual's life — from mimicking his mother's letter-writing at age three to completing a twenty-volume collected works. At different times, the pen served as messenger, walking stick, judge's gavel, weapon, and companion. Persisting in "pure handwriting" is not technical stubbornness but a declaration of lifelong fidelity.

QuestionIn the age of computers, amid twenty years of slanderous storms, why does a writer still insist on writing by pen? What does the pen mean to him?

AnswerThe pen is far more than a tool — it is responsibility (writing letters for villagers), a walking stick (writing in secret during catastrophe), a judge's gavel (fair evaluation of professors), a frontier explorer (fieldwork chronicles), and ultimately a raft across the dark waters of slander. Upon completing his twenty volumes, he tells the pen: "You have exhausted my entire life, yet I have not wasted too much of your ink."

1
1950s

How did a tiny brush pen enlighten a life?

At age three, while his mother wrote letters for villagers, Yu Qiuyu mimicked her with a small bamboo brush pen — this was the pen's first lesson: writing is a responsibility to one's community.

At seven, his mother gave him a dip pen longer than a brush, and from then on he wrote letters and kept accounts for the entire village. One dip of ink yielded seven characters. The villagers watched the pen tip's every stroke — "They hardly looked at me; they only looked at the pen."

This experience laid the foundation for two of his future achievements: first place in the citywide essay contest (from writing hundreds of letters for villagers) and a major mathematics prize (from keeping all those accounts). His mother was not surprised — with a single pen, she had completed her son's entire enlightenment.

2
1966–1971

What filled those 600,000 characters of "confession"?

From age 19 to 21, Yu Qiuyu used a 30-fen ballpoint pen to write over 600,000 characters of "confession" for his father, who had been "overthrown" — carbon paper underneath each page to keep a copy, writing until his fingers went stiff and his palms cramped.

At first he urged his father not to bother writing, but as he continued, he realized the true stories stretching back to his grandfather and maternal grandfather held immense historical and literary value. He began asking more questions, verifying details with his grandmother and mother — all of this became the starting point for My Family's History (吾家小史), completed forty years later.

The reason for his father's downfall was heartbreaking: he was not a cadre, not a capitalist, not an intellectual — merely a man with four sons, deemed "prosperous in progeny" and therefore envied. In those years, "prosperous progeny" meant inevitable starvation.

3
1971–1976

How do you write a 680,000-character work in forbidden territory?

Taking advantage of Zhou Enlai's push to resume education, Yu Qiuyu sneaked into the still-forbidden foreign-language library and, with a one-yuan fountain pen, single-handedly completed the 680,000-character World Drama Studies — compiling the complete dramatic theory of thirteen nations.

These materials still have not been fully translated into Chinese, over forty years later. He copied foreign-language originals extensively and translated paragraph by paragraph with dictionaries. A single window pane away, one critical word against the "Revolutionary Model Operas" meant imprisonment. The pen became his walking stick, "tracing a vast academic edifice through dark quagmire."

This book later became the sole national textbook for this major discipline, never replaced. Yu says: "Compared to this academic construct, my later, more famous works lost a quality — a life-or-death vitality."

4
1980s

Can a pen also be a gavel of justice?

After the catastrophe, Yu Qiuyu was exceptionally promoted to academy president and concurrently chaired Shanghai's professorial review committees in both Chinese and Arts. He lifted that pen and wrote rejection verdicts again and again — "In those days, the pen almost became a judge's gavel: resounding, decisive, authoritative, indisputable."

But he understood the pen's nature too well: it only wanted to sign its name on essays, not on official documents. After resigning twenty-three times before final approval, he went to the Gansu plateau — and halfway to Yangguan Pass, the old fountain pen that had accompanied him so long was lost on an ancient battlefield.

He was at peace: "This pen was an old friend who had accompanied me for so long. From now on, let it keep company with the frontier soldiers and border poets of a thousand years ago on my behalf."

5
1990s–2000s

How do you grip a pen at gunpoint?

In the terrorist regions of the Middle East, South Asia, and Central Asia, where death could come at any moment and ink bottles would be suspected of being dangerous items, Yu Qiuyu switched to transparent plastic-barreled ink pens, collecting them from every small hotel room to make sure his diary never stopped.

Passing through the borderlands of Iraq, Iran, Pakistan, Afghanistan, and Nepal, he clutched his diary manuscript wrapped in a plastic laundry bag to his chest, another pen gripped in his hand. "Even if I am captured, even if my luggage is taken, my paper and pen will still be with me. I can still write."

"Crossing the English Channel in a storm, two elderly British ladies steadied themselves on the railing behind me and exclaimed: 'Look! Such beautiful Chinese characters! He can still hold his pen in such wild waves!' — Beautiful Chinese characters, writing even in the wildest waves. Is that not, in some way, symbolic?"

The stacks of thin manuscript paper mailed from roadside postboxes in Gansu were given a title: "Let's call it Cultural Perplexity (文化苦旅)." A later collection was named A Mountain Dwelling Journal (山居笔记) — with the character for "pen" (笔) deliberately added.

6
2014

Where does the pen find its final home?

Upon completing the twenty-volume Yu Qiuyu Collection, he faced repeated impulses to break, discard, destroy, and bury the pen — confronting not just mockery from the computer age, but a cultural ecology composed of "collective revelry" and "collective slander."

His wife Ma Lan, a brilliant performing artist, was frozen out of her career by officials after declining nine invitations to a "top-level gala." He himself faced unceasing mudslinging no matter what he did. Yet he did not emigrate — he discovered his attackers were precisely the creators of Borderland New Seedlings, the play that once celebrated the abolition of education, and the former leaders of rebel factions. "All cultural malignancy extinguishes culture through cultural means. Simply put, they use the pen to snatch the pen."

So, amid the howling storms of slander, he wrote The Chinese Cultural Context, What Is Culture, The Way of the Noble Person, and other works — "Not a single volume of these twenty applied for a single yuan of funding. With the pen, everything was enough." In memory of the pen: kneeling and offering, remembering and reflecting, rejoicing and weeping.

💡
Key Takeaway

The pen is the most important object in Yu Qiuyu's life — it witnessed the responsibility of enlightenment, the writing of catastrophe, the stealth of creation in darkness, the justice of judgment, the exploration of wilderness, and ultimately, perseverance through storms of slander. The pen taught him this: writing is not entirely for the reader. A great part is for the storm itself, for the boat, for the pen. A seven-year-old boy who once wrote letters for villagers — what else can he still write for Chinese culture? This is the true meaning of "in memory of the pen": not farewell, but the confirmation of a lifetime's fidelity.

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Cultural Perplexity (文化苦旅), Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
