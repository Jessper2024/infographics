Wenhua Kulv · Ch.26 "Homage to the Pen"

One pen, one life—I bow and offer, remember and reflect, rejoice and weep

On the night the twenty-volume Collected Works was completed amid New Year's firecrackers, the author set down his pen and wrote this "Homage to the Pen"—a life review threaded by the pen. From age three imitating his mother with a tiny bamboo brush, to age seven writing letters for villagers; from drafting 600,000 words of "confession" for his imprisoned father, to secretly writing the 680,000-word World Dramatic Theory in a forbidden foreign-language library; from resigning to trace Tang dynasty ruins and beginning the Cultural Perplexity (Wenhua Kulv), to clutching manuscripts while traversing the terrorist zones of North Africa and the Middle East; and finally, amid storms of slander, writing volume after volume—twenty in all. One pen, one lifetime spent, yet sustaining an entire spiritual world. "With the pen, everything is enough."

01
First Pen
A bamboo brush at three, writing letters for villagers at seven—the pen came with responsibility to the people
At three, watching his mother write letters for villagers, the author imitated with a tiny bamboo brush. At four, teachers dragged him from under the table to school—his face smeared with ink, every teacher carried him to the creek to wash after class. At seven, his mother gave him a dip pen longer than a brush and a bottle of blue ink, tasking him with writing letters and keeping accounts for villagers—"One dip in the ink bottle wrote seven characters. The nib's movement across the paper drew every villager's eyes. They barely looked at me, only at the pen." At nine, attending middle school in Shanghai, his father bought him a "Guan Leming" fountain pen—soon lost. After winning Shanghai-wide composition and math prizes, his father forgave him but never bought good pens again; the author used only cheap, light pens thereafter.

02
Pen's Ordeal
600,000 words of "confession" for Father—writing until hands cramped, knuckles deeply indented
From nineteen to twenty-one, during the early Cultural Revolution, his imprisoned father dictated "confessions" for the author to transcribe—an attempt to explain the truth to the "revolutionary masses." Eight family members depended on this. Using ¥0.30 ballpoint pens with carbon paper underneath for duplicates, his fingers stiffened, his palms cramped. He wrote over 600,000 words—yet learned vast family history, which became the seed of his later memoir My Family History. His father's only "crime"? Having four sons, "thriving progeny." The real center of the Cultural Revolution was theater—beginning with the denunciation of Hai Rui Dismissed from Office, continuing with "revolutionary model operas." The author, studying dramatic theory at the Shanghai Theatre Academy, found his discipline turned into a "concentration camp for inverting truth and falsehood." He decided to break his pen and change course.

03
Breaking & Sneaking
Threw all pens in the trash, then picked them up again—using the pen as a walking stick in a dark foreign-language library
Before being sent down to the countryside, all students were forced to watch Borderland New Seedlings, a play that utterly negated education and culture. "Good heavens—theater again!" He threw every pen into the trash—a double protest: professional shame, and no one left to write to. On the farm, "we sank into the mud as we slept. Always to the fields before dawn, back after dark, too exhausted to remember words, to remember the pen, to remember being someone who could write." In 1971, Zhou Enlai's push to resume classes brought the author back to Shanghai. For ¥1 he bought a fountain pen and secretly entered the still-forbidden foreign-language library, writing the 680,000-word World Dramatic Theory—compiling dramatic theory from thirteen nations, alone, with only a pen as his walking stick through the darkness. A single wrong word about "model operas" outside the window could mean prison. Decades later, this work remains the only national textbook in the field.

04
Pen Roaming the World
From resigning to trace the Tang dynasty to perilous journeys through North Africa—clutching manuscripts in storms
After resigning his university presidency—twenty-three times before approval—he put on a thin gray coat and headed for the Gansu plateau. Near Yangguan Pass, he lost his old fountain pen among ancient battlefield mounds; that pen had always "insisted on signing my name only on articles, never on documents." From roadside mailboxes he sent thin manuscript pages; on the back of an envelope he scribbled: "Let's call it Wenhua Kulv" (Cultural Perplexity). In the terrorist zones of North Africa, the Middle East, South Asia, and Central Asia, with crisis every day, he wrote his diary in abandoned trenches, on jeep wheels, under guard-post canopies. Crossing the Iraq-Iran border—"dark shadows loomed, gun muzzles gleamed"—he clutched completed manuscripts wrapped in a plastic laundry bag against his chest, a pen gripped in hand. Crossing the English Channel in a storm, the entire ship pitching, two elderly English ladies marveled: "Look! What beautiful Chinese characters! And he still grips his pen in such waves!"

05
Pen Against Pen
Amid slanderous storms, writing twenty volumes stroke by stroke—you spent my life, I did not waste much of your ink
After completing his world-civilization fieldwork, a new cultural binary emerged: "collective revelry" and "collective slander." His wife Ma Lan, an extraordinary performer, was "frozen" out of work for declining a "top-level gala" nine times; the author became the prime target of cultural slander. They fled to an obscure city in Guangdong. Investigating the sources, he discovered the slander operations were run by the very people who had created Borderland New Seedlings, leaders of the old rebel command, and academics whose professorship applications he had vetoed. "All cultural malignancy destroys culture through cultural means—the pen seizing the pen." He picked up his pen again with the strictest rule: not a single drop of ink wasted on refuting slander. Through nights of howling slander, in a secluded cottage far from all "cultural spectacles," he wrote volume after volume—The Chinese Literary Tradition, What Is Culture, The Way of the Junzi, and more—twenty volumes in all, never applying for a cent of funding. "You have spent my entire life, yet I have not wasted too much of your ink."

🔑 Key Takeaway
"The most important object in a person's life must be the pen." From age three imitating letters to age seventy compiling twenty volumes, a single pen threads Yu Qiuyu's entire life: the responsibility of writing for villagers, the suffering of writing confessions for his father, the secret triumph of World Dramatic Theory, the epic journey of Cultural Perplexity, the life-or-death passages through North Africa and the Middle East, and finally, the twenty volumes written pen-against-pen amid slander. "Thus I pay homage to the pen. I bow and offer, remember and reflect, rejoice and weep."
