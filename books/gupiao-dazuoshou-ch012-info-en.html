Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Chapter 12
"Feint East, Strike West — Adapt to Every Change"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer From escaping a corn corner by feinting with oats, to unloading July cotton using a newspaper article — Livermore demonstrates the core quality of a top trader: in desperate straits, don't collide head-on; exploit others' psychological expectations to create your own liquidity. Trading isn't brute force — it's intelligence. A $3,000 oats decoy unlocked 10 million bushels of corn; a newspaper article became the perfect cover, clearing 140,000 bales of cotton in ten minutes of trading. The professional, like a billiards player, thinks several shots ahead. In speculation, flexibility matters more than correctness.

When conventional methods fail, how does a trader survive? Trading isn't brute force — it's intelligence. Stratton cornered corn; Livermore's 10-million-bushel short faced massive losses. He didn't buy back directly — that would be cutting his own throat. Instead, he shorted 200,000 bushels of oats to trick traders into selling corn, bought back 6 million bushels in ten minutes, with net cost just 1.5 cents away. In July cotton, stuck with 140,000 bales he couldn't unload, the New York World ran a front-page story accidentally creating the perfect exit window — he seized it and was completely out within ten minutes of the open. Feint east, strike west; adapt to every change: in speculation, flexibility matters more than correctness.

01
Narrow Escape
Corn Corner: 200K Bushels of Oats to Unlock 10M Bushels of Corn
A $3,000 decoy that defused a massive crisis. Stratton had cornered corn; Livermore was short 10 million bushels. Buying back directly would drive prices even higher against him. He thought of a feint: had four brokerage houses simultaneously sell 50,000 bushels of oats each at market. Oats fell sharply. Traders assumed the Armour interests were attacking Stratton — corn would be next — and rushed to sell corn. Livermore bought back 6 million bushels in ten minutes. His total grain loss across the entire campaign was just $25,000 — a tiny cost that averted a catastrophe.

02
Leveraging Events
The Cammack Story: Why Buy on Bearish Insider News?
Bear master Cammack was bearish on the whole market. A tipster told him: William Rockefeller is heavily selling St. Paul. Cammack didn't sell — he ordered 15,000 shares bought. His logic: Rockefeller's selling was pressuring the market, which would provide ammunition for his massive short campaign. Once Rockefeller stopped, Cammack had accumulated 60,000 shares of St. Paul long — then used that position as leverage to short the entire market savagely. St. Paul fell from the 90s to 44; Cammack cleaned up. He wasn't trading St. Paul — he was preparing ammunition for an entire short campaign.

03
Perfect Exit
July Cotton: How a Newspaper Article Became the Perfect Cover
An unexpected article solved the unsolvable exit problem. Livermore had bought 140,000 bales of July cotton — virtually all the floating contracts. He knew unloading such a position without collapsing the price was nearly impossible. Then the New York World ran a front-page story: "July Cotton Cornered by Larry Livingston." The market went wild, prices surging 30 points in a day. Ten minutes after the open next day, Livermore was out — every single bale, mostly at the day's high. "I seized the opportunity because I had to. What else could I do? Missing your exit window can cost you millions. Don't hesitate — opportunity knocks but once."

Adaptability Gradient
Feint East, Strike West: Four Levels of Trading Adaptability
Through three stories — the corn corner, Cammack's foresight, and the cotton exit — Livermore reveals different tiers of trading flexibility. From reactive survival to proactive master strategy, adaptability is not a personality trait but a professional instinct trained through "position-first thinking." The professional trader, like a billiards player, thinks several shots ahead, always putting "what does my position tell me" before "what do I want to do."

🔑 Key Takeaway
Chapter 12's core lesson is "adaptability." In the corn corner, Livermore resolved the crisis with a feint — not by head-on collision, but by exploiting others' psychological expectations to create liquidity for himself. In July cotton, he demonstrated the ability to seize an unexpected opportunity — not creating it, but recognizing and exploiting it. The Cammack story further shows: professional traders think about the entire chessboard, not isolated moves. The common revelation from all three stories: in speculation, flexibility matters more than correctness — only by adapting to every change, and transforming "position-first thinking" into professional instinct, can one find a way out of desperate straits.
