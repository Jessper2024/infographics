Siege of Bianjing · Chapter 15 "The Great Chu Regime"

The Old Emperor Deposed · The New One Hard to Install · Birth and Struggle of a Puppet Regime After the emperor was deposed, the Jurchen pressed the city to choose a new ruler. Under fear and coercion, the officials "elected" Zhang Bangchang. He did not volunteer to be emperor — he exchanged his entire clan's lives for the safety of a city's inhabitants. The establishment of the Great Chu regime was the most absurd political drama following the fall of Northern Song — how a puppet, in unwillingness and humiliation, strove to protect the country and people he once served.

In the second month of Jingkang Year 2, both Emperor Huizong and Emperor Qinzong had been deposed, and the Jurchen army demanded that Bianjing install a new ruler. With no one daring to volunteer, Zhang Bangchang — who was still away on a diplomatic mission to the Jurchen camp — was "elected" as emperor of the Great Chu. Yet he knew nothing of this. Upon returning to the city, he fasted for four days and drew a blade to take his own life. Under the threat of a city-wide massacre, he ascended the throne with a tragic pledge: "nine clans for one city." After his enthronement, he refused to use the main hall, never called himself "Zhen" (the imperial "We"), and sealed the palace chambers — doing everything to signal he was merely a temporary regent. At the same time, he successfully petitioned the Jurchen to waive the remaining gold and silver demands and secured the release of thousands of detainees. As a puppet, Zhang Bangchang protected Bianjing far better than either of the two real emperors ever did.

01
Puppet Selection
"Electing" an Absent Man: Why Zhang Bangchang Became the Only Choice
Zhang Bangchang's selection was no accident — it was the product of careful Jurchen calculation. He had previously entered the Jurchen camp as a hostage alongside Prince Kang during Woliubu's first siege, surviving through outstanding diplomatic skill, and left the Jurchen with a favorable impression. He advocated ceding Hebei; though a peace-faction figure, the Jurchen saw him as the "friendliest" official at the chancellor level. Compared to Zhang Shuye and Sun Fu's hostility, He Li's inept execution, Wang Shiyong's poor popular reputation, and Fan Qiong being merely a "loyal dog" — Zhang was the only person who could both satisfy the Jurchen and be minimally acceptable to the city. On the 11th day of the second month, Song Qiyu, a ministry secretary, learned from Wang Shiyong that the Jurchen favored Zhang. He wrote "Zhang Bangchang" on a piece of paper and showed it — the assembled officials promptly endorsed the choice.

02
Nomination Storm
Those Who Refused to Sign: Three Modes of Resistance by Zhang Shuye, Sun Fu, and Qin Hui
The nomination of Zhang Bangchang did not proceed smoothly — at least three senior ministers objected in different ways. Sun Fu tried to accompany the crown prince out of the city but was intercepted by Fan Qiong; he later staged a hunger strike and was taken away by the Jurchen. Zhang Shuye, facing threats of death, still declared he "would rather die than sign" and was eventually marched north. Qin Hui, as deputy chief censor, placed his name first on a petition urging the enthronement of a Zhao clan descendant — whether coerced or voluntary, this letter led to his capture by the Jurchen, and thus began the legendary journey of his eventual escape south and rise to chancellor. Each of the three preserved, in his own way, the last vestiges of scholar-official dignity at the moment of national collapse.

03
Jurchen Extortion
The Death of the Four Collection Chiefs: A Bloody Purge Over Insufficient Gold and Silver
To squeeze out the last of the gold and silver, the Jurchen used massacre as a pressure tactic. The emperor had appointed four wall collection commissioners to oversee gold and silver requisition in each quarter: Mei Zhili (east), Cheng Zhen (south), An Fu (west), and Chen Zhizhi (north). When they reported that they had exhausted all means and still could not meet the quota, the Jurchen were enraged. On the 25th day of the second month, eight officials were brought before the commanders. The four deputies were each whipped fifty times on the back (Hu Tanglao died on the spot); the four commissioners were taken near Nanxun Gate, forced to kneel, and beheaded one by one. The Jurchen claimed they were Prince Kang's collaborators — in reality, it was exemplary terror. Thereafter, Kaifeng Prefecture continued to levy quotas by nine official ranks and three commoner tiers, but people no longer feared death, and even the threat of a city massacre could no longer drive collection forward.

04
Aborted Coup
Wu Ge's Uprising: The Last Desperate Stand of Northern Song's Military Class
Not everyone accepted the puppet regime. Garrison Commander Wu Ge had earlier proposed substituting the real crown prince with a fake one, but his plan was rejected. As Zhang Bangchang's enthronement approached, Wu conspired with Lü Haowen, Ma Shen, and others to launch a coup on the 8th day of the third month: first eliminate Fan Qiong and other pro-Jurchen generals, then lead the entire city's army in a final battle against the Jurchen forces, and bring back the two emperors. But faulty intelligence — fifty carts seen heading east from Qingcheng, which Wu mistook for the emperor being relocated — disrupted the plan. At dawn on the 6th day of the third month, several hundred palace guards acted prematurely, forcing Wu to move hastily. Fan Qiong lured Wu Ge and his son to their deaths through a feigned surrender; the hundreds who joined the uprising were slaughtered along the Jinshui River bank. The last resistance of Northern Song's military officers thus met its end.

05
Puppet's Conscience
The "Fake Sovereign's" Real Responsibility: How Zhang Bangchang Protected Bianjing
After ascending the throne, Zhang Bangchang went to great lengths to distance himself from a "real emperor": he refused the main hall, sealed palace chambers with notes reading "Respectfully Sealed by Your Subject Zhang Bangchang," never used "Zhen" (the imperial "We") but called himself "Yu" (I), and referred to his orders not as "Sacred Edicts" but as "Palace Decrees." He donned imperial robes only when the Jurchen were present — even the guards mocked him as a play-acting "Fake Sovereign." Yet he accomplished what the real emperors could not: on the 15th day of the third month, he personally went to the Jurchen camp with seven petitions and successfully persuaded the commanders to waive remaining gold and silver demands, spare the Zhao imperial tombs, and release thousands of detainees. The Jurchen even voluntarily reduced the annual tribute to 300,000 taels of silver and bolts of silk — matching what Northern Song had once paid to the Liao. As a puppet, Zhang Bangchang secured for Bianjing a margin of survival that neither of the two real emperors had ever managed to obtain.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Great Chu regime was a puppet state imposed on Bianjing by the Jurchen, but Zhang Bangchang — the man thrust onto the throne — resisted with extraordinary conscience. He fought back through hunger strikes and attempted suicide; after enthronement, he refused the title "Zhen," stayed off the main hall, and sealed palace chambers — signaling to all under heaven that he was merely a temporary substitute. More critically, he leveraged his "emperor" identity to negotiate with the Jurchen, successfully securing the waiver of enormous remaining gold and silver obligations, rescuing thousands of detainees, and protecting the Zhao imperial tombs. A man reviled for a millennium as a "traitor to the nation" in fact protected Bianjing far better than the two real emperors in its darkest hour.
