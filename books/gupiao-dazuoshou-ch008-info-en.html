Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch.8
"Timing! Timing! Timing!"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Livermore reveals the core principle of position-building: never average down, never add to a losing position. A good beginning is half the battle — trading success depends critically on choosing the right moment to begin. How to build positions on the way up and add only when right? Buy on the way up, sell on the way down. The correct method is pyramiding: every new lot must build on the profit of the previous one.

In one sentence: Livermore completely rejected averaging down, instead advocating pyramiding up — probe → confirm → add, with every new lot resting on the profit of the prior one. The Deacon White story perfectly illustrates testing information through actual trades. Key data: Saratoga tuition $40K | Position-building range 110-114 | Base position 6,000 shares | Test cost 1 point.

01
Position Rule
Pyramiding Up: Why Must You Buy Higher, Not Lower?
Never wish to buy too cheap or too easily. Livermore's system was explicit: buy 2,000 shares at 110. If it rises to 111 — correct, buy another 2,000. At 114 — still correct, buy another 2,000. Average cost 110¾ for 6,000 shares. Wait for a pullback. If it drops to 112¼ and rallies, buy 4,000 at 113¾. The key is watching how those 4,000 fill: if prices rise as you buy, you're right; if fills are scattered, something's wrong. Iron rule: "After your first trade, unless it shows a profit, don't make a second."

02
Classic Case
The Deacon White Story: Testing Information Through Action
Information alone proves nothing; only market reaction reveals truth. A tipster told Deacon White: "H.O. Havemeyer is buying Sugar heavily." White immediately ordered to sell 10,000 shares. His logic: sell to test the market's absorptive capacity. When it was absorbed smoothly, he sold another 10,000 — also absorbed. Conclusion: real buying power existed. He covered and bought 30,000, making a fortune. "The only way to find out if anybody is buying that stock is just what I did — test it."

03
Big Money
The Art of Selling Large Positions: Sell When You Can, Not When You Want
You can't expect the market to absorb 50,000 shares as easily as 100. Livermore highlighted the core dilemma of large-scale trading: easy to enter, hard to exit. The holder of large positions must patiently wait for sufficient buying power to absorb them. When the moment arrives, act decisively — "he must sell when he can, not when he wants to." This demands tremendous patience and discipline — impulsive full commitment is the number one killer of beginners.
❌

Amateur Mistake

Go all-in immediately without considering market conditions. Use the full 500-share limit at once, leaving no room for probing. Easy entry, disastrous exit.

✅

Professional Approach

Buy 100 shares as a probe. Add only when profitable. Observe, test, wait. When selling, equally assess market absorptive capacity — sell when you can, not when you want.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Livermore's position-building philosophy can be distilled into one sentence: never add to a loser, only pyramid on winners. Probe → Confirm → Add → Wait → Add again — this cycle strictly follows the iron rule that every step must rest on the profit of the previous one. Deacon White's classic case further reveals the methodology of information verification: never trust any tip; test the market with real trades and let the market tell you the truth.
