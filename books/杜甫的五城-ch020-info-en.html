Five Cities of Du Fu · Ch20 "The Commoner of Xianyang"

Hancheng · Sanmenxia · Fufeng · Pingliang · Guyuan · Linyou · Xianyang

From Pucheng to Hancheng to visit Sima Qian's hometown — his Yuan-dynasty cenotaph shaped like a Mongol yurt, a donkey standing eternal vigil beneath a willow. Searching for the legendary "Pillar in Midstream" at Sanmenxia Dam — the innkeeper announced "It collapsed!" Raining, he stood before two rocks, unable to confirm which was the true pillar. "Walking far to Pingliang" — the refuge from early Tang annals. Woken at 4 AM in Guyuan by the Muslim call to prayer, identical to what he heard growing up in Malaysia. After a winding mountain route, arriving at Linyou to stand before Ouyang Xun's famous stele — "I had finally found their source." Peering through a crack at a Tang Dynasty well, "as if glimpsing a secret of the Tang court." At dawn on the way to Xianyang airport, recalling Yu Xin's "Lament for the South" — "The commoners of Xianyang are not alone in longing for home."

01Hancheng: The Donkey at Sima Qian's Shrine
Two years earlier he had missed Hancheng; this time it was non-negotiable. The city was poor and shabby — "an uninformed observer would never guess this city… produced Sima Qian, a historian rivaling the sun and moon." Master Feng, a tricycle driver with "the air of a Daoist immortal," waited patiently while he ate lunch, then drove him to Zhichuan Town. The Yuan-dynasty cenotaph looked like a Mongol yurt. From the highest tier, the Yellow River shimmered in the distance "like a thin silver ribbon." Below the shrine, a donkey with a cart stood motionless beneath a willow — "A donkey's eyes are always mournful. It stood there beneath that willow, giving one a sense of the eternal, as if it had stood there accompanying Sima Qian's shrine for over two thousand years."
02Sanmenxia: The Mystery of the Collapsed Pillar
Hancheng to Sanmenxia took an entire day, crossing the Yellow River twice. The innkeeper dropped a bombshell: "The Pillar in Midstream collapsed! It collapsed when they built the dam. A big chunk broke off — now only a small piece remains above water." He couldn't sleep that night. Taking the dam workers' special train, he searched in the rain. Two rocks stood in the river: one small near the bank, the other ten times larger with "its upper half clearly collapsed." Workers pointed to the small one as the pillar. He doubted it — "this pillar was so small, so close to shore — how could it match its legendary majestic image?" Later, at the Guo State chariot pit museum, he stumbled upon Kang Youwei's four-character inscription "中流砥柱" — "weren't these characters carved on the pillar itself? How did they end up in a museum?" He left with the mystery unsolved.
03Walking Far to Pingliang: Tang Refuge to Guyuan Dawn Prayers
The Fengxiang ticket seller coldly said "Not running" and returned to her knitting. Spotting a Baoji-Xifeng bus, he recalled the phrase from early Tang annals — "walked far to Pingliang" (远走平凉), where defeated generals fled to safety. He followed suit, visiting the Northern Grotto Temple near Xifeng, so hidden it wasn't rediscovered until the 1950s. Pingliang's terrain was dramatic, squeezed between two mountains — "indeed a defensible place, far more strategic than Xi'an." North to Guyuan, in the Liupan Mountains, July felt like autumn at 23°C. At 4 AM, he was woken by the Muslim call to prayer — "the melodious Arabic chant seemed familiar… it was exactly the same tone as the dawn prayers I grew up hearing in my Malaysian hometown. For a moment, I didn't know where I was."
04Linyou: The Jiucheng Palace Stele and the Tang Well
Determined to see Ouyang Xun's stele, he took a winding route through Lingtai and Liangting — the town names themselves poetic ("Two Pavilions," as if offering shelter to travelers). A dwarf drove cattle home with a broom. He sat eating watermelon, "savoring this small town's simplicity and tranquility." The 1:30 bus the Fengxiang seller claimed didn't exist was running after all. The mountain road grew cooler, the scenery suddenly green — "a bright surprise, like returning to the verdant hills of northern Fujian." Standing before the stele: "In the sixth year of the Zhenguan era, the fourth month of summer, the Emperor sought cool refuge at the Palace of Nine Perfections…" The stone was a deep green granite, "silently enduring over a thousand years of ink-rubbing, the characters ground nearly flat." Returning, he peered through a crack at a Tang Dynasty well discovered in 1980 — "perfectly preserved… as if a Tang palace maid had just finished drawing water and walked away moments ago."
05The Commoner of Xianyang: Yu Xin and the Road Home
Stuck in Xi'an for three extra days — "as if I'd been gifted three free days of time" — he visited the Qinglong Temple ruins and Yang Guifei's bath. At dawn, heading to Xianyang airport, the name "Xianyang" triggered a memory of Yu Xin's "Lament for the South" (哀江南赋): "The commoners of Xianyang are not alone in longing for home." Yu Xin, a southern poet sent as envoy to the Northern Dynasties, was detained as hostage for twenty-five years, never to return to Jiangnan. Du Fu wrote of him: "Yu Xin's life was the bleakest of all; in his twilight years, his poetry moved the realm." Yet in two hours, Lai Ruihe would fly from Xianyang airport, "back to my south." "It was a most singular experience" — both men from the south, both sojourners, fifteen centuries compressed into a single place name. The one who longed to return could finally go home.
💡 Key Insight
The longest chapter geographically, spanning four provinces. Three interwoven threads: Sima Qian's eternal presence (embodied by that mournful donkey), the unresolved mystery of the Pillar in Midstream (Kang Youwei's calligraphy displaced to a museum), and the ancient-modern dialogue of homecoming (Yu Xin's 25-year captivity vs. Lai Ruihe's imminent flight). Lai Ruihe is not merely a Tang historian but a poet who resurrects history through place names — "Xianyang" triggers a 1,500-year-old memory; the Guyuan dawn prayer echoes a Malaysian childhood. Geography is alive. History is not dead text but something that can be revived in a 4 AM prayer call, in a Tang well glimpsed through a crack, in the word "Xianyang" itself.
