The Elements of Style · Chapter 004「Words and Expressions Commonly Misused」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Word choice is the final mile of writing precision. All right or alright? Compare to or compare with? Less or fewer? Singular or plural pronoun after "each"? English contains dozens of pairs commonly confused, misused, and abused — some wholly unacceptable (like I did), some controversial (split infinitives), and many perfectly good words used in the wrong place. This chapter is not a "substitution table" — it is a precision-writing correction manual. When any of these expressions appears, the best fix is to rewrite the sentence entirely.

This is the longest practical reference chapter in the book, listing over 40 words and expressions commonly misused, arranged alphabetically. It covers grammatical construction errors (different than → different from, like I did → as I did), redundant expressions (the fact that, character, nature, system), colloquial degradations (fix, get, kind of), semantic confusions (affect/effect, compare to/with, less/fewer), and clichéd padding to avoid (factor, feature, interesting). Strunk's advice runs throughout: when you find a misuse, do not merely substitute one or two words — rewrite the entire sentence.

Grammar Traps
Common Grammatical Construction Errors
These errors directly violate English grammar norms, instantly exposing a writer's weak linguistic foundation — from confused comparative structures to pronoun-number disagreement, from case errors to wrong relative pronouns.

02
Redundancy
Delete Empty Padding Words
These words are not incorrect in themselves, but in most contexts they add no new information — their presence makes sentences longer, weaker, and fuzzier. Strunk's solution is simple and radical: delete them outright.
✂️
character: acts of a hostile character → hostile acts
✂️
nature: acts of a hostile nature → hostile acts; like character, nature is frequently redundant.
✂️
case: In many cases, the rooms were poorly ventilated → Many of the rooms were poorly ventilated
✂️
system: Dayton has adopted the commission system of government → government by commission
03
Colloquialisms
From Spoken to Written: Vocabulary Downgrade
Many expressions natural in everyday speech — fix, get, kind of, sort of, most (for almost), can (for may) — become casual or incorrect when they enter writing. In formal prose, stick to the literal meaning of these words, not their colloquial extensions.
❌
Fix: Do not use fix to mean arrange, prepare, mend. In writing, fix means only "fasten, make firm."
✅
Get: In writing, avoid have got for have. The past participle is got, not gotten.
❌
Kind of / Sort of: Do not use kind of for rather (before adjectives) or something like (before nouns). Use only literally: Amber is a kind of fossil resin.
✅
Can / Most: can = able (not may). Most ≠ almost. Keep them distinct in formal writing.
04
Word Confusions
Precise Boundaries Between Similar Words
English contains many word pairs confused because of similar pronunciation or spelling — their differences are subtle but fundamental. Mastering these boundaries is the step from "correct" to "precise" writing.
Affect · Effect
→ →
affect (v.) = to influence; effect (n.) = result, (v.) = to bring about. Never confuse them.
Compare to · with
→ →
compare to = point out resemblances between different things; compare with = point out differences between similar things.
Due to · Owing to
→ →
due to for predicate/noun modifier (This invention is due to Edison); loosely as adverb, use owing to or because of.
While · Although
→ →
while literally means "during the time that." Using it as a substitute for although or and/but creates ambiguity.
05
Clichés to Avoid
Eliminate Hollow Padding and Mechanical Emphasis
Factor, feature, interesting, certainly, very — these words are not incorrect, but their overuse has emptied them. Using them is not precise description; it is laziness. The greatest danger: they make writers feel as if they are expressing something, when in fact they are saying nothing.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Over 42 problematic entries weave a protective net for precise writing — from hard grammatical errors (different than, like I did, less/fewer) to the subtraction of redundant padding (character, nature, case, system), from colloquial downgrades (fix, get, kind of) to the subtle boundaries between confusable words (affect/effect, compare to/with), and finally to the alertness against clichés (factor, feature, interesting, very). Strunk does not offer a 'substitution table' — he offers a complete methodology: when you spot a misuse, rewrite the entire sentence. Precision writing is never achieved through patching; it comes from reconstruction.
