Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance · Ch.18 'Withdrawing Quality: The Discovery of Squareness'

What would a world without Quality look like? — Father and son in a rainy tent

Phaedrus hated aesthetics in philosophy school — 'these aestheticians cut Quality into pieces and slowly deliver them into their mouths with knife and fork, which makes me sick.' If you refuse to define Quality, the entire field of aesthetics collapses. He runs a thought experiment: withdraw Quality from the world. Art vanishes. Symphonies vanish. Poetry vanishes. Comedy vanishes. Sports vanish. Markets are reduced to basic foodstuffs. What remains is 'Squareness' — the inability to perceive Quality. The world of knowledge can operate without Quality, but life becomes 'hardly worth living.' That evening, Chris sprains his ankle and breaks down crying. Father and son sit in silence in the tent while rain drums overhead.

01
01 H 01 Hating Aesthetics: Phaedrus the Rebel
Phaedrus deliberately failed his aesthetics course; his paper infuriated the professor. He hated this discipline, criticizing nearly everything about it. It wasn't any particular aesthetician that provoked him, but the field itself — 'because they subordinated Quality under certain academic categories, debasing and insulting it.' He wrote: what these aestheticians were licking was something they themselves had long ago murdered and left to rot.
"These aestheticians treat their studies like peppermint lollipops,
unashamedly licking away with their thick lips.
What they're licking is precisely what they themselves
have long since murdered and left to rot."

— Phaedrus's attack on the field of aesthetics
02
02 T 02 Thought Experiment: A World Minus Quality
Phaedrus argues: to prove Quality exists, remove it from the environment — if the environment ceases to function normally, it exists. He extrapolates: art vanishes (whether you hang a painting or not makes no difference), symphonies vanish (scratching the record sounds as good as the music), poetry vanishes, comedy vanishes, sports vanish (scores become meaningless statistics), the market is reduced to basic food. Applied science and technology change radically; only pure mathematics, philosophy and logic remain. Conclusion: a world without Quality is 'hardly worth living' — the very word 'worth' contains Quality.

03 T 03 The Discovery of Squareness: Hip vs Square
Phaedrus recalls traveling across America with Black artist friends who complained about 'squareness' being too dull. They called educated white people 'square' — boring, no desire to associate with them at all. And what these Black friends called 'soul' was Quality. Phaedrus sees two worlds simultaneously: the knowledge world (Squareness) and the Quality world. Remove Quality and you get Squareness — Squareness is the inability to perceive Quality. So his target shifts from Quality to analysis itself: 'Quality is healthy and untroubled, but analysis seems defective, because it blocks recognition of Quality.'

04 T 04 The Split: A Word That Cuts the World in Two
Phaedrus discovers that Quality is a word that can split the world in two. Take your analytical knife, tap it on the word 'Quality' — the entire world divides: hip and square, classic and romantic, technological and human — the cut is clean. 'He wished Kant were still alive. He would appreciate this. He would find it a super diamond knife — and refusing to define Quality is the key.' On either side of the crack is a kind of Quality: fundamental (romantic) and squared-off (classic). But this isn't what he wants — he wants to synthesize them.

05
05 R 05 Rainy Tent: Father, Son, and Thoreau
Chris sprains his ankle and breaks down: 'I hate all of this! I wish I'd never come!' Father carries all the packs, making multiple trips; Chris deliberately looks away. In the afternoon, rain comes. They move everything into the tent, lie in sleeping bags watching rain drum on the tent roof. Father reads Thoreau aloud to Chris — but Thoreau only describes technology's evils without solutions. 'He was speaking of things in another time and place. He wasn't speaking to us.' Father puts the book down. Both ponder alone. 'Just Chris and me, a patch of forest, and the rain. No book could guide our way anymore.'
"We both fell silent, each lost in thought.
Just Chris and me,
and a patch of forest, and the rain.
No book could guide our way anymore."

— Thoreau said his piece. Now it was our turn to think.
💡 Key Takeaway

The thought experiment of withdrawing Quality reveals its importance: without it, the world doesn't stop functioning — it just stops being worth living in. This discovery splits the world in two: romantic and classic, hip and square. Phaedrus wants to find the balance, not let either side monopolize Quality. In the rainy tent, father puts down Thoreau and sits in silence with Chris — no book can guide the way, and that's precisely the essence of living in the present. It's raining, so listen to the rain. The soup is ready, so drink the soup. 'I think tomorrow will be sunny.'

Source: Robert M. Pirsig, Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance
