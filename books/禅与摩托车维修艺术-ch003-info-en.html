Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance · Chapter 3 · Storms and Ghosts

Between Scientific Spirits and the Truth of the Mind

Chased by a thunderstorm, four motorcycles race across the plains at 95 mph. After finding shelter, an evening discussion about ghost stories spirals into a philosophical vortex: if scientific laws — like gravity — have no mass, no energy, and occupy no space, how are they different from ghosts? The author reveals the striking symmetry between modern "scientific ghosts" and ancient spirit beliefs.

03
Debate The Ghost Story Debate
At night, Chris wants ghost stories. The narrator says he doesn't believe in ghosts — "they lack scientific proof." But then he reverses: "The laws of science also occupy no space, possess no energy, and exist only in the human mind — so the fully scientific attitude is to believe in neither ghosts nor science." John laughs; Sylvia, excited, asks where these ideas come from.

04
Philosophy Gravity: The Modern Ghost
"Before the earth, the sun, the moon, the stars — gravity existed. But it has no mass, no energy, and exists nowhere in space. If you believe it exists, I don't know what non-existence means." The narrator forces the only logical conclusion: gravity did not exist before Newton was born. The laws of nature, like ghosts, are human inventions. Moses, Jesus, Buddha, Newton — they are all "ghosts" that govern how we perceive the world.

05
Specter Phaedrus: The Ghost-Seeker
The narrator confesses to Chris: there was a man named Phaedrus who spent his whole life chasing a ghost — and he found it, beat it up, and became a ghost himself. The narrator admits: none of tonight's ideas about science, ghosts, and caring are his own. "They were stolen from him." Phaedrus has been watching all along, traveling with them — and there is no escaping him.
"I once knew a man who spent his entire life doing nothing but chasing a ghost. He found it, beat the hell out of it — and then became a ghost himself."
💡 Key Takeaway

We mock the ancients for believing in ghosts, yet we hold our own "scientific ghosts" with unquestioning faith. The difference may be smaller than we think.

Source: Robert M. Pirsig, Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance
