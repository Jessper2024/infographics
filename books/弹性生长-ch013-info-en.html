「What Is the Real Reason Behind Housing Price Fluctuations?」

Inflation and Perception — Housing Prices Are Future Value Discounted to the Present

📖 Chapter Overview

In early 2022, the central bank declared real estate a "real economy," followed by provinces lowering down payment ratios. The author dissects the deep ties between real estate and China's economy: urbanization drove industrialization, land-sale revenues funded infrastructure. At its core, housing prices are future expectations "discounted" to the present. Tier-1 prices remain resilient, driven by inflation and collective perception. As Sapiens revealed: all value is an imagined consensus—tier-1 real estate is inflation plus conviction.

🔍 Deep Dive
Real Estate: Engine of the Real Economy
Real estate is undeniably a real economy. Over 25% of heavy industry depends on it; furniture, appliances, renovation, food service are all downstream. Even more critical is infrastructure: land-sale revenue serves as the "down payment," leveraging bank loans for industrial parks. Without "seven connections and one leveling" (road, electricity, water, drainage, gas, heating, internet, land grading), factories cannot be built. Two decades of urban transformation were funded by real estate money circulating into other sectors.
The Essence of Housing Prices: Discounted Future Value
Apply discounted cash flow logic to housing: 100 yuan today is worth more than 100 yuan a year from now. Tesla's high stock price came from Cathie Wood calculating 23 million vehicles of capacity by 2030. Housing works the same way—if people believe Beijing/Shanghai will reach 300,000 yuan/sqm, then 150,000 yuan/sqm today looks cheap. Wanliu Academy once sold at 360,000 yuan/sqm. The wealthy's logic: money is infinitely printable, land isn't. Scarcity plus perception betting—that's the whole housing price mystery.
Blurring Real and Virtual: The Power of Perception
Sapiens' core insight: humans first invent an imagined thing; those who believe put resources into it, and sometimes it succeeds. Those who weave grand visions—successful ones are geniuses, failures are frauds. Whether prices rise or crash, whether money printing is eternal—all are matters of perception. Tier-1 property boils down to two things: inflation and perception. The future largely depends on how many people believe. If everyone expects worsening and stops investing, things will indeed worsen.
The Stew Theory: Assets vs. Money Printing
Assets and money are like meat stewing in a pot, with water constantly added. You must scoop the broth into your own bowl early, before it's diluted. Money enters via credit—Q1 2022 household deposits surged by 7.82 trillion yuan, a record. That's 800 billion yuan entering banks daily over 90 days, following the 1:9 rule. Economic downturn kills industrial investment; parked money with nowhere to go may eventually flow back into tier-1 real estate, chasing higher prices.
Five Core Conclusions
① Outside a few cities, most housing prices will stabilize—no investment value, no bubble, gently declining. ② Tier-1 city consensus on rising prices likely persists; no alternative investments for the rich. ③ During the pandemic, tier-1 areas kept rising while foreclosure listings surged nationwide. ④ Tier-1 cities no longer need land-sale revenue, but historically they've always led price movements. ⑤ Even if tier-1 cities lead this time, cash-strapped regions may not follow.
💡 Key Takeaway

Tier-1 real estate is fundamentally about inflation and perception. Housing prices aren't set by developers or fully controlled by government—they're market-driven bets and collective imagination. The end of the golden 40 years means stop expecting universal price surges, but tier-1 cities' "discounting game" for scarce assets will continue. For most people, prices drifting downward is good news—no bubble means no anxiety, and renting becomes a perfectly fine life choice.
