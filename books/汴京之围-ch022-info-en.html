The Siege of Bianjing · Epilogue "The Disappearance of Genyue"

Part IV · The Jingkang Calamity · Epilogue A wonder of the world — six years to build, mere months to destroy. Along with the garden vanished the lives and ambitions of every protagonist of this era.

The Epilogue weaves together two layers of destruction through the eyes of the Sichuan monk Zuxiu, who visited Genyue twice. Material layer: Huizong's "wonder of the world," built over six years with the entire nation's resources — in just months, rare birds were released, exotic beasts became food, prized rocks turned into catapult ammunition, ancient trees chopped for firewood. Human layer: Zhang Bangchang hanged at Tanzhou, Song Qiyu beheaded at the Court of Judicature, Chen Dong and Ouyang Che executed in the street, Qin Hui framing Yue Fei to death... From puppet emperor to student opinion leader, from surrendered Liao officials to Jurchen marshals, every figure accelerated toward their final end. The Epilogue also reveals the deepest cultural scar of the Jingkang calamity — how it catalyzed the rise of Daoxue Neo-Confucianism, whose doctrine that "starving to death is a small matter, losing chastity a grave one" would shackle Chinese women for nearly a millennium.

01
Eyewitness
The monk Zuxiu entered Genyue twice — the first time he saw paradise, the second, ruins
In 1127, as Bianjing fell and Emperors Huizong and Qinzong were marched north, Zuxiu became Genyue's last true witness. When the Jurchen breached the outer city, Qinzong opened the imperial garden to refugees. Zuxiu stepped inside for the first time. The snow had just cleared; commoners shivered on the slopes fearing for their lives, but the monk was stunned by the beauty before him — "all the world's beauty, all the scenic wonders of past and present, concentrated in this single garden." Months later, when he returned, the rare birds and beasts were gone — waterfowl released into the moat, land animals eaten by starving commoners. The priceless rocks, transported at unimaginable cost, had all become catapult ammunition hurled beyond the walls. The inscribed stelae of nobles and officials had been chiseled blank and tossed into ditches — the "wonder of the world," six years in the making, destroyed in mere months.

02
Imperial Garden
Three mountains, three lakes, a hundred named rocks — reconstructing Genyue's extreme opulence
Genyue was not natural landscape but a man-made marvel — Huizong personally designed it to "gather all the scenic wonders of the world in one place." Based on Zuxiu's "Record of Yanghua Palace" and Huizong's own "Genyue Sketch," the garden spanned over ten li in perimeter, centered on three mountains (Wansui, Shou, and Wansong Ridge) and three lakes (Yan Pond, Dafang Marsh, and Feng Pond). Water diverted from the city moat formed deep gorges; waterfalls were created by laborers carrying water uphill. The garden held lychee, loquat, orange, and pomelo trees from the south, along with jasmine, tuberose, and countless rare herbs. Dozens of named exotic rocks — Rising-Dragon-to-the-Sun, Reclining-Dragon-Gazing-Clouds, Proud-Head Jade-Dragon — dotted the grounds. The three caves of Bixu Cavern had all doors and windows carved from agate.

03
Song Figures
Hanged in Tanzhou, beheaded in the street, framed at Fengbo Pavilion — no Song figure met a good end
After Gaozong's enthronement, those who had survived the Jingkang calamity fell one by one in the "post-war purge." Zhang Bangchang — who had arguably protected Bianjing more than anyone — was hanged at Tanzhou's Pingchu Tower on the charge of "defiling a woman of the imperial house." Song Qiyu, guilty only of being the first to sign the petition electing Zhang Bangchang, was beheaded after the emperor asked, "If Zhang Bangchang had truly become emperor, where would that leave me?" Student leader Chen Dong, who petitioned for Li Gang's reinstatement, was executed in the street alongside Ouyang Che. Qin Hui, who "miraculously" escaped Jurchen captivity, became chancellor, framed Yue Fei to death under the banner of "peace advocacy," suppressed all opposition, and died peacefully in bed — only to be enshrined in the History of Song's "Biographies of Treacherous Ministers." Yuwen Xuzhong spent years as a Song mole inside the Jin court, secretly aiding northern Han Chinese — but Qin Hui proactively sent Yuwen's entire family north to Jin, and all hundred members were burned alive.

04
Jin & Liao Figures
Nianhan died depressed, Wushi fell to factional infighting, Yelü Yudu was lured to his death — Jurchen internal slaughter eclipsed external conquest
The secret of the Jurchen rise lay in the unity of Taizu and Taizong's brothers and kinsmen. But when resources flooded in too quickly and distribution became immediately unequal, internal butchery proved more ferocious than external war. Wushi (Wanyan Xiyin) launched a purge under the pretext of "Khitan conspiracy," first killing the Yanjing garrison commander Gaoli, then marching toward Yunzhong — Yelü Yudu fled toward Western Xia (refused entry for having too few men), then to the Tatars (lured to his death, his head presented to Jin). Wushi even stormed into Nianhan's residence and killed his concubine (former Liao Empress Xiao). Thereafter, Nianhan and Wushi jointly installed Emperor Xizong — only to be stripped of military power by the very emperor they enthroned. After Nianhan's confidant Gao Qingsi was executed, Nianhan himself died of depression. Wushi was briefly reinstated to purge rivals Zongjuan and Zongpan, then killed in a conflict with Wanyan Zongbi — the Western Army's "troika" all perished in infighting. The Jurchen impulse for external expansion had given way to a fever for internal slaughter.

05
Civilization Shock
The Jingkang calamity gave birth to "starving is small, losing chastity is grave" — Daoxue used this to dominate China for a millennium
Nearly every Zhao woman was taken north by the Jurchen during the Jingkang years — this trauma directly catalyzed the mass indoctrination of Daoxue Neo-Confucianism. Before this, Northern Song had been a pragmatic dynasty: emperors did not demand absolute loyalty from ministers, only competence. Practical learning flourished — people valued political science, engineering, and economics over empty moralizing. But during the Jingkang Incident, so many officials cooperated with the Jurchen to send the emperor away; almost no one died as a martyr. This profoundly shocked the court and public. Daoxue scholars seized the moment, demanding the re-establishment of rigid hierarchies — "ruler-ruler, subject-subject, father-father, son-son" — advocating the "extinction of human desire" and packaging blind loyalty as "heavenly principle." At the same time, control over women tightened dramatically: the doctrine that "starving to death is a small matter, losing chastity a grave one" would lock Chinese women's autonomy for nearly a thousand years. Consort Wei, though officially recorded as a "virtuous empress" after returning south, was forever shadowed in popular memory by the suspicion that she had lost her chastity in the north.
"The Jingkang calamity gave Daoxue its moment to dominate the realm... Before this, Northern Song was a pragmatic dynasty. But during the Jingkang years, such momentous disaster occurred, yet so few officials showed loyalty. From then on, the entire social atmosphere intensified moral indoctrination, and Daoxue seized this opportunity to become the mainstream."
🔑 Key Takeaway
Genyue and the Northern Song empire shared a hauntingly parallel fate — six years to build, months to destroy. The Epilogue is not merely an elegy for a garden but a final scan of every protagonist's end: Zhang Bangchang, Song Qiyu, and Chen Dong perished in Gaozong's political purge; Qin Hui crushed the hawks under the banner of "peace"; Nianhan, Wushi, and Yelü Yudu died in the Jurchen's own internal slaughter. But the heaviest legacy was civilizational — the Jingkang calamity became the catalyst for Daoxue's millennium-long dominion, and "starving is small, losing chastity is grave" would bind the fate of Chinese women ever after. By the time Jin's palaces had evolved from "earthen hovels" to the glazed magnificence of Yanjing, a new era had arrived — except this time, the northern regime had grown so powerful that the south would never reclaim it.
