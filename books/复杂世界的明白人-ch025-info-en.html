Clear Thinker in a Complex World · Ch.25 "Growing Trends Toward Greater Efficiency and Fairness"

From Efficiency-First to Fairness-First · Poverty Alleviation Is a Work of Immeasurable Merit "Involution," "Houlang," "dagongren"—behind the surge of new words, society pivots from unregulated free competition toward fairness. America's 650 richest added 33% to their wealth in 8 months, reaching $4 trillion. China invested ¥700 billion in poverty relief. The internet amplifies emotions. The whole world has tumbled into the pit of inequality. Efficiency and fairness are not opposites: reducing inequality itself is an economic lever that boosts domestic demand and unleashes grassroots creativity.

This chapter uses recent social buzzwords (involution / houlang / dagongren) as an entry point to reveal China's fundamental shift from "efficiency first" to "balancing fairness." The core logic chain: the primitive accumulation stage needs unregulated, high-return incentives to motivate pioneers → at a certain stage wealth concentrates irreversibly → social tensions intensify, amplified by the internet → global reckoning → poverty relief + redistribution + unleashing grassroots creativity → efficiency and fairness can harmonize. Ray Dalio warns: when wealth and political gaps widen, internal tensions can spiral from manageable to revolution or civil war—this is not just an economic issue but one of social stability.

01
Social Transition
From Unregulated Free Competition to a Fairness Transition: After the Wild Era, Red Lines Must Be Drawn
Society is shifting from unregulated free competition toward "fairness." The earlier era was a wilderness that needed pioneers. The conditions: no rules, high returns, "what you earn is yours." Back then China lacked efficiency and needed breakneck speed—it aimed to catch up with a century of Western progress in years. Resources could only be concentrated in the hands of the strong. After a period of free competition, the steady state is a small minority taking most of the resources. Companies prioritize profit. Workers are constrained by KPIs. Professional managers have revenue targets. Morality weakens before the target. The author worked over a decade and personally witnessed the shift—from "openly asking candidates if they can work overtime" to today's uproar over sudden workplace deaths.

02
Global Crisis
Global Social Reckoning: The Internet Amplifies Emotion—Inequality Shifts from "Personal Problem" to "Systemic Issue"
The internet amplifies emotion without limit. One person feels frustrated alone. Then they go online and discover countless others feel the same way. Frustration turns to anger. A little provocation and it becomes a riot. Dalio warns: after excessive consumption and borrowing widen wealth and political gaps, internal tensions can spiral from manageable to revolution or civil war. This pit snared the whole world. Before the internet, Americans struggling to find work blamed themselves first. With the network, they realized it was society-wide, and a little prodding ignited rage—hence "zero-dollar shopping" and the Capitol storming. Europe didn't build welfare states out of a love of fussing about—social tensions forced them to relieve pressure.

03
Distribution Trap
Wealth Concentration Is the Enemy of Domestic Demand: The Rich Buy Overseas Assets While Ordinary People Can't Spend
Rich people rarely buy domestic cars. Most of their spending goes to overseas luxury goods or domestic asset purchases. The higher one's income, the lower the share spent on daily expenses. Surplus income goes into asset purchases—which in turn drives up asset prices. This does almost nothing for domestic demand. Ordinary people are the real consumption engine—but they have no money. China's reality: number one in luxury consumption, yet the consumer goods market depends heavily on imports. The rich spend massively overseas; ordinary people lack purchasing power. E-commerce killed offline middlemen and became the middleman itself. It killed offline landlords and became the online landlord—essentially, the landlord consolidated under one company name.

04
Institutional Design
From Consensus to Action: Fair Taxation, Shared Public Gains, Reducing Inequality Is an Economic Issue
Reducing inequality itself is not a moral issue—it is an economic issue. China will now run on domestic demand. If the wealth gap is too wide, a few control too much wealth, and the rest cannot consume. Several baseline considerations: ① Every stratum should bear roughly comparable obligations—"if you stop taxing because it's hard, you punish those who follow the law." ② Prime-location housing premiums come from surrounding public amenities—these were built with public money, but the benefits are captured exclusively by residents. ③ Redistribution—even famously egalitarian Nordic countries use redistribution to push down their Gini coefficients. Most important is "the opportunity to develop"—poverty shackles creativity and limits everyone's freedom.

05
Immeasurable Merit
The Profound Meaning of Poverty Alleviation: Not Just Helping the Poor—It Unlocks Creativity Shackled by Poverty
Poverty's greatest problem is not poverty itself—it shackles creativity. Most people's talents go unrealized. They can change neither themselves nor their surroundings. Poverty alleviation invests to get local economies turning: build roads to activate economic flow, build reservoirs to yield a thousand acres of farmland, experiment with farming, livestock, tourism, photovoltaics—something will take off. At the same time, it transmits economic know-how and a picture of the outside world. This is a work of "immeasurable merit." When the New York Times said China spending ¥700 billion on poverty relief was not worth it, Americans below retorted: "So our money going to arms dealers to bomb other countries is worth it?" Justice speaks for itself. In 2020, China issued negative-yield bonds that Europe scrambled to buy—proof the world has entered the age of slow growth. The call for "fairness" will only grow louder.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Efficiency and fairness are not opposites. The shift from efficiency-first to fairness-first is an inevitable stage of development. The wild era needed unregulated incentives to motivate pioneers; the internal-integration phase demands curbing polarization. Reducing inequality is itself an economic issue: expanding domestic demand requires ordinary people with money to spend; unlocking creativity requires freeing people from poverty. Poverty alleviation earns "immeasurable merit" not merely for helping the poor escape poverty, but for activating talent locked away by poverty, expanding the domestic market, and easing social tensions. The world has entered the age of slow growth—negative-yield bonds snapped up globally prove that growth investment opportunities are scarce. Only organizations that harmonize efficiency and fairness have a future.
