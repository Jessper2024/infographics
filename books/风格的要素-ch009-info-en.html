The Elements of Style · Ch.9 "Make the Paragraph the Unit of Composition"

One paragraph, one topic · Each paragraph's beginning signals a new step in the argument Strunk's Rule 8 opens his "Elementary Principles of Composition." He makes a simple, powerful point: paragraphs are not arbitrary line breaks — they are a navigation system built for the reader. From a brief one-paragraph summary to a seven-paragraph poetry critique, the purpose of paragraphing is constant: to let the reader know, at the start of every paragraph, that a distinct unit of thought is about to begin.

This is the first rule in Chapter III — Elementary Principles of Composition — and Strunk's core teaching on paragraph-level organization. Rule 8 establishes a three-tier decision framework: ① When not to subdivide — if the subject is narrow and the treatment brief, a single paragraph suffices. ② When to subdivide — a large subject requires division into smaller topics, each forming its own paragraph. ③ How to determine paragraph count — depends on the scope of each subtopic. Strunk provides three concrete templates: poetry critique (7 paragraphs), novel analysis (4 paragraphs), and historical event (3 paragraphs). He also emphasizes three exceptions: transitional sentences, dictionary-style entries, and dialogue — each new speaker requires a new paragraph.

01
Core Principle
One paragraph per topic: the sole purpose of paragraphing is to build a clear navigation system for the reader
Paragraphing serves the reader, not the writer. Strunk states plainly: the beginning of each paragraph signals to the reader that a new step in the argument is about to begin. This is a pure reader-experience principle. The writer already knows the terrain; the reader approaches it for the first time. Paragraphs are the map the writer draws, marking "a new topic starts here." A large subject should be divided into smaller topics, each given its own paragraph — not for the writer's convenience, but for the reader's cognitive comfort.

02
When NOT to Subdivide
A narrow subject with brief treatment: write one paragraph, then check if subdivision would be better
More paragraphs is not always better. If the subject is narrow — a brief description, a book summary in brief, a short account of an incident, an outline of an action, a single idea — it is best written as a single paragraph. The crucial step: examine afterward whether one paragraph serves better than several. This after-the-fact test transforms paragraphing from guesswork into verification. If the one-paragraph version reads worse than a subdivided one, rewrite. If not, leave it.

03
Paragraph Templates
Three classic paragraphing templates: from 2 to 7 paragraphs, a progressive structure system
Strunk provides three concrete, reusable paragraphing templates covering analysis from simple to complex. ① Book/poem brief review (2 paras): A. Account of the work → B. Critical discussion. ② Poetry critique (7 paras): A. Facts of composition and publication → B. Kind of poem; metrical form → C. Subject → D. Treatment of subject → E. For what chiefly remarkable → F. Wherein characteristic of the writer → G. Relationship to other works. ③ Novel analysis (4 paras): A. Setting → B. Plot → C. Characters → D. Purpose. ④ Historical event (3 paras): A. What led up to → B. Account of → C. What followed. Strunk notes: in practice, some subtopics may need further subdivision.

04
Three Exceptions
Single-sentence paragraphs allowed: transitions, dictionary entries, and dialogue speaker changes
As a rule, single sentences should not be written as paragraphs. Three exceptions exist. ① Transitional sentences: in exposition or argument, a brief sentence indicating the relation between parts commonly stands alone as a paragraph. ② Dictionary-style entries: in textbooks or guides, many topics are touched upon briefly, each as its own short paragraph. ③ Dialogue: each speech, even if only a single word, is a paragraph by itself; a new paragraph begins with each change of speaker. When narrative and dialogue are combined, Strunk recommends learning this rule from well-printed works of fiction.

05
The Art of Subdivision
Templates are a starting point, not a prison: subdivide further when the material demands it
Strunk's templates are a starting point, not an endpoint. He specifically warns: when actually writing on either of these two subjects — poetry or fiction — the writer may find it necessary to subdivide one or more of the topics. For poetry, paragraphs C (Subject) and D (Treatment) vary with the poem — C may need to state the actual or imagined circumstances, then indicate the subject and outline its development; if the poem is a narrative in the third person, C need contain no more than a concise summary of the action. D should indicate the leading ideas and show how they are made prominent, or indicate what points the narrative chiefly emphasizes. Paragraph design must flex based on the specific content, not mechanically follow a template.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
Paragraphing is not formatting convention — it is reader-experience infrastructure. Strunk's rule is elegantly simple: one paragraph, one independent unit of thought; each paragraph's opening announces "a new topic begins." Whether a one-paragraph brief or a seven-paragraph poetry analysis, the principle is constant — it serves the reader, building an ordered cognitive map through unfamiliar content. When unsure how to paragraph, start with Strunk's three templates, then adjust in the writing. Good paragraphing makes the reader forget paragraphs exist, focusing only on your ideas.
