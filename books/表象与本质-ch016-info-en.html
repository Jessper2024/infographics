Surfaces and Essences · Acknowledgments

Two authors, two languages, two lives. A book's birth requires more than minds and ink—it needs the unconditional love and companionship of family, the intellectual inspiration and collision of colleagues, the warmth and goodwill of friends. This is scholarship, and this is life.

This section records the gratitude both authors owe to family, colleagues, and friends over the seven-year writing journey. Hofstadter thanks his lifelong partner Baofen, his children Danny and Monica, and his sister Laura's family; Sander thanks his wife Cécile and three children, as well as his parents Jean-Pierre and France. Both also tenderly memorialize loved ones lost during this period. On the academic side, Sander especially thanks the entire Paragraphe laboratory at the University of Paris 3 and the CRAC research group; Hofstadter thanks every member of FARG (the Fluid Analogies Research Group) over its more than thirty years—from early pioneers Marsha Meredith and Melanie Mitchell to the newest generation of researchers. Both authors also thank the editorial teams at Basic Books and Odile Jacob, as well as friends who proofread the English and French manuscripts. The acknowledgments close with warmth: in the course of years of working side by side, Hofstadter's friends became Sander's friends, and Sander's friends became Hofstadter's friends—the boundaries of categories were blurred by friendship.

Family
Muses, Children, and Bloodlines—The Family Bedrock of Both Authors
The acknowledgments open with family—because a book's birth depends first on emotional support. Hofstadter first thanks his lifelong partner Baofen—"she was our muse, full of charm, admirable, loving us and loved by us"; son Danny and daughter Monica—"full of humor, vitality, idealism, and artistic imagination," qualities mostly inherited from their late mother Carol. Sander thanks his wife Cécile, and three children—Michael (loving, intensely emotional), Tom (sensitive, sociable, reliable), and Talia (playful, witty, creative). Both authors also thank members of their extended families: Hofstadter remembers his late parents Robert and Nancy, and thanks his sister Laura's family; Sander thanks his brother David's family and his parents Jean-Pierre and France. During the seven years of writing, both lost dear ones—on Sander's side, Raphaël, Agnès, Maurice, Esther, and others; on Hofstadter's side, his mother Nancy, dear friend Steve Larson, Valentino Braitenberg, and Paolo Bozzi—the acknowledgments tenderly memorialize each one.

02
Research Teams
FARG and Paragraphe—The Intellectual Confluence of Two Research Camps
This book about analogy was itself born from the collision of ideas between two research teams. Hofstadter's FARG (Fluid Analogies Research Group) has devoted over thirty years to computational modeling of "that rich and elusive mental phenomenon called analogy-making," with members including the first pioneers—Marsha Meredith (Seek-Whence), Melanie Mitchell (Copycat), Robert French (Tabletop), Gary McGraw and John Rehling (Letter Spirit), James Marshall (Metacat), Harry Foundalis (Phaeaco)—as well as later joiners Matthew Hurley, Ben Kovitz, William York, David Bender, and others. Sander's Paragraphe laboratory (University of Paris 3) is his academic home, with lab director Imad Saleh described as "warm, generous, and rigorous." The CRAC research group ("Comprehension, Reasoning, and Knowledge Acquisition"), co-directed by Sander and Raphaële Miljkovitch, brings together researchers across many areas of developmental psychology—Jean Baratgin, Christelle Bosc-Miné, Rémi Brissiaud, and over a dozen others, along with successive doctoral students whose "fresh and open minds" were "invaluable treasures" to Sander. The intersection of these two camps is the fundamental reason this book can encompass perspectives ranging from cognitive science experiments to computational modeling to philosophical reflection.

Global Friendships
From Paris to Bloomington, from Italy to the World—Intellectual Partners Across Continents
The two authors' lists of thanks span four continents. Hofstadter's circle of friends begins in France—François Vannucci, Jacqueline Henry, Serge Haroche, and many more; extends to Italy—"where he is always warmly welcomed," including Benedetto Scimemi, Giuseppe Trautteur, Francesco Bianchini, and dozens of others; then to North America—from Scott Buresh and Kellie Gutman to heavyweight figures in cognitive science and philosophy such as Daniel Dennett, John Holland, Bob Axelrod, Dick Nisbett, Gilles Fauconnier, and Mark Turner; and finally worldwide—Francisco Claro (Chile), Peter Smith (UK), Marina Eskina (Russia), Hakan Toker (Turkey), and others. Sander also thanks numerous colleagues and friends who gave him "profound influence," from Jean-François Richard ("the role of unfailing guardian") to longtime friends Youri Beltchenko, Florence Deluca Boutrois, and others. Notably, both authors mention that "many of Hofstadter's friends have become Sander's friends, and many of Sander's friends have become Hofstadter's friends"—the boundaries of categories were blurred by friendship—a formulation that itself provides a vivid footnote to the book's central theme.

04
Publishers & Proofreaders
A Transatlantic Publishing Journey—The Parallel Birth of English and French Editions
A unique aspect of this book is that both authors simultaneously wrote the English edition (Surfaces and Essences) and the French edition (L'Analogie, cœur de la pensée). This required close cooperation between two publishers. On the American side, Basic Books' John Sherer "believed in this book for many years"; after his departure, T. J. Kelleher and Lara Heimert "kept the flame of enthusiasm burning"; Nicole Caputo, Andrea Cardenas, Tisse Takagi, and other team members made enormous contributions to the English edition's publication. On the French side, Odile Jacob and Bernard Gotlieb gave "steadfast support"; Jean-Luc Fidel "read the manuscript carefully and offered penetrating comments." Proofreading of both editions was meticulously arranged: David Bender, Steven Williams, and Jane Stewart Adams handled the English text; Christelle Bosc-Miné and Karine Duvignau handled the French text. Greg Huber, Tom Seeber, and D. Alvin Oyzeau made "various adjustments" to the book's figures. This complex process of bilingual, synchronous publication is itself an "analogy"—two authors expressing the same system of thought in two languages, two publishers disseminating the same book in two cultural markets.

05
Coda
When Category Boundaries Are Blurred by Friendship—This Book's Human Warmth
The close of the acknowledgments contains the book's warmest human footnote. "It goes without saying that many of Hofstadter's friends have become Sander's friends, and many of Sander's friends have become Hofstadter's friends, thus blurring the boundaries of all these categories. This exchange has been the best unexpected reward of working side by side for so many years." This passage is not merely a polite gesture—it is a personal enactment of the book's core theme: category boundaries are never rigid—just as the boundaries of categories like "dog" or "chair" are fuzzy, the categories "Hofstadter's friends" and "Sander's friends" gradually merged over years of collaboration. In this sense, the acknowledgments are not simply a list of names, but the final piece of testimony for this book's theory about "categorization and analogy-making." Finally, the two authors write: "In this process, we learned so much—about thinking, about writing, about language. We hope you will find pleasure and inspiration in our collaboration."
1
The fusion of category boundaries: Hofstadter's circle of friends and Sander's circle of friends—"blurring the boundaries of all these categories"—this itself enacts the book's central theme.
2
An unexpected reward: "This exchange has been the best unexpected reward of working side by side"—the genuine emotion of scholarly collaboration.
3
Seven years to forge a book: "At times, we truly felt the process was too long, as if it would never end. But at last, we have reached this point."
4
The final blessing: "We hope you will find pleasure and inspiration in our collaboration"—a simple and warm farewell.
🔑 Core Takeaway
These acknowledgments are not a perfunctory list but a warm portrait showing how an intellectual life is nourished by family, friendship, and scholarly community. The two authors spent seven years, crossing the boundaries of language and continent, weaving together theoretical reflections on analogy with personal experiences of friendship. When Hofstadter writes "our muses" to describe their respective life partners, when Sander mentions that Paragraphe lab colleagues are their "precious friends," when both marvel that "many of Hofstadter's friends have become Sander's friends"—category boundaries dissolve naturally in human connection. This book is not only a theoretical exposition about analogy; it is itself an analogical practice that crosses languages (English and French), cultures (America and France), and disciplines (cognitive science, philosophy, mathematics, physics). And the acknowledgments are the final chapter of this grand practice—they transform the theoretical proposition that "categorization and analogy-making are the same thing" into a love letter addressed to all who participated.
