Great Country, Big City · Ch.6「Introduction: Our Predicament」

Left-behind Children · Migrant Workers · Spatial Agglomeration · Urban Problems · Migration At 3 AM in the matsutake picking season—two months, two people, 5000 yuan. This is the story at the beginning. 61 million left-behind children, 270 million migrant workers—these are the other faces of China's urbanization. City dwellers complain about traffic, smog, and overcrowding, forgetting that every "outsider's" migration is deeply connected to each of us. The author says: "I've decided to talk to ourselves." The entire story begins at 3 AM in a matsutake forest.

This introduction opens with the story of matsutake mushroom gatherers from the CCTV documentary "A Bite of China," revealing the core contradictions of China's urbanization. On one side, 61 million left-behind children in rural areas await their parents' return. On the other, 270 million migrant workers power China's economic miracle in cities. Urbanites complain about traffic congestion and smog caused by "too many people," while local governments raise barriers to reduce migrant children's access to schools. Meanwhile, population outflow regions face hollowing and aging. The author poses the central question: global economic activity concentrates in a few regions—this is a universal law. Will China follow a different path? How are urban problems related to population concentration? The entire book unfolds from this starting point.

01
Starting Point
Matsutake at 3 AM: Two Months of Rain, 5000 Yuan
"It takes about one kilometer to find one matsutake," says Dainzin Droma, a Tibetan woman in Shangri-La. Every day during the rainy season she leaves at 3 AM, walking 30 km into primeval forest searching for wild mushrooms. After two months, she and her mother earn 5000 yuan. This is a story from the CCTV documentary "A Bite of China." But this food documentary accidentally highlights another number: 61 million left-behind children. Matsutake is a delicacy on city tables; left-behind children are the hidden cost of urban prosperity. Between these two realities lie a billion moving Chinese people and an overlooked economic question: How are cities and countryside, coast and inland, connected?
02
The Other Side
A Thousand Kilometers Away: The World's Densest Factories
Depart from China's interior, and a thousand kilometers away lies the coast—with the world's densest concentration of factories and buildings, endless ships flowing through busy ports connecting this "world factory" to every corner of the planet. Over 160 million migrant workers labor in eastern regions—nearly 60% of all migrant workers. But mass mobility hasn't brought integration—it has brought panic. Urbanites complain about traffic and overcrowding, even attributing smog to "too many people." At one urban population policy meeting, a government official listed among achievements: "In about one year, by raising various thresholds, we reduced the number of migrant children attending local schools by 20,000."
03
Outflow Regions
Northeast in Trouble: From Inflow to Outflow
Between 2000–2010, China's three northeastern provinces experienced a net population outflow of 2 million. The "eldest son of the Republic" transformed from a population recipient to a population sender. Not just the northeast—Anhui, Sichuan, Hubei—the list of net-outflow provinces is long. People express nostalgia and blame urbanization for "draining the blood" from the countryside. But the author questions: Is this really the case? Is population migration a natural result of urbanization, or a distortion caused by institutional barriers? Are left-behind children and empty-nester elderly the fault of urbanization itself, or the pain of incomplete urbanization—"semi-urbanization"—imposed by the Hukou system?
04
Author's Statement
Deciding to Talk to Ourselves
In discussions of public policy, many don't realize that what blocks social welfare improvement is our own biases and misunderstandings. So the author declares: "I've decided to talk to ourselves. I've decided to talk about the country and its cities, and about their relationship with us. I've decided to set out." This is the book's core motivation: each of us—whether urban or rural, official or ordinary citizen—has our own views and interests regarding urbanization. But these views are often based on intuition rather than facts, on the local rather than the whole, on the short-term rather than the long-term. This book seeks to "talk to ourselves"—eliminate cognitive biases and build a framework based on economic logic.

05
View from Above
What Night Lights Reveal
Thanks to modern technology, looking down at our planet is easy. NASA satellite data-produced night light maps reveal a simple fact: The brightest areas are always concentrated on a tiny fraction of the world's land. Between countries, light intensity reflects economic development—the contrast between South Korea and North Korea is starkest. Within a country, light intensity reflects economic and population concentration—the US East Coast megalopolis and narrow West Coast corridor are brightest. Europe's brightest areas cluster around major western cities. This map shatters a popular illusion—that the world is "flat." In reality, the world has never been flat. The geographic concentration of economic activity is a universal law.
06
Core Concept
Spatial Agglomeration: Half the Global Economy on 1.5% of Land
The World Bank's "World Development Report 2009" opens by stating: Half of global economic activity occurs on just 1.5% of the world's land area. Cairo produces more than half of Egypt's GDP on 0.5% of its land. Brazil's three south-central states generate over half of national output on just 15% of its land. These figures show: spatial agglomeration is not an exception but the norm. Not a policy failure but an economic law. So, will China's population distribution follow a different path from every other country?

07
Core Question
Urban Problems & Population Concentration: What's the Link?
For those living in big cities, what is the relationship between their troubles—traffic congestion, air pollution, high housing prices, strained public services—and the concentration of population in large cities? This is one of the core questions the entire book attempts to answer. In many people's view, the answer is obvious—"too many people." But the author argues a more productive question is: What truly causes "urban disease"? Is it population size itself, or lagging planning, management, and infrastructure? Should governments control population, or improve urban governance? These questions require the full economic logic of the entire book to answer.
08
Unique Perspective
From Urban Population to the Euro Crisis: A Leap
"Jumping from urban population distribution to the European debt crisis—is that too fast? Don't worry, follow me." The author uses a compelling argument to connect seemingly unrelated issues. The core contradiction of the Euro crisis: unified currency without unified fiscal policy, and insufficient labor mobility—this is precisely the European version of China's current problem. China is a unified great nation with a unified currency and common market, yet its institutional barriers to labor mobility are greater than those between EU countries. Will China face "Europeanization"? That is: a common market in name only, while the negative effects of a unified currency manifest through enormous regional gaps. This is the book's core warning.
09
Triple Paradox
Unity, Development, and Balance: The "Impossible Triangle"
The book's analytical framework revolves around three goals: the efficiency benefits of unity (unified market and currency), the needs of economic development (especially urbanization), and the social goal of regional "balanced development." These three goals have internal tensions. The author argues in subsequent chapters: if "balance" is interpreted as uniform resource distribution pursued through administrative means, then both efficiency and unity will suffer. The solution lies in redefining "balance"—shifting from uniform resource distribution to "per capita" balance, which can actually be achieved through free population mobility. This is the book's theoretical thread.

10
Book Roadmap
Follow the Author: From Matsutake to the Road Ahead
Departing from 3 AM in the matsutake forest, the journey takes us through an intellectual exploration of economic geography. Chapter 1 begins with the Euro crisis, demonstrating the danger of China's "Europeanization." Chapter 2 uses international experience to show the path toward balance through agglomeration. Chapter 3 presents and resolves the "impossible triangle." Chapter 4 proves that big cities do not die. Subsequent chapters address land policy, Hukou reform, urban systems, housing prices, and more. The book's core message is consistent throughout: eliminate institutional barriers to labor mobility, let market forces guide resources to where they are most efficient; achieve per capita balance through agglomeration—this is the only viable strategic path for a great nation.
「城市发展的本质不是均匀分布，而是集聚——人口向大城市集中是全球规律，中国的城市化也必然走向集聚。」

🔑 Core Conclusion
The core problem-awareness of "Great Country, Big City" originates from two seemingly unrelated phenomena. Behind the hard work of matsutake gatherers and the plight of 61 million left-behind children lies the incompleteness of China's urbanization: labor moves to cities, but families are split by the Hukou system, turning urbanization into "semi-urbanization." Starting from the Earth at night, the author reveals the universal law of spatial agglomeration and presents the analytical framework: China as a unified great nation faces an "impossible triangle" of unity, development, and balance. The solution is to redefine "balance"—from uniform resource distribution to per capita balance. The entire book enters through the Euro crisis, examines every controversy of urbanization through economic logic, and seeks to answer one fundamental question: Which path should China, as a great nation, take?
