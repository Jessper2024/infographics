Naming the Dynasty — The Politics Behind "Ming"

Zhu Yuanzhang named his new dynasty "Ming" — influenced by folk religion, feng shui, and ultimately his own will.

The Yuan took its name from the I Ching ("Great is the Qian Yuan"). Folklore claims he named it after the Mingjiao religion, while others argue Southern fire "Ming" was meant to suppress Northern water "Yin."

He first considered claiming Song Neo-Confucian scholar Zhu Xi as his forebear, but Zhu Xi lived too recently to fake. He often called himself "Huaiyou Banyi" — but this title was his alone, others dared not utter it.

Three Theories for "Ming"

1. Mingjiao religious origin
2. Feng shui: Fire conquers Water
3. Zhu's personal will — No consensus

The Ancestor Claim Fiasco

Zhu Xi lived too close in history to fake. The "glorious ancestor" plan was abandoned before it began.

Agriculture First — Zhu Yuanzhang's Rural Revival

Zhu made economic recovery his top priority, using three simultaneous policies — tax-free land reclamation, penal labor for farming, punitive taxation on enemies.

In 1370 he issued a land reclamation edict: cultivate wasteland, enjoy three years of tax exemption, "this land is yours." Over 10,000 officials in Fengyang were sent to plant rice seedlings. Jiangsu-Zhejiang was the sole exception — taxes several times higher for supporting Zhang Shicheng.

Anti-Commerce — The Shen Wansan Case

Zhu Yuanzhang harbored deep prejudice against merchants, pursuing a pro-agriculture/anti-commerce policy with tycoon Shen Wansan as its most famous casualty.

Shen Wansan financed one-third of Nanjing's city walls, attempting political investment. But Zhu thought: How dare a merchant build the imperial capital's walls? After the walls were finished, he moved to execute Shen. Only Empress Ma's intercession saved him — but Zhu confiscated all property and exiled him to Yunnan.

Imperial Examination — Ming's Elite Production Machine

The Ming established a tiered imperial examination using the "eight-legged essay," selecting countless talents while imprisoning intellectuals' thinking within the Zhu Xi interpretive framework.

Four levels: county exam (童生→秀才), provincial exam (举人), palace exam (贡生), and imperial interview (进士). Examination hall cells just 5 chi by 4 chi wide. Candidates hunched for three days and two nights with three candles — the most grueling job interview in history.

The eight-legged essay (八股文) was a Ming innovation. Strictly limited to Four Books and Five Classics, mandating eight-part parallel prose, capped at 500 characters. Candidates could not express their own ideas — only think like Zhu Xi. This system lasted nearly 500 years.

05
Institutional Legacy — The Chancellor's Fate

Zhu Yuanzhang largely copied the Yuan framework, establishing the Central Secretariat and Six Ministries — but after Hu Weiyong's case, he abolished the chancellorship, initiating unprecedented imperial autocracy.

At founding, Li Shanchang served as chancellor with the title Duke of Han. After Hu Weiyong's case erupted in 1380, Zhu acted decisively — within one month he abolished the chancellor position, forbidding its restoration for all future generations.

The personal cost was staggering: from September 14-21, 1384, just eight days, Zhu reviewed 1,666 documents and handled 3,391 matters — averaging 200 files and 400 matters daily. He needed no chancellor, for he WAS the CEO, CFO, and CTO of the entire nation.

💡 Core Conclusion

Founding a dynasty is not merely a dynastic change. Zhu established a nation through naming, agricultural consolidation, intellectual co-optation, and the abolition of the chancellorship — each step a carefully calculated act of statecraft. His institutional framework profoundly shaped Ming political development for 276 years, with echoes extending into modernity.
