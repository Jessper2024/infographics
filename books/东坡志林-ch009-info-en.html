Dongpo Zhilin · Ch.9「Alternative Judgments on Historical Figures」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer History books fix people into standardized images—Yao and Shun are sage-kings, Liu Bang is a magnanimous ruler, Confucius is the eternal teacher, Li Yu is the tragic poet-king. But Su Shi insists on reading between the lines. Was the Yao-Shun abdication really noble virtue? Was Liu Bang truly forgiving? Did Confucius execute Shaozheng Mao too hastily? And Xunzi's famous maxim "blue comes from indigo"—Su Shi calls it "no different from dream-talk." When orthodox narratives collide with the wrinkles of reality, how does Su Shi dissect historical figures with surgical precision and deliver stunning "counter-narratives"? His answer: reject every unquestioned verdict. Through seemingly playful marginal notes, he dismantles imperial halos, sage myths, and literary pretensions, revealing a more complex, flesh-and-blood human truth.

This chapter collects the first half of Su Shi's "Figures" section—fifteen character critiques: The Affairs of Yao and Shun, On Emperor Gaozu's Marquis of Gengjie, Emperor Wu Receives Wei Qing on the Toilet, Minor Differences in Imperial Edicts, Postscript to Li Yu's Lyrics, The Trust of Zhenzong and Renzong, Confucius Executes Shaozheng Mao, A Playful Note on Yan Hui, Refuting Xunzi on "Blue Out of Indigo," Yan Zhu's Skill in Poverty, Zhang Yi Deceives Chu, Zhao Yao's Scheme to Replace Zhou Chang, Huang Ba's "Divine Sparrow," Wang Jia's Lightening of Laws, and Li Bangzhi on Zhou Yu. From ancient sage-kings to Han emperors, from Confucian disciples to Warring States strategists—Su Shi applies "reverse thinking" to challenge every orthodox verdict.

01
Deconstructing Emperors
The Yao-Shun Abdication & Liu Bang's Grudge—Ripping Off Imperial Halos
Regarding the noble Yao-Shun abdication, Su Shi scoffs: "A scholar who shows distress on his face over a bowl of rice and soup—I myself would not believe such a man." The legends of Yao offering the throne to Xu You, who refused in shame—Su Shi finds them preposterous: "To hand over the world is that difficult; how could it be so casually passed around?" On Liu Bang, Su Shi is even more devastating. Liu Bang is praised as "magnanimous, not holding grudges"—yet he nursed a lifelong resentment because his sister-in-law once pretended the soup was finished, scraping the pot with a spoon. He sealed his nephew as "Marquis of Pot-Scraping." Su Shi asks: aren't you afraid that your father remembers how at Guangwu, you begged Xiang Yu "share a cup of the stew"—meaning your own father's life? On Emperor Wu of Han, one brief note: receiving Wei Qing while on the toilet, yet refusing to see Ji An without formal dress. "Nothing worth praising—except receiving Wei Qing on the toilet... that's somewhat commendable."

02
Textual Correction
Minor Variants in Classics & Li Yu's "Tears"—Truth Through Close Reading
Su Shi is exquisitely sensitive to textual variants. He notices that imperial edicts from the Han dynasty quote the Analects and Classic of Filial Piety with "minor differences" from the received texts: "I suspect vulgar scholars added words on their own authority." On Li Yu's famous lament—"tears shed before palace maidens"—Su Shi's criticism is severe: "Betrayed by Fan Ruoshui, he surrendered the entire nation. He should have wept bitterly before the ancestral temple, apologized to his people, then departed. Yet he merely shed tears before palace maidens, listening to farewell music!" When the nation falls, one should weep for ancestors and people—not palace women. Regarding Emperors Zhenzong and Renzong, Su Shi's perspective is uniquely illuminating: twenty years after Li Hang's death, Zhenzong still refused to employ a man Li Hang had called "not a gentleman." Su Shi praises this "trans-temporal trust": "Solely because he had no selfish motives—that is why."
Most heart-wrenching, the day I fled the temple, / The music academy still played farewell songs, / I shed tears before palace maidens.
—Li Yu · Su Shi rebukes: "He should have wept before the ancestral temple"
03
Reassessing Sages
Confucius Kills Shaozheng Mao & Xunzi's "Blue from Indigo"—Dismantling Academic Myths
Su Shi's attitude toward Confucius refuses blind reverence. Confucius, as Minister of Justice of Lu, executed Shaozheng Mao within seven days—"some thought this too hasty." Su Shi's dark humor: "This old fellow probably knew his own head was square-shaped and his fate thin—he wouldn't hold the post long. So he hurried to deal with the matter before leaving office." On Yan Hui, Su Shi is playful yet tender: "If Yan Hui had eaten two more bowls of rice and half a gourd more of water, would he have lived past twenty-nine? Yet Heaven regularly gave Robber Zhi two days' worth of stolen goods—enough to feed Yan Hui for seventy years. I fear only that Yan Hui wouldn't want it." The most spectacular is Su Shi's demolition of Xunzi's "blue comes from indigo": "Blue is indigo, ice is water. Fermenting rice into wine, slaughtering sheep for delicacies—if one said 'wine is sweeter than rice, cooked mutton is finer than sheep,' even a child would laugh. Yet Xunzi used this to make his argument. Believe it—these are drunken, dream-reversed words!" The famous analogy dismissed as child-level sophistry.

04
Wisdom in Adversity
Yan Zhu's Poverty & Zhang Yi's Deception—Skill and Clumsiness in Survival
Su Shi both admires and critiques Yan Zhu's famous dictum: "Eat late to substitute for meat, walk steadily to substitute for riding." Yan Zhu refused the King of Qi's lavish patronage and returned to poverty. Su Shi calls him "skilled in dwelling with poverty," yet "he had not attained the Way"—because "to still speak of 'substituting for meat' and 'substituting for riding' shows he still had meat and riding on his mind." Su Shi, having lived long in poverty himself, understands this nuance: "Without my own long poverty, I could not know Yan Zhu's skill." On Zhang Yi's deception of the King of Chu—promising 600 li of land, delivering only 6—Su Shi's perspective transcends conventional outrage: "What are 600 li worth? Zhang Yi was not Chu's minister; he schemed for Qin. Why deeply blame him?" The truly contemptible are later ministers who deceive their own rulers: "Follow my words, and the world will be at peace"—yet "in the end, not a shred of gain." Such ministers, Su Shi concludes, are worse than Zhang Yi serving Chu.

05
Historiography & Governance
Huang Ba's "Divine Sparrow" & Wang Jia's Legal Reform—Governance and Historical Ethics
Through his father's friend Shi Jingchen, Su Shi mocks Huang Ba's falsified achievements: "Huang Ba mistook a pheasant for a divine bird—what, then, was the so-called 'phoenix of Yingchuan'?" Huang Ba initially championed moral education, then resorted to petty tricks—"vulgar!" The harshest criticism targets historiography: Wang Jia, a worthy chancellor, "lightened laws and reduced punishments"—a great virtuous act—yet Ban Gu's Book of Han does not record this! The Liang Tong biography, opposing leniency, argued "Gao, Hui, Wen, Jing prospered with harsh laws; Ai, Ping declined with lenient ones." Su Shi's refutation is masterful: "This is like a man who, young and reckless with drink and women, remained healthy; old and abstemious, fell sick—and from this concludes that drink prolongs life. Can that be right?" Liang Tong's words brought divine retribution: his sons died unnatural deaths, his grandson's entire clan exterminated. Finally, Li Bangzhi's self-deprecation: "Zhou Yu at twenty-four aimed to conquer the Central Plains. Now I, at forty, merely sleep well and eat heartily." Su Shi leaves this without comment—a silent echo of lost ambition.
Like a man who, reckless in youth with drink and women, stayed healthy; old and abstemious, fell sick—and concludes drink prolongs life. Can this be right?
—Su Shi · Refuting "harsh laws build nations," using alcohol as metaphor for governance

TAKEAWAY · Core Insight
Su Shi's character critiques are less historical research than a declaration of spiritual independence. He refuses to believe the myth of Yao and Shun, Liu Bang's magnanimity, the justice of Confucius executing Shaozheng Mao, even Xunzi's classic analogy. He doubts every "truth" wrapped in orthodox packaging. But his doubt is not nihilism—it is driven by profound insight into human nature. He knows emperors hold grudges, sages have private motives, and classics have been tampered with by later hands. Behind this "reverse thinking" is a soul that never flatters authority, never fears convention—freely scrutinizing history. Fifteen short essays, together, constitute Su Shi's "historical skepticism"—independence is the true character of a scholar.
