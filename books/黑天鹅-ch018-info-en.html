The Black Swan · Chapter 18 "The Uncertainty of the Phony"

The uncertainties of games and quantum physics cancel out on average; those of socioeconomic and weather variables do not There are two kinds of uncertainty in this world. The first is like a casino roulette wheel — certainty emerges in the long run (the house always wins). The second is like a market crash or a hurricane — it never averages out; a single extreme event can define the entire outcome. Those who treat the second as if it were the first are "phonies" — they pretend to understand the incomprehensible. This chapter is the ultimate guide to spotting them.

This chapter is the finale of Part Three, revisiting and deepening the core insight of the Ludic Fallacy. Taleb distinguishes two fundamentally different types of uncertainty: Type 1 (found in games and quantum physics) cancels out on average over the long run and can be precisely described by probability; Type 2 (found in socioeconomic and weather variables) never cancels out — extreme events define the entire distribution. Those who treat Type 2 as Type 1 — the philosophers, economists, and risk managers who use Gaussian mathematics, probability models, and precise forecasts to "manage" Extremistan — are the phonies. They may understand the difference between the two types of randomness in theory, but they do not apply that understanding in real life. The chapter offers a simple test for spotting phonies: look at what they do with their own money.

01
Fundamental Taxonomy
Two kinds of uncertainty: one cancels out, the other never does
Uncertainty is not a single thing — the difference between swimming and skydiving is the difference between two types of uncertainty. Type 1 uncertainty exists in games and quantum physics: though individual outcomes are unpredictable, after many repetitions the probability distribution converges to a definite mean — the uncertainty "averages out." Type 2 uncertainty exists in socioeconomic and weather variables: extreme events define the entire distribution, and uncertainty never cancels out. Black Swans belong to Type 2 — a single event can define an entire system's behavior, and we know nothing about the probabilities of such events.

02
Ludic Fallacy
The Ludic Fallacy redux: real life is not roulette — the rules are never fully defined
In a casino, you know all possible outcomes and their probabilities; in the real world, you don't even know what game you're playing. Games (including "games" in probability mathematics) involve domesticated uncertainty — rules are known, outcomes can be enumerated, probabilities can be calculated. Real-world uncertainty is wild — you don't know all possible outcomes, let alone their probabilities. When economists model markets as "random walks" and when risk managers use "Value at Risk," they are committing the Ludic Fallacy: treating real-world uncertainty as if it were a game with known probabilities. This is not an approximation — it is a category error.

03
The Ultimate Test
Find the phony: watch what they do with their own money
Philosophers can be dangerous — while they understand the difference between Extremistan and Mediocristan in their writings, in real life they invest their pensions in efficient-portfolio-theory-driven funds. The simplest way to spot a phony: if someone manages your risk using Gaussian methods but invests their own money using an entirely different strategy, they are a phony. If an economist discusses "fat tails" of extreme events in their papers but holds a "balanced" portfolio in their retirement account — they are a phony. The alignment between words and actions is the most reliable test of genuine knowledge versus pretense.

04
Knowledge Risk
Can philosophers be dangerous to society? When knowledge does not translate to action
Understanding Extremistan in theory and living in Extremistan are two different things — the latter requires you to bet your survival. Many philosophers and intellectuals elegantly describe unpredictability and fundamental uncertainty in their writings, yet they do not apply these ideas to their own practical decisions. They continue to rely on deterministic models, continue to trust forecasts, and continue to delegate their wealth to Gaussianized investment strategies. This is not mere inconsistency — it is intellectual hypocrisy, and this hypocrisy carries a social cost: it allows pseudoscience to keep masquerading as real science, leaving society continuously exposed to Black Swan strikes.
📚

Knowing in Books

Elegantly discuss randomness, uncertainty, and Black Swans — with sophisticated terminology and polished citations. Acknowledge "fat tails" in academic papers.

🏠

Living the Knowledge

Put 85–90% of assets in extremely safe instruments, use 10–15% for highly speculative Black Swan bets. Never bet your survival on Gaussian models.

05
Survival Wisdom
How to live with the unpredictable: admitting ignorance beats pretending to know
In the face of Type 2 uncertainty, the only honest position is "I don't know." The uncertainty of socioeconomic variables is not like the uncertainty of quantum physics — it does not cancel out on average, cannot be precisely described by probability distributions, and cannot be tamed by mathematical tools. Trying to "manage" this kind of uncertainty with Gaussian methods is like measuring temperature with a tape measure — the tool is the wrong fit for the object. The correct way to coexist with Type 2 uncertainty: (1) admit we don't know the probabilities, (2) prepare for extreme negative events, (3) expose yourself to positive Black Swan opportunities, (4) never bet borrowed money on Type 2 uncertainty.

🔑 Key Takeaway
There are two kinds of uncertainty in the world: the kind that cancels out on average (games, quantum physics) and the kind that never does (markets, weather, history). Most theorists and risk managers commit the Ludic Fallacy — they treat the real world as a game with known rules, using Gaussian mathematics to tame the untamable. A "phony" is someone who acknowledges Extremistan in theory but depends on Mediocristan tools in practice. Spotting them takes only one question: "Where is your pension invested?" The only honest way to live with Type 2 uncertainty: admit ignorance, defend against negative Black Swans, expose yourself to positive ones, and never bet borrowed money on the unpredictable.
