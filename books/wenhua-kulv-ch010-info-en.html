Wenhua Kulv · Ch.10 "Abandoned Well, Detached Gaze"

The millennium-long destruction of a great Asian capital, leaving only the indifferent stare of an abandoned well

The tragedy of the Bohai Kingdom—from "Prosperous State East of the Sea" to a single fire that the Khitan reduced to ash—reveals a civilizational law: when a highly developed civilized city is conquered, the conquerors find they can neither control, integrate, nor transform it. Destruction becomes the only option. Seven exiled Qing-dynasty scholars, pausing to rest while carrying firewood, identified the ruins of the Bohai capital from a few massive stone slabs. The legend of "Li Bai's Drunken Letter" awakened long-dormant knowledge. And that Eight-Treasure Glazed Well, its walls built of basalt rock, virtually undamaged—for over a thousand years, it has watched like an eye that has seen too much and finally grown weary, witnessing cycle after cycle of Bohai → Liao → Jin → Yuan → Qing destruction.

01
Ruin Encounter
Who discovered a vanished capital on the road to exile?
Seven beggar-like exiled Qing-dynasty scholars, pausing while carrying firewood, recognized column bases and palace patterns on several massive flat stones—they had been the intellectual elite of the nation, escaped death in the imperial examination scandal, yet it was on this wasteland that they awakened learning buried for years. These old men had been imperial examiners, exiled to Ningguta and reduced to burning lime kilns and tending horses. Four years of hard labor nearly erased all cultural memory, but the carved patterns on the stone brought everything rushing back. The silence was broken by four words: "Li Bai's Drunken Letter." Someone immediately responded: "Bohai Kingdom!"
02
Prosperous State
How glorious was the Bohai civilization?
In the ninth century, under King Dae Insu, the Bohai "Upper Capital Longquan Prefecture" stood as an equal to Chang'an—one east, one west—as the two great civilizational centers of Asia. The capital consisted of three nested rings: outer city, inner city, and palace city, with the outer wall spanning over thirty li, the entire city laid out on Chang'an's grid plan. Five great bridges crossed the Mudan River to the west and north—today a modern city of tens of thousands manages perfectly well with just one. The Bohai state fully absorbed Tang civilization, used Chinese script universally, and operated on Tang institutional models.

Lurking Danger

Surrounding tribes remained nomadic. The contrast brought admiration and allegiance—but also intense jealousy and hatred.

03
Logic of Destruction
Why are civilized cities always utterly destroyed?
Throughout human history, when highly civilized cities are conquered, their fate is always particularly tragic—because the victors discover that a civilizational order far above their own has formed inside. After conquest, they can neither control it, integrate into it, nor transform it. Destruction is the only option. In 926 CE, the Bohai Kingdom fell to the Khitan. The entering Khitan discovered that not only were the citizens' eyes and expressions cold, but even the city bricks and street stones seemed to resist. After looting valuables, they ordered the capital evacuated, moved the entire population south, then set fire to the entire city. Archaeological excavation revealed bricks, tiles, and stones fused together by fire, massive road stones cracked throughout.
04
Millennium's Cold Eye
What cycles of rise and fall has this well witnessed?
The Eight-Treasure Glazed Well, its walls built of basalt rock, virtually undamaged—for over a thousand years, it has watched like an eye that has seen too much and finally grown weary. The Khitan destroyed Bohai, and exactly two hundred years later, the Khitan themselves were destroyed by the Jurchen Jin. The Jin dynasty lasted only 120 years before being destroyed by the Mongols. So many destructions, each one accompanied by raging fire, each one witnessed by that ancient well boiling dry—yet underground water always remained, slowly regaining its shimmer, but the cold eye remained a cold eye.

05
Detached Transcendence
How do ruins grant the exiled a broader perspective?
Facing a great capital reduced to stone fragments and an abandoned well, the justice or injustice of the imperial examination scandal and the wrongful deaths of loved ones become mere specks in history's folds. The place where those old scholars carried firewood was itself a volcanic crater. Before the crater, the scale of time is even more staggering—dynastic changes are measured in centuries, volcanic movements in tens of millennia. The crater too is an abandoned well, its cold eye making even the Earth shiver. "History is indifferent, in most cases unconcerned with right and wrong, with sentiment. More indifferent than history is nature." Yu Qiuyu believes that if these exiles, amid their shock, also glimpsed the well's cold eye, they would surely have become more detached about their own suffering—this is the ultimate lesson ruins bestow upon humanity.
🔑 Key Takeaway
The Eight-Treasure Glazed Well amid the Bohai ruins is a cold eye that has watched a thousand years of rise and fall. It tells us: civilizations can be created, and they can be destroyed. History is indifferent and unfeeling, but human nobility lies in this—even before ruins, we can transcend our personal joys and sorrows with a broader, more detached perspective.
