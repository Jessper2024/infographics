Surfaces and Essences · Epilogue「Categorization and Analogy-Making Are the Same Thing」

A dialogue between Katie and Anna—two friends fiercely debate what lies at the core of cognition. Katie insists that categorization and analogy-making differ on nine fundamental dimensions; Anna refutes each one. The conversation thoroughly explores the nature of thinking: how we perceive the world, form concepts, understand, and communicate—ultimately revealing that categorization and analogy-making are merely two sides of the same coin.

The book concludes with a dialogue. Katie and Anna debate what lies at the core of cognition. Katie believes categorization holds that role and differs from analogy-making in many ways. Anna argues that analogy-making sits at the core of thinking and does her utmost to show that analogy-making and categorization are the same thing. Katie proposes that categorization and analogy-making differ on nine dimensions: categorization is an eternal necessity while analogy-making is a rare luxury; categorization is routine while analogy-making is innovative; categorization is unconscious while analogy-making is conscious; categorization is automatic while analogy-making is deliberate; categorization favors similarity while analogy-making favors difference… Anna refutes every single point. The discussion touches on how we perceive the world, form concepts, understand, and communicate—ultimately unfolding a thorough exploration of what thinking truly is.

01
Katie's Position
Categorization Is an Eternal Necessity, Analogy-Making a Rare Luxury
Katie fires the opening shot: "In the thinking process, nothing is more universal or fundamental than placing things and situations into known categories. Everything around us is perpetually changing—without classification, the environment would be chaos. Analogies are rare mental events—if categorization is the meat and potatoes of cognition, analogy-making is an exotic spice, nice but optional." Anna counters: "Every time you approach an unfamiliar staircase, doorknob, or shower head, you are making an analogy—linking the new to past experience. If analogy lets us see the new through the lens of the old, it is as ubiquitous as categorization. Analogies are not the gems on the crown of cognition; they are the entire crown, gems and all!"
🥔

Katie: Meat & Potatoes vs. Exotic Spice

Categorization is a survival necessity, happening constantly. Analogy-making, though occasionally inspired, is a cognitive luxury—one could live a full life without ever making an analogy.

👑

Anna: Analogy IS the Crown Itself

Every staircase you climb, every doorknob you turn, every magazine you flip through—each is an analogy linking the new to the old. Analogies aren't crown jewels; they are the entire crown, jewels included.

02
Core Clash
Categorization Is Routine, Analogy-Making Is Innovative? Counterexamples Abound in Both Directions
Katie concedes that analogies are everywhere, but insists the difference lies in creativity: "Analogies can be intensely creative, while categorization has never produced anything new—it merely exploits previously acquired knowledge." Anna laughs: "You underestimate categorization! When I say 'mathematics is a game,' 'mathematics is a language,' 'mathematics is an art'—aren't these creative categorizations? Poincaré described mathematics as 'the art of giving the same name to different things'—the art of discovering unusual, subtle new categories. Conversely, the routine analogies you make with every staircase and doorknob are as dull as dishwater. So: there are plenty of boring analogies, and plenty of creative categorizations."

03
Consciousness & Control
Unconscious vs. Conscious? Automatic vs. Deliberate? The Boundaries Are Equally Blurred
Katie proposes two more distinctions: categorization is unconscious (opaque), while analogy-making is conscious (you can mentally visualize the solar system and the atom); categorization is automatic and unstoppable (you can't prevent yourself from seeing a table), while analogy-making requires deliberation. Anna refutes again: Chapters 5 and 7 are filled with cases where people don't realize they're making analogies—naive analogies like "division is just equal sharing" lurk deep in nearly everyone's mind, leading them astray. Conversely, astronomers consciously debated whether Pluto belongs to the planet category; courts consciously adjudicate whether a defendant is "guilty" or "innocent"—these are conscious categorizations.

04
Similarity & Difference
Categorization Favors Similarity, Analogy-Making Favors Difference? The Tale of Two Thumbs
Katie proposes her final distinction: "The closer something is to a category's center, the easier it is to classify—similarity is the key to categorization. But the quality of an analogy depends precisely on how different the compared situations are. Analogy's power comes from penetrating surface differences to reveal deep commonalities." Anna responds with a brilliant example: "Is your right thumb similar to your left thumb?" Katie: "Of course." Anna: "Are they identical?" Katie: "Not entirely—one is right, one left, but extremely similar." Anna: "So when you categorize your right thumb as a 'thumb,' you simultaneously perceive similarity (both are thumbs) and difference (left vs. right). Categorization and analogy-making are indistinguishable on this point—both require processing similarity and difference simultaneously."
✋

Similarity & Difference in Categorization

When categorizing your right thumb as a "thumb," you see both its similarity to the left thumb and the left-right difference. Categorization never requires perfect identity.

🔗

Similarity & Difference in Analogy-Making

The solar system–atom analogy depends on both their deep structural similarity and their enormous surface differences—which is precisely what gives the analogy its power.

05
Book's Closing
Categorization and Analogy-Making—Two Sides of the Same Coin
Anna's final statement strikes home: "Katie, for every distinction you've proposed, I've found counterexamples. But the point isn't who wins or loses—it's what we understand. Plato in The Sophist said we should cut 'along the joints' like a good butcher—but thought is not beef, and there is no clean joint between categorization and analogy-making. They truly are the same thing. Every act of categorization is an act of analogy-making—you compare a new entity to members of a known category, judging whether it's 'similar enough' to belong. That itself is an analogical judgment. Conversely, every act of analogy-making creates or activates a category—when you compare the atom to the solar system, you create a new category that accommodates both. This is the book's ultimate answer: categorization and analogy-making are fundamentally the same thing, and both lie at the core of human cognition."

🔑 Core Takeaway
After eight chapters and this debate, a unified picture emerges: categorization IS analogy-making, and analogy-making IS categorization. Whenever we place a new entity into a known category—whether recognizing a chair, understanding a metaphor, or judging whether someone is trustworthy—we are making an analogy, comparing the new entity to known members of the category and judging whether they are "similar enough." Conversely, whenever we discover a deep connection between two seemingly different things—whether atom and solar system, light and gas, or life and a journey—we are creating a new category that accommodates both. Two seemingly disparate cognitive activities are merely two manifestations of the same fundamental mental mechanism. All the book's arguments converge here: analogy is the core of human cognition—the source of thought, the fire of thinking.
