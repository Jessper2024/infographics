弹性生长 · Chapter 27
"Making the Grassroots Richer Has Never Been More Important"

Lessons from Germany and Japan's divergent fates after Plaza Accord—wealth distribution determines economic resilience

📖 Chapter Overview

Japan and Germany both suffered export collapses after the Plaza Accord, but their outcomes diverged dramatically: Japan cut rates and flooded money → the rich borrowed to speculate in stocks and real estate → asset bubble burst → three lost decades. Germany, by contrast, gained cheap labor and a massive market through reunification with East Germany, lowering manufacturing costs and absorbing the shock. The core lesson: "big money" and "small money" spend differently—channeling funds to grassroots consumption digests excess capacity, while funneling them into asset speculation creates bubbles. China's common prosperity push rests on this logic: only when the grassroots have purchasing power does the economy gain true resilience.

🔍 In-Depth Analysis
The Plaza Accord Impact
By the 1980s, Japan's GDP was 15% of the world total; per capita income was 145% of America's. Japanese and German products far outclassed American ones, and the US saw this as exploitation. From 1971 onward, sustained currency pressure culminated in the 1985 Plaza Accord and 1987 Louvre Accord—the core goal: force yen and mark appreciation to reduce competitiveness. In 1986, Japan's export growth crashed from 3.9% to -16.8%; Germany's GDP growth was halved.
Japan's Panicked Response
Japan's government tried to make citizens consume the surplus by cutting rates. But big money and small money spend completely differently: ordinary people with an extra few thousand yuan improve their lives and buy appliances; the wealthy with tens of millions in loans can only invest. With excess capacity everywhere, all the money flooded into real estate and stocks. The middle class, seeing prices rise, mortgaged homes to borrow more—lifting themselves by their own bootstraps. The government's goal failed: companies all went to speculate instead.
Triple Nogami and the Bubble Burst
In late 1989, "Mad Primitive" Yasushi Mieno became BOJ governor—a Manchuria-born economic maverick who detested market speculation. He raised rates five times; by August 1990, rates surged from 2.5% to 6%, while tightening money supply. Stock and real estate markets lost their lifeblood, and nobody could take the other side. 1990-1992: stocks lost over half, real estate dropped 46%. Housing continued declining for 30 years—aging demographics meant more people dying and vacating homes than newlyweds buying.
Germany's Windfall
Germany, scarred by hyperinflation memory, was cautious about massive rate cuts. Their luck: East and West Germany reunified. Germany paid Russia a fortune in marks (plus permanently ceded Kaliningrad—once East Prussia's capital, Kant's hometown) for Soviet acquiescence. Reunification brought a massive economic market and labor pool—floods of cheap, well-educated workers lowered German manufacturing costs dramatically, absorbing the shock Japan couldn't handle.
The Real Meaning of Grassroots Wealth
Japan's core lesson: rate cuts → banks favor the rich → the rich's "big money" can only buy assets → bubble → bust, while ordinary people's "small money" buys products → digests capacity → real economy circulates. Germany gained cheap labor to cut production costs, but the real solution for demand is broad-based grassroots purchasing power. China's common prosperity drive rests on this deep logic: only when ordinary people get richer does the economy gain true anti-risk resilience and sustained momentum.
💡 Key Takeaway

Japan's three lost decades teach one lesson: economic stimulus that only enriches the wealthy—whose "big money" flows into asset speculation, not consumption—fails to digest excess capacity. Germany got a labor windfall through reunification, but the real answer is making ordinary people richer. Their "small money" gets spent on improving daily life, which digests capacity and sustains the economic cycle. Common prosperity isn't a slogan—it's the foundational logic of economic resilience.
