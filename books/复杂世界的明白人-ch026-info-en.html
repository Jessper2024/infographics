Understanding Our Complex World · Ch.26 "Why EVs Are Our Future"

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer China has the world's largest car fleet, yet its oil import dependency has breached the 70% red line. Traditional ICE engines and transmissions are locked behind a century of Western patents—a race China could never win. How can China escape both energy dependence and its status as a perpetual "hired hand" in the auto industry? Answer: The EV track reset—same starting line, inherently suited for software and autonomous driving, with market scale fueling technological breakthroughs.

The author believes this paradigm shift will complete within a decade—miss it, and you'll wait another century.

01
Energy Security
Oil Stranglehold: Import Dependency Over 70%—Alternative Energy Is Urgent
China has the world's largest car fleet, and oil dependency has surged past 70%, posing a serious threat to national energy security. Oil can only be shipped from a handful of countries—Saudi Arabia, Russia—while electricity sources are far more diverse: solar, hydro, wind, and nuclear. China's vast western highlands (Gansu, Inner Mongolia, Xinjiang) are ideal for solar farms; Golmud, Qinghai already has a solar thermal tower. Full vehicle electrification would dramatically reduce oil dependence. Moreover, EV batteries and future controlled nuclear fusion both require large quantities of lithium and rare earths—resources China happens to have in abundance. The global push for rare-earth mines and African iron ore is all strategic preparation for the post-oil era.

02
Tech Blockade
ICE Patent Wall: Forever the "Hired Hand," Forever Chasing
In the ICE domain, China has been like a hired hand—working tirelessly on production while the lion's share of profits goes to the West. When Chinese engine development began, quality was low and prices high; the market wouldn't buy, so companies couldn't recoup R&D costs or improve. Worse still, the West had already sealed off virtually every viable path with a century of patents. The transmission is so complex it's almost unsolvable. China took a detour down "technology for market access"—and lost both: the market was handed over while breeding a crop of parasites waiting for technology, with no real progress. On the EV track, everyone starts from the same line, the patent landscape is blank, and the transmission is discarded outright—a once-in-a-century opportunity.

03
Technical Edge
Simplified Architecture + Precision Control: EVs Are a "Software-Defined" Species
EVs replace the ICE engine and transmission with electronically controlled motors, making the overall architecture far simpler and delivering ten times the precision and responsiveness of ICE vehicles. If an ICE car's control precision is like a regular ruler, an EV's is a vernier caliper. The car of the future will be like a smartphone—packed with countless code modules—and all of that demands massive electricity, which EVs are naturally suited to provide in a modular fashion. If traditional ICE cars are Nokia's Symbian OS, EVs are Apple's iOS: they look vaguely similar but are fundamentally a different species. This is precisely why smartphone giants want to build EVs—genetically, they share far more DNA with EVs than traditional automakers ever will.

04
Learning Curve
Market Compounding: Sell More → Costs Fall → Geniuses Flock to Problems
Industrial learning curves dictate that the more you produce, the cheaper it gets—battery costs plunged nearly 90% in one decade and will continue falling in the next. China has an unmatched advantage: an enormous market. As long as products can sell, companies can keep iterating. Problems that seem unsolvable today (range, unit cost) are essentially just "not producing enough yet." Scale up → issue a bounty → geniuses swarm → the problem is conquered. In a free market, demand comes first—as long as someone is willing to pay, any obstacle will be overcome. The author cites a colleague who achieved financial freedom by going all-in on EV stocks: if you believe EVs are the future, don't worry about the "messy problems"—every one of them will be conquered.

05
Industry Shakeout
China's Supply Chain Opportunity of the Century: BYD and Niche Competition
As long as China controls the supply chain, challengers and competitors will inevitably emerge within China, along with the supporting ecosystem. BYD is the standout story of this wave: its batteries rank among the world's top three, its electronic controls are fully independent of overseas imports, and it has been developing its own chips since 2002. Consumer markets won't see a single dominant player like social media does—instead, they'll settle into "one superpower plus multiple strong contenders," much like the smartphone market (Apple + various Android brands). The EV market's growth will also catalyze adjacent ecosystems like LiDAR (Huawei) and solar (LONGi). The author issues a warning: companies chasing quick money without deep commitment to technology will be swiftly ejected, not even getting to earn "slow money."

🔑 Key Takeaway
EVs are not a choice—they are an inevitability. Three converging forces—oil security, patent barriers, and software-defined architecture—combine with China's market scale and learning curve to power a virtuous flywheel. In the ICE era, Chinese companies were forever the "hired hands"; in the EV era, everyone starts from the same starting line. Miss this once-in-a-century paradigm shift and you'll wait another hundred years. The key isn't whether today's technology is perfect—it's that once the market expands, geniuses will convert every "unsolved problem" into "solved." This is the iron law of the tech industry: back the people, not the problems.
