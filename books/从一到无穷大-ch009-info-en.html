Number theory: the purest crown of mathematics, from the infinite mystery of primes to the geometric wonder of imaginary numbers

Mathematics is hailed as the queen of sciences, and number theory is her purest crown jewel. This chapter explores two seemingly opposite directions: "natural numbers" — primes are infinite, yet no formula can generate them all; and "artificial numbers" — imaginary numbers, once dismissed as non-existent, found elegant geometric interpretation. Euclid's proof of prime infinity by contradiction, Goldbach's conjecture, Fermat's Last Theorem, and the treasure-hunt problem solved by imaginary numbers together weave a splendid tapestry of number theory.

Mathematical Thought
The Purest Mathematics: Number Theory
Mathematicians wish to keep mathematics pure. Yet every branch of pure mathematics eventually finds use in explaining the physical universe — including abstract group theory, non-commutative algebra, and non-Euclidean geometry. Number theory, however, is considered the purest of all, with no practical application other than mental training. Ironically, most number theory theorems were first "discovered empirically," much like physical laws — mathematicians observed patterns in numbers before proving them rigorously.

02
Classic Proof
Euclid's Reductio ad Absurdum: The Infinite Prime Proof
A prime is a number that cannot be expressed as a product of two or more smaller numbers. Euclid first proved that the number of primes is infinite. His method, called "proof by contradiction" (reductio ad absurdum), is a favorite among mathematicians. Assume primes are finite, with N as the largest. Compute the product of all known primes plus 1: (1×2×3×5×7×11×13×…×N)+1. This number is either prime itself or divisible by a prime larger than N — both contradicting the assumption that N is the largest prime.
Euclid's Proof by Contradiction:
Assume largest prime = N → Compute (1×2×3×5×7×11×…×N) + 1
→ Not divisible by any prime ≤ N → Either prime or divisible by a larger prime
→ Contradiction → ∴ Primes are infinite ✓
03
Distribution Law
Prime Distribution: Rarer but Never Ending
As numbers grow, the proportion of primes decreases but never reaches zero. The law of prime distribution is one of the greatest discoveries in all of mathematics: the proportion of primes from 1 to n is approximately 1/ln(n), and the larger n gets, the closer the approximation. This law was first proposed empirically and only rigorously proven in the late 19th century by Hadamard and de la Vallée-Poussin.

04
Unsolved Mysteries
Two Great Puzzles: Goldbach & Fermat
Goldbach's Conjecture (1742): Every even number can be written as the sum of two primes. 12=7+5, 24=17+7, 32=29+3 — easy to check, but neither proven nor disproven. Schnirelmann (1931) proved "every even number = sum of at most 300,000 primes," later reduced to "4 primes" by Vinogradoff. But the final step from 4 to 2 remains elusive. Fermat's Last Theorem: xⁿ+yⁿ=zⁿ has no integer solutions for n>2. Fermat claimed a "marvelous proof," but for three centuries no one could reproduce it.

Imaginary Wonder
The Mysterious √-1: From "Non-Existent" to Pirate Treasure Maps
Cardano in the 16th century first dared to write the square root of negative numbers into a formula, knowing it was "meaningless." Two centuries later, Wessel and Argand gave a simple geometric interpretation: the complex number 3+4i corresponds to a point (3 horizontal, 4 vertical), and multiplying by i rotates it 90° counterclockwise. Application: a pirate treasure problem — the gallows had disappeared, but knowing only the oak and pine tree positions, complex numbers locate the treasure exactly at +i, with the unknown gallows position magically canceling out.

🔑 Key Insight
The world of natural numbers is full of surprises: primes are infinite yet no generating formula exists; their distribution follows a law as elegant as a cosmic code. Artificial numbers (imaginary numbers) evolved from "non-existent" to the foundation of four-dimensional geometry, proving that the most abstract mathematical concepts often give rise to the most powerful tools for understanding the universe.
