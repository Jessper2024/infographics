"A War of Life and Death"

April 1400: The Battle of Baigou River — Li Jinglong's 600,000-strong Southern Army presses on Beiping; Zhu Di turns the tide with elite cavalry, brilliant tactics, and two "divine wind" miracles.

Situation In April 1400, the Southern Army launched its largest offensive of the campaign — 600,000 troops under Li Jinglong versus Zhu Di's 100,000-strong Northern Army at Baigou River.
Conflict Zhu Di faced his greatest crisis: outnumbered 6-to-1, encircled on the riverbank, his best general Zhang Yu killed — while Li Jinglong's sheer numbers created overwhelming pressure.
Question How could a commander with fewer troops and inferior resources reverse a seemingly lost battle and seize battlefield initiative?
Answer Central-break cavalry charge + exploiting opponent's errors + Zhu Yunwen's "no-harm" edict as a bulletproof vest + two sandstorm miracles — a masterclass in asymmetric warfare.

01
Beiping Defense: Zhu Gaochi's "Ice City" Miracle
Li Jinglong besieged Beiping with 500,000 troops; the defender was Zhu Di's eldest son Zhu Gaochi — a lame, disabled man whom many dismissed as useless. Southern forces used heavy artillery, scaled walls in human waves, and briefly broke through Shuncheng Gate. City women fought with bricks and tiles to boost morale. Zhu Gaochi ordered soldiers to pour water on walls overnight — by dawn Beiping had become an impregnable "ice city." This defense earned him the political capital that later secured his imperial throne.

Zhu Gaochi's Strategy

Disabled yet resolute — the "Ice City" tactic turned Beiping into an unconquerable fortress and built his political capital.

Li Jinglong's Failure

02
Battle of Zhengcunwa: Mongol Cavalry & Central Break
Zhu Di returned with the elite Duoyan Three Wei Mongol cavalry, dramatically raising his army's combat power — and faced Li Jinglong at Zhengcunwa, 20 km from Beijing. The Mongol cavalry executed a central-breakthrough charge, smashing seven Southern camps. Advisor Ma Sanbao identified Li Jinglong's center as the weak point; Zhu Di launched a flanking pincer attack and Li Jinglong fled overnight. Key outcome: Ma Sanbao was rewarded with imperial surname "Zheng" — becoming the legendary Zheng He.

03
Baigou River: Zhu Di's Darkest Hour & Miraculous Reversal
April 1400 — Baigou River was Zhu Di's most dangerous moment of the entire campaign. Pioneer commander Ping An bypassed the Northern vanguard and struck the rear; Qu Nieng's coordinated assault nearly annihilated Zhu Di. Zhu Di, encircled on the riverbank, fought with bow, sword, and three successive horses — arrows exhausted, sword broken, horses killed. As Qiu Fu's frontal attack failed, Qu Nieng pressed the final assault — then a divine wind broke the Southern flagpole. Southern troops scattered in superstitious panic. Zhu Di counterattacked; Li Jinglong abandoned his army and fled south. Xu Huizu's rear-guard action saved the Southern Army from total destruction.

04
The "No-Harm" Edict: Zhu Di's Institutional Bulletproof Vest
Throughout the Jingnan Campaign, Zhu Yunwen issued a standing order: do not harm Zhu Di's person. This edict gave Zhu Di a systematic battlefield advantage — Southern soldiers dared not fire arrows or strike him directly. At Baigou River, Zhu Di survived multiple close encounters because no soldier would bear responsibility for killing the emperor's uncle. At Jiahe,盛庸's firepower could not target Zhu Di himself. The edict's deepest effect was psychological: soldiers calculated that if Zhu Di and Zhu Yunwen ever reconciled, the man who killed Zhu Di would be purged — this "reconciliation expectation" made Zhu Di the safest person on the battlefield.

05
Li Jinglong: The Southern Army's Unwitting Supply Captain
Son of famed general Li Wenzhong, Li Jinglong achieved infamy through staggering incompetence — becoming, in effect, Zhu Di's greatest ally within the Southern Army. Four catastrophic mistakes defined his command: stopping Qu Nieng from breaching Beiping's gate out of jealousy; abandoning his entire army overnight to flee south; deserting Dezhou and leaving over 1 million shi of grain for Zhu Di's forces; and finally opening Jinchuan Gate alongside Gu Wang Zhu Xi to let the Northern Army into Nanjing. Zhu Di's assessment: "A soft egg with a hard shell."
🤦

Four Fatal Blunders

Stopped Qu Nieng; abandoned army; left Dezhou's grain; opened Jinchuan Gate. Each gift to Zhu Di cost the South dearly.

🎯

Zhu Di's Read

"Soft egg inside a hard shell" — Zhu Di consistently exploited Li's predictable weaknesses to turn defeat into victory.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Battle of Baigou River was the decisive turning point of the Jingnan Campaign. Zhu Di defeated a 600,000-strong Southern Army with just over 100,000 troops through four critical advantages: cavalry central-break tactics, two sandstorm "miracles," Li Jinglong's catastrophic incompetence repeatedly feeding strategic resources to the North, and Zhu Yunwen's no-harm edict granting Zhu Di de facto battlefield invincibility. After this battle, Zhu Di controlled the initiative — and Zhu Yunwen's strongest military force was strategically destroyed.
