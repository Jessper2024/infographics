Surfaces and Essences · References

An academic landscape spanning philosophy, cognitive science, linguistics, the history of mathematics, and physics—from Plato to Wittgenstein, from Poincaré to Einstein, from Lakoff to Gentner: these references are not decoration but the intellectual bedrock supporting every argument in the book.

This section collects all scholarly works cited throughout the book, organized by chapter and accompanied by brief authorial commentary. The General section lists foundational works relevant to all chapters: Fauconnier and Turner's The Way We Think places conceptual mapping at the center of cognition; Holyoak and Thagard's Mental Leaps, along with the anthology edited by Gentner et al., have become standard references in analogy research; Murphy's The Big Book of Concepts is the authoritative resource on categorization; Lakoff and Johnson's Metaphors We Live By revolutionized understanding of the relationship between metaphor and thought. Subsequent chapter references span concept development, categorization, analogical reasoning, naive physics, naive mathematics, and analogy in scientific discovery—forming one of the most complete bibliographies on "analogy and thought" in contemporary cognitive science.

General
Foundational Works on Analogy and Categorization in Cognitive Science
The General section's references are essential reading for understanding the book's theoretical framework. Fauconnier and Turner's The Way We Think: Conceptual Blending and the Mind's Hidden Complexities (2002) places conceptual mapping at the center of cognition—strikingly similar to the book's core thesis, but using a different terminological system ("conceptual blending" rather than "analogy"). Holyoak and Thagard's Mental Leaps (1995) systematically expounds analogy's role in human reasoning from a multidisciplinary perspective and has become a standard reference in the field. Gentner, Holyoak, and Kokinov's edited volume The Analogical Mind: Perspectives from Cognitive Science (2001) collects cutting-edge research. Hofstadter and the Fluid Analogies Research Group's Fluid Concepts and Creative Analogies (1995) is the authors' prior research, laying the methodological and theoretical foundations for this book. Murphy's The Big Book of Concepts (2002) is the authoritative overview of categorization. Lakoff and Johnson's Metaphors We Live By (1980) revolutionarily demonstrated that metaphor is not rhetorical decoration but a fundamental mode of thought.

02
Intro + Chapters 1–3
The Foundations of Analogy: Concepts, Words, and Hidden Mechanisms of Thought
The Introduction and first chapters' references emphasize the philosophical foundations and cognitive science evidence for analogy as a mode of thought. Alberic, Bachelard, Bartha, and Bouveresse examine analogy's reliability from philosophical perspectives. Anderson's paper highlights the adaptive value of categorization—why humans need to reduce an ever-changing world to stable categories. The papers by Tversky, Medin, Goldstone, and Gentner explore the psychological nature of similarity—what actually happens in the brain when people judge two things as "similar"? Chapters 1–2's references are unprecedentedly broad: developmental psychology studies by Carey, Gelman, Keil, and Pinker reveal how children build their first concepts through analogy; experiments by Barsalou, Glucksberg, and Medin explore the psychological mechanisms of adult categorization; the work of Boroditsky, Lakoff, and Turner liberated metaphor research from rhetoric and repositioned it as a central topic in cognitive science.
🧒

Developmental Psychology Perspective

Carey, Gelman, Keil, Pinker, and others reveal: children's concept acquisition is essentially a series of analogies—every time a new object is placed into the category of "dog" or "chair," an analogical leap is made.

🧠

Adult Categorization Mechanisms

Barsalou's ideal-point theory, Medin's context-dependence theory, Goldstone's interactive activation model of similarity—these experimental studies provide precise evidence that "categorization = analogy-making."

03
Chapters 4–6
Abstraction, Manipulation by Analogy, and Creative Thinking—Experiments and Computational Models
The middle chapters' references focus on the psychological mechanisms of abstract thinking, the unconscious manipulation of thought by analogy, and computational modeling of analogy. Chapter 4 covers the cognitive science foundations of the abstraction ladder—how people flexibly slide between different levels of abstraction. Chapter 5's references are particularly fascinating: Maslow's "If all you have is a hammer, everything looks like a nail"—this famous "law of the instrument" is essentially a psychological insight about how naive analogies constrain thinking. Khong's political science analysis of the "Munich analogy" and "domino theory" in the Vietnam War is a classic case study of analogy at work in real historical decision-making. Chapter 6 introduces the computational modeling perspective: Holyoak and Thagard's Mental Leaps is not only a theoretical work on analogy but also contains early computational models; Gentner, Rattermann, and Forbus's research reveals the developmental trajectory of children's analogical ability.
🔨

The Law of the Hammer

Maslow (1966): "If all you have is a hammer, everything looks like a nail."—When your category repertoire is too impoverished, every new entity is forced into one of your few available categories.

🎮

Computational Models

Hofstadter & FARG's Copycat, Metacat, and other programs are the author team's computational research—attempts to simulate human analogy-making in computers. These models provide computational evidence that "analogy is the core of thinking."

04
Chapters 7–8
Naive Analogies and Earth-Shaking Analogies—From Education to Scientific Revolution
The final chapters' references form the collection with the greatest historical depth in the book. Chapter 7 traces the historical evolution of naive analogies in mathematics education: Recorde (1557)—the inventor of the equals sign—described it as "two parallel lines, because no two things can be more equal," which is itself a naive analogy. Bezout's (1833) mathematics textbook defined division as "dividing one number by another is finding how many times the latter is contained in the former"—this naive definition influenced nearly two centuries of mathematics education. Chapter 8 traces the greatest analogical moments in the history of science: Einstein's 1905 light-quantum paper—analogizing the distribution of radiant energy with the energy distribution of gas molecules, deriving the revolutionary conclusion that light is composed of particles. Hoffmann's authoritative biography records Einstein's thinking process in detail—"with an intuition of the unity of the universe." Quotations from Poincaré and Ulam provide first-person testimony from mathematicians about analogy's role in professional mathematical thinking.
1
The historical trajectory of naive analogies: Recorde (1557) → Bezout (1833) → today's classrooms—the "division = sharing" analogy has traversed 450 years and still echoes in classrooms worldwide.
2
The analogical moment of scientific revolution: Einstein (1905) discovered light quanta through the radiation↔gas formal analogy—"a principle of such generality."
3
First-person testimony from mathematicians: Poincaré (1911) "the guide is chiefly analogy"; Ulam (1976) "good mathematicians see analogies between theorems; great ones see analogies between analogies."
4
The unity of naive and advanced: the same cognitive mechanism generates both the naive biases that trap the public and the scientific discoveries that change the world.
05
Epilogue
Categorization = Analogy-Making—The Documentary Convergence on the Book's Theme
The Epilogue's references elegantly converge on the book's central theme. Spalding and Murphy's (1996) statement that "categories allow people to treat new things as familiar" and Gick and Holyoak's (1983) statement that "analogy allows us to see new things as familiar" form a precise parallel structure. Polya (1957) asserts that "analogy pervades our thinking"; Oppenheimer (1956) declares that "faced with novelty, we inevitably compare it with the familiar"—a mathematician and a physicist, each in their own language, articulating the same core insight. Plato's ancient metaphor in The Sophist—to cut "along the joints" like a good butcher—is given new meaning by the Epilogue: thought's natural "joints" do not lie between categorization and analogy-making.
🔗

Parallel Formulations

Spalding & Murphy → "Categories let people see the new as familiar." Gick & Holyoak → "Analogy lets us see the new as familiar." Different terms, identical functional description—categorization and analogy-making have been independently described in the literature as the same thing.

🔪

Plato's Butcher

Plato (1950): Cut "along the joints" like a good butcher. The Epilogue reveals: there is no natural "joint" between categorization and analogy-making—they are the same process.

🔑 Core Takeaway
These 500+ references are not decorative academic furnishings—they are the intellectual bricks that construct the book's arguments. Together they trace an academic trajectory spanning 2,500 years: from Plato's philosophical reflections on similarity, through Wittgenstein's "family resemblance" redrawing of category boundaries, to Einstein's discovery of light quanta through analogy—all ultimately validated by precise experiments in 21st-century cognitive science laboratories. Reading this bibliography is like reviewing a "mobile army of metaphors"—each entry is a scholar testifying to the proposition that "analogy is the core of cognition." Together they demonstrate: this is not the fanciful notion of two authors, but the accumulated, collective insight of humanity's finest minds, built up over centuries.
