Five Cities of Du Fu · Ch.17 "The Grand Historian’s Regret"

Qufu · Mount Tai · Ji’nan · Anyang · Mancheng

From Jiaxiang to Qufu—the Confucius Temple has become a tourist trap, dilapidated and neglected; the Yan Hui Temple, by contrast, is serenely quiet, embodying the spirit of studying in a humble lane without losing joy. Climbing Mount Tai fulfills the regret of Sima Qian’s father, who wept on his deathbed: ‘Is this fate? Is this fate?’ Lai eats scarlet fish atop Mount Tai in the rain, silently thanking the downpour. In Ji’nan—whose springs have dried and whose Daming Lake swarms with tourists—he stumbles upon Keemun black tea near Shandong University! For three years he searched all China for black tea; now, he has found it. At Anyang, he visits the Yinxu oracle bone site—the Huan River thin and small, everything exactly as on some early morning three thousand years ago. Finally, climbing Lingshan in Mancheng, he enters the Western Han tomb of Prince Liu Sheng, solving the mystery of cutting into the mountain to build the tomb—the tomb passage extends like a railway tunnel into the peak, confirming Tang Taizong’s Zhao Mausoleum followed the same design.

01Qufu: Confucius Temple’s Decline & Yan Hui’s Tranquility
Qufu has become a tourist hotspot—the Confucius Temple dilapidated and neglected everywhere, souvenir shops selling Qufu-branded cakes. Renting a bicycle to Yan Hui Temple—few visitors there, far quieter and more serene than the Confucius Temple, truly embodying the spirit of Yan Hui studying in a humble lane without losing his joy. At the Confucius Forest, searching for the grave of Kong Shangren, author of The Peach Blossom Fan—the grave was surrounded by weeds, untended, with a forlorn feeling.
02Mount Tai: Fulfilling the Grand Historian’s Regret
Sima Qian’s father, unable to climb Mount Tai, fell ill with indignation and died, grasping his son’s hand and weeping: ‘Is this fate? Is this fate?’ Lai climbs the Eighteen Bends, panting to South Heavenly Gate. Rain falls at the summit—he reads Tang Emperor Xuanzong’s personally inscribed Mount Tai Memorial stele in the rain: the characters gilded, gleaming in the rain. Stranded atop, he finds scarlet fish, Mount Tai’s famous delicacy—enjoying this sublime dish alone, I felt truly at the summit of the human world. Descending, massaging aching legs: I knew I would never, in this life, have the Grand Historian’s regret.
03Ji’nan: A Keemun Black Tea Miracle by Daming Lake
Lao Can’s Ji’nan has changed—Baotu Spring dried up, Daming Lake swarming. Cycling toward Shandong University—where renowned historian Professor Wang Zhongluo worked. On the way, a tea shop window displays Keemun Black Tea. For three years searching all China, never finding it—now, in Ji’nan: The saleswoman opened an old-fashioned large wooden chest behind the counter. 500 grams for ¥10. Not fresh, but an unexpected delight. The university campus was shabby and simple—Wang Zhongluo wrote his masterpieces here.
04Anyang Yinxu: An Early Morning Three Thousand Years Ago
Anyang, the late Shang capital. Cycling to Xiaotun village, the oracle bone discovery site covered by a large earthen mound. The new Yinxu Museum was deserted; the palace foundation area was planted with vegetables, blooming with nameless little flowers—An uninformed person might mistake it for someone’s vegetable garden. The Huan River, thin and small, glittered in sunlight—everything exactly as on some early morning three thousand years ago. One could imagine a Shang palace maid carrying a pottery vessel to the river to fetch water.
05Mancheng Han Tomb: Solving the Mountain-Cut Tomb Mystery
Making a special trip to Mancheng to see the Western Han tomb of Prince Liu Sheng of Zhongshan. Finally understanding cutting into the mountain to build the tomb—the tomb chamber sits within the peak of Lingshan, the passage extending levelly into the mountain’s belly like a railway tunnel. Confirmed: Tang Taizong’s Zhao Mausoleum followed the same design, its passage seventy-five zhang long (about 230 meters), roughly four times Liu Sheng’s passage—no wonder it took thirteen years to build. Workers were erecting a cable car—After resting here quietly for over two thousand years, the ghosts of Liu Sheng and his wife could not, in the end, escape the ravages of modern tourism.
💡 Key Insight
From fulfilling the Grand Historian’s Mount Tai regret to solving the thousand-year mystery of the mountain-cut tomb—this journey spans the entire arc of Chinese civilization from Shang Dynasty to Western Han to High Tang. With a Tang historian’s eye, Lai Ruihe eats scarlet fish in Mount Tai’s rain, encounters Keemun black tea by serendipity in Ji’nan, imagines a palace maid fetching water three millennia ago in Anyang, and decodes imperial tomb engineering with a railway tunnel analogy in Mancheng. History is not text in books—it is carvings you can touch, tomb passages you can measure, and mountaintop delicacies you can savor in the rain.
