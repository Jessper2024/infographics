High-Score Self-Study · Ch.2 "Epilogue: Draw 20 More Boxes"

The moment a first-grader discovered that progress is joy — when 10 dictation boxes became 200, the world changed completely

In first grade, the author did 10-slot dictation homework every day. One day he noticed extra space on the paper, drew 10 more boxes, and wrote 20 words — the teacher gave him two "Well Done" stamps. From that moment, the boxes multiplied: 20 became 40, then 80, and eventually 200 boxes on a single sheet of A3 paper — completing in one day what classmates took a month to finish. Soon after, his dictation exams were nearly error-free. This epilogue uses a pure childhood story to reveal the book's deepest truth: progress is fun — and that moment of awakening may be the instant that changes your life forever.

01
Critical Instant
The Second 10 Boxes Became 20 — Life's Gear Began to Turn
This was the starting point of everything. That day, the author finished drawing 10 boxes on A3 paper when he noticed plenty of space left — so he drew 10 more boxes and wrote 20 words. The next day, the teacher gave him two stamps. He was stunned, discovering "I can actually get two stamps!" This seemingly tiny decision triggered a chain reaction: 20 became 40, 40 became 80, and eventually 200 boxes crammed onto one sheet. In a single day he completed work that took his friends an entire month.

02
Positive Feedback
Effort → Reward → Confidence: How Successful Experiences Form a Virtuous Cycle
The author didn't originally love dictation — he'd get 2 or 3 wrong out of 10. But after doing several times more dictation than others, before long he almost never made mistakes. Everything happened in an instant: the moment he drew 10 extra boxes, the moment he received two stamps — "I put in effort, and I received the corresponding reward. These were all successful experiences." Effort leads to progress, and progress is fun — this belief was rooted in childhood experiences of success.

03
Counterfactual
What If the Teacher Had Given Only One Stamp? — The Other Side of the Decisive Moment
The author raises a profound counterfactual: if the teacher had given only one stamp, or asked "why are you doing this unnecessary extra work?", then he absolutely would not have voluntarily completed 40, 80, then 200 items a day — might never have found progress enjoyable — and might never have written this book. This "what if" reveals a crucial principle: a tiny positive feedback signal from the environment (two stamps instead of one) can become the decisive turning point in a person's lifelong growth trajectory.

04
Core Insight
Progress Is Fun: The True Theme of This Book
The author confesses: "In this book, I introduced many methods for studying well. What we are really discussing is how to make yourself progress." The essence of high-score self-study is not technique — it is the art of creating the "progress experience." That first-grade moment when 10 boxes became 20 appeared to be about doing more dictation, but in essence it was about firsthand experience of the "effort → progress → joy" feedback loop. The ultimate purpose of every method in this book is to help learners encounter, at some stage of life, their own "awakening moment."

🔑 Key Takeaway
Every method in this book points to one core insight: progress is fun. That life-changing moment could be drawing 10 extra boxes on a sheet of paper, an unexpected dose of positive feedback, or this very moment of reading this book. When the "effort → progress → joy" feedback loop is activated, the learner no longer needs external pushing — their intrinsic drive will carry them the whole way. This is the true message of High-Score Self-Study Method: not the techniques themselves, but helping you find your own moment of awakening.
