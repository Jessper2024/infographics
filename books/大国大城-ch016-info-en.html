Great Country, Big City · Ch.8「The Predicament of Urban Social Division」

Hukou System · Identity · Consumption · Income Inequality · Social Integration The hukou system makes rural migrants leave their youth and health in the city and take only themselves back home. A "new dual society" has formed within the city, with consequences reaching far beyond social injustice — it generates efficiency losses and threatens social harmony.

This is Book Chapter 8, revealing the triple consequences of social division created by the hukou system. First — migrant health depletion: migrants are healthier than urban residents upon arrival, but working in dirty, demanding, and dangerous conditions with minimal labor protection (contract signing rate: 41.3%) and social security, unhealthy migrants return to their hometowns in middle age — cities keep the health, villages bear the burden. Second — consumption loss: hukou constraints reduce migrant consumption by ~30% compared to urban residents, equivalent to 1.8% of GDP annually. Third — identity and income gaps: hukou-related income inequality harms not only migrants but also reduces the happiness of local residents, creating a "can't change lanes" tunnel-effect resentment. The chapter concludes with research on dialect and cultural integration, arguing that global city competition ultimately becomes cultural competition.

01
Health
Migrants Leave Youth and Health, Take Themselves Home
The chapter opens with migrant poet Xu Lizhi's "Insomnia" — "We run along the railway tracks into places called cities, selling youth, selling labor… until all that remains is a cough and a discarded bone." Research using CGSS 2010 data shows migrants arrive healthier than urban residents, but work "3D" jobs — Dirty, Demanding, Dangerous. 56.8% work in manufacturing and construction with only 28.5% work injury and 17.6% medical insurance coverage. The health depletion effect means they return to hometowns in middle age while healthier migrants stay — creating an illusion that migrants are not suffering health loss. Low contract rates (41.3% nationally, only 24.4% for construction workers) and the difficulty of cross-region medical reimbursement under the New Rural Cooperative Medical System force unhealthy migrants to return home. Cities consume their youth and health; villages bear the burden of illness.
02
Discrimination
Principles of Discrimination: Rationality, Efficiency, Fairness
The author uses friend Liu Weiwei's "Sunflower" public welfare project — providing educational resources for migrant children in Shanghai aged 5-12 — to introduce the issue of public service discrimination. Big cities restrict education access for migrant children as a "population control" method. The author analyzes through three principles: Rationality — labor migration has two motives: economic (higher income) and public-service-seeking. Employment and social security records should identify the latter, not subjective occupational selection. "Six small industries" crackdowns (small restaurants, barbershops, etc.) target services actual city residents need. Efficiency — the market is the decisive force in resource allocation; population is a core economic resource. Governments don't know which industries cities need. Fairness — "Focus on behavior, not people": the price mechanism is fairest because it applies equally. Governments should address income inequality directly, not discriminate administratively. "Do not do to others what you would not have done to yourself" — if you wouldn't move to a small town, don't advocate that migrants should settle locally.
03
Consumption
Hukou-Induced Consumption Loss: ≈ 1.8% of GDP Annually
No country resembles China — agricultural GDP has fallen to 10% yet rural population is still ~50%. No country has such a low consumption rate: final consumption/GDP was only 48% in 2009; household consumption was only 35%. China's uniqueness lies in the hukou system — a wedge driven into the market system. Research by the author, Chen Bingkai, and Zhong Ninghua: migrants without local hukou consume ~30% less than local residents. Multiplying by total migrant population, the "consumption loss" equals 1.8% of GDP — roughly 1/5 of annual GDP growth. 2007 data showed the gap narrowed to 16-20%, but rising migrant numbers kept total losses large. Causes: uncertainty about settling prevents durable goods purchases; lack of social security increases precautionary savings; the prospect of returning home and lower future income drives more saving. Crucially — constraining migrant consumption ultimately harms locals too: Liang Wenquan's research shows that the more migrants remit home, the lower the wages of local consumer service workers.

04
Identity
Why Can't Hukou Be Identity? — The Tunnel Effect
Nobel laureate Amartya Sen: when society divides people purely by a single identity, social conflict rises. In China, the most important identity is hukou. Tunnel effect analogy: stuck in a tunnel, cars in the other lane move — you're happy (hope!). That's "good" inequality. But if your lane can't move while the other does — and you can't change lanes — you become angry. Hukou-related income gaps are exactly that "can't-change-lanes" inequality. The author's research: reducing the hukou-related income ratio by 1 increases happiness equivalently to a 53.2% rise in household income — ~5 years of urban income growth, or 7.6 years of rural growth, or 30m² more housing space. Taiwan's "mainlander" issue, India's caste system, US racial divides — all cautionary tales of identity-based inequality. In China, hukou-based identity is institutionally created and should be easier to fix — if left unresolved, its impact on social harmony is deeply alarming.

05
Inequality
Income Inequality in Big Cities — A Global Phenomenon
Higher income inequality in big cities is a global phenomenon. The author's analysis of 2005 census data confirms: larger Chinese cities have higher inequality. Unlike the US, China's big-city inequality is driven mainly by absorbing large numbers of migrants — and the gap among less-educated workers is actually larger (because less-educated workers are more entrepreneurial, widening internal differences). High-skilled incomes go "global" — to attract international talent, you pay global salaries. Meanwhile, low-skilled workers concentrate in big cities because high-skilled workers create massive service demand. Given that big-city inequality is largely inevitable, direct intervention in market-determined incomes should be cautious. More effective: equalizing public services to narrow real welfare gaps — targeting all resident populations. In Shanghai, 50.4% of post-80s migrants are in near suburbs, 30.9% in outer suburbs — the mismatch between public service distribution and population distribution is worsening. Per-capita education resources across districts vary by 2-3×. Without proactive spatial adjustment, future conflicts will intensify.
06
Culture
Cultural Integration — The Return to Dialect and Global City Competitiveness
The author, Richard Freeman (Harvard), Chen Zhao (Fudan), and Chen Bingkai (CUFE) studied the economic returns to dialect. In China, where Mandarin is the common language, local dialect primarily functions as an identity signaling mechanism rather than information transmission. Using the 2005 1% census sample survey and China's seven major dialect regions, the study found that speaking Shanghai dialect significantly increases migrants' probability of entering sales — because it signals identity and builds trust with local customers. In Shanghai, barbers from northern Jiangsu speak fluent Shanghainese; parking attendants and domestic workers learn it. A banker who doesn't speak Shanghainese loses local clients. The better migrants integrate, the less local dialect should serve as an identity marker. Compare NYC (36% foreign-born, 48% speak non-English at home) and London (37% foreign-born, White British now below 50%) — global cities must be composed of locals, newcomers, and global talent, and their culture must be multicultural fusion. As institutions marketize, competition for talent ultimately becomes cultural competition — without inclusive, diverse culture, global city competitiveness is impossible.
07
Dual Society
The "New Urban Dual Society" — The Danger of Residential Segregation
The hukou system creates not just urban-rural division but a "new dual society" within cities. Research consistently finds significant income gaps between hukou and non-hukou residents — and even with equal education, hukou holders earn more. The author's team with Chen Zhao found: non-hukou residents are less happy, less trusting of government, society, and neighbors. Shanghai-specific research: non-hukou populations cluster residentially — even excluding distant suburbs and dormitories. Non-hukou households have worse living conditions and rate communities lower. Most alarmingly, intergenerational transmission — residential segregation means migrants' children grow up in worse environments with worse education, perpetuating poverty across generations and deepening identity cleavages. Shanghai: ~25M residents in 2014, 40% without local hukou; Pearl River Delta cities exceed 50%. If this group continues growing — with lower incomes, less happiness, less trust, and residential concentration — the threat to social harmony is enormous. Rather than spending ever more resources on maintaining stability, it's better to dismantle the "new urban dual structure" now.
08
Policy
Urban Management: Focus on Behavior, Not People
The author invokes Rawls' "veil of ignorance" — when discussing public policy, imagine you don't know your own identity. Chinese wisdom is more practical — "Do not do to others what you would not have done to yourself": those who argue for local urbanization of migrants or against their entry to mega-cities — ask yourself why you don't move to a small town or countryside. As a parent, if policy separated you from your child — how would you feel? Urban policy can only target behavior, not people. Shortages always occur when prices are too low — if the government decides at below-market prices who gets to consume, that is the true unfairness. In most cases, fair markets are also most efficient. China faces two efficiency losses: aggregate efficiency loss — each locality protecting its own interest, sacrificing cross-regional resource allocation; long-term efficiency loss — short-sighted citizens and short-tenured officials reinforcing short-term policies. A friend's comment: "Things happen according to their own laws. We should respect science and law, not judge by our preferences, measure by our interests, or distort by our power."
09
Global Comparison
The Integration Path of Global Cities
Globalization and urbanization make cross-regional migration inevitable — the ability to integrate diverse ethnic groups is increasingly critical. The blending of Shanghainese, Mandarin, and English — precisely the vitality of an immigrant city. In the early 20th century, Shanghai attracted fortune-seekers from around the world and China; Shanghainese was influenced by Suzhou, Ningbo dialects, and English. This linguistic fusion could re-emerge. China's first-tier cities will all become "global cities" — composed of locals, new citizens from elsewhere in China, and global talent. Their culture must be a fusion of local heritage, other Chinese regional cultures, and cultures from around the world. Western Europe's lesson: the problem isn't immigration — it's immigration without integration. Immigration brings complementarity and diversity — cultural and ethnic diversity generates great ideas. But without integration, diversity can become conflict. How China's mega-cities handle the impact of rapidly growing domestic and international migration and promote social integration must be put on the agenda immediately — no further delay. In this great era of globalization, local culture should be cherished but never rigidly guarded.
10
Conclusion
Social Integration: The Ultimate Challenge for a Great Country with Great Cities
Across six dimensions — health depletion, discrimination principles, consumption loss, identity and happiness, income inequality, cultural integration — the social division caused by hukou is multi-dimensional and pervasive. It harms not only migrants but ultimately local residents too. This is not just a fairness issue — it's an efficiency issue (consumption loss = 1.8% of GDP/year), and a social harmony issue (identity-based inequality reduces everyone's happiness). The prescription is clear but not simple: ① Public services gradually cover all resident populations — not just hukou holders. ② Reduce the link between hukou and public services — ultimately achieve equalization. ③ Promote social and cultural integration — make diversity a source of vitality, not conflict. ④ Manage cities by behavior, not identity — let price mechanisms and market forces guide resource allocation. China's urban administrators and residents alike should consider: rather than spending more on stability and security, it's better to dismantle the "new urban dual structure" now — and use those resources for people's happiness. On the road from a great country to great cities, social integration is the final and most difficult frontier.

🔑 Core Conclusion
The "new urban dual society" created by the hukou system is the deepest challenge facing contemporary China. Migrants leave their youth and health in cities and return home with broken bodies — cities consume labor power; villages bear the health burden. Hukou-driven consumption losses reach 1.8% of GDP annually. Identity-based income inequality reduces everyone's happiness — a "can't change lanes" tunnel effect. Big-city income inequality is a global phenomenon; the solution is not market intervention but public service equalization. As institutions marketize, talent competition ultimately becomes cultural competition — without inclusive, diverse culture, global city competitiveness is impossible. Social integration is not an optional add-on but the ultimate challenge a great country with great cities must confront directly.
