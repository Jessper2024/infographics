Understanding a Complex World · Ch.13 "Why Some Successful People Hustle Even Harder Than Ordinary Folks"

The Higher You Stand, the More You See, the More You Panic · Fear Motivates More Powerfully Than Any Other Drive Successful people keep running not out of greed but because they can see the full picture. A captain knows what weather tomorrow may bring and panics terribly. Many bosses look polished on the surface but walk a tightrope — one misstep wipes out decades of accumulation. Successful people's work itself resembles playing a game — stimulating, never dull, especially when the money flows, leaving them in an unbeatable state. Entrepreneurship is a one-way road. Once you start, you cannot turn back.

This chapter offers a distinctive lens on why "the rich hustle harder than the poor." The argument unfolds in three layers: First, illusory security — frontline employees feel safe precisely because they are ignorant. Only a captain standing higher and seeing the full picture panics more, suffers more. Second, all businesses are temporary — profit is essentially information asymmetry. Big companies swarm out like bees, ready to switch tracks at any moment; nothing is permanent. Third, successful people experience work completely differently — once you become a boss with choice, you outsource the dull tasks and focus on challenging ones. Work transforms into something stimulating like a game, powerfully addictive. Once you start a business, you basically can't go back.

01
Blind Spot
Frontline Employees Feel Secure Through Ignorance — Only the Captain on Deck Sees the Real Danger
The market constantly fluctuates. A company is like a ship sailing stormy seas. Only the captain sees the full picture and knows what weather tomorrow may bring — he panics terribly. Meanwhile, people below feel perfectly secure. Middle managers at big tech companies only discover upon being laid off that they haven't written code in years and have become technically obsolete. Many American programmers deliberately avoid promotions and raises, refusing management — they understand that when the company hits trouble, the first to be cut are high-salaried, unclear-output middle layers. Those big-tech roles aren't truly stable — cogs simply don't know their product's market position and feel secure through ignorance.

02
Business Essence
Every Company Is a "Ship of Theseus" — After Enough Patches, It's No Longer the Same Ship
Big companies don't "focus on core business" because they can't be sure how long the core will last. Tencent and Alibaba constantly change, forever opening new fronts, breaking through on every axis — whichever gains traction counts. The money wasted every year is incalculable. It's like a swarm algorithm — individual bees have negative intelligence, but by swarming everywhere with whoever finds resources counting, they achieve the daunting task of locating distant flowers. Profit is essentially information asymmetry — once others know what you know, you can forget about making money. Hence everyone operates on "ten thousand years is too long, seize today."

03
Game Mechanics
After Becoming a Boss, Work Feels Like Playing a Game — Intense, Stimulating, Addictive, and You Can Never Go Back
Games contain addictive stimulus elements: "bounded challenge," "short-term rewards," "immediate feedback," and "random prizes." Humans depend heavily on feedback. Do something that generates a quick response, and the reward mechanism fires — it feels deeply satisfying. A game also needs a touch of challenge — too easy is boring, too hard drives you away. When you gain choice, you outsource tedious work to subordinates and focus on challenging tasks. Work's nature changes — it becomes like playing a game, stimulating rather than dull. Especially when money flows, that tense excitement and the joy of making your own decisions and collecting your own rewards is overwhelmingly addictive. Entrepreneurs basically can never return to cubicle life.

04
Fear-Driven
Fear Energizes People More Powerfully Than Any Other Drive — Bosses' Assets Are Just a Chain of Debt
Most bosses walk a tightrope. One misstep and decades of accumulation evaporate. A boss's money is mostly book value: Zhang owes me two hundred million, I owe Li one hundred million, therefore I have one hundred million in assets. If Zhang goes bankrupt, I go from +100M in assets to -100M in debt overnight. The pandemic turned many bosses into "negative-net-worth" figures overnight. "Second-generation rich kids" generally live quite carefree — like frontline employees, they can't see the full picture and harbor an illusory sense of security. Meanwhile, "first-generation rich" are often diligent and disciplined, working overtime day and night. The higher you stand, the fuller the picture — you must worry about operations, maturing debts, ad effectiveness, competitor moves, cash reserves, policy changes. Basically no one can sleep soundly.

05
Captain Mindset
Adults Who Know Society's Truths Should Prepare Early — Shed the Illusory Security and Start Hustling
Humans are not wired to dislike challenge. It's in our genes. People with some foundation who keep fighting relentlessly often carry a "captain's mindset" — they stand at the wind's edge, can see both risks and opportunities, and thus rise to the challenge. Choose the right difficulty, select appropriate challenges, keep leveling yourself up. The author advises adults to prepare early after grasping society's truths: figure out which tasks are building the boss's down payment and which are building your own. Shed the illusory security and bake change into daily life. Don't be afraid — start hustling. You are not actually alone — those exceptionally impressive people also struggle, also hit creative blocks, also have to grind it out slowly.
⚓

Standing on deck seeing the storm → fear-driven → proactive engagement → rising to challenge. Prepare for uncertainty, never stop hustling.

🛏️

Passenger's Mindset

In the cabin, can't see the sea → illusory security → passive waiting. Only on layoff day do you realize it's already too late.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
Successful people weren't born loving hardship. Their position forces them to endure it. The higher you stand, the more of the full picture you see — business risks, maturing debts, competitors emerging, the cash burn rate — these make fear the most powerful motivator. Meanwhile, autonomy transforms work into a massive immersive strategy game, powerfully addictive. Once you've experienced it, you can never tolerate cubicle tedium again. So it's not that you are not working hard enough — it's that your work lacks true fear as a driver and lacks the game-like thrill of making your own decisions and collecting your own rewards. Adults who see the truth should proactively shed illusory security and start hustling — figure out which tasks are building the boss's down payment and which are building your own.
