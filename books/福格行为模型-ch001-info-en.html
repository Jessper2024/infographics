Tiny Habits · Ch.1「The Fogg Behavior Model」

B=MAP · Behavior = Motivation + Ability + Prompt Have you been blaming yourself for "lacking discipline" or being "lazy"? You've been wrong about yourself all along. The real reason behaviors don't happen isn't weak willpower — it's poor design. The Fogg Behavior Model reveals: a behavior occurs when only 3 elements converge — Motivation (M), Ability (A), and Prompt (P). Miss one, and nothing happens.

This is the foundational chapter of the book. Dr. Fogg introduces the revolutionary B=MAP formula: Behavior = Motivation + Ability + Prompt. When these three elements converge simultaneously, behavior happens; when any one is missing, it doesn't. This model applies universally to all behaviors — from flossing to running a marathon, from donating money to exercising. Once you understand B=MAP, you'll never again blame character flaws or lack of willpower for behavioral failures. Instead, you'll analyze and design behavior systematically across three dimensions: motivation, ability, and prompt.

01
Core Formula
B=MAP: When Motivation, Ability & Prompt Converge, Behavior Happens
Behaviors are like different models of bicycles — they look different, but the core mechanism is the same. Dr. Fogg's personal experience donating to the Red Cross while on the elliptical at the gym perfectly illustrates B=MAP: Motivation (wanting to help earthquake victims) + Ability (texting a donation is simple) + Prompt (receiving the text) = Behavior (completing the donation). If the Red Cross had called asking for credit card details, or emailed where it landed in spam — any one element missing — the behavior wouldn't have occurred. This is the universality of B=MAP: from online shopping to exercise, all behaviors are driven by the same mechanism.

02
Motivation Trap
The Motivation Wave: Peaks Crashing Back to Reality
Motivation is like a fair-weather friend — fun to party with, but don't count on them to pick you up at the airport. People set sky-high goals at motivation peaks (like 10 New Year's resolutions on Day 1) and abandon everything when motivation dips. Katie's story: she woke at 4:30 AM intending to exercise, but got sucked into the digital vortex — the red notification badges on her phone consumed her attention and she forgot to work out entirely. She had intense motivation, but it was hijacked. The lesson: don't rely on motivation. Design systems. Motivation is unreliable; behavior design is not.
📉

❌ The Peak-Motivation Mistake

Setting overambitious goals during a motivation high, then self-blaming and quitting when the wave crashes.

✅

✅ The Right Approach

Accept that motivation is fickle. Invest your energy in designing ability and prompts instead. Motivation starts you; systems sustain you.

03
Ability Diagnosis
The Ability Chain: Five Dimensions — Break One, Break All
Ability isn't a single "I can do it" — it's a chain of five dimensions. ① Time — Do you have time? ② Money — Can you afford it? ③ Physical Effort — Do you have the energy? ④ Mental Effort — Is your cognitive load manageable? ⑤ Routine — Does it fit into your daily flow? The weakest link in this chain determines whether behavior happens. Improving ability isn't about "trying harder" — it's about finding the tightest link in the chain and loosening it. Make the behavior simpler.

04
Prompt System
Without a Prompt, the Strongest Motivation and Ability Are Useless
A prompt is the "ignition switch" for behavior — it determines the exact moment action occurs. Prompts come in three types: ① Person Prompts (relying on memory/alarms) — unreliable; people forget. ② Context Prompts (sticky notes/app notifications) — moderately reliable but easily ignored due to notification fatigue. ③ Action Prompts (Anchors) — the most reliable, attaching new behaviors to existing habits. Action prompts beat any motivational speech or willpower training: you don't need to "remember" to act — you design a cue you're guaranteed to encounter.

05
Mindset Shift
Stop Blaming Yourself! It's Not You — It's the Design
Katie's two habits reveal the truth: tidying her desk (automatic) and scrolling her phone (uncontrollable) — both operate on the exact same mechanism. She's not lazy, and she doesn't lack willpower. Tidying her desk happened to satisfy all three B=MAP elements, so it became automatic. Phone scrolling was hijacked by powerful digital product design that weaponized prompts and motivation. When you analyze every "failed" behavior through the B=MAP lens, you find the missing element — motivation too weak? Make it more appealing. Ability too low? Make it simpler. Prompt invisible? Design an unmissable signal.
😞

Old Mindset: Self-Blame

"I'm lazy" / "I lack willpower" / "I'm just not capable" — attributing failure to character flaws.

🔧

New Mindset: Design Thinking

"Which B=MAP element is missing?" — treating failure as design information to systematically optimize M, A, or P.

🔑 Key Takeaway
There is only one formula for behavior: B=MAP. Every behavior — from tidying a desk to doomscrolling your phone — is driven by motivation, ability, and prompt. You don't need more willpower; you need better behavior design. When a behavior doesn't happen, don't blame yourself — check which of M, A, or P is missing. The entire method in this book is built on this simple formula — it's the operating system for changing any behavior.
