Layout of the World · Chapter 5
「Types of Division: Collapse vs. Erosion」

Wang Fuzhi's insight — two modes of dynastic demise, and four geostrategic patterns of fragmentation

Wang Fuzhi classified dynastic demise into two modes: wǎjiě (collapse) and tǔbēng (erosion). Collapse means comprehensive chaos — highly autocratic dynasties with singular power structures (Qin, Xin, Sui, Yuan) experience a direct explosion between the apex and base, leaving no local order behind, yet their reunification is typically swift. Erosion means local orders persist even after overall collapse — dynasties with intermediate power-sharing groups (Eastern Han, Western Jin, Tang, Song) experience prolonged decline, and political-centrifugal forces combine with geographic-centrifugal forces to produce lasting fragmentation. Building on this, the chapter anatomizes four geographic types of division: north-south standoff, east-west conflict, triangular balance, and quadratic fragmentation — revealing how China's chessboard geography shapes distinct forms of disunity.

1
Wang Fuzhi
Collapse vs. Erosion: Two Modes of Dynastic Demise

Wang Fuzhi wrote: 'Tǔbēng and wǎjiě — both lead to extinction, yet their dynamics differ. In collapse, there is nothing to repair; one can only watch it crumble. In erosion, one might wish to hold it together but cannot.' Collapse (wǎjiě) — total chaos — characterized Qin, Xin, Sui, and Yuan. These highly autocratic dynasties had singular power structures, lacking intermediate strata to share power with the throne. This created direct antagonism between the apex and the base. 'The people suffer and the ruler is indifferent; below they complain and above they are unaware.' Once the regime weakened, any spark could trigger universal explosion — even local order could not survive. Yet collapse-type chaos was usually brief, and reunification rapid.

2
Erosion's Path
Erosion-type Decline: Intermediate Powers Meet Geographic Fragmentation

Erosion dynasties (Eastern Han, Western Jin, Tang, Song) contained intermediate power-sharing groups — Eastern Han's provincial governors, Western Jin's princely fiefs, Tang's regional military commissioners. These groups were both forces weakening the throne and buffers preserving partial order. After the overall order collapsed, these intermediates could maintain or establish local order. 'Political centrifugal forces, combined with geographic centrifugal forces, readily push erosion-type situations toward fragmentation.' Eastern Han's warlordism became the Three Kingdoms; Western Jin's decay became the long North-South standoff; Tang's military commissioners became the Five Dynasties and Ten Kingdoms. 'Erosion must persist for centuries before yielding to collapse; only when the collapse is complete does the realm become tranquil.'

3
N-S Standoff
Bipolar Type I: North-South Standoff — the Military Logic of Geographic Partitions

North-South standoffs centered on the Huai River–Qinling line. Northern nomads excelled in cavalry and field combat, best in autumn and winter. Southern Han people were agrarian, strong in water warfare, fortification defense, and canal logistics, best in spring and summer. Each side held advantages in the Yellow-Huai region — what favored one disadvantaged the other, producing grinding attrition. South of the Huai, dense water networks blunted northern cavalry; north of the Huai, rivers froze in winter and had short rainy seasons, offering little barrier. The Huai, China's geographic N-S dividing line, became the de facto military dividing line. The Western Jin–Sun Wu standoff, the Northern-Southern Dynasties, Jin–Southern Song, and Mongol–Southern Song all followed this pattern.

4
E-W Conflict
Bipolar Type II: East-West Conflict — the Advantage of Elevation

East-West conflicts unfolded between China's second and third topographic tiers, with the eastern edge of the second tier as the frontier: from Youyan south along the Taihang, through Songshan, Fangcheng, to the Tongbai, Dabie ranges into western Hubei. Key passes included Juyong, Feihu, Zijing, Daoma, Jingxing, Fukou, Tianjing, and Chenggao. The West held topographic advantage: Guanzhong's higher elevation, if combined with control of the Chenggao line, provided an immensely favorable offensive posture. The East controlling Chenggao at best achieved a decent defensive posture. Historically, Western powers prevailed more often — Qin's unification of the Six States, Liu Bang's defeat of Xiang Yu, both represented the West overpowering the East. Yet east-west conflict was typically transient, neither durable nor stable.

5
Triangular & Quadratic
Triangular Balance and Quadratic Fragmentation: The Chessboard Fission

The most typical triangular balance pitted Guanzhong (topographic advantage), Hebei (strength in numbers), and Jiangnan (Yangtze barrier) against each other — a rough equilibrium. The Wei-Jin period saw six triangular configurations, all ultimately converging into north-south standoffs. When Sichuan established an independent regime on top of the three, the result was quadratic fragmentation — the four corners squared off. The Warring States' Seven Powers also fit: Qin held Guanzhong, Qi held Shandong, Chu held Fangcheng and the Han, Yan held northern Hebei — four powers backed by secure corners; Han, Zhao, and Wei in the center were strong united, weak divided.

📌 Key Takeaways

The two modes of dynastic demise — collapse and erosion — stem from differences in social power structure. The more autocratic the dynasty, the closer its fall to collapse (total chaos but rapid rebuilding); the more intermediate power-sharing, the closer to erosion (lasting local order but prolonged fragmentation). The fusion of political and geographic centrifugal forces generates four geographic types of division: north-south standoff as the military expression of the Huai-Qinling dividing line, east-west conflict as a contest of topographic advantage, triangular balance (Guanzhong-Hebei-Jiangnan) as the geographic pattern's most balanced configuration, and quadratic fragmentation as the natural fission of the chessboard. Different types of division dictate different paths of reunification — providing a geographic key to understanding China's historical rhythm of fragmentation and unity.
