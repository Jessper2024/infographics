01
Decade Shift
From Cubicle Anxiety to Ten Million Followers: Striving Wasn't Painful After All
Ten years ago he stood bewildered on Xi'erqi streets, debugging code overnight in a cubicle, anxious about failing his internship, nervously waiting outside meeting rooms for promotion news — the author lived through it all. But looking back, he says striving was never purely painful: writing and sharing kept him company. Once-distant goals were achieved without him noticing, from a twenty-something kid to a thirty-something man, with ever-higher positions and ever-better writing.

02
Knowledge Mgmt
Life as a Loot Game: Find a Place for Each Fragment to Craft Advanced Tools
Life resembles "loot collecting" in video games — explore every box and cabinet at a location, find useful items, and when your skills level up, synthesize junk into advanced tools. These tools become your power multipliers, enabling faster leveling and stronger combat capability. That's power-law growth: incredibly slow at first, incredibly fast later. The problem: you don't know which items will be useful when picking them up, and often discard ones you'll need later. Life becomes a bear breaking off corn cobs — picking and dropping until nothing remains.

03
Feynman Method
Writing = Feynman Learning + Knowledge Anchoring: An Ever-Brighter Lamp
When you understand something, describe it in your own words and share it — that is the very core of the Feynman Technique. Meanwhile, these ideas gain anchors and links, connecting to your existing knowledge tree. The writing process itself is the learning process. Writing is a lamp: you nurture it, and it cares for you. It is not just an output tool but an engine that integrates scattered information into a structured knowledge system.

04
Growth Mindset
Why Write About Unfamiliar Fields? Because Not Knowing Demands Learning
People often ask: "You're not familiar with this field — why write about it?" His answer is direct: precisely because he doesn't know, he needs to learn. The more you learn, the broader your thinking, the easier problem-solving becomes. He defines himself as a "growth-focused sharing blogger," not a science popularizer — he always has an impulse to study topics he doesn't fully understand. Constant writing and sharing have benefited him immensely: higher positions, better writing, and an ever-growing audience.

05
Impact Others
That Lamp, Lighting a Little of Others' Paths
After Growing Upward was published, teachers at several prestigious Beijing schools gave the book to students heading into society, hoping "that lamp, repeatedly polished, might light a little of their path." Some readers said the book alleviated their depression. The author reflects: "Hearing this, I am genuinely happy." — This is writing's unexpected bonus: it not only helps you grow but can inadvertently change others' life trajectories.

"Writing itself is power, a lamp that grows ever brighter. You nurture it, and it takes care of you."

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
Life is like a "loot collecting" game — the key lies in finding a place for every fragment. Writing is the best storage engine: describe knowledge in your own words via the Feynman Technique, share it, anchor it to your knowledge tree, creating power-law growth. Striving need not be painful, because the lamp of writing grows ever brighter — you nurture it, it nurtures you, and it can inadvertently light others' paths as well.
