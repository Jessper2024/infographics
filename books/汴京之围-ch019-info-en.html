Siege of Bianjing · Chapter 17 "Evacuation"

14,000 Marching North · The Road of Humiliation from Bianjing to Wuguo City The Jurchen army marched north laden with gold, silver, and women — seven batches of captives departed in succession. Of the 14,000 who set out, only four in ten men survived to reach Yanjing. Emperor Qinzong prostrated on the ground, weeping as he bid farewell to Bianjing; Zhang Bangchang, in white mourning robes, set up incense altars to see them off. The two emperors lived out their remaining years in humiliation — the Sheep-Leading Rite, Duke of Faint Virtue, Marquis of Double Folly. The Zhao clan's blood merged into the north; the Jurchen, in turn, embraced agrarian civilization.

From late third month to early fourth month of Jingkang Year 2, the Jurchen army dispatched captives north in seven batches. Wolibu took the Retired Emperor via the Hebei route; Nianhan took the young emperor via the Shanxi route. Before evacuating, the two emperors made their final farewell to Bianjing — Emperor Qinzong prostrated weeping, "Heaven and earth grieved, the city walls echoed," while Zhang Bangchang led the officials in white robes to see them off. The road north was littered with devastation: the Retired Emperor's son, Prince Yan, died en route, his body burned in a horse trough; of the 3,000-plus imperial clansmen, only some 1,000 reached Yanjing, nine in ten sick. Thereafter, the two emperors were relocated again and again: Yanjing → Zhongjing → Shangjing → Hanzhou → Wuguo City. The Sheep-Leading Rite, Duke of Faint Virtue, Marquis of Double Folly — each humiliation worse than the last. In the fifth year of Shaoxing, the Retired Emperor died at Wuguo City; the young emperor lived on alone for another twenty-one years.

01
Evacuation Deployment
An Army Laden with Loot: How the Jurchen Secured Their Northern Return
When they invaded, the Jurchen relied on blitzkrieg; by the time they withdrew, they had transformed into a different army — laden with gold, silver, and jewels, horses carrying women instead of warriors. Every man was driven by the urge for riches and lust for women, unwilling to fight any longer. The Jurchen's greatest fear was interception by Northern Song forces to the north. On the fourth day of the third month, Nianhan's younger brother Alan went ahead with 1,050 carts of books and ritual vessels, flying the Northern Song imperial banner from the carts — if Prince Kang let this convoy pass, it proved the road was safe. The Jurchen also defeated Prince Kang's forces in succession at Kaide, Xingren, and Puzhou; Zong Ze's chariot formation was destroyed in the Battle of Nanhua. Prince Kang was more concerned with restoring order after the Jurchen departure than with trapping them in the interior.

02
Farewell to Bianjing
Heaven and Earth Grieved: The Two Emperors' Final Farewell to Bianjing
On the 27th day of the third month, Emperor Qinzong prostrated on the ground and wept as he bade farewell to the Bianjing he would never see again. "Heaven and earth grieved, the city walls echoed with the sound." Zhang Bangchang employed the full imperial honor guard, yet dressed entirely in white — the color of mourning. At Nanxun Gate and Wuyue Temple, he set up incense altars and led the officials and commoners (also clad in white) in tearful farewell to the Retired Emperor and the Emperor. At dawn on the 28th day of the third month, the captives at Qingcheng were consolidated at Liujia Temple. The Retired Emperor met for the final time with the princes and imperial sons-in-law; the princesses emerged from Shousheng Temple to pay respects to their parents and husbands — this was the last family reunion of their lives. That night, the Jurchen troops withdrew from the city walls and handed the gates over to the Great Chu army.

03
The Road North
From Bianjing to Wuguo City: The Two Emperors' Endless Northern Relocations
The two emperors' northern journey was a road of ever-escalating humiliation. The Retired Emperor traveled with Wolibu via the Hebei route and was relatively well treated — Wolibu drank and played ball with him, and when they passed Zhongshan, had him urge the defenders to surrender (they refused). The young emperor, in Nianhan's custody, suffered far more: dressed in black, capped with felt, riding a black horse, even his sobs were silenced. Upon reaching Yanjing, the two were lodged at Yanshou Temple and Minzhong Shrine. But after Prince Kang declared himself emperor, the Jurchen ruler deemed Yanjing too close to the south and moved them to Zhongjing (990 li from Yanjing). In the second year of Jianyan, when Ma Kuo raised troops at Wuma Mountain — and the Jurchen suspected a link to the two emperors — they were moved again to Tongsai Prefecture to farm their own food. Finally, in the fourth year of Jianyan, they were sent to Wuguo City in the Huligai circuit — the final resting place of the Retired Emperor and his consort.

04
Triumph Ceremony
The Sheep-Leading Rite and Duke of Faint Virtue: The Ultimate Humiliation of Emperors
On the 24th day of the eighth month, Jianyan Year 2, the two emperors endured the most humiliating day of their lives. Wolibu had died the previous year; Liu Yanzong was also no more. No one remained to protect the two emperors. The Jurchen emperor staged a grand triumph ceremony: the two emperors, their empresses, and consorts were stripped of their robes and wrapped in sheepskins, then led before the Jurchen ancestral temple to perform the Sheep-Leading Rite — clutching sheepskin ropes, symbolizing submission like sheep to the slaughter. Empress Zhu, unable to bear the humiliation, hanged herself, was rescued, then drowned herself. On the 25th day of the tenth month, the Retired Emperor was invested as "Duke of Faint Virtue" and the young emperor as "Marquis of Double Folly" — these insulting titles were the ultimate mockery of the Northern Song monarchy. Zhao clan women, bare-chested and wrapped only in sheepskins, were distributed by the thousand on the spot to Jurchen guards.

05
Bloodline Merger
The Zhao Clan Merges into the North: Princesses Marry Princes · Clansmen Wed Khitan Princesses
Not all members of the Zhao clan met a tragic fate — many gradually adapted to life in the north. Princess Xunde (Fujin) became the wife of Prince Sheyema; Princesses Maode and Huifu also married Jurchen princes; Princesses Rongde, Ningfu, Huafu, and Qingfu married the Jurchen emperor himself, becoming secondary consorts. The Duke of Xiang married a Khitan princess of the Yelü clan; Prince Jian'an married an "inner consort" of the Jurchen emperor. The Zhao clan completed its transformation from agrarian to nomadic civilization — "southern blood and northern blood rapidly merged together." And the Jurchen, originally nomads, were simultaneously turning toward agrarian civilization, growing accustomed to Song culture and an enormous imperial harem.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Jurchen army's northern withdrawal was not merely a military retreat but a great civilizational migration and fusion. Fourteen thousand captives departed Bianjing; by Yanjing, only four in ten men survived — more women lived, for women cross civilizational boundaries more readily than men. The two emperors lived out their remaining days in humiliation: the Sheep-Leading Rite reduced monarchs to livestock, and the title "Duke of Faint Virtue" etched their disgrace into the historical record. Yet the Zhao princesses married Jurchen princes and emperors, and the Zhao clansmen took Khitan princesses as wives — their descendants would become subjects of the Jin Empire. The tragedy of the Jingkang Incident closed with bloodline fusion as its historical conclusion.
