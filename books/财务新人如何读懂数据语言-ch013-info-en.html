Chapter 13
Two Worlds in One Interview Question: Accounting Profit vs Economic Profit
The Citibank interview question: buy a chicken for $8, sell at $9; buy back at $10, sell at $11. Profit or loss? Neil Shen (沈南鹏), later founder of Sequoia Capital China, answered "lost $4" — exactly what the investment-bank chiefs wanted to hear. Earning $2 is accounting profit — faithful to historical records. Losing $4 is economic profit — introducing opportunity cost for comparison. The single best trade was 11−8 = $3; doing it twice yields $6 maximum. Actually earning only $2 means missing out on $4 relative to the best possible outcome. Less earned equals loss. Both answers are correct — one reflects bookkeeping, the other economic choice.

Chapter 13 · Applications
Three Layers of Opportunity Cost: Evaluation, Review, and Budgeting
From the $8 chicken story to real-world management decisions, opportunity-cost thinking spans three levels. Performance Evaluation: Replace accounting profit with EVA (Economic Value Added) for top management — if earnings don't beat risk-free bank returns, management has underperformed. Project Review: Use NPV (Net Present Value) to weigh investments, embedding the cost of capital in discounting. Budget Allocation: $1M budget; Engineering proposes doubling output vs Manufacturing proposes halving labor — both NPV-positive, which wins? Automation replacing workers delivers accelerated compounding returns as labor costs rise annually. The only difference: accountants look backward, investors look forward.

Chapter 14
Alternative Financial Freedom: Studying Finance Is About Gaining Freedom
Harari wrote in Sapiens that "studying history is about gaining freedom" — by the same logic, studying finance is about gaining freedom. The author unpacks this freedom across three dimensions:
The Right May Not Need Persisting
→
A bottleneck workstation produces 100K units/month. Product A: +20% margin, Product B: +15% — both profitable. Client offers Product C at +30% but consumes half the capacity. B is profitable yet should be dropped. GE CEO Jack Welch sold profitable divisions outside the top 3 — breaking the cage of "profitable so keep it"
The Wrong May Not Need Correcting
→
A departing employee's meal card has a few hundred yuan overpaid; a dozen executives spent half an hour discussing it — their collective salary already exceeds the issue itself. February depreciation treated the same as 31-day months — because designing a correction system costs more. When fixing costs > error costs, leave it alone
Do Not Worship Numbers
→
In a DCF valuation, changing the perpetual growth rate from 3% to 5% shifts the answer from one billion to ten billion. Which company grows at X% perpetually? That's not a finance question — it's a philosophical question only God can answer. Dinner with the founder trumps a spreadsheet

Chapter 15
T-Accounts: The Visualization Tool That Draws Out Complex Transactions
T-accounts are the visual version of double-entry bookkeeping — converting complex business transactions into professional financial language. Core steps: List all relevant accounts → record each transaction sequentially (subdivide into 1a, 1b) → ensure debits equal credits horizontally → after all transactions, the net change after offsetting reveals the income-statement impact. The book illustrates with a property-flipping case: Father Wang starts with $100K, buys 100㎡ at $5K/㎡ (borrowing $400K), flips it at $8K/㎡ to his son (who puts $160K down + $640K mortgage), son defaults and the bank repossesses — step-by-step T-accounts reveal every party's gain or loss at a glance.

Chapter 16
The Rule of 72: A Supercharged Mental Arithmetic Shortcut for Compound Interest
A handy shortcut taught by an NTU Singapore professor: 72 ÷ annual return rate = years to double your principal. 4% → 18 years; 9% → 8 years; S&P 500 averaging ~7% → roughly 10 years to double. Reverse application: Want to double in 3 years? Annual growth must exceed 24%. Instant sanity check: When doing NPV, if WACC = 18%, the present value of year-4 cash flow is roughly halved — any significant deviation signals a calculation error. Such mental arithmetic often leaves subordinates wide-eyed: "Boss is a genius!"

🔑 Key Takeaway
The evolution of financial thinking: from accounting profit that records the past, to economic profit that introduces opportunity cost, to cognitive freedom that transcends the numbers themselves. Accounting profit is loyal to history but only explains what happened — true investors always look forward. The essence of finance is packaging future cash flows into present value. Yet one layer beyond forward thinking is even more important: "do not worship numbers." Every DCF valuation model rests on fragile assumptions. The real purpose of studying finance is gaining the freedom to choose — the right need not be persisted with, the wrong need not be corrected, and clear judgment must operate beyond the cage of digits. T-accounts are the mental scaffolding; the Rule of 72 is the mental weapon. Tools serve decisions; decisions serve freedom.
