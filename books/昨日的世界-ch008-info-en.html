The World of Yesterday · Ch.8 "Europe's Light and Shadow"

Pre-war Europe's golden age and its undercurrents — from prosperity and confidence to a single gunshot in Sarajevo

This is one of the book's most sweeping chapters, recording Europe's light and shadow in the final years before World War I. The unprecedented prosperity, technological progress, and optimism of European integration brought by forty years of peace stood in sharp contrast to the secretly expanding ambitions of nations and the danger of surplus energy. Zweig recalls his precious friendship with Romain Rolland, experiences the shock of the Redl spy affair, and witnesses the eruption of hatred against Kaiser Wilhelm in a small French cinema. Ultimately, the gunshot in Sarajevo shattered the entire world. The chapter reveals a painful lesson: we were deceived by the thing we loved most — our optimism.

1 The Golden Age: Unprecedented Prosperity and Confidence

Pre-war Europe had never been so powerful, wealthy, and beautiful — forty years of peace energized national economies, technology accelerated the pace of life, and cities grew more magnificent by the day.

Berlin in 1905 had become a world city, far surpassing its 1901 self. Book circulations multiplied three-, five-, tenfold. New theaters, libraries, and museums rose everywhere. Bathrooms and telephones — once privileges of the few — entered petit-bourgeois households. Even the working class began to prosper.

When a Zeppelin circled above Strasbourg Cathedral, crowds cheered wildly. When Blériot flew across the English Channel, Viennese celebrated as if he were their national hero. "If an airplane can easily cross borders, what meaning do borders have!" European consciousness was forming; people believed they saw a new dawn.

2 Romain Rolland: The Conscience of Europe

Zweig discovered Romain Rolland by chance in a Florentine sculptor's cluttered studio, picking up a literary magazine — this friendship later "at times even determined the course of my life."

On their first meeting, Zweig climbed five flights of narrow spiral stairs and sensed "a special serenity." Rolland's vivid blue eyes were "the clearest, kindest eyes I had ever seen in a human being." Tall but very thin, his ascetic frame concealed enormous spiritual labor.

"Art can satisfy each of us individually, but it is powerless against real life." — Romain Rolland

Rolland explained Jean-Christophe's threefold mission: to pay tribute to music, to affirm his belief in European unity, and to awaken nations to reflection. He warned: "Those who stir up hatred are, by their base nature, more aggressive than those who seek reconciliation." Zweig knew at first sight: "In the critical hour, he would represent Europe's conscience — time proved me right."

3 The Redl Affair: Espionage at the Empire's Heart

Colonel Redl, head of Austrian military intelligence, was himself a Russian spy — this shocking discovery made Zweig feel the fear of war for the first time.

During the 1912 Balkan crisis, Austria's most secret "march plans" were leaked to Russia. Colonel Redl was assigned to find the traitor — he was the traitor. A suspicious mail delivery of crisp new kronen notes from a Russian border station exposed everything. Detective work, horse-cab tracking, café identification — Vienna played out a real-life thriller.

📬 The Mail Clue
A post-restante letter from a Russian border station, addressed to "Opelbal," containing crisp new thousand-kronen notes.
🏇 Cab Chase
The detective missed the suspect while getting a morning drink, but a stable-hand remembered the cab's number.
☕ Café Identification
A waiter at Café Giselhof was certain: the man was Colonel Redl, no one else.
💀 Midnight Gunshot
Officers placed a revolver before Redl. At 2 AM, a single shot. The next day, a brief obituary in the evening papers.

Redl had been a homosexual blackmailed for years. Everyone understood: had war broken out, one man alone could have destroyed tens of thousands of lives. "Only then did we Austrians realize that during the past year we had stood at the critical moment of a world war."

4 One Second in Tours: The Depth of Hatred

In spring 1914, in a suburban cinema in Tours, France, the moment Kaiser Wilhelm appeared on screen, the dark hall erupted in piercing whistles and stamping feet — in a single second, Zweig saw that hatred had penetrated the marrow of ordinary people.

That evening's newsreel showed: an English boat race (audience chatting), a French military parade (no one noticed), then — Kaiser Wilhelm visiting Emperor Franz Joseph in Vienna. When the aged emperor appeared, the Tourangeaux laughed kindly. But the moment Wilhelm's upturned mustache appeared —

"The dark hall instantly erupted in piercing whistles and stamping feet; men, women, and children all jeered spontaneously. I was profoundly shocked, seized with terror." — Zweig

Zweig did not sleep that night. Had it happened in Paris, he would have been less shaken. What terrified him was that hatred had penetrated the provinces, the honest, simple common people. Rolland said: "The more naive the people, the more easily they are deceived." Most friends dismissed it: "The French never take politics to heart."

5 The Last Summer: The Shot in Sarajevo

In the summer of 1914, everything was like a promising harvest — friendships deeper than ever, Paris more carefree than ever. Zweig said goodbye to Verhaeren in Rouen, promising to meet again on August 1. Two years later, at the same spot, a train — the very machine Verhaeren had praised — crushed him to death.

On his last days in Paris, Zweig accompanied Verhaeren to Rouen. At night they stood before the cathedral, its spire glittering in moonlight — "Could such beauty belong to only one 'fatherland' and not to all of us?" At the station, Verhaeren embraced him: "August first, at my Caillou-Qui-Bique."

The Light

  • Paris in full bloom, friendships deeper than ever
  • New friends in "enemy countries"
  • Jules Romains's pranks, schoolboy-like joy
  • Belief that reason would stop war at the last moment

The Shadow

  • National ambitions spreading like epidemic
  • "Surplus energy" demanding release
  • Anti-war forces gathered too slowly, too timidly
  • Intellectuals' passive indifference

"On June 28, 1914, a shot in Sarajevo shattered in an instant the world in which we had been educated, had made our home — a safe, rational world — like an empty clay pot." — Zweig

💡 Key Takeaway

Pre-war Europe's tragedy stemmed not from a lack of peace-loving forces but from "surplus energy" — forty years of accumulated vitality with no outlet, ultimately transformed into destructive war. Zweig's deepest insight is that we were deceived by the thing we loved most — our optimism. Romain Rolland had already seen that "art is powerless against reality"; Bertha von Suttner shouted in the street "the war has already begun" — but no one listened. When hatred penetrates the marrow, when "Lebensraum" becomes cover for aggression, when optimism blinds us to crisis, civilization's collapse is not an accident but an inevitability. This chapter stands as the most solemn warning for every era of peace.
