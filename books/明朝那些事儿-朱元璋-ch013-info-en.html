Chapter 12 "Poyang Lake! Fight to the Death!"

36 Days of Blood on the Lake — Fire Attack Shatters the Iron Chain Fleet, Tyrant Chen Youliang Falls at Poyang

Situation: On July 20, 1363, Zhu Yuanzhang's 200,000-strong fleet met Chen Youliang's 600,000-strong armada on Lake Poyang — a decisive naval battle for China's fate. Zhu's fleet was mostly small captured fishing boats against Chen's towering three-deck iron-armored warships linked in a ten-li chain. Conflict: On day two, Zhang Dingbian's suicidal charge nearly killed Zhu himself; on day three, Chen's iron-chain fleet became a death trap. Question: How could an inferior fleet destroy a seemingly invincible armada in four days? Answer: Xu Da's wolf-pack tactics opened the fight; Chang Yuchun's single arrow saved Zhu; Ding Pulang's last stand bought time; Guo Xing's fire-ship plan and a timely northeast wind turned the lake into an inferno — destroying Chen's fleet and ending his reign forever.

First Day
Small Boats vs Giants: Xu Da's Wolf-Pack Tactics

Facing Chen's mountain-sized warships, Xu Da split his fleet into eleven independent teams — surrounding and boarding giants from multiple angles, capturing one entire warship on day one.

When both fleets met at Kanglang Mountain, Zhu's soldiers were shocked. Chen's largest warships were 15 zhang long, 2 zhang wide, 3 stories high — so tall that archers on Zhu's boats could only look up. Zhu's fleet still relied on captured vessels from the Longwan battle plus fishing boats. Xu Da divided his fleet into eleven small attack teams, each targeting different angles of the giant ships. Simultaneously striking from multiple directions, one team successfully boarded and captured a Chen warship — killing over 1,000 enemy sailors. First blood to Zhu.

02
Life or Death
Zhang Dingbian's Charge: Taking the General's Head from 100,000

Chen Youliang's mightiest general Zhang Dingbian broke through Zhu's entire front line with just three ships — and was stopped only by a single arrow from Chang Yuchun.

Zhang Dingbian was the most fearsome warrior of the age — brave, scholarly, and Chen's childhood friend. During the stalemate, he launched an unsanctioned solo charge: three ships cutting straight through Zhu's vanguard, killing generals Han Cheng, Chen Zhaoxian, and Song Gui along the way. Zhu's flagship fled, turned too sharply, and ran aground — completely defenseless. Just as Zhang Dingbian closed in, Chang Yuchun stood calmly on his nearby ship, asked which man was Zhang, and shot him with a single arrow. The arrow hit its mark on a moving target. Zhang Dingbian, having pulled off a Zhao Zilong-like feat, withdrew. Zhu was not Cao Cao — no rule prevented shooting.

03
Fatal Error
Iron Chains Across the Lake: Chen Repeats Cao Cao's Red Cliffs Mistake

To keep his giant ships in formation, Chen Youliang linked them with iron chains — a ten-li floating fortress that became a death trap the moment fire broke out.

On day three, Chen addressed a critical problem: his warships moved at different speeds, making coordinated attacks difficult. His solution: connect them all with iron chains — the exact same catastrophic error Cao Cao made at Red Cliffs. The chained fleet stretched ten li across the lake, seemingly impregnable. But when the northeast wind finally rose on the third afternoon, Zhu's seven fire ships sailed straight into the chain. Fire spread instantly from ship to ship, the chains preventing any from escaping. Chen's entire fleet became a floating inferno — the lake ran red with blood.

04
Heroism
Ding Pulang and Chang Yuchun: Two Kinds of Courage

Ding Pulang charged into the enemy ranks to avenge his sworn brother Zhao Pusheng — his head cut off, his body still stood fighting. Chang Yuchun's one arrow saved Zhu Yuanzhang and changed history.

After Zhang Dingbian's dramatic charge, Ding Pulang charged next. As Zhao Pusheng's sworn brother, he had only one goal: revenge against Chen Youliang, who murdered Zhao. He was severely wounded in the charge — Ming history records that even after his head was severed, his body remained standing, weapon in hand, so terrifying that Chen's soldiers thought a god had descended. Meanwhile, Chang Yuchun stood calmly on his ship while others panicked, calmly identified Zhang Dingbian, and fired a single arrow that brought him down — saving Zhu from certain capture. Both forms of courage became eternal legends.

05
Tyrant's End
The Fall of Chen Youliang: 36 Days from Invincible to Dead

After the fire attack destroyed his fleet, Chen Youliang issued an execution order for all Zhu captives — driving his own soldiers to desert. While attempting escape, a stray arrow ended his life.

Chen Youliang's army collapsed after the fire attack. His response was to order all captured Zhu soldiers executed on the spot. Zhu issued the opposite order: treat Chen's prisoners well and release them. Chen's soldiers, already disillusioned, now deserted in droves. After 30 more days of futile fighting, Chen tried to break out through Hukou. As he personally stood on his ship's bow directing the retreat, a stray cold arrow pierced his skull. Zhang Dingbian retrieved his body and Chen's son and fled to Wuchang. The 36-day Battle of Poyang Lake ended in Zhu's total victory — laying the foundation for his unification of China.

🔑 Core Conclusion
The Battle of Poyang Lake is one of history's most legendary naval engagements — 200,000 men with fishing boats defeating 600,000 with iron-armored giants in just 36 days. Xu Da's wolf-pack boarding tactics opened the fight; Chang Yuchun's single arrow saved Zhu from certain death; Ding Pulang's suicidal charge embodied the highest human courage; Guo Xing's fire-ship plan — waiting for the northeast wind — was the tactical stroke of genius. Chen Youliang's iron-chain fleet repeated Cao Cao's Red Cliffs error with fatal precision. The tyrant died confused, having failed to understand that violence alone cannot command loyalty. Zhu Yuanzhang emerged from Poyang as the undisputed architect of China's future.
