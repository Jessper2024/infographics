Great State Needs Big CityCh.2 · Balance Through Agglomeration

Agglomeration · Efficiency · Balance · Free Migration · Factor Mobility The more developed a country, the higher its economic concentration — this is not a problem but a law. People commonly believe agglomeration widens regional gaps, but the opposite is true: every country that achieved regional balance did so through free labor mobility. The essence of regional economics is one sentence: Balance through agglomeration.

This chapter addresses three popular misconceptions about spatial agglomeration: (1) Is China over-agglomerated toward a few regions? No. (2) Without government controls, would population agglomerate indefinitely? No. (3) Does agglomeration widen regional gaps? On the contrary. Using cross-country comparisons and empirical evidence, the author demonstrates: more developed countries are more agglomerated; agglomeration stabilizes at a certain stage (it doesn't grow infinitely); and agglomeration ultimately leads to per capita income balance — as long as labor can move freely. The chapter's core is the essence of regional economics: achieving balance through agglomeration.

Misconception #1
"Is China Over-Agglomerated?" — Actually, China's Concentration Is Low
Cross-country comparison: the more developed a country, the higher its agglomeration. Developing countries (Ethiopia, Botswana) are at the bottom-left — low agglomeration. Developed countries (US, Japan, Europe) are at the top-right — high agglomeration. China ranks not just below developed nations, but below its developing peers (Brazil, South Africa). Why? Modern economies are dominated by industry and services, which naturally require agglomeration — services even more than industry. China's secondary and tertiary sectors account for over 90% of GDP, yet policies still limit big cities with an agricultural-era mindset — a fundamental mismatch between thinking and reality.

02
Misconception #2
"Will People Agglomerate Indefinitely?" — No, Market Prices Provide a Natural Brake
Agglomeration does not continue forever, because factor prices auto-correct. In developed countries, agglomeration rises rapidly, then stabilizes — not because of government controls, but because land prices, housing costs, and wages in central areas become so high that producers and consumers naturally recalculate "is it worth staying in the big city?" Tokyo is the classic case: Japan's total population is declining, but Tokyo's population keeps growing — that's the power of agglomeration. Market price mechanisms are far more effective "population regulators" than any administrative decree.

📈

The Dynamic Process

Rapid growth → population concentrates in core areas → land, housing, wages rise → some people/firms move out → agglomeration slows but doesn't reverse → Tokyo population continues to grow.

🏙️

Market Adjustment Mechanism

Agglomeration benefits (higher income, diverse consumption, knowledge spillover) vs. costs (high housing, congestion, pollution) jointly determine equilibrium. Factor prices (land, housing, wages) are the core adjustment variables.

03
Misconception #3
"Does Agglomeration Widen Regional Gaps?" — It Depends: Total or Per Capita?
People confuse "total output gaps" with "per capita income gaps." Making every city as large as Shanghai is meaningless and violates economic laws. What matters is minimizing the gap in real income and quality of life across regions. The World Bank's conclusion: every country that achieved regional balance did so through free labor mobility; no country that pursued balance through administrative resource allocation succeeded. Developed countries followed an "inverted-U" curve — gaps widen first, then shrink. Balance through agglomeration leads to per capita income convergence — this is the essence of regional economics.

04
Free Migration
Free Migration Is Far More Than a Right — It's the Engine of Regional Balance
Free migration is not just a basic right; it's the fundamental mechanism for narrowing urban-rural and regional gaps. Back to the matsutake story: Why can't Zhuoma roast matsutake in a city restaurant? Institutional barriers — hukou, land rights, public services — are obvious obstacles. Beyond these, ethnic minorities face language and cultural hurdles. The dual mechanism of free migration: those who move to cities see their incomes multiply; those who stay behind benefit from fewer people sharing the same local resources, so per capita income rises too. Ultimately, when real income gaps across regions shrink to zero (after accounting for cost-of-living and psychological costs), population stabilizes — this is true balance.

05
Core Mechanism
3M + 3D: The Micro-Mechanisms of Spatial Agglomeration
Why does agglomeration boost efficiency? Economics reveals three core mechanisms: Share — in agglomerated areas, firms and workers share infrastructure, supplier networks, and labor pools, lowering average costs. Match — larger markets make it easier for firms and workers to find the best counterpart, improving match quality and efficiency. Learn — talent concentration generates knowledge spillover effects; interpersonal interaction accelerates skill diffusion and innovation. These three — the "3M" effects — combined with the "3D" framework of Density, Distance, and Division, constitute a complete analytical toolkit for understanding spatial economic development.

🔑 Key Conclusion
All three popular misconceptions about spatial agglomeration are false: (1) China's agglomeration level is low internationally, not high; (2) agglomeration doesn't continue indefinitely — factor price adjustment provides a natural stabilizing mechanism; (3) agglomeration doesn't widen regional gaps — on the contrary, free labor mobility is the only route to per capita income balance across regions. The essence of regional economics is one sentence: "Balance through agglomeration" — this is not a slogan but a law of economics repeatedly validated by global experience. China's hukou system and land policies that restrict population mobility are precisely the biggest obstacles to balanced regional development.
