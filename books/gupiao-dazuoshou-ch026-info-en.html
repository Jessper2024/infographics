Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 26
"Appendix: Jesse Livermore Chronology"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Born July 1877 in Massachusetts. At 14, started as an office boy. At 15, made his first trade — a $3.12 profit on Burlington Steel. A legend was born. Livermore's life encompassed four great rises and falls: from bucket-shop prodigy to NYSE bankruptcy, from millionaire to debt-ridden, from resurgence to ultimate tragedy. What enabled his repeated successes? And what caused his final failure and self-destruction?

Jesse Livermore's life is the most legendary canvas in the world of speculation — entered the trade at 14 as an office boy, rose and fell four times, made $1 million shorting in 1907, profited $100 million in the 1929 Crash, and ended in bankruptcy and tragedy. This chronology records not just his trading milestones but serves as a mirror reflecting the fate of every speculator. Four rises came from "following principles"; four falls came from "departing from principles." His successes and failures used the same code — the only difference was whether he remained faithful to his own principles.
01
First Rise & Fall
1891–1901: From Prodigy to Bankruptcy
At 14, Livermore worked as an office boy at Paine Webber. At 15, he traded Burlington Steel for a $3.12 profit. He then accumulated $1,000 then $10,000 at bucket shops — "the first rising phase." At 21, banned from bucket shops, he took $2,500 to Wall Street, only to lose everything due to the NYSE's delayed order execution. At 23 (May 9, 1901), he went from $50,000 back to bankruptcy in a single day.
📈

First Rise (Age 15–20)

Built from $3.12 to $10,000 at bucket shops. His unmatched talent for reading tape prices made him the "boy wonder" feared by bucket shops everywhere.

📉

First Fall (Age 21–23)

NYSE's tape was so behind the actual market it killed his trading. On May 9, 1901, dropped from $50,000 to bankrupt in one day. Lesson: don't cling stubbornly to familiar tools.

02
Second & Third Rises & Falls
1901–1914: A Millionaire's Two Downfalls
After returning to bucket shops for a year, he accumulated again — second rise. In 1906, acting on a premonition before the San Francisco earthquake, he shorted for a $250,000 profit. In October 1907, during the crash, he shorted heavily and earned his first $1 million, buying a yacht. However, meeting Percy Thomas in 1908 became the pivotal turn — he violated his rules, bought cotton, added to the losing position, and sold his profitable wheat — bankruptcy again.

03
Fourth Rise & Fall
1914–1940: The Final Glory and the End
In 1914, Livermore declared bankruptcy — "Freed from the burden of debt, my mind and body are now liberated to devote themselves entirely to trading." In 1917 he repaid all debts. During the 1929 Crash he shorted for a $100 million profit, reaching the absolute peak of his wealth. Yet in 1934 he was bankrupt again. On November 28, 1940, he shot himself, leaving the note: "My life has been a failure."

Pattern Summary
The Same Code Behind Every Rise and Fall
Looking across Livermore's four rises and four falls, a clear pattern emerges. Every rise stemmed from adherence to principles; every fall began with departure from them. This comparison is not moralizing — it is the brutal truth verified by Livermore's entire life.

05
Ultimate Revelation
The Greatest Enemy Was Never the Market, But the Person in the Mirror
Livermore's entire life verified his most famous statement: "The principles that guide success in stock speculation are based on the assumption that people will continue to repeat past mistakes." His greatest tragedy was becoming the most thorough footnote to this very statement. Knowing the principles and following the principles are two entirely different things.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Livermore chronology is the most profound parable in the world of speculation: four rises came from "following principles"; four falls came from "departing from principles." Even the master who wrote down this wisdom could not consistently adhere to his own principles in the heat of battle. The ultimate lesson of this chronology is not to teach you how to trade — but to warn you: the greatest enemy of trading has never been the market, but the person in the mirror. Knowing the principles and following the principles are two entirely different things.
