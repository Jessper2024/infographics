Chapter 9 "Inevitable Decisive Battle"

Strategic Choices and the Great Victory at Longwan — Zhu Yuanzhang Overwhelms Overwhelming Odds

Situation: After defeating Zhang Shicheng, Zhu Yuanzhang focused on Chen Youliang. Under the "build walls high, store grain abundantly, delay kingship" strategy, Zhu steadily built power. Chen Youliang had seized the throne of Tianwan and commanded the strongest fleet on the Yangtze. Conflict: Chen's navy attacked Ying Tian (Nanjing), capturing Caishi and Taiping — Ying Tian was exposed. Question: How could Zhu defeat an overwhelmingly superior enemy with inferior forces and navy? Answer: Zhu sided with the minority to strike Chen first, and Liu Bowen's brilliant strategy — an ambush at Longwan — turned the tide and secured victory.

01
Strategic Wisdom
Two Decisions That Won the Empire

"Build walls high, store grain abundantly, delay kingship" and the decision to strike Chen Youliang first made Zhu Yuanzhang the ultimate victor.

While other rebel leaders hastily declared themselves emperor, Zhu quietly built his base. More remarkably, when advisors overwhelmingly favored attacking the weaker Zhang Shicheng, Zhu recognized Chen's arrogance made him the greater threat. His logic was precise: attack Chen and Zhang stays neutral; attack Zhang and Chen deploys all forces, forcing a two-front war. Truth often stands with the minority.

Strike Chen First

02
Life or Death
Devastating Assault: Chen's Iron Tide

Chen Youliang's fleet seized Caishi and Taiping at lightning speed, leaving Ying Tian defenseless and Zhu on the brink of annihilation.

Chen's warships bore fearsome names: "Mixing River Dragon," "Blocking River," "Toppling Mountain." Three-story giants equipped with cannons, nearly invincible. With only 10,000 fishing-boat warships, Zhu faced annihilation. Worse, his advisors were in panic — many had packed their belongings to flee and serve a new master. Even Zhu's strongest commanders advised retreat.

03
Key Talent
Liu Bowen: The Strategist Who Turned the Tide

Liu Bowen's arrival brought extraordinary military genius and cosmic strategy to Zhu's council, becoming the decisive factor in the Longwan ambush.

Chang Yuchun's brutal execution of 3,000 prisoners enraged Chen and invited disaster. But Zhu's greatest strength was attracting talent. Liu Bowen — recruited after Chang's victory at Chizhou — understood astronomy, geography, and military strategy. When Zhu's advisors advocated surrender or flight at the crisis of Taiping's fall, Liu's passionate insistence on fighting at Longwan rallied everyone. He embodied the turning point from despair to defiance.

04
Reversal
The Great Victory at Longwan

At Longwan, Zhu's outnumbered force destroyed Chen's advancing army with a brilliant ambush — the tide turned in a single battle.

Liu Bowen devised a lure: abandon the Jiangdong Bridge and draw Chen into the Longwan wetland. When Chen found the bridge was stone (not the wooden bridge promised by the double agent Kang Maocai), he panicked but pressed on to Longwan. Zhu's five-pronged ambush caught the entire Han army in a killing zone. With tides receding and ships grounded, Chen's forces collapsed — 20,000 dead, 7,000 captured, 100 warships seized. The victory decisively shifted the balance of power.

05
New Balance
One Battle Reshaped All China

After Longwan, Zhu and Chen entered a strategic stalemate — but Zhu now held the initiative for the first time.

Zhu seized over 100 warships from Chen, significantly upgrading his navy. The victory established his reputation as a decisive commander. Chen suffered his worst defeat since rising to power, and his aura of invincibility was shattered. The balance shifted from "Chen strong, Zhu weak" to a genuine dual-hegemony. Zhu's monk's robe poem — "Killing a million soldiers in Jiangnan, my blade still drips blood" — echoed across the land, signaling a new era was dawning.

🔑 Core Conclusion
The Longwan victory was Zhu Yuanzhang's military turning point. Facing annihilation with inferior forces, he used strategic vision (striking Chen first), human talent (Liu Bowen's genius), and unconventional tactics (ambush and fire attack) to reverse the tide. This battle proves: true strength is not the absence of fear, but the courage to face a superior enemy. True victory comes not from brute force but from reading the moment and striking unexpectedly. Zhu found the key to the throne in his darkest hour.
