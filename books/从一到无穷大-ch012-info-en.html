One Two Three … Infinity · Ch.12 "The Four-Dimensional World"

Time as the fourth dimension: from hypercube to Minkowski spacetime

This chapter establishes time as the fourth dimension of the physical world, opening a new perspective on four-dimensional spacetime. We cannot "imagine" a fourth spatial direction in our minds, but time serves perfectly as the fourth dimension — every physical object has four dimensions: length, width, height, and time. Using the speed of light to convert time into spatial units, Einstein's four-distance formula uses the sum of spatial squares minus the square of time to determine intervals between events. Minkowski transformed time into an imaginary coordinate, making the Pythagorean theorem elegantly self-consistent in four-dimensional spacetime.

Fourth Dimension
Time as the Fourth Dimension: Every Physical Entity Has Four Dimensions
The concept of a fourth dimension often invites mystery and skepticism. As 3D beings, we cannot "imagine" a 4D shape in our minds — just as we can't squeeze a 3D object into a 2D plane without distortion. But we can understand through projection: a 4D hypercube's projection into 3D space is two nested cubes connected at vertices — just as a 3D cube's projection onto a 2D plane is two nested squares. More crucially: we use the fourth dimension every day — it is time. Your house has not only length, width, and height, but also a time dimension spanning from construction to demolition.

Hyper-Geometry
Structure of the Hypercube: 16 Vertices · 32 Edges · 24 Faces
A 4D hypercube is not a mystery — it is simply an ordinary cube existing over a specific time interval. Suppose you build a cube on May 1 and dismantle it one month later. Each corner point extends along the time direction for a month, forming a "time edge." Count: 12 spatial edges at formation, 8 "time ribs" (one per vertex), 12 spatial edges at the end — total 32. Similarly, 16 vertices (8 start + 8 end). 24 faces: 6 original square faces + 18 rectangular "half-temporal" faces formed by edges extending along time.

03
Light Speed
Speed of Light: The "Translation Factor" Between Space and Time
To convert time into spatial units, a standard speed is needed as a "translation factor." The speed of light is the only speed in physics with the required universal properties — all forces (electrical, gravitational) propagate at the same speed; light speed represents the upper limit of any possible material velocity. Galileo first attempted to measure the speed of light (with two lanterns) but failed — light was too fast for mere miles. Roemer succeeded by observing Jupiter's moons eclipsing. Fizeau used a toothed-wheel apparatus: at 1000 revolutions per second, light precisely passed through the gaps between two gears.

04
4D Distance
Einstein's Four-Dimensional Distance: Sum of Space Squares Minus Time Square
How to compute the four-dimensional distance between two events? Take the 1945 NYC bank robbery and Empire State plane crash: spatial distance 3360 ft, time interval 15 min (800 billion light-feet). Einstein proposed: 4D distance = √(sum of spatial coordinate squares - time coordinate squared). The minus sign before the time square preserves the physical difference between space and time — time's irreversibility. If two events have an imaginary 4D distance, they are "timelike" (possible causal connection); if real, they are "spacelike" (no causal relation possible).
Einstein's Four-Distance Formula:
NYC events spatial distance: √(3200²+400²+936²) = 3360 ft
Time interval: 15 min = 8×10¹¹ light-feet
4D distance = √(3360² - (8×10¹¹)²) → imaginary (timelike)

Minkowski's Imaginary 4th Coordinate:
Use x⁴ = i·ct (i=√-1) as 4th coordinate
4D distance = √(x₁² + x₂² + x₃² + (i·ct)²)
= √(x₁² + x₂² + x₃² - c²t²) ← automatically includes minus sign

World Lines
World Lines and World Bands: Every Atom Has Its Own Trajectory Through Spacetime
In four-dimensional spacetime geometry, the historical trajectory of each material particle is called a "world line." Since atoms are indestructible, the decomposition of a human body after death is merely individual filaments dispersing in different directions. World lines of the Sun, Earth, and comet: the Sun's world line is a straight line parallel to the time axis; Earth's world line is a spiral around the Sun's line; a comet's world line approaches and then recedes from the Sun. From a 4D perspective, the entire universe is a bundle of interwoven world lines — the topology of the cosmos merges with history into a harmonious picture.

🔑 Key Insight
Time is the fourth dimension — every entity has four dimensions: length, width, height, and time. Using light speed as the "translation factor," spacetime becomes a unified four-dimensional geometric structure. Einstein and Minkowski showed us: the distance in four-dimensional spacetime is the square root of the sum of spatial squares minus the square of time. A real 4D distance corresponds to a spatial interval; an imaginary one to a time interval — explaining why we can never turn a ruler into a clock.
