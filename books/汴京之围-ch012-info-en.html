The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.12 "New Waves"

Emperor Emeritus Returns · Treaty Collapse · Old Generals Fall · The Feckless War Hawk Huizong returned to the capital; Cai Jing and Tong Guan were purged. Qinzong reneged on ceding the Three Towns — then repeated his father's folly with a wax-sealed secret letter to Jurchen general Yelü Yudu. Veteran general Zhong Shizhong fell at Shaxiong Ridge at 68. Yao Gu was dismissed, Zhong Shidao died of illness. Li Gang was neutered — four relief armies all failed. Northern Song's last chance slipped away.

This chapter spans February to October 1126, chronicling Northern Song's futile struggles after the Jurchen withdrawal. Though the Jurchen had left, three festering problems remained: Prince Su detained, the impossible ransom, and the Three Towns' cession. Huizong returned from the south, surrendered the main palace, and saw his favorites Cai Jing, Tong Guan, and Zhao Liangsi purged — Zhao being the most tragic victim, executed on March 27 and immortalized in the "Biographies of Treacherous Officials." Qinzong, while declaring "not an inch of ancestral land shall be given away," secretly wrote a wax-sealed letter to Jurchen general Yelü Yudu, offering him Shanxi as a hereditary fief if he'd rebel — the letter was handed intact to the Jin court. Father and son fell into the same abyss. Militarily, three veteran generals faded one by one: Zhong Shidao retired, Zhong Shizhong died fighting at Shaxiong Ridge at 68, Yao Gu was exiled. Li Gang, outmaneuvered by the peace party, was appointed theater commander with a hollow 12,000-man army — no horses, no funds — then stripped of troops. Four relief armies all failed to relieve Taiyuan. In October, Zhong Shidao died — his final counsel to move the capital to Chang'an was derided as defeatism. Northern Song's last hope was buried with its three old generals.

01
Emperor Emeritus Returns
Huizong's Return: Dignified Homecoming, Ruthless Purge of His Entourage
After the Jurchen withdrawal, Huizong returned to Bianjing — but his homecoming was a carefully designed surgery to strip away all remaining power. Huizong, in Yangzhou, had been intercepting tax revenues and blocking southeastern relief troops, threatening to split the empire. Li Gang was dispatched to "persuade" the retired emperor: surrender the main palace, move into the converted Xieqing Garden, accept his son's ritual visits — but relinquish all real authority. Once Huizong returned, the purge of his favorites reached its final stage: Cai Jing died en route to exile, Tong Guan was executed, Zhu Mian was sent home then killed. Zhao Liangsi's fate was the most poignant — executed on March 27. The architect of the Maritime Alliance could have succeeded had the Song army been competent; instead he was condemned to the "Biographies of Treacherous Officials" in the official Song History. It took nearly a millennium before historians questioned: Zhao deserved exoneration, not infamy. In the remaining months of 1126, Northern Song achieved political "unity" by purging the old regime — while squandering its most precious window to prepare for war.

02
Treaty Collapse
Three Towns Refuse Cession & the Wax-Sealed Letter: Father and Son Fall Into the Same Abyss
After the Jurchen withdrawal, Qinzong immediately reneged — the Three Towns refused to surrender, and the emperor declared "not an inch of ancestral land shall be given away." When cession envoys arrived at the towns, they were driven off by arrows and stones: the garrisons of Zhongshan and Hejian shot at Prince Su and Zhang Bangchang as they read the imperial edict. Neo-Confucian Yang Shi warned: once the Three Towns were lost, enemy cavalry could reach the capital in days. The emperor sought pretexts to claim the Jurchen had broken the treaty first (looting during withdrawal, not releasing Prince Su on time), while dispatching Zhong Shidao, Yao Gu, and Zhong Shizhong with troops. But the fatal mistake followed — Qinzong, after detaining Jurchen envoys, repeated his father's catastrophic error: he wrote a wax-sealed letter on imperial yellow silk to Jurchen general Yelü Yudu, offering him Shanxi as a hereditary fief if he'd rebel against Jin. The letter was handed intact to the Jin court. Father and son — Hui and Qin — fell into the identical abyss. The Jurchen from that point abandoned all hope in the Song and began preparing a new invasion.

03
Old Generals Fall
Zhong Shizhong Died at Shaxiong Ridge: Soldiers Fought for Silver, Scattered Without Reward
In May 1126, armchair strategist Xu Han, head of the Bureau of Military Affairs, forced Zhong Shizhong to advance light and fast to relieve Taiyuan — threatening him with the capital crime of "hesitation in battle." The old general, knowing it was a death march, marched anyway. Zhong crossed Jingxing Pass and recaptured Pingding Army, Shouyang, and Yuci — within striking distance of Taiyuan. But Yao Gu, deceived by his subordinate Jiao Anjie's false report that "Nianhan has returned from the north," halted his troops. The two-pronged attack became Zhong's lone stand. More fatally: light advance meant no supply train. Soldiers received one ladle of beans per day for three days. On May 12, the Jurchen launched their full assault — Zhong's right wing collapsed, his vanguard fled. He held the center with Divine-Arm crossbows, driving the enemy back. Then the archers demanded their reward — but the quartermaster had nothing: only a dozen silver bowls, nowhere near enough. Soldiers realized there was neither food nor wealth; continuing to fight meant death with nothing to show. They scattered. A hundred men stayed with Zhong to the end — urged to flee, he refused. He fought to the death at age 68. Yao Gu was defeated and exiled to Guangzhou. Three generals — Zhong Shidao retired, Zhong Shizhong dead, Yao Gu dismissed — Northern Song's most experienced commanders all fell to command dysfunction.

04
Li Gang Neutered
Li Gang's "War Chariot Experiment": A Civil Official's Helpless Struggle in the Military System
The peace party's Geng Nanzhong used a classic political maneuver — "agree and promote" — to push Li Gang out of the capital: appoint him Theater Commander to relieve Taiyuan. It was political exile disguised as a promotion. Li Gang knew it was a trap but had no choice. What resources did the emperor give him? 20,000 recruits — but the Jurchen had already seized all horses, so they had no mounts. A request to purchase civilian horses was blocked by Kaifeng Prefecture as "disturbing the people." A request for 1 million each in silver, silk, and cash for military expenses — the court provided 200,000 total. The 20,000 were split into five armies; 6,000 were reassigned, leaving 12,000. They departed hastily on June 25. Li Gang trained them at Heyang for ten days, then at Huaizhou experimented with war chariots designed by Zhang Xingzhong — 25 men per chariot with crossbows, spears, and shields to counter cavalry. This infuriated the emperor: "I sent you to relieve Taiyuan, and you're building chariots in the rear!" The emperor disbanded the entire recruited force. Li Gang became a commander without an army — then watched four independent columns all collapse. He was exiled to Jianchang, then further to Ningjiang (modern Fengjie, Chongqing). When the Jingkang catastrophe arrived, he was far from the stage.

05
Final Counsel
Zhong Shidao's Deathbed Memorial: Move the Capital to Chang'an — Dismissed as Defeatism
Zhong Shidao, who succeeded Li Gang, was a commander without a single soldier — arriving at Heyang, he discerned from Jurchen envoy Wang Gu's words that a new invasion was imminent. The old general immediately memorialized: move the emperor to Chang'an. Chang'an sat in the Guanzhong basin, ringed by natural fortifications the Jurchen could not breach; Bianjing without the emperor would be easier to defend, since guards wouldn't have to split their attention protecting the Son of Heaven. This counsel was denounced by the peace party as "defeatism" and rejected. In early October, the gravely ill Zhong Shidao surrendered his command — authority was hastily transferred between Liu Ge and Fan Ne. The old general passed away, receiving the emperor's final honors, spared from witnessing the dynasty's funeral. Before this, Li Gang had proposed eight defense measures: establish northern military governorships, revive local militia, breed warhorses, restore Hebei marshlands, repair fortifications, waive taxes, attract merchants, and restore Jiezhou salt supply — all shelved due to administrative paralysis. From Zhong Shidao's retirement to his death, from Li Gang's hollow command to his distant exile, Northern Song lost its last cohort of military-minded leaders.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The eight months after the Jurchen withdrawal were Northern Song's last window for self-rescue — completely wasted by serial internal dysfunction. Qinzong oscillated between "resist and break the treaty" and "appease at any cost": declaring not an inch would be surrendered while sending wax-sealed letters to enemy generals (repeating his father's folly); appointing Li Gang as commander while stripping him of troops, horses, and funds; relying on the Zhong brothers' military experience while threatening them with execution for "hesitation." When Zhong Shizhong fought to his last breath at Shaxiong Ridge, when Li Gang's chariots drew the emperor's scorn, when Zhong Shidao's deathbed counsel to move the capital was mocked — Northern Song had exhausted every shred of luck. The loss of its three old generals was not merely a military setback but the extinction of an era's accumulated wisdom. The Jingkang catastrophe was now inevitable.
