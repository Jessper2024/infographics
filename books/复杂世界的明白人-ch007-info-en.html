Understanding a Complex World · Ch.7 "Why You'll Live a Painful, Depressed Life"

Three Self-Destruct Programs: Easy Choices · The Isolation Trap · The "I'll Start When Ready" Illusion — How Many Are You Running?

Why are some people doomed to a painful, depressed life? Drawing on personal experience, the author reveals three core causes: persistently choosing easy options — from stock market fodder to lifelong retreat, easy choices bring momentary pleasure at sustained cost; staying isolated — sealing yourself off creates a downward spiral where your brain's reward system goes dormant; insisting on being "ready before starting" — waiting for perfection but never daring to act, action matters more than knowledge. This chapter is a sobering exposé of the behavioral patterns pushing you toward the abyss.

Lesson 1
Persistently Choosing Easy Options: Virtual Stock God vs. Real-World Bagholder
The author crushed simulated stock trading, nearly achieving financial freedom — then entered the real market and suffered devastating losses. A private-equity friend's explanation is razor-sharp: stock trading is a battle against yourself. The overwhelming majority always choose the easier idea and rationalize it as "more reasonable." In the stock market, "easy options" paired with "herd mentality" form the deadliest combination — entering at the peak, exiting demoralized after the crash.

Herd Trap
The Cost of "Wait and See": Easy-Mode Grinding Keeps Your XP Permanently Low
This pattern is everywhere in life: facing new things, most people choose "wait and see" — but early entry has the lowest barrier. Cheap assets are ignored; bought expensive, they make you the bagholder. When challenges arise, the instinct is to retreat — only to realize years later that life's turning points were exactly those challenges. It's like choosing easy mode in a game: grinding is simple, but XP never rises, your level stalls, bigger monsters remain out of reach, and you're completely stuck.

Lesson 2
Staying Isolated: Shut Everyone Out — It Works Remarkably Well
Stephen Chow was hilarious on screen but profoundly isolated in real life. Isolation creates a downward spiral: you seal yourself off, others don't understand you, assume you dislike them, relationships stay shallow, friends dwindle, and you grow even more isolated. Scientific evidence shows social joy inhibits pain receptors — much like ibuprofen. Isolation may leave your brain's reward regions chronically deactivated, making depression far more likely.

Lesson 3
"Ready Before Starting": Why the Intern Principle Is More Powerful Than You Think
In some elite education systems, there's a principle called "do first, think later," also known as the "Intern Principle." Four years of college teach less than a few months of internship — you dive in, learn on the job, and immediately apply what you learn. Often, if you fully grasp the difficulties in advance, most people would be scared off entirely. Many successful entrepreneurs admit: if they had known how many problems lay ahead, they would never have started.

05
Action Philosophy
Tesla's Evolutionary Logic: Produce While Iterating
Tesla operates by producing while researching the next iteration, making its EVs evolve like mammals. A decade ago, the industry dismissed EVs — battery costs were too high. But over the next ten years, sustained effort across battery manufacturers and massive R&D investment drove battery costs down nearly 90%. Now EVs are mainstream, while hydrogen is scarcely mentioned. The principle holds: start cheap with whatever idea you have — excessive advance planning yields nothing.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
A painful, depressed life has three self-destruct buttons: persistently choosing easy options — momentary joy at sustained cost, after one or two decades you're left with nothing; staying isolated — sealing off creates a downward spiral as your brain's reward system shuts down; insisting on being ready before starting — perpetually stuck in preparation, your original intent forgotten. The antidote: choose the hard but right path, maintain one or two deep social connections, and start cheap now. Action always beats knowledge.
