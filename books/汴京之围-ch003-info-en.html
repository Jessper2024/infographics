The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.2 "Northern Nomads: Decline and Rise"

The Chanyuan Treaty's Century of Peace · The Jurchen Lightning Conquest In 1004, Song and Liao signed the Treaty of Chanyuan — one of the most successful peace agreements in Chinese history. For an annual indemnity of 100,000 taels of silver and 200,000 bolts of silk — less than 1% of revenue — the Northern Song purchased a century of peace, while the Liao evolved from a nomadic confederation into a semi-settled empire. Yet a hundred years later, a Jurchen chieftain named Aguda refused to dance for the Liao emperor at a ceremonial fish banquet. His 2,500 cavalrymen would, within a decade, demolish the vast Liao Empire and ultimately turn their blades toward Bianjing.

This chapter maps the geopolitical transformation of the Song-Liao-Jin triangle. The Chanyuan Treaty established a rare example of equal international relations in the ancient world — each side sent biannual embassies, governed by an elaborate protocol system. But Sinicization eroded Liao military prowess, while the "Wild Jurchens" of the northeastern forests, led by Wanyan Aguda, rose with 2,500 cavalry, successively capturing Ningjiang Prefecture, Chuhudian, and Huanglongfu, swallowing half the Liao Empire within a few years. Internal Liao rebellions (Gao Yongchang's Bohai uprising, General Zhangnu's mutiny) accelerated the disintegration. Meanwhile, the Northern Song's war hawks — centered on the eunuch Tong Guan and the Liao defector Zhao Liangsi (born Ma Zhi) — saw a heaven-sent opportunity to "recover the Sixteen Prefectures" and were plotting a fatal gamble: a joint attack on Liao with the Jurchens.

01
Century of Peace
The Chanyuan Treaty: history's most successful peace deal — 1% of annual revenue bought 100 years of stability
The Chanyuan Treaty created an entirely new international order — Song and Liao were not lord and vassal, but fraternal equals. In winter 1004, Liao launched a massive southern invasion. Emperor Zhenzong, pressured by Chancellor Kou Zhun, personally led the army to Chanzhou. Both sides, exhausted by prolonged warfare, signed a treaty: declared brother-nations, borders unchanged, Song to pay Liao annual indemnity of 100,000 taels silver and 200,000 bolts of silk. This was less than 1% of annual revenue — far cheaper than continued war. Liao equally cherished the indemnity, causing the Song virtually no trouble for a century except during Renzong's era. This mutual long-term peace enabled Song's economic development and Liao's civilizational maturity — a rare win-win in the ancient world.

02
Elaborate Peace
Biannual embassy exchanges: an ancient diplomatic system already remarkably modern
Song-Liao diplomatic exchanges were already strikingly modern, unlike typical relations between ancient states. Each side dispatched envoys twice yearly — for the emperor's birthday and New Year. A Liao envoy's Song journey: upon entering Song territory, a reception commissioner (jieban shi) took charge; banquets at Baigou Station; gifts of tea and medicine at Beizhou; another banquet at Damingfu; at the capital outskirts, the Kaifeng prefectural judge received them; the Duting Posthouse served as guest quarters. Audience-day gifts were lavish: the chief envoy received a gold-inlaid silver crown, eight garments, 200 taels of silverware, 200 bolts of colored silk. Even the retinue (18 senior, 20 mid-rank, 85 junior attendants) received graded rewards. At departure, the Emperor poured five rounds of wine at Changchun Hall. Behind this elaborate ritual lay both sides' profound appreciation of peace.

03
Fragmented Empire
Liao's five-capital system: an empire carved by mountains, where every fragment fought alone
Liao territory was carved by the Yan, Yin, Taihang, and Greater Khingan mountain ranges into disconnected fragments, forcing a five-capital system of isolated defense. The Supreme Capital Linhuang (Khingan foothills) was the ancestral homeland, defending against northern tribes like the Mongols. The Central Capital Dading (valley hub between mountain ranges) served as communications nexus. The Eastern Capital Liaoyang (Liaodong plain) defended against Goryeo and the Jurchens. The Southern Capital Xijin (Youzhou, modern Beijing) faced the Song — also the richest grain-producing region. The Western Capital Datong (Yunzhou) faced the Western Xia. Each capital housed different bureaucracies — Youzhou managed the Finance Commission, Datong managed Transport, Dading managed Expenditure, Linhuang managed Salt & Iron, Liaoyang managed the Revenue Ministry. When a new power rose in the northeast, this fragmented system exposed a fatal vulnerability.

04
Lightning Conquest
How Aguda, starting with 2,500 cavalry, blasted through half the Liao Empire in four years
The Jurchen fighting capability caught the Liao court completely off guard — from a small tribal alliance of 2,500 men to over ten thousand after Huanglongfu, culminating in the Emperor's personal 700,000-strong expedition. At the 1112 Fish Banquet, Aguda refused to dance for Emperor Tianzuo — the Liao emperor considered executing him but was talked out of it. That was his last chance. Two years later Aguda inherited tribal leadership and used Liao's demands for gyrfalcons and disputes over fugitives as pretexts for war. First victory: Ningjiang Prefecture. Second: Chuhudian, crossing the river at dawn to rout 10,000 Liao troops. Third: Wulinpo. Binzhou, Xiangzhou, and Xianzhou surrendered. In 1115 Aguda proclaimed the Jin Dynasty. Emperor Tianzuo personally led 700,000 in a two-pronged attack — but General Zhangnu's internal mutiny doomed the campaign. Aguda seized the moment, routed the Liao army, and completed an epic reversal against overwhelming odds.

05
Crossroads
Tong Guan and Zhao Liangsi: the Northern Song chose its most dangerous gamble — ally with Jin to destroy Liao
For the Northern Song, there were only two choices: keep Liao as a neighbor, or accept the Jurchens as a neighbor. Liao was a century-old friendly neighbor with declining military strength; the Jurchens were an uncontrollable new force. But the Song court and populace had never forgotten the "shame of the Sixteen Prefectures." In 1111, the eunuch Tong Guan visited Liao as deputy envoy and met the Liao defector Ma Zhi (later renamed Zhao Liangsi) near Marco Polo Bridge. Ma Zhi keenly observed the Jurchen rise and proposed a joint Song-Jin attack to recover the lost prefectures. Emperor Huizong was delighted and granted him the imperial surname Zhao. Initially shelved when the sea route was closed, the plan revived after refugees from Liaodong drifted to Dengzhou, proving the sea route open. The Northern Song initiated secret diplomacy with the Jurchens — a fatal gamble that would reshape all of East Asia.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The century of peace represented by the Chanyuan Treaty is a rare win-win case in ancient human history — two equals building an elaborate diplomatic system, with an annual indemnity under 1% of revenue buying stability for all East Asia. But peace's hidden cost was mutual military atrophy: Liao's fighting capacity declined through Sinicization, allowing a mere 2,500 Jurchen tribesmen to blast through half the empire in four years. The Northern Song faced a fateful choice: support Liao as a buffer, or ally with Jin to destroy Liao and recover lost territory? Tragically, the hawks led by Tong Guan held power — they saw the opportunity to "recover ancestral lands" but failed to see that the next neighbor they were choosing would be ten times more ferocious than the Khitan.
