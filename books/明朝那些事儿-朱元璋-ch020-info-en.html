Ming Dynasty Those Stories · Chapter 20 "Wrongful Cases"

The Empty Seal Case and the Guo Huan Case — two shocking major cases implicating tens of thousands of officials, many honest ones among them. Zhu Yuanzhang, in the name of "stability," ruled through terror, and the shadow of the Four Great Hongwu Cases loomed over the entire empire.

📖 Chapter Overview · SCQA

Situation (S): During the Hongwu era, corruption ran rampant in officialdom. Zhu Yuanzhang enforced iron-fisted anti-graft measures. The Empty Seal Case and Guo Huan Case were two of the Four Great Hongwu Cases, each sweeping up tens of thousands of officials.

Complication (C): The Empty Seal Case: officials used pre-stamped blank documents as a practical workaround, but Zhu deemed it contempt for imperial authority. The Guo Huan Case: corruption was real but the amounts were suspiciously inflated, with implicating figures covering all six ministries.

Answer (A): Zhu's deep-rooted distrust of officials made him treat "contempt for imperial authority" as more serious than corruption itself. The Empty Seal Case punished defiance of authority; the Guo Huan Case combined anti-corruption with a broader power purge. Behind both cases lay the emperor's fundamental distrust of the entire bureaucratic class.

The Empty Seal Case — A National Wrong Arising from Workplace Convenience

The Empty Seal Case (空印案) was a nation-wide wrongful case of unprecedented scope — officials used pre-stamped blank documents as a practical necessity, but Zhu Yuanzhang transformed it into a major political case of "contempt for imperial authority."

The Ming required all provinces to send emissaries to the capital with financial accounts for annual audit. An emissary from Guangxi needed at least one to two months each way. If one number was wrong, back they went — another four months round trip. Officials discovered the key bottleneck: the seal. The seal couldn't travel, but blank documents already stamped could be carried in advance.

Pre-stamped blank documents became an open secret throughout the government — except Zhu didn't know about it. In 1376 (Hongwu Year 9), Zhu accidentally discovered the practice and flew into a rage. Officials knew the full truth but no one dared explain — fearing they would be branded accomplices. Zhu knew the truth but chose the harshest possible answer.

02
Zheng Shili's Courage — An Ordinary Man's Historic Act

When every official kept silent in fear, a common scholar named Zheng Shili stepped forward with a lengthy letter explaining the Empty Seal Case — the use of margin-fold seals, the complexity of verifying grain figures. He was not an official, had no backer, only courage.

Zheng Shili's letter became famous in history. He clearly explained: the blanks used margin-fold seals (not one-seal-per-page), and grain figures required item-by-item verification, making pre-stamped blanks operationally necessary. Remarkably, he also skillfully defended Zhu Yuanzhang — writing that the emperor's prohibition on blank seals was also motivated by fear that corrupt officials would use them to harm the people. Your Majesty also acts for the people!

Zhu's reward was to send him to hard labor. But Zheng Shili accomplished what no serving official dared — speaking the truth. Because of his brave act, this ordinary commoner (not a relative of the emperor, not a famous minister) was recorded in the History of Ming — courage is never forgotten.

The Guo Huan Case — Corruption and Suspicion Entwined

Unlike the Empty Seal Case, the Guo Huan Case (郭桓案) contained genuine corruption — but the scale was staggering and the figures riddled with doubt. A mere vice-minister's embezzlement allegedly equaled an entire year of Ming state revenue, with accomplices spanning all six ministries.

In March 1385 (Hongwu Year 18), censor Yu敏 and Ding Tingju accused Beiping Provincial Administration Commission officials of colluding with Vice Minister of Revenue Guo Huan to embezzle. The Great Announcement (大诰) detailed Guo's methods: privately seizing tax revenues from Taiping and Zhenjiang; diverting 2 million+ of 4.5 million shi of autumn grain from Western Zhejiang; inventing levies for river transport, cart transport, food, warehouse fees, and even "Buddha fees."

The doubts: Ming's annual revenue was only 24 million+ shi. Could Guo Huan really embezzle an entire year's worth? And why would a mere vice-minister need accomplices from all five other ministries — did he want witnesses? The case's last victim was the chief judge himself, Wu Yong, executed after sentencing thousands.

Fang Xiaoru's Father — An Honest Official's Wrongful Death

Fang Keqin, father of the future loyalist martyr Fang Xiaoru, was a model honest official who rarely ate meat and wore patched clothing — yet as the seal-official in the Empty Seal Case, he lost his head without knowing why.

Fang Keqin served as prefect of Jining in Shandong, governing with spotless integrity and earning deep affection from the people. In the Empty Seal Case, all seal-officials — regardless of whether they were corrupt or clean — were executed. Fang Keqin could not escape. His death is the most tragic footnote of the case: a man of unimpeachable character, killed in a case unrelated to corruption.

Many honest officials perished in the Empty Seal Case. Zhu Yuanzhang was not a madman — he knew to only execute the seal-holders, giving deputies 100 lashes and exile. Killing every official would have been stupid, and he was not stupid. Yet when he knew the truth, he chose severe punishment anyway — because to him, an official's disrespect for his authority was a graver offense than corruption itself.

05
Li Shanchang's Fate — Chasing the First Minister to the End

The Hu Weiyong Case never truly ended. Li Shanchang survived the initial purge, but Zhu's nature made survival impossible. In 1390 (Hongwu Year 23), on charges of borrowing 300 soldiers and pleading for a convicted criminal, Zhu exterminated Li's three clans — finally killing the first minister who had followed him for 36 years.

Li Shanchang asked Tang He to lend him 300 soldiers for house repairs. Tang He immediately reported this to Zhu. Three hundred soldiers could do little, but it was the first straw on the camel's back. More fatally, Li memorialized Zhu requesting leniency for his convicted associate Ding Bin. Zhu, furious, imprisoned Ding Bin and had him tortured. Ding Bin revealed Li Cunyi's role in Hu Weiyong's plot, and Li's own words — "汝等自为之" (do it yourselves) — became Zhu's most damning evidence.

Even the Imperial Astronomical Bureau got involved, reporting an unlucky celestial sign and recommending "removing a high minister" — Li Shanchang was doomed. Before the execution, Zhu met Li for the last time. One was his most trusted subordinate, the other his most sincere friend. Thirty-six years earlier, they had talked through the night, kindred spirits — Li Shanchang was 40, Zhu Yuanzhang 26. Hongwu Year 23 (1390), Li Shanchang's journey ended. The sky was beautifully blue that day.

💡 Core Conclusion

The essence of wrongful cases is never simply "Zhu Yuanzhang was deceived." In the Empty Seal Case, officials used blank documents as a practical workaround; Zhu knew the truth but chose to punish anyway — because to him, defiance of authority mattered more than justice. The Guo Huan Case contained real corruption but suspiciously inflated figures, implicating all six ministries. Li Shanchang followed Zhu for 36 years and still could not escape. The shared undercurrent of these cases is the emperor's deep-rooted distrust of the entire bureaucratic class. When imperial power sits above all institutions, truth and justice become optional accessories in the game of power. The Four Great Hongwu Cases cast their longest shadow not over the officials who died, but over the dynasty itself — for the poison of terror once released never fully returns to its bottle.
