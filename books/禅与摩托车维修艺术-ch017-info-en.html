Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance · Ch.17 'Quality Cannot Be Defined, Yet Everyone Knows It'

Chris breaks down on the mountain — interwoven with Phaedrus's classroom experiment

While climbing, Chris is exhausted and emotionally shut down. His father tells a story of encountering a massive buck deer at dawn — it looked at him, then at the gun, and 'slowly walked away with a smile.' Meanwhile, Phaedrus gives his class the assignment 'Quality in Thought and Statement,' igniting fury: 'How can we possibly know what quality is?' He doesn't give them the answer; instead he has them compare two essays and vote: 28 to 2, the better one is unanimously recognized. He writes on the blackboard: Quality cannot be defined through thinking — yet you still know what it is. Like climbing a mountain — the self-centered climber and the selfless one look the same on the outside, but their inner experience is worlds apart.

02 T 02 The Buck Story: A Smile at Gunpoint
Years ago, camping near this mountain at dawn, they heard falling rocks, then a thundering sound. Father crawled out of his sleeping bag, grabbed his pistol. In the dim light, an enormous buck appeared — antlers as wide as a man, the most dangerous animal on the mountain after the grizzly. It jumped to just three yards away and stared. Father aimed between its eyes. The buck looked at him for five seconds, then at the mother, then at the gun — and slowly walked away with a smile. Later hiking, he stepped on the same buck again — 'I think it must have been quite disgusted with us.'
"The buck looked at me for about five seconds,
then looked at your mother, then looked at me again,
then looked at the gun in my hand,
and smiled and slowly walked away."

On such a fine morning, having met us,

03 C 03 Classroom Experiment: The Quality Vote
Phaedrus assigns 'Quality in Thought and Statement.' Students are furious: 'How can we know what Quality is? You should tell us!' He replies he doesn't know either, and would very much like to find out. On the blackboard he writes: 'Quality is a characteristic of thought and statement that is recognized by a non-thinking process. Because definitions are a product of rigid, formal thinking, quality cannot be defined.' Then he adds: 'But even though Quality cannot be defined, you know what it is.'

04 T 04 Teaching Breakthrough: From Rules to Quality
Students stop asking 'What is quality?' and start asking 'How do I get it?' Phaedrus lists its dimensions: Unity, Vividness, Authority, Economy, Sensitivity, Clarity, Emphasis, Flow, Suspense, Brilliance, Precision, Proportion, Depth. Outlines and footnotes — the basics of freshman composition — are not ends in themselves but methods to enhance Quality. Soon students can judge quality on their own. One comes to his office: 'I used to really hate English. Now I spend more time on it than anything else.'

05 S 05 Self-Climbing vs Selfless Climbing: Two Different Paths
In India, Phaedrus attempted to climb the Himalayan source of the Ganges with a holy man and his disciples. By the third day, he gave up — physical strength insufficient, motivation insufficient. He realized: the other pilgrims reached the summit because they fully received the mountain's sacredness, making each step an act of devotion. The self-centered climber is like a mistuned instrument — breathing too fast or too slow, refusing to live in the here and now. Every step is laborious because the goal is always somewhere else. 'If you want to climb a mountain to prove how great you are, you almost certainly won't make it. And even if you do, it's an illusory victory.'

💡 Key Takeaway

Phaedrus proved a paradox in the classroom: Quality cannot be defined, but everyone knows what it is. The 28:2 class vote is the simplest proof. This was the beginning of 'crystallization' — a truth he stumbled upon that was extraordinary: when all rational methods fail, the non-rational works. Like climbing: the self-centered climber always seeks a distant goal, while the selfless climber lives in each step. Chris broke down on the mountain precisely because summer camp taught him to prove himself through the summit — 'any goal that seeks self-glory ends in misery.'

Source: Robert M. Pirsig, Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance
