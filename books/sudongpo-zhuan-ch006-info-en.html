The Gay Genius · Ch.6
"Father and Sons Enter the Capital"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer The three Sus traveled along the Yangtze waterway to the capital, traversing 220 miles of the magnificent and perilous Three Gorges — rapids, cliffs, mythology, and brushes with death. "My native place is already far behind, my thoughts go forward without limit." This 1,100-mile journey was not just a spatial crossing, but a spiritual initiation. How did the Gorges change Su Dongpo? They kindled his poetic genius and awakened his longing for freedom.

Traversing the Three Gorges was both a geographical passage through danger and a spiritual journey of awakening. The clouds and rain of the Wu Gorges inspired Su Dongpo's poetry and his rational inquiry, while brushes with death in the rapids made him question whether official rank and fame were worth sacrificing the freedom of civilized life. The entire journey covered over 1,100 miles — 700 by water, 400 by land — departing in October and arriving in February. The two brothers composed over one hundred poems along the way. Su Dongpo was both inspired by the magnificent scenery and drawn to a gray eagle circling freely in the sky, doubting whether fame justified sacrificing a life of freedom.

01
Departure
A Journey of Hope: Two Young Couples' Waterborne Odyssey
Father, sons, and daughters-in-law set off for the capital — this time was different from before. Literary honors had been won, official posts were almost guaranteed. They changed course to travel down the Yangtze, covering over 1,100 miles. The two young couples boarded at Jiazhou, near the colossal Buddha of Leshan, beginning their hopeful journey.
🚢

This Time: Triumph Assured

The brothers had already passed the highest exams. Both newlywed couples and the first grandson traveled together — full of confidence and bright prospects.

🐎

Last Time: Future Unknown

The three Sus had traveled overland to take the exams — crossing the Qinling Mountains via Xi'an — their fate entirely uncertain, mere aspirants in a vast empire.

03
Myth vs Reason
Clouds and Rain of Wu: Beauty and Rational Inquiry
In the Wu Gorges, peaks rose higher, cliffs drew closer, the river narrowed — you could glimpse the sun only at noon, a sliver of moonlight only when the moon was at zenith. Goddess Peak, since a poet in the 3rd century BC described it in passionate prose, became the most famous of the twelve Wu peaks.

04
Life & Death
Man-Pickle Jar and Roaring Rapids: Skirting Death
The most dangerous spots included the "Man-Pickle Jar" — a great rock occupying four-fifths of the channel, forcing boats into a sharp turn — and the "Roaring Rapids" — grotesque rocks lining both banks extending into midstream. Anyone passing through alive would treat the boatman as a second father.

05
Awakening
The Gray Eagle: Choosing Between Fame and Freedom
Su Dongpo's attention was caught by a gray eagle circling freely and carefree in the sky — he wondered whether official rank and fame were worth sacrificing the freedom of civilized life. The gray eagle became a symbol of human liberation. This was the first time on the journey that he seriously pondered the relationship between fame and freedom.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Three Gorges journey was the turning point in Su Dongpo's spiritual growth: magnificent natural landscapes kindled his poetic genius, dangerous rapids tested his views on life and death, Wu Gorge myths sparked his rational inquiry, and the gray eagle circling free in the sky awakened his longing for a life of freedom. This journey completed his transformation from a civil-service candidate to a poet — from then on, the tension between fame and freedom would run through his entire life.
