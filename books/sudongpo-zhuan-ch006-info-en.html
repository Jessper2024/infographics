Core QuestionHow did the Three Gorges journey on the way to the capital influence Su Dongpo's spiritual world?

Core AnswerThis time they took the Yangtze water route — over 1,100 miles total, 700 by water and 400 by land, departing in October and arriving the following February. The Three Gorges stretch 220 miles: Qutang Gorge with the peril of the Yanyu Reef, Wu Gorge with the marvel of the Goddess Peak, and Xiling Gorge with the danger of "Human Pickle Jar." The brothers composed over a hundred poems along the way — Su Dongpo was both inspired by the magnificent scenery and began to question the value of official rank and fame. Drawn to a gray eagle soaring freely in the distant sky, he wondered whether fame and position were worth sacrificing the freedom of civilized life.

1
Departure

A Journey of Hope: Two Young Couples' Waterborne Trek

Father, sons, and daughters-in-law set out for the capital — this time was different, with literary fame already achieved and official positions nearly assured. They took the Yangtze downstream, traveling over 1,100 miles.

They boarded a boat at Jiazhou, site of the Great Buddha of Leshan. The two young couples began a journey of hope, their hearts sensitive, earnest, and full of confidence. "My hometown already drifts far away, my will to go forward is boundless." Sichuan was China's largest province, roughly the size of Germany. It took them a month just to reach Sichuan's eastern border — where the Three Gorges lay. Su Dongpo's son, born that year — the Su family's eldest grandson — was with them.

Neither of the brothers' wives had ever left their hometown. They knew they were accompanying their Presented Scholar husbands, but they had no idea their household contained three of the era's greatest prose masters, one of whom was a great poet. Ziyou's bride came from the ancient Shi family of Sichuan. Su Dongpo's wife, the eldest in status and age, was practical, sensible, and capable.

2
Natural Wonder

The Perilous Gorges: Qutang, Wu, and Xiling

The Three Gorges stretch 220 miles with rapids flowing in and out among sheer cliffs — claiming many boats and travelers every year. A local saying went: "When Yanyu is big as a horse, Qutang cannot be descended; when Yanyu is big as an elephant, Qutang cannot be ascended."

Going upstream through the gorges was the boatmen's hardest work, with sixty or seventy men shouldering a long cable to pull a boat against the current. Going downstream was even more dangerous — boats were pushed by the rapids, relying entirely on the helmsman's supreme skill. Su Dongpo wrote: "Entering the gorge, at first there is no path; the joined mountains suddenly seem like a shrine. Winding into vastness, shrinking into deep pools. Wind passes like breathing, clouds arise like exhaling."

Travelers could only entrust everything to the gods, usually praying before entering the gorges and offering thanks after emerging. Boats too close together were dangerous, so regulations required one boat to travel half a mile ahead before the next could depart. High officials traveled with soldiers stationed at intervals holding red flags, which they waved once the preceding boat had safely passed through dangerous sections.

3
Myth and Reason

Clouds and Rain of Wu Gorge: Myth's Beauty and Reason's Inquiry

In Wu Gorge, the peaks rose higher, cliffs pressed closer, and the river narrowed — one could glimpse the sun only at noon, and a sliver of moonlight only when the moon was at its zenith. Strange rocks lined both banks, peaks forever hidden in clouds, the high wind shifting the clouds in endless transformation. "Clouds and Rain of Wu Mountain" remains a euphemism for sexual intercourse to this day.

The Goddess Peak resembled a nude female form, and since a poet of the third century BCE described it in passionate, imaginative prose, it has been the most famous of Wu Mountain's twelve peaks. The air seemed filled with immortals and spirits frolicking among the clouds. At this point, Su Dongpo's rationalism spoke up — ancient legends didn't make logical sense: "The world loves spirits and marvels, their tales startling the naive." He wrote a poem saying: "The Chu rhapsodies are but idle tales — how could immortals exist?"

But the old boatman spoke up, saying that in his youth he had climbed to the summit and bathed in the mountain pool. There were apes on the mountain, but once one reached the peak, bird calls and ape cries all vanished, leaving only stillness and mountain wind. At the Goddess Temple was a special bamboo whose soft branches swept the stone shrine clean as if worshipping the immortal clouds. Su Dongpo was deeply moved: "Immortals may indeed exist — the difficulty lies in forgetting worldly ambition." Throughout his life, he maintained an open-minded view about the possibility of encountering immortals.

4
Life and Death

Human Pickle Jar and Roaring Rapids: Brushing Against Death

There was a place called "Human Pickle Jar" — a massive rock occupying four-fifths of the river's width, turning the Yangtze into a narrow alley and forcing boats into a sharp turn. Any traveler who passed through without dying would surely treat the boatman as a second father.

The three Sus passed through "Eastern Rapids," where waves churned and the boat was tossed about like a withered leaf in a tiny whirlpool. Just when they thought the danger had passed, they encountered the even more perilous "Roaring Rapids." Grotesque rocks lined both banks and extended into the heart of the river. After emerging from Wu Gorge, they soon reached Zigui — a small town of only three or four hundred households, perched on a steep mountain slope. The residents were poor, but this place had produced two great poets: one famous man, and one historically famous woman.

Having passed only two gorges, the most dangerous stretch still lay ahead — thirty years earlier, a landslide had sent sharp rocks into the river, making it completely impassable. River transport was blocked for about twenty years before a narrow passage was finally opened, hence the name "New Rapids." The Su family was stranded here by a blizzard for three days. Su Dongpo wrote: "Neck withdrawn against the night cold like a frozen turtle — only the traveler first knows when snow arrives."

5
Spiritual Awakening

The Symbol of the Gray Eagle: Choosing Between Fame and Freedom

Su Dongpo's attention was captured by a gray eagle soaring freely and carefree in the distant sky — he wondered whether official rank and fame were worth sacrificing the freedom of civilized life. The gray eagle thus became a symbol of the human heart's liberation.

Occasionally they passed lonely little huts and saw country boys chopping firewood, standing tall between heaven and earth. The bare huts proved they owned nothing — the roofs were mere wooden planks without even tiles. Su Dongpo reflected on the toil of human life, his attention drawn to the gray eagle circling freely in the distance. This was the first time on the journey that he seriously contemplated the relationship between fame and freedom.

After passing Toad Rock, the wild beauty of nature began to fade, and the drama of angry rocks and waters drew to a close. Below Yichang, the scenery became tranquil and peaceful. The setting sun illuminated vast rice paddies, houses, and chimney smoke — travelers realized they had returned to a habitable world. By custom, they congratulated each other on surviving the raging waters. Looking back, it felt like an incredible, unbelievable dream. They disembarked at Jiangling and switched to carriages for the overland route to the capital — by this point, the two brothers had already composed a hundred poems.

💡
Key Takeaway

The Three Gorges journey marked a turning point in Su Dongpo's spiritual growth: the magnificent natural scenery awakened his poetic genius, the perilous rapids tested his view of life and death, the Wu Gorge myths provoked his rational inquiry, and the gray eagle soaring freely in the sky first stirred his longing for a free life. This journey completed the transformation from a Presented Scholar to a poet — from then on, the tension between fame and freedom would run through his entire life.
