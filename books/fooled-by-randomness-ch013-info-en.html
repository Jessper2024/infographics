Fooled by Randomness · Ch.13 "Probability and Skepticism"

Nothing is certain — only conclusions of varying degrees of probability. From ancient Greek rhetoricians to Soros's cognitive revolution.

Probability found a home in the mathematics of gambling, but its roots lie much deeper — it began as a subjective measure of belief in the hands of ancient Greek rhetoricians. Modern financial academia attempts to "scientifically" measure market risk — forgetting that nature's game has no written rules. This chapter asks: do we have the courage to embrace skepticism — to admit uncertainty, and overturn our most firmly held opinions at a moment's notice? Answer: scientists are more dangerous than science. True rationality lies not in precise calculation, but in refusing to defend your past self.

01
CLASSICAL SKEPTICISM
Carneades in Rome: Two Truths, One Location
In 155 BC, the Greek skeptic Carneades arrived in Rome and delivered a dialectical performance at the Senate that would echo through two millennia. In the same place, over two days, he persuaded the same audience of two completely opposite arguments. Day 1: justice stands at the apex of human motivation. Day 2: with equally compelling reasoning, he demolished his own argument—justice should rank at the bottom. The elderly Censor Cato was outraged and persuaded the Senate to expel the three envoys from Rome — he could not tolerate such intellectual "uncertainty."

02
HISTORICAL ROOTS
The Birth of "the Most Probable": Probability as Argument
The earliest known probability thinking emerged in 6th-century BC Sicily. The rhetorician Corax first introduced "the most probable" into legal argument — when direct evidence is lacking, decide based on the most likely scenario. Probability began as a tool of argument and judgment, not cold mathematical computation. It is inherently about "what we believe" — perfectly aligned with Taleb's definition in Chapter 3: probability concerns belief in alternative outcomes, causes, or motives.

INTELLECTUAL LIBERATION
The Right to Contradict Oneself: Proust vs. Soros
In 1968, during the French student uprising, Paris protesters scrawled: "We demand the right to contradict ourselves!" — the weight of intellectual consistency had become suffocating. Yet our culture treats changing one's mind as betrayal. Proust pilloried the Marquis de Norpois for public contradiction; Soros reversed from bearish to bullish within days—made a fortune without embarrassment. His actions are never shackled by the past: every day is a blank slate.

04
BEHAVIORAL TRAP
Path-Dependent Beliefs: Would You Keep the Painting?
Evolution has wired us to defend ideas into which we've sunk time — we treat them like our own children. Taleb proposes a simple test that exposes this path dependence: you own a painting bought at $20,000, now worth $40,000. If you didn't own it, would you buy it at the current market price? If not — you're clinging to the status quo; holding it is emotional investment. Many cling to ideas until the grave. Politicians don't switch parties based on new evidence. Academics won't say anything that undermines past achievements. Soros-types may be products of a genetic defect — and that makes them rational.

05
SOBER REFLECTION
Science Is Great, Scientists Are Dangerous: LTCM & the "Measurement" Illusion
Nobel laureates Merton and Scholes believed they could "scientifically" measure market risk. In 1998, their Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) nearly brought down the global financial system. After the crisis they denied any misunderstanding, refused to admit their method was flawed, and blamed market "vultures." Soros, by contrast, tells anyone patient enough to listen: "I am fallible." Science evolves not through scientists changing their minds, but through old ones exiting and new ones entering. So too does the market.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
Probability is a measure of belief, not a tool of computation. Embrace skepticism, admit uncertainty, refuse to defend your past self — those unshackled from path dependence are the ones who survive and profit from randomness. Science advances not because scientists admit error, but because generations of old guards exit. So too does the market. As Taleb says: "People often confuse science with scientists. Science is great, but individual scientists are dangerous. They are human and contaminated by human biases, perhaps even more so."
