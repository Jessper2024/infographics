Dongpo's Miscellany · Chapter 3「Worldly Bonds and Transcendent Thought」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Su Shi's friendships spanned the entire social spectrum — from high officials to Buddhist monks and Daoist recluses, from lifelong companions to chance acquaintances in remote villages. He wrote Zen-infused farewells to pagodas and deep-night dialogues on Buddhist scripture with wandering monks. How did a man straddling officialdom and the spiritual life find balance between engagement and withdrawal? His answer: treat everyone with a sincere heart — friends with profound affection, Buddhist and Daoist teachings with genuine inquiry, and worldly affairs with forthright honesty.

This chapter assembles Su Shi's writings on farewells, current affairs, retirement aspirations, reclusion, Buddhism, and Daoism. His circle of friendship bridged court and countryside, lay and monastic worlds. He poured genuine emotion into every relationship, and his explorations of Buddhism and Daoism never lapsed into empty talk. His revision of the Guanyin Sutra — changing its vengeful curse into "may both sides be safe" — and his playful account of eating meat while chanting sutras perfectly exemplify his way of learning from Buddhism without becoming its prisoner.

01
Human Farewells
"I climbed the high hill to watch him go" — Su Shi's Affectionate Goodbyes
Su Shi wrote six farewell pieces, each with a different tone. Bidding farewell to Wenfu Zibian, he accompanied his friend to the river, where "a light breeze and fine rain fell as the leaf-like boat drifted across the river." Su Shi "climbed the high hill at Xia'ao's tail to watch — it seemed I could see the boat reach Wuchang before I turned back." This watching-until-vanishing devotion is deeply moving. In bidding farewell to Prince Zhi, the recluse Wang Yuan traveled a thousand miles to visit Su Shi in Danzhou and "stayed seventy days before departing." Most delightful is "Farewell to the Stone Pagoda" — the pagoda speaks: "If I had no seams, how could I contain the ants and worms of this world?"
"I saw him off at the river, a light breeze and fine rain falling as the leaf-like boat drifted across the river. I climbed the high hill at Xia'ao's tail to watch him go, and it seemed I could see the boat reach Wuchang before at last I turned back."
— "Bidding Farewell to Wenfu Zibian"
02
Worldly Clarity
"Inequality has existed since ancient times — even Heaven cannot equalize it" — A Village Elder's Political Wisdom
"The Words of Old Man Tang Village" records a conversation: a seventy-year-old villager asked, "Why does the Prime Minister torment us with the Green Sprouts Loan?" When told the law aimed to equalize wealth, the old man laughed: "Inequality has existed since ancient times — even Heaven cannot equalize it. Wealth and poverty are like pottery, some areas thick, some thin. You want to grind the thick parts down to match the thin — but before the thick parts are touched, the thin parts are already worn through!" This is the plainest critique of Wang Anshi's reforms. In "Recording Anonymous Accusations," Su Shi expressed his concern about a culture of denunciation — "though the offense is light, the practice of informing must not be encouraged."
👴

Old Man Tang: Folk Wisdom

"Even Heaven cannot equalize" — one sentence punctures the idealism of the reforms. Su Shi's respect for folk wisdom shines through.

⚖️

Anonymous Accusations: Rule of Law

Su Shi agreed that informing must not be encouraged — procedural justice matters more than case-specific results, a remarkably modern legal insight.

03
Retirement Dreams
"Congratulate retirement, not promotion" — Su Shi's Clear-Eyed View of Officialdom
In "Celebrate Retirement, Not Promotion," Su Shi wrote: people congratulate promotions, but the true wisdom is to celebrate retirements. "An official, after a term of service, leaves without external slander and without internal shame. Setting down the burden and departing is like traveling far on a hot day — before reaching home, finding a cool inn, loosening one's robes and washing — already joy enough!" He witnessed Ouyang Xiu's desperate desire to resign and return to farming. Su Shi himself repeatedly planned to buy land for retirement — in Guangling, in Jingkou, in Meishan — but never succeeded. "Now I have land but cannot return — am I not breaking my promise to the river god?"

04
Buddhist Wisdom
"Contemplate Guanyin's power — may both sides be safe" — Su Shi's Practical Buddhism
Su Shi studied Buddhism without dogmatism. Reading the Platform Sutra, he used the eye as a metaphor for the "three bodies" of the Buddha: seeing is the Dharma-body, the capacity to see is the Reward-body, what is seen is the Manifestation-body — translating lofty doctrine into comprehensible analogy. Even better was his bold revision of the Guanyin Sutra. The original reads: "Curses and poisons meant to harm one's body — contemplate Guanyin's power and they shall return upon the curser." Su Shi criticized this as unworthy of Guanyin's compassion, rewriting it as: "Contemplate Guanyin's power — may both sides be safe." Rather than rebounding curses, let both parties be unharmed. He also jotted down the playful anecdote of eating meat while chanting sutras.
"Guanyin is compassion itself. When people suffer curses and contemplate Guanyin's power only to have them return upon the curser — how could this be Guanyin's heart? I now revise it: 'Contemplate Guanyin's power — may both sides be safe.'"
— "Revising the Guanyin Incantation"
05
Transcendent Friends
Yang Pu's Wife and Su Shi's Wife — Two Faces of the Reclusive Spirit
When Su Shi was arrested, his family wept as they saw him off. He defused the sorrow with a story: Yang Pu, summoned by Emperor Zhenzong, claimed he couldn't compose poetry, but his concubine had written a poem: "Today they've dragged the official away — this time they've done in the old fellow's head!" The emperor laughed and released him. Su Shi asked his family: "Can you not send me off with a poem like Yang Pu's wife did?" His family broke into laughter through their tears. He also recorded encounters with Daoist eccentrics: Li Ruozhi, who could transmit healing qi, and the Daoist at Xiangguo Temple who sold a "guaranteed gambling win formula" for a thousand gold pieces — the formula read simply: "Just stop gambling."
🏔️

Yang Pu's Reclusion: Humor as Last Defense

Summoned by the emperor, he used his concubine's poem to escape. Su Shi, arrested, used the same story to make his family laugh — in the darkest hours, humor is the spirit's last fortress.

🎲

The Daoist's Joke: Wisdom in Jest

A "guaranteed gambling win formula" sold for a fortune — opened, it read three words: "Just stop gambling." The Daoist earned a thousand gold with a joke, yet deceived no one. Wisdom is often so simple and direct.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Su Shi's bonds and transcendent reflections reveal the two faces of his soul: one face deeply affectionate toward the human world — watching a friend's boat until it vanished at the horizon — and the other sincerely inquiring into the transcendent — revising the Guanyin Sutra, studying the Platform Sutra, visiting Daoists, never blindly following dogma. His "congratulate retirement, not promotion" is the clearest appraisal of fame and power; his concern about a "culture of informing" far exceeded his era's understanding. And those eight words — "may both sides be safe" — best represent the Dongpo way of thinking: compassion should not be curse-rebound but mutual safety. He drew wisdom from Buddhism and Daoism without ever surrendering his own judgment — this is the true meaning of "learning from Buddhism without being bound by it."
