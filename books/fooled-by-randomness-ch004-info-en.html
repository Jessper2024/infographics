Fooled by Randomness · Ch.4
"Randomness, Nonsense, and the Scientific Intellectual"

When nonsense wears a scientific lab coat — why are humanities scholars always fooled by randomness?

In the 1990s, a group of physicists launched a "science war" from Vienna: they insisted all statements fall into only two categories — deductive (like 2+2=4) or inductive (empirically verifiable). Everything else is garbage. The deeper question: can humanities scholars distinguish genuine scientific thinking from elegant-sounding gibberish? Taleb answers with Monte Carlo generators, the Dada Engine, and a corporate buzzword generator — with results that are both hilarious and sobering.

01
SCIENCE WARS
The Vienna Circle: Beyond Deduction and Induction, Everything Is Rubbish
The Vienna Circle (Wittgenstein, Popper, Carnap) introduced rigor to intellectual life. Their rule: a statement must either be deductively derived from axioms, or empirically/statistically verifiable. Hegelian philosophy — garbage from a scientific standpoint, and inferior to music from an aesthetic one. This standard continues to shape philosophy and science today.
🔬

Scientific Intellectuals

Can recognize others' original work; express rigorous arguments in plain prose — like Dawkins' The Selfish Gene, containing zero equations yet reading like a translation from mathematics.

📚

Humanities Scholars

Can't distinguish scientists from eloquent non-scientists; casually reference "uncertainty principle," "Gödel's theorem," "relativity" — out of context or even backwards. A bibliography of science references deceives them completely.

02
TURING TEST REVERSED
Monte Carlo Generator: If a Computer Can Replicate You, You Lack Intelligence
A Monte Carlo generator can randomly produce a literary work that readers believe is genuine. But you can never randomly produce a scientific work. Words can be randomly assembled, but genuine scientific knowledge cannot be faked. This is the reverse of the Turing test — the ultimate method for separating pretentious windbags from true thinkers.

03
POSTMODERN HOAX
The Dada Engine: Flawless Grammar, Completely Empty Content
Monash University's Dada Engine uses recursive grammar to randomly combine words, producing grammatically perfect but utterly meaningless sentences that sound like Derrida or Paglia. Sokal published a hoax paper of pure nonsense that was accepted by a prestigious academic journal — Taleb read it on a plane and laughed so hard people stared.

"Many narratives assume that the role of the author is a visible observer. If cultural narrative holds, we must choose between the dialectical paradigm of narrative and neoconceptual Marxism."

"The premise of the neodialectical paradigm of expression suggests that, if reality is distinct from consciousness, consciousness may be used to reinforce class structures; otherwise, we can assume that language has intrinsic meaning."

—— Generated by the Dada Engine · Monash University

05
AESTHETIC TRUCE
Poetry, Religion, and Randomness: We Don't Need Rationality Everywhere
Taleb admits: when it comes to art and poetry, he's happy to be fooled by randomness. After the war, Surrealist poets played "exquisite corpse" in cafés — each person adding a word without knowing the others' contributions, colliding randomly to produce stunning verses. Religion's power lies in its aesthetic force, not scientific verification. The places we truly need rationality are markets and economics — not poetry and faith.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
If a Monte Carlo machine can produce your prose — you possess no genuine intelligence. Scientific knowledge cannot be faked, but nonsense dressed in scientific garb easily deceives humanities scholars. Yet we don't need rationality everywhere: before poetry, religion, and art, Taleb is happy to be fooled by randomness. Markets need mathematics; poetry needs randomness.
