Livermore's Trading Method · Ch.2 "Deciding Timing by Rules"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Trends have their own breathing rhythm — rising on volume, retracing on lighter volume, then accelerating. Most speculators trade every day, fixating on minor fluctuations while missing major moves. How to decide timing with objective rules rather than emotions — knowing when to wait and when to exit? Answer: distinguish normal vs. abnormal retracements; exit at danger signals as you would jump off the tracks before an oncoming train; seize only 4–5 major opportunities per year.

This chapter contains Livermore's deepest insights on "timing." He reveals a truth overlooked by countless traders: trends have an intrinsic rhythm — rising on volume, retracing on lighter volume, then accelerating to new highs. The most fatal mistake is not panic-selling during retracements, but hesitating after seeing danger signals. Livermore uses the train analogy: if an express barrels toward you at 60 mph, you jump off — you don't stand there. Once it passes, you can step back anytime. He also recounts the story of a legendary speculator living in the California mountains, who traded only two or three times a year using quotes delayed by three days — yet was far more successful than those who traded every day.

01
Trend Rhythm
The Natural Breathing of Trends: Normal Retracements & Volume
Trends have an intrinsic rhythm — rising on volume, retracing on lighter volume, then accelerating to new highs. Livermore illustrated with a stock starting at $50: Phase 1 rises to $54, normal retracement to $52½; Phase 2 accelerates to $59–60, intraday pullbacks of only 1–1.5 points; Phase 3 leaps to $68–70 with barely any retracement — the stock becomes "scarce." Volume shrinks from enormous → moderate → thin — the hallmark of a healthy trend.
🌊

✅ Normal Retracement: Trend's Breath

Rising on volume, retracing on lighter volume, moderate in scope, falling along trend lines. No need to fear — stay patient. Each retracement low should fall on the same trend line.

⚡

⚠️ Abnormal Retracement: Danger Signal

New high then 6+ pt intraday reversal, completely unexpected, never seen before in this move. Exit decisively — don't hesitate.

02
Danger Signal
Abnormal Retracements: The Market Is Flashing Red — Jump Like a Train Is Coming
Livermore described the classic danger-signal scene: the stock surges 6–10 points on extreme activity, but in the final hour, an abnormal 7–8 point break appears. The next morning it slips another point, then rallies to a firm close. But on the third day, it fails to hold its gains. His advice: "When I see a danger signal handed to me by the market, I don't argue with it. I get out!"

03
Human Weakness
Wishful Thinking & Hesitation: Your Worst Enemy Is Yourself
Livermore painted the classic psychological trap: the speculator sees a danger signal but hesitates. The market moves against him. He tells himself, "I'll sell on the next rally." When the rally finally comes, he forgets his plan because "the market looks right again." That rally is only a brief upward flicker — the market then truly declines, and he's still in, because he hesitated.

04
Time Element
Less Is More: Sitting on Your Hands Is Part of Trading
There are only a few opportunities each year — perhaps four or five — when you may permit yourself to take a position. Livermore recounted the legendary speculator in the California mountains who received quotes three days late and only phoned his broker two or three times a year. His secret: "A real movement does not end in one day. By living far from the city, I give these movements the time they need."

05
Integrated Rules
The Bystander's Dividend: Profiting from Others' Mistakes
Livermore reveals a harsh truth: while you stand aside, those other speculators who feel they must trade every single day are laying the foundation for your next venture. You will profit from their mistakes. The first position should be profitable from the very start. After that, all you need to do is stay alert for danger signals.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Trends have rhythm, and signals can be true or false. Hold patiently through normal retracements; exit decisively at abnormal danger signals — like jumping off the tracks before an oncoming train. Seize only 4–5 major opportunities per year. The rest of the time, stand aside, keep records, and let the time element work for you. Rules are more reliable than feelings. Discipline outlasts talent.
