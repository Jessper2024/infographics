🔍 In-Depth Analysis
Return to First Principles: How Did We Get Rich?
Rather than speculate about the future, ask: how did we rise from dire poverty to today? Wen Yi argues China's 40-year prosperity came from three things: bottom-up, demand-driven markets; proactive mercantilist governance; and population resources. 900 million urban residents—nearly the entire population of all developed nations combined. Each person goes out daily to earn a living, with their own small goals, individually insignificant but collectively forming a monster-class market that ignited two industrial revolutions.
How Markets Create Wealth
Markets work when each person takes responsibility for their own future and pursues happiness—under specialization and coordination, efficiency skyrockets, naturally creating enormous wealth. Markets spontaneously create larger markets—someone builds a branch outside the red ocean, investments accumulate, breakthrough opportunities emerge, technology iterates forward. As Marx observed: "capitalism is inherently outward-looking, innovative, and expansionary"—one year tops a thousand. The market is a one-way road: once you take it, there's no going back.
Market and Law: Two Sides of One Coin
Problems like inequality and runaway capital are legal issues, not market problems—all solvable through legislation. "996" and "involution" aren't worsening trends of recent years; rather, rights consciousness has awakened—people no longer accept what they once took for granted. This is humanistic progress. Law and social justice advance this way—barely perceptible at first, transformative after a decade. We can't eliminate capital because it does evil—without capital, what funds technological innovation? Without venture capital, there is no Silicon Valley.
The Value of Proactive Governance
Markets aren't naturally occurring—they're created through matching diverse demands, at enormous cost. Most countries lack the capability to host them. China has consistently provided infrastructure and basic conditions for commerce. More importantly, governance "can correct major policy mistakes through experimentation and micro-institutional innovation from the top down." After every major crisis, reflection and improvement follow—pragmatism is deeply etched in our genes. As long as China adheres to market economics and proactive governance, all troubles will pass.
Life Finds a Way
Markets and life are the same—once they appear, they continuously reshape their environment, evolving together. Kevin Kelly's logic (borrowed by Liu Cixin in The Three-Body Problem): remove all life from Earth, and it becomes just like Mars, no longer blue. Innovation requires three things, with purchasing-power middle class ranked first—because even if you build advanced tech, if nobody can afford it, companies can't recoup investments to iterate. Market scale determines innovation level. China has formed all three at scale with self-reinforcement—nearly impossible to kill.
💡 Key Takeaway

A good world is where everyone can pursue their own happiness. China rose from dire poverty through the fundamental logic of market economics—letting each person take responsibility for their future, creating enormous wealth through specialization and collaboration. Market and rule of law are two sides of one coin; proactive governance provides infrastructure and course-correction. Pandemics pass. As long as we unwaveringly stick to market economics without turning back, we will keep developing—because the market transcends individuals, with its own logic of spontaneous multiplication and world-changing power.
