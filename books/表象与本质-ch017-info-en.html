Surfaces and Essences · Translator's Afterword

Translation is hard—especially translating Hofstadter and Sander. Three translators, acting as "third authors," creatively "transposed" a book about analogy across languages and cultures—which in itself is the most vivid practice of analogical thinking.

The Translator's Afterword chronicles how three translators—Hu Hai, Chen Qi, and Liu Jian—brought the English edition Surfaces and Essences into Chinese. They followed Hofstadter's principle of "transposition": not seeking word-for-word correspondence, but deeply grasping the original's essence and creatively adapting it for Chinese readers. From "once bitten, twice shy" becoming "once bitten by a snake, fear ropes for ten years," from wine classification becoming a discussion of tea, from spaghetti becoming tofu—every localization is a living example of analogical thinking. The translation process also profoundly changed the translators themselves: they began discovering analogies everywhere in daily life. The afterword concludes with detailed translation assignments, acknowledgments, and a tribute to Hofstadter's classic GEB translation.

001
The Art of Transposition
Becoming the "Third Author"—Hofstadter's Translation Philosophy
Before beginning, Hofstadter told the translators: "You are not just translators—you must also imagine yourselves as a third author!" To him, translation is a creative act; translators are never mere parrots, but must deeply grasp the original's essence and artfully convey it in light of the target readers' cultural background. Fidelity to the original does not mean surface-level word correspondence—it means the thought expressed between the lines in the translation should match the original. Hence English proverbs often cannot be translated literally; one must find an appropriate Chinese proverb. For example, translating "once bitten, twice shy" as "one bite, doubly cautious" cannot resonate like "once bitten by a snake, fear ropes for ten years." The English saying "try to catch a falling knife"—where the "knife" is not a real knife—finds its counterpart in the Chinese "pie falling from the sky." On the surface they seem unrelated, but essentially both turn events into concrete objects: one turns misfortune into a "knife," the other turns good fortune into a "pie." This is the principle of transposition.
🔪

"catch a falling knife" → "pie from the sky"

The English idiom abstracts misfortune as a "knife"; the Chinese idiom imagines good fortune as a "pie"—superficially different, essentially the same: both use concrete objects to metaphorize abstract events.

🐍

"once bitten, twice shy" → "once bitten by a snake..."

Literal translation as "one bite, doubly cautious" cannot resonate with Chinese readers. Only by finding culturally existing proverbial equivalents can the original's essence be conveyed.

Full-book Adaptation
From Wine to Tea, from Spaghetti to Tofu—Per-Chapter Localization
Transposition is especially common in the early chapters, sometimes even involving full paragraph rewrites. The preface's zeugma was specially conceived and written by the translators for the Chinese edition. Chapter 1's "A Little Discussion about Yidianr" completely rewrote the original "Much Ado about Much" section, replacing the English category Much with the Chinese category yidianr ("a little bit"). In "Categories in Syntactic Structures" and "More Elusive Categories," every original example was replaced with Chinese examples. Chapter 2 overflows with transpositions: from compound words (dongxi, mahu) to abbreviations (haigui, haidai), to idioms (duiniu tanqin, jitong yajiang), to proverbs (steal a needle as a child, steal gold as an adult; caution keeps the ship sailing ten thousand years). Chapter 4's wine and spaghetti became tea and tofu, far more familiar to Chinese readers. Chapter 5's English slips of the tongue became Chinese slips. Chapter 6's final section on machine translation originally discussed English-French translation, but since Hofstadter was then translating Yang Jiang's We Three, it became English-Chinese translation instead. However, the translators did not replace every example—they wanted readers to still learn about American and French contexts, so terms like "hub" and "bait-and-switch" were basically directly translated.

003
Translation's Gift
When Translators Become Analogy Discoverers—How This Book Changed Its Translators
Apart from the original authors, arguably no one knows a book better than its translators—and the translators are inevitably influenced by it in subtle ways. Since beginning to translate this book, the three translators unconsciously began discovering interesting new analogies in daily life. For instance, a friend told a story about an electrical fault at her home: an electrician came and found a component had burned out. He replaced it for free, waving off payment. But then he discovered a serious problem with the circuit board. In the end, the circuit board replacement exceeded her budget. She concluded: "Money saved in one place ends up being spent in another." The translator's mind immediately leaped to the saying "When God closes a door, He opens a window." Though on the surface the electrical fault is a case of "misfortune breeding fortune," while the door-and-window proverb is about "fortune breeding misfortune," in that moment the translator involuntarily linked the two, forming an analogy. The deeper the translation went, the more analogies and associations they found everywhere in life—the best verification of the book's central thesis.

Translation Team
Three Translators, Many Helping Hands—The Team and Acknowledgments
The translation was divided among three people: Hu Hai handled the preface, introduction, and chapters 1–3; Chen Qi handled chapters 4–6; Liu Jian handled chapters 7–8 and the conclusion. Full-book proofreading was undertaken by Liu Jian and Mi Aolan (Department of East Asian Studies, Indiana University)—Mi Aolan, with her training in Chinese literature, made the translation far more fluent, reading through the entire manuscript sentence by sentence. Chapter 8's specialized physics terminology was proofread by Ye Jingqiang of UC San Diego's Physics Department. In addition, David Moser (translator of GEB, Associate Dean of Yenching Academy at Peking University) patiently discussed the translation's zeugmas and slips of the tongue, and contributed Chinese slip-of-the-tongue examples he had collected over many years. Over a dozen friends—including Cao Nan, Guo Shuyu, Huang Jiahui, and Huang Ruoze—helped find appropriate Chinese examples. Liu Jian especially thanked Jian Xue of Cheers Publishing, who "took responsibility for the entire translation process from start to finish," as well as his "wife who toiled behind the scenes" and his elder brother.

Coda
A Tribute to GEB—Translation as the Art of Analogy
The final paragraph of the Translator's Afterword is filled with warmth and reverence: "Throughout the translation process, we constantly thought of the translation journey of Professor Hofstadter's early classic Gödel, Escher, Bach: An Eternal Golden Braid. Our admiration for those predecessor translators spurred us onward, while also urging us to put our whole hearts into producing an excellent translation." The three translators hoped they had not let their predecessors down, and hoped that readers would gain deeper insight into human cognition after finishing the book. This transmission across time and works is itself an analogy: just as Hofstadter and Sander argue throughout the book, categorization and analogy-making are the same thing—and translation, the work of building bridges between different linguistic category systems, is analogy-making in its purest and most profound form.

🔑 Core Takeaway
Three translators, using "transposition" as their methodology, creatively reconstructed a book about analogy across different languages and cultures. This is not simple linguistic conversion, but a massive exercise in analogical practice: every English example had to find a counterpart with a similar cognitive structure in Chinese culture—"catch a falling knife" corresponding to "pie from the sky," wine to tea, spaghetti to tofu. The translation process also profoundly changed the translators themselves: they began discovering analogies everywhere in life, from a friend's story about an electrical fault leaping to "When God closes a door, He opens a window"—precisely verifying the book's central thesis: analogy constantly defines our thinking. As Hofstadter said, translators should not be parrots but "third authors"—the essence of translation is creative analogical mapping between different categorical systems.
