Great State Needs Big CityIntroduction · Between City & Countryside

The Matsutake Story → China's Puzzle Tibetan villagers in Shangri-La wake at 3 AM to forage matsutake mushrooms, earning 5,000 yuan in two months. Meanwhile, coastal factories face labor shortages, and over 61 million children are left behind in the countryside. Urban-rural divide, regional imbalance, and restricted mobility — China stands at the crossroads of a "great nation's dilemma."

This chapter opens with the matsutake mushroom foraging story from the documentary A Bite of China, introducing the core contradictions in China's urban-rural and regional development: Why can hardworking farmers not earn a decent income in cities? Why is there a labor shortage on the coast alongside an employment crisis inland? Why are over 61 million children left behind in rural areas? Behind these problems lie deep structural issues — restricted labor mobility, the hukou (household registration) system, and the conflict between administrative resource allocation and market forces. The book unfolds from here, asking how China, as a vast unified nation, can achieve both efficiency and balance in a single market.

Opening Story
One Kilometer for One Matsutake: The Poverty Trap of Labor-Intensive Agriculture
"Generally, you walk one kilometer to find one matsutake." Zhuoma and her mother in Yunnan's Shangri-La head into the forest at 3 AM every day during rainy season, earning 5,000 yuan over two months. Too many people, limited resources, coordination difficulties — this is the microcosm of inefficient rural production. When too many people work the same limited land, everyone works harder but nobody earns more. The only way to raise rural incomes is to reduce the agricultural population.

02
Dual Structure
Two Chinas: Coastal Boom vs. Interior Decline
Coastal cities bristle with factories and busy ports, while inland villages face hollowing, aging populations, and left-behind children. Destination cities complain of overcrowding and pollution, blaming "too many people." Source regions like the Northeast, Anhui, and Sichuan worry about "economic emergencies" and elderly left alone. Few realize that the root cause may lie thousands of miles away — institutional barriers that block labor mobility and prevent market forces from operating properly.
🏭

Coastal Cities (Inflow Areas)

Factories can't find workers, wages rising, subway overcrowding, smog, soaring housing prices. Local governments raise barriers to reduce migrant children's access to schools.

🌾

Inland Countryside (Outflow Areas)

61M left-behind children, rural aging and hollowing, elderly living alone, agricultural inefficiency. Northeast shifts from population inflow to outflow; provinces like Anhui and Sichuan see continuous net outflows.

03
Core Thesis
A Book's Mission: A Conversation with Ourselves
"In discussions of public policy, many don't realize that the very obstacle to improving social welfare is ourselves." Author Lu Ming poses the book's central question: As a unified great nation, how can China achieve both efficiency and balance through free labor mobility? Why do all countries' economies concentrate in a few areas, while China tries to allocate resources evenly through administrative means? The book is divided into two parts — Part I "The Great Nation's Puzzle" discusses the Europeanization of China's economy, agglomeration and balance, the impossible triangle; Part II "The Great City Solution" focuses on why big cities survive, the urbanization debate, and tackling urban diseases.

04
Global Perspective
The World Is Not Flat: Global Economic Concentration
The World Bank's 2009 World Development Report notes that half of global economic activity occurs on just 1.5% of land. Satellite nightlight images show bright areas where economies thrive — this is no coincidence but economic logic. The more developed a country, the higher its economic concentration. The US economy concentrates on the East and West Coasts; Japan's three major metropolitan areas hold over 50% of its population. China's per capita GDP gap exists precisely because population agglomeration is insufficient, not excessive.

🇨🇳

China's Unique Position

Compared with developed nations (US, Japan, Europe) China's population concentration is lower; compared with developing peers (Brazil, South Africa), it's also low. China's secondary and tertiary industries account for over 90% of GDP, yet policies still reflect an agricultural-era mindset restricting big cities. East of the Hu Line: 43% of land holds 93% of the population and 95% of GDP.

05
Starting Point
"The More You Stretch History, the More You Believe in Universal Laws"
"What determines the trajectory of human development are universal laws, and each country's distinctive features only blossom under these laws." This is the book's methodological foundation. China's path has unique characteristics, but it will not escape universal economic laws — agglomeration, population mobility, urbanization, industrial upgrading. Part I approaches from four angles: the lesson of European monetary union, the dialectic of spatial agglomeration and balance, the great nation's "impossible triangle," and the distorting hand of administrative intervention. Each chapter pursues the same question: How can China work with economic laws rather than against them, achieving sustainable development as a unified great nation?

🔑 Key Conclusion
China faces a fundamental dilemma: labor shortages and rising wages on the coast coexist with left-behind children and rural hollowing inland. This is not a problem of economic development itself, but of institutional barriers that block free labor mobility and prevent market forces from working properly. The solution lies not in allocating resources evenly through administrative means, but in following economic laws — letting labor flow freely to the most productive locations, achieving balance through agglomeration. This is the book's theoretical starting point and core argument.
