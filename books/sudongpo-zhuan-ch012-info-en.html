Su Dongpo Biography · Ch. 12 "Paradise on Earth"

1071–1074, Hangzhou Vice-Governor — transforming bitterness into beauty through poetry, reconciling contradictions with humor

During his three years in Hangzhou, Su Dongpo penned the immortal line comparing West Lake to the beauty Xi Shi. He befriended monks and sing-song girls alike, finding wisdom and joy in equal measure. Hangzhou won his heart, and he won Hangzhou's eternal love. Yet the city was not all lotus blossoms — seventeen thousand prisoners awaited trial, locusts and famine tested the vice-governor's resolve. Amid beauty and hardship, Dongpo transcended political bitterness through poetry and humor.

01
Immortal Verse
A Single Line That Defined West Lake Forever
"The shimmering water brightens in fair sunshine; the misty mountains amaze in rain. I would liken West Lake to the Beauty Xi Shi — gorgeous whether in light or heavy makeup." Rain or shine, West Lake held intoxicating charm. Su Dongpo beautified the lake with perfect artistic touch — Su Causeway, Three Pools Mirroring the Moon, willows lining the banks remain his legacy today.
🏞️

Nature's Gift

West Lake stretches ten miles around, surrounded by 360 temples. From Phoenix Hill, one sees river sails, lake mist, temples, and villas in a single panorama.

✍️

Poet's Touch

"I would liken West Lake to the Beauty Xi Shi." With just seven characters, Dongpo engraved West Lake into eternity — West Lake became Xi Shi, Xi Shi became West Lake.

02
City of Delights
Hangzhou as Wonderland: Night Markets Thriving at 2 AM
Hangzhou was truly "paradise on earth" — and Su Dongpo immersed himself fully in its quotidian joys. From his official residence atop Phoenix Hill, he could survey the Qiantang River's sails, West Lake's mist, and countless temples and villas. Night markets hummed until 2 AM, candy vendors blew sugar into animal shapes, boatmen caught fish to sell to tourists who set them free as acts of merit.

03
Monks & Courtesans
Senses and Spirit: Where Monks and Sing-Song Girls Coexist
In Su Dongpo's eyes, the life of the senses and the life of the spirit were one and the same — monks and courtesans, wisdom and joy, ran parallel. He once brought a singing girl into the temple of Abbot Datong and had her sing an apology — even the stern old abbot burst out laughing. With the wild monk Foyin he traded Zen barbs: "Who does Guanyin worship?" "She who seeks outside herself finds nothing."

04
A Wise Wife
Madame Su's Wisdom: Trust Is Stronger Than Control
Madame Su guarded her husband in the wisest possible way — with complete trust, never driving him into the arms of a courtesan. She knew her husband was a man neither wife nor emperor could restrain. She wrote no poetry, sang no songs — she simply cooked his favorite Meizhou dishes and ginger soup. When he grew frustrated and despondent, she said: "Come, let me pour you some wine."

05
Child of Nature
The Poet Is Nature's Child: Napping Naked in a Bamboo Grove
Su Dongpo was a great philosopher who was no puritan, a Confucian who was no drunkard — he possessed a "uniquely healthy mystical outlook." He often wandered alone in the hills, composing verse at secluded peaks. He would borrow a reclining chair from a temple, carry it into a bamboo grove, strip off his clothes, and nap naked. Young monks peeped with awe, seeing — or imagining — seven black moles on his back arranged like the Big Dipper.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Paradise on earth lies not in landscape but in attitude. Su Dongpo's three years in Hangzhou proved: true freedom is finding one's own sphere of fullest expression within an unchangeable environment — through poetry, through beauty, through humor. He likened West Lake to Xi Shi, placed monks and courtesans in the same poem, transformed a wife's trust into an inexhaustible warmth. Paradise was not in the distance — it was in every "half-drunk" homecoming, every bamboo-grove afternoon nap, every verse that sprang spontaneously from his lips.
