Sensible in a Complex World · Ch.34 "What Japan and Korea's Population Collapse Tells Us About Birth Rates"

Japan and Korea are the advance scouts already in deep water — industrialization + Confucian culture + consumerism: how this triple contraceptive affects China's future

To some extent, Japan and Korea are the advance wave running ahead of us: culturally similar, identical postwar development paths — OEM → foreign trade → tech. Japan's 65+ population is 29%, birth rate ~0.7%, deaths outpace births every year; South Korea's total fertility rate is 0.84, population decline began in 2021. Three root causes: education delays childbearing, economic pressure crushes quality of life, and the internet makes "relative deprivation" devour hope. East Asian culture's unique "striving hangover" afflicts the new generation with a "fear bundle" — afraid to strive, afraid to support kids, afraid to take responsibility. A moderate population decline isn't necessarily bad, but we must guard against structural imbalance: when the elderly ratio exceeds 30%, the pension burden becomes crushing.

01
Current Reality
Japan and Korea's birth rates have reached alarming territory — a gerontocracy
Japan and Korea's today may well be our tomorrow. Japan's 65+ population is 29%, deaths exceed births every year. South Korea's TFR is 0.84, entering negative population growth in 2021. The consequence is a pension crisis — your pension feeds your parents, your kids' contributions are supposed to feed you. If society has too few children, the elderly have to go back to work. Every aging country eventually raises the retirement age — perhaps until retirement simply doesn't exist. Save for retirement yourself? Money saved when young may be nothing by the time you're old — just like 5,000 yuan felt like a fortune decades ago, 1 million yuan today may feel like 1 million yen when you're old.

02
Three Causes
Why industrialization is inherently contraceptive — education · economy · psychology
The "worldwide same-chill" problem of industrialization suppresses the desire to have children from three dimensions. ① Education delay: women delay childbearing from their early teens to their twenties or even thirties — the later the decision, the harder it gets. ② Economic pressure: raising a child = a massive investment, drastically squeezing the couple's quality of life — especially in East Asia, where spending on kids knows no bounds. ③ Psychological collapse: the internet exposes wealth gaps in raw brutality — "a lifetime of striving won't catch someone else's starting line," "free-to-play users are just props for the paying players" — triggering an urge to "delete your account and uninstall the game." Hegang's housing prices fell to the floor without drawing a population surge — proving housing prices are just one part of the "despair" equation.

03
East Asian Twist
Confucianism's "striving hangover" — the fear bundle
East Asia's endurance, diligence, and restraint made the three nations the only latecomers to become powerful — but not a single one avoided over-exerting during the sprint phase. Under Inamori, people worked 18 hours a day — doing nothing but eating, sleeping, and working. These workaholics became managers and relentlessly boast about the old days, leaving the young not inspired but terrified — "you all worked that hard, and still ended up in the great stagnation." Striving needs motivation: fear of poverty (which the young generation never experienced) and longing for a better life (which the young don't aspire to). The result is a "fear bundle": fear striving → fear raising kids → fear parenthood → fear competition.

04
Pro-Natalist Rhetoric
Why "just have kids" doesn't work — the final form of consumerism
"Not having children" is itself part of consumerism — trading saving and investment for personal ease. Once this idea takes root, it's largely irreversible. Hollow slogans without real benefits have little effect on fertility rates. Japan and Korea's cruelest dilemma: either open immigration and watch the nation change color, or slowly fade away. Germany's example: heaviest tax on singles, next heaviest on childless couples, massive subsidies for families with kids — still couldn't stop the decline, had to import population from Turkey.

05
Way Out
A moderate population decline isn't bad — China's advantages and path forward
Many of China's problems are simply too many people chasing too few resources — trimming down a bit isn't a bad thing — just don't go too fast. China has vast land, a huge population base, and relatively more time. Southeast Asia has over 20 million ethnic Chinese with strong fertility. What we must guard against is structural imbalance: when the elderly ratio exceeds 30%, the pension pressure is enormous. The path: ① Don't attack people who want to have children; ② Give massive policy advantages to childbearing women (extended leave, longer breastfeeding periods); ③ Give tax and loan incentives to companies hiring women of childbearing age; ④ Through subsidies, effectively make childless households bear more of the social cost.
"If others don't want kids, don't point fingers. But if someone wants to have kids, there's even less reason to attack them. Chances are, going forward, massive preferential policies will be offered to childbearing women — if having a baby means losing your job, who would still want to?"
🔑 Key Takeaway
Industrialization + education delay + economic pressure + internet-driven psychological collapse = irreversible fertility decline. East Asia's "striving hangover" saddles the young with a "fear bundle." A moderate population decline isn't bad — but don't go too fast. China's advantages: a large base, vast territory, and ample time. The key: don't attack those who have kids, give policy advantages to childbearing women, tax incentives for companies hiring women of childbearing age. Ultimately, like all developed nations, China will head down the path of pro-natalist policy.
