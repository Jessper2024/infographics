One Two Three … Infinity · Ch.5「1961 Author's Preface」

Thirteen Years of Scientific Progress: From the H-Bomb to Virus Synthesis, from the Galaxy to Palomar

Thirteen years after the first edition (1948), George Gamow wrote a new preface for the 1961 edition, cataloging the major scientific advances in the interim. Thermonuclear energy had become reality; the estimated age of the universe had expanded from 2–3 billion to over 5 billion years; the 200-inch Hale telescope pushed human vision deeper into space; and biochemistry had taken the first steps from lifeless matter to synthetic life. With characteristic humor, the physicist also addressed the flood of letters asking about his son's cowboy dream.

01
Book Background
A Long-Lived Classic: From 1948 to 1961
First published in 1948 and revised in 1961, "One Two Three … Infinity" was fortunate: it appeared right after several major scientific advances, so only light revisions were needed. The original was dedicated "To my son Igor, who wanted to be a cowboy" — a quip that drew so many reader letters asking whether Igor actually became a cowboy that Gamow answered it in the new preface.

02
Nuclear Breakthrough
Thermonuclear Reaction: From Theory to the H-Bomb
The most important development in 13 years was the successful demonstration of thermonuclear reactions — the hydrogen bomb. Even more promising, controlled thermonuclear processes (bringing the Sun's power source to Earth) were progressing slowly but steadily. Gamow's original Chapter 11 described thermonuclear principles and their astrophysical applications; the new edition only needed additions at the end of Chapter 7.

The Universe Grows Up: Age Doubled, Vision Expanded

04
Life's Miracle
From Chemical Elements to Life: The Virus Synthesis Milestone
In the first edition Gamow wrote: "Perhaps in the not-too-distant future, some talented biochemist will be able to synthesize a virus molecule from ordinary chemical elements — and can announce: 'I have just endowed a nonliving piece of matter with life!'" Within a few years, this was accomplished in California. The new edition added material on simple biological syntheses at the end of Chapter 9, along with updated illustrations.

05
Preface Anecdote
A Scientist's Humor: His Son's "Cowboy Dream"
The first edition's dedication read: "To my son Igor, who wanted to be a cowboy." This line prompted so many reader letters over 13 years that Gamow addressed it in the new preface: No, Igor did not become a cowboy. He majored in biology, was graduating in the summer of 1961, and planned to work in genetics. This human touch illustrates Gamow's unique approach to popular science — delivering profound insights wrapped in stories and warmth.
Gamow's Signature Humor:
"The first edition of this book was dedicated 'To my son Igor,
who wanted to be a cowboy.' Many readers have written to me asking
whether he actually became a cowboy. The answer is no — he majored
in biology, will graduate this summer, and plans to work in genetics."
— George Gamow, University of Colorado, November 1960
06
Scope of Coverage
From Atoms to Galaxies: Science in One Book
The unique charm of "One Two Three … Infinity" lies in unifying mathematics, physics, astronomy, and biology within a single narrative. The 1961 revisions touched every frontier: nuclear physics (H-bomb), astrophysics (universe age and scale), biochemistry (virus synthesis), and the underlying mathematical foundations (large numbers and infinity). Gamow demonstrated the power of a "polymath" approach — letting readers see the whole landscape of science in one volume.

07
Revision Strategy
Minimal Changes: How to Keep a Science Book "Young"
Gamow followed a "minimal change" principle: core framework unchanged, only incremental updates in fast-moving fields. He explicitly wrote that science books date quickly, but this one was fortunate to have appeared right after major advances — requiring only "slight changes and additions." This preserved the original's integrity and coherence while reducing readers' cognitive cost for the new edition.

Scientific Prediction
Seeing the Future: Prophecies in the First Edition
Gamow made several prescient predictions in the first edition. Most striking was his forecast of virus synthesis — when most scientists considered it a distant dream. Another was his anticipation of controlled thermonuclear progress. These predictions demonstrate Gamow's deep scientific insight and show that great popular science not only transmits known knowledge but sketches the future landscape of discovery.

09
Science Communication
Gamow's Philosophy: Science With a Human Face
The 1961 preface is a key to understanding Gamow's approach to popular science. He candidly admits: "All science books… soon become obsolete" — humility itself is part of the charm. He turns scientific advances into vivid stories: the universe's age "increased from two to three billion to five or more billion years"; virus synthesis is "I have just endowed a nonliving piece of matter with life!" He even closes with his son's career choice, blending the personal with the public.

10
Historical Context
Science in 1960: The Horizon When Gamow Wrote
When Gamow wrote at the University of Colorado in November 1960, the world was at a high point in science history. The space age had just begun (Sputnik, 1957), particle physics was booming, and molecular biology was emerging. Gamow himself had made pioneering contributions to cracking the genetic code (the "triplet" hypothesis). The 1961 edition of "One Two Three … Infinity" is more than a reprint — it's a precious snapshot of mid-20th century science.

🔑 Core Takeaways
The 1961 preface is both a scientific progress report and a manifesto for great popular science. Using 13 years as his measuring rod, Gamow charts the leaps in nuclear physics, astrophysics, and biochemistry. With humble "slight changes," he sketches a rapidly transforming scientific world. And through his son's story, he reminds us: science is deeply human — encompassing both the most abstract mathematical theories and a father's hopes for his child's future.
