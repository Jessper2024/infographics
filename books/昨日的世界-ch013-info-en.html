War was buried, but peace was not yet real peace Zweig emerges from Salzburg isolation, revisits Italy, witnesses Fascist violence firsthand; sees Rathenau assassinated and Germany consumed by hyperinflation; reaches literary fame in the golden decade—only to find it all fragile.

This chapter covers the dramatic shifts from 1919 to 1933. Zweig steps out of postwar isolation to discover that genuine goodwill among ordinary people was never truly touched by wartime hatred propaganda. In Venice, he witnesses Fascist squads in action for the first time—organized, trained, terrifying. After Rathenau's assassination, Germany plunges into the madness of hyperinflation where all morality collapses. During the golden decade of 1924–1933, Zweig reaches his literary peak—250,000 copies of Star-Hour, most-translated author globally—yet remains清醒 about the fragility of it all.

01
Reunion
Crossing Borders: Hatred Never Touched Real People
In 1919, Zweig braved crossing into Italy. The Verona porter saw "Austria" and exclaimed: "How glad I am to see you! An Austrian at last!" All wartime agitation and hatred only overheated minds briefly, never touching Europe's true masses. In Milan, old friend Borghese embraced him within five minutes; in Florence, painter Striniga rushed to hug him. Everything felt more sincere than before the war—but the war was not truly over.

02
Fascist Violence
Venice Square: First Eyewitness of Fascist Squads
During a general strike in Venice, a squad of young men marched through the piazza in disciplined formation, wielding clubs to attack striking workers—Zweig's first glimpse of Fascism's true face. This was no rabble but a superbly organized force. From then on, he could no longer agree with his dismissive Florentine friends. Europe's surface was calm, but dangerous currents lurked beneath—peace was not yet real peace.

03
Rathenau's Murder
From Rapallo to the Gutter: Rathenau's Death and Inflation Madness
Rathenau achieved brilliant results at Rapallo, then was assassinated on a Berlin street—"With this tragedy, Germany's misfortune and Europe's misfortune began." The Mark plummeted: 50,000 for a morning paper, 100,000 by evening; a shoelace cost what once bought a shoe shop; beggars discarded 100,000-Mark notes in the gutter. Stinnes devoured a quarter of Germany's wealth through devaluation. All values shattered; Berlin became the world's cesspool of depravity.
"Nothing could make the German people so full of hatred and murderous intent as inflation did—a lesson we must heed forever."

Golden Decade Fame: Most-Translated, Most-Compressed Writer
Zweig's writing secret was extreme aversion to verbosity and relentless pursuit of compression—"If of a thousand pages, eight hundred go into the wastebasket leaving two hundred精选, I would not complain." American publisher Benjamin Huebsch secured rights to all his writings. Zweig became the world's most-translated author.

Fame and Freedom
Name vs. Self: A Bestselling Author Who Craved Anonymity
Zweig remained清醒 about fame—"For anyone to become famous in any way means their natural equilibrium has been disrupted." He never sought the spotlight, always sat in the back row, loathed having his photo published.
