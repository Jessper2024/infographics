Surfaces and Essences · Further Reading

Four classic works of cognitive science, twenty-three book-wide annotations—the intellectual resources and knowledge network of this book, laid out in full view. From Dennett's thinking tools to Pinker's essence of thought, this is a cognitive-science intellectual tour.

The Further Reading section recommends four classic works of cognitive science closely related to this book's themes. Daniel Dennett's Intuition Pumps and Other Tools for Thinking provides a set of practical thinking tools; Steven Pinker's The Stuff of Thought offers a profound exploration of the relationship between language and thought; How the Mind Works answers the fundamental question "What is intelligence?"; and the Edge volume Thinking brings together insights from the world's greatest minds on thinking, problem-solving, and decision-making. Additionally, this section includes twenty-three annotations spanning the entire book—translator's notes, editor's notes, and author's notes—that provide indispensable supplements for understanding the cross-cultural analogies, linguistic nuances, and academic background in the text.

Thinking Tools
Intuition Pumps and Other Tools for Thinking—Dennett's Thinking Toolkit
World-renowned philosophical titan Daniel Dennett, bridging computer science, psychology, neuroscience, linguistics, and artificial intelligence, shares all the useful thinking tools he has collected throughout his life. Born in the university freshman classroom, this book strives to be "accessible to everyone." It employs a wealth of convenient, auxiliary thinking tools to expand imagination and sustain focus, allowing us to think properly and elegantly about genuinely difficult problems. Using these thinking tools to clear away the fog of various ideas, you will discover that so many "obvious" views are not "obvious" at all. This book of "intuition pumps" forms an excellent complement to Surfaces and Essences—one teaches how not to be deceived by surfaces, the other reveals the analogical relationship between surfaces and essences.

Essence of Thought
The Stuff of Thought & How the Mind Works—Pinker's Exploration of Language and Mind
Steven Pinker's The Stuff of Thought is a window into the human mind, a profound exposition on the relationship between language and thought. Through convincing, vivid, and engaging examples, the book embarks on a journey exploring the essence of thought. And his How the Mind Works goes a step further, answering the profound question "What is intelligence?" and tackling the robot challenge. The book analyzes the four major faculties of the mind in detail, deciphering "how the mind works"—a window into the wonder and mystery of human mental activity, a fantastical journey exploring the essence of mind. Together with Surfaces and Essences, these two works form a trilogy about "how the mind understands the world through categorization and analogy." Pinker focuses on the macroscopic landscape of language and mind, while Hofstadter and Sander place analogy at the very core of cognition.

Idea Platform
Thinking—Edge Series: The World's Greatest Minds on Cognition
Edge, the world's smartest website, once a year brings together 100 of the world's greatest minds around the same table to collectively answer the same big question concerning human destiny—launching an intellectual expedition, a journey of thought! As part of the Edge series, Thinking focuses on various problems of thought, problem-solving, and decision-making, as well as topics of popular interest such as theory of mind, morality, evolution, and prediction. This kind of polyphonic collision of ideas is itself an interdisciplinary analogical practice—bringing together physicists, biologists, philosophers, and computer scientists to understand the same question through the categories of their own fields, perfectly demonstrating how analogy serves as the "universal currency" of human cognition.

Book Annotations
Twenty-Three Annotations—The Knowledge Network Behind the Text
The book's annotations comprise twenty-three entries spanning from the introduction to the conclusion. They include translator's notes—such as "In French, the root of 'happiness' is heur," "In English, both moon and satellite are moon," "In English, division means both splitting and the mathematical operation"—helping readers understand linguistic-level analogies; editor's notes—such as "Achilles' heel," "the sword of Damocles," "from Wang Guowei's Renjian Cihua"—supplementing cultural background; as well as original author's notes and Cheers Publishing notes. These annotations are not dispensable appendages but keys to understanding the book's theme of "cross-linguistic analogical thinking." For instance, annotation (22) reminds readers that "division" means both splitting and the mathematical operation in English—without understanding this, one cannot fully grasp the discussion of how naïve analogies shape mathematical understanding.
