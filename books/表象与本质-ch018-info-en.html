Surfaces and Essences · Further Reading

Four classic works of cognitive science, twenty-three book-wide annotations—the intellectual resources and knowledge network of this book, laid out in full view. From Dennett's thinking tools to Pinker's essence of thought, this is a cognitive-science intellectual tour.

The Further Reading section recommends four classic works of cognitive science closely related to this book's themes. Daniel Dennett's Intuition Pumps and Other Tools for Thinking provides a set of practical thinking tools; Steven Pinker's The Stuff of Thought offers a profound exploration of the relationship between language and thought; How the Mind Works answers the fundamental question "What is intelligence?"; and the Edge volume Thinking brings together insights from the world's greatest minds on thinking, problem-solving, and decision-making. Additionally, this section includes twenty-three annotations spanning the entire book—translator's notes, editor's notes, and author's notes—that provide indispensable supplements for understanding the cross-cultural analogies, linguistic nuances, and academic background in the text.

Division of Labor
Three Translators, Three Voices — The Artful Division by Chapter
The translators' division of labor was carefully designed: Hu Hai took the preface, introduction, and chapters 1-3 — these lay the book's theoretical foundation and contain abundant linguistic/cultural examples, requiring deep English proficiency and fine control of Chinese expression. Chen Qi took chapters 4-6 — these delve into analogy's specific mechanisms, involving cognitive science experiments and philosophical discussions, requiring relevant domain expertise. Liu Jian took chapters 7-8 and the conclusion — covering naive analogies, analogies in mathematics and physics, and the book's final synthesis, requiring interdisciplinary vision. Proofreading was shared by Liu Jian and Mi Aolan — “without her meticulous proofreading and polishing, this translation would have been greatly diminished.” Chapter 8's physics terminology review was done by Ye Jingqiang — “making the translation more accurate.” This division reflects an ultimate pursuit of translation quality: each person deepens their strongest domain, then cross-proofreading achieves overall unity.

002
David Moser
David Moser's Unique Contribution — Treasury of Chinese Speech Errors and Co-Creation of Zeugmas
The translators first thank David Moser — the English translator of Gödel, Escher, Bach with over 40 years of friendship with Hofstadter, who played a vital role in this Chinese translation's birth. “He patiently discussed the translation's zeugmas and speech errors with us, providing many brilliant examples.” Moser's years of collecting Chinese speech errors form a treasury — when translating chapter 5 (“How Analogies Manipulate Us”) and chapter 6 (“How We Manipulate Analogies”), these Chinese speech error materials allowed translators to fully transplant the original's speech error analysis into a Chinese context. His examples “benefited us immensely.” Moser's dual identity — both a senior translator of Hofstadter's works and a researcher of Chinese speech errors — makes him the perfect bridge connecting the English original and Chinese translation.

Example Contributors
Finding the Right Chinese Examples — The Generous Help of Eleven Friends
The first six chapters' many examples involving language and culture needed to be replaced with suitable Chinese examples. Finding these was not done by translators alone — “in searching for examples, many friends helped us.” The afterword lists eleven friends: Cao Nan, Guo Shuyu, Huang Jiahui, Huang Ruoze, Miao Lin, Qin Xuqiang, Wu Yuyan, Xi Ruiwen, Zhang Tian, Zhu Mengze, Zhu Zhiqiang — “we express our sincere thanks to them here.” This list reflects translation's social nature: a good translation is not just the product of individual ability, but the result of a knowledge network working together. From compounds (dongxi, mahu) to abbreviations (haigui, haidai), from idioms (duiniu tanqin, jitong yajiang) to proverbs — behind every “just-right” Chinese example, there may be a flash of inspiration from one of these eleven friends.

Editor & Family
The Editor's Professional Guardianship and Family's Warm Support — Unsung Heroes Behind a Book
Liu Jian particularly mentions three key figures. Cheers Publishing editor Jian Xue — “he took responsibility for the translation process from beginning to end, doing his utmost to answer every question we raised” — as the bridge between translators and publisher, his work is often invisible to readers, yet is the institutional guarantee enabling the translation's smooth publication. Liu Jian's elder brother — “helped unstintingly throughout the translation process, answering questions and resolving doubts” — a form of familial support transcending work relationships. Liu Jian's wife — “she read the entire book and offered many valuable revision suggestions” — as a “first reader,” her feedback represents ordinary readers' genuine reactions, an irreplaceable calibration reference for translators. “We must also thank our families, who were this translation's first readers and tirelessly pointed out its shortcomings” — the role of family as first readers is a warm recurring theme in many scholarly works and translations.

005
Tribute to Predecessors
To Predecessor Translators and to Readers — “We Hope We Did Not Let Them Down”
Throughout the translation process, the three translators constantly thought of the translation journey of Hofstadter's earlier classic Gödel, Escher, Bach (GEB) — “our admiration for our predecessor translators urged us forward, while also spurring us to give our all and produce an excellent translation.” GEB's Chinese edition has had profound influence in the Chinese-speaking world; its translation difficulty and creativity make it a benchmark in popular science translation. The three translators viewed GEB's translation as a summit to aspire toward. “We hope we did not let them down, and we hope that after reading this book, readers will gain a deeper understanding of human cognition” — this is the afterword's final sentence, at once a humble self-reflection and a sincere wish. Translation is difficult, especially of Hofstadter and Sander's book. But when translation itself becomes the practice of analogical thinking, when the principle of “transposition” makes the Chinese edition a standalone, complete work, this difficulty transforms into a precious gift for Chinese readers.

🔑 Core Takeaway
The Further Reading and book-wide annotations together constitute the academic background and knowledge network of Surfaces and Essences. The four recommended works—Dennett's Intuition Pumps, Pinker's The Stuff of Thought and How the Mind Works, and Edge's Thinking—trace a clear cognitive science reading path from "how to think" to "what is the mind" to "the relationship between language and thought." The twenty-three annotations weaving through the entire book connect linguistic details (French word roots, English polysemy), cultural backgrounds (Homeric epics, classical Chinese poetics), and academic concepts (Gestalt psychology, Platonic concepts) into a network. Particularly noteworthy is that many annotations themselves demonstrate analogical thinking—such as "division means both splitting and the mathematical operation in English," a linguistic fact that is key to understanding how naïve analogies shape mathematical cognition. The Further Reading is not a mere appended "tail" but a toolbox inviting readers to continue exploring: from this book, venture into the broader world of cognitive science.
