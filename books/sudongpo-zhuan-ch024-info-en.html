The Gay Genius · Ch.24 「Disaster Relief」

Farmers stripped elm bark to make porridge; in bumper years they fled their homes pursued by debt collectors — "both plenty and famine are sickness." When the system becomes a cage, what can one man's resistance change?

Su Tungpo was recalled to the capital, surrounded by enemies, and three months later demoted to Yingzhou. What he saw was more shocking than political struggle: farmers suffering "both plenty and famine are sickness" — in famine years they starved; in bumper years they were jailed for debt. He spent two years petitioning the court to cancel twenty years of accumulated debts. The bureaucracy obstructed, memorials were "lost," delays stretched endlessly — yet he never stopped pleading for the people.

01
Systemic Trap
Both Plenty and Famine Are Sickness
The legacy of Wang Anshi's reforms created an absurd predicament: in famine years, you starved to death; in bumper harvest years, debt collectors threw you in prison. Farmers had to "choose" between these fates. In his Yangzhou memorial Su wrote "both plenty and famine are sickness." Wheat fields were green but farmhouses stood empty — farmers dreaded bumper years because officials would come to collect Green Sprout loans. Trade was paralyzed; wine-tax revenues dropped from 300,000 to under 200,000 strings per year. Government records were a mess — no one knew who the legal owners were.

02
Bureaucratic Disease
108 Days of Silence
Su petitioned for debt cancellation and waited 108 days — no response. When he inquired, the Revenue Ministry claimed the memorial was "lost" and asked him to resubmit. He sent a copy, adding that only restoring the people's credit could increase tax revenues. After nearly two more years, still no action. Bureaucrats played word games: they insisted property restitution applied only to "seizure" cases, not "commuted payment" cases — a difference of one character that cost countless people their homes.

03
Scene of Disaster
New Year's Eve in the Snow: 20,000 Refugees and a Sleepless Governor
On New Year's Eve in the snow, Su climbed the city wall and saw 20,000 refugees trudging through deep snow. He stayed up all night researching relief plans. At dawn he woke his colleague Zhao Lingshi: "I couldn't sleep all night. We must find a way to save these refugees." Zhao suggested using thousands of measures of relief grain and hundreds of thousands of catties of coal from the wine bureau. Su said: "Good. Let's do it immediately." Afterwards he kept writing to stop grain taxation in disaster areas, but officials in Huainan continued collecting — the systemic indifference of low-level bureaucrats was more lethal than any natural disaster.

"I couldn't sleep all night. We have to find a way to save these refugees. Perhaps we can allocate some official grain and make wheat cakes for them." — Su Tungpo on New Year's Eve

04
Historical Lesson
One Man's War: Prevention Costs Half of Relief
Su pointed out that if his proposed preventive measures had been adopted earlier, the cost would have been less than half the current relief expenditure: "Small minds see only saving money for the court, not saving people for the sovereign." The 1092 famine was devastating — over half the population of Suzhou, Huzhou, and Xiuzhou died. Though floodwaters receded, field boundaries were destroyed: "Fields without people, people without grain, grain without seeds, seeds without oxen. Among the dead, the survivors looked like dried ghosts." He estimated that even with full government assistance, recovery would take ten years.

05
Pleading for the People
Letter After Letter with No Reply
Su's disaster relief campaign was a solitary marathon. He wrote memorial after memorial: requesting return of property, cancellation of debts, cessation of bad-debt collection, relief for refugees. The bureaucratic system's responses, in order: silence, loss of documents, delay, word games. But he never gave up. He saw the root cause — not the randomness of nature, but a system that systematically harvested the poorest. The phrase "both plenty and famine are sickness" reveals more deeply than any treatise the institutional catastrophe left by Wang Anshi's reforms.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Su Tungpo's disaster relief campaign reveals an eternal truth: good policies require people of conscience to execute them. When the system becomes a cage and bureaucracy becomes an obstacle, one person's persistence may not change the entire system, but it lets future generations see what justice looks like. He spent two years writing letter after letter that received no reply — and those letters themselves became the loudest answer. "Both plenty and famine are sickness" — these four characters still let us hear, a thousand years later, the cry of a solitary soul.
