The Gay Genius · Ch.22 "The Art of Retreat"

Nonconformist, dissenter, truth-teller — why a man who refused to play politics became the target

📖 Overview

There is an art of advancement, and an art of retreat. Su Dongpo specialized in the latter. He was never a good party man — "his personal standards were too high." When his own party held power, the Empress Dowager admired him, and he enjoyed great fame and high office, all he wanted was to resign. Yet while he fled politics, politics pursued him. He violated every rule of official success: he couldn't speak in empty words, he refused to conform, he wouldn't stop thinking independently. Within months, dozens of memorials attacked him. The Dowager supported him, but he submitted four petitions to leave the capital.

Core Question: Why does a man who wants no office become the target of factional warfare?

Key Insight: The reason for Su's political failure is precisely his most admirable quality: refusing to go with the crowd, insisting on independent thought.

01
RULES

The Rules of the Game: Su as Anti-Textbook

Su Dongpo violated every rule of official success — and he knew it perfectly well.

Lin Yutang summarized the unwritten rules of Song political success: First, a good politician says much while revealing nothing, using only negatives. Second, he bestows favors on friends. Third, he is careful never to offend — "a man who never speaks rashly, who loves to use cultured, soft, pleasant whispers, and who enjoys dispensing small favors — even if he never reaches the highest office, he will not lose his post." Su was the opposite: he spoke bluntly, offended everyone, and never cultivated relationships. When Chaoyun's son was born, he wrote: "I only wish the child to be dull and stupid, so he may reach the highest offices without disaster or difficulty."

❌ Su Dongpo

02
PRINCIPLE

"Not Following": The Sin of Independence

Su refused to join any camp of "yes-men" — whether following Wang Anshi or Sima Guang, he saw them as fundamentally the same thing.

In a letter to his friend Yang Gui, he wrote: "The gentlemen of the past took only Jing [Wang Anshi] as their master. The gentlemen of today follow only Wen [Sima Guang]. What they follow differs, but the act of following is the same. I knew Wen as deeply as anyone, throughout without rift, yet in many things I did not follow." This independent stance made him enemies in both camps simultaneously. When over forty memorials attacked him, he wrote a 2,000-character letter to the emperor defending the principle of dissent.

Between mid-December 1086 and January 10, 1087, the court received four or five memorials impeaching Su. The Dowager ordered officials to stop — they defied her and submitted more the next day.

03
FIGHT

Fighting for Free Speech and Independent Thought

At the peak of his power, Su chose the most dangerous battle — fighting for freedom of speech and independent thought.

He memorialized the Dowager: good governments and good emperors have always been willing to receive anyone. Tang Taizong even permitted commoners without rank to enter the palace and speak. Now fewer than a dozen people could see the Dowager — "How can these dozen know what is happening in the entire country? If these dozen are all mediocre and dare not tell her the truth, the Dowager will surely believe the people have no grievances." He criticized Wang Anshi's greatest fault: "insisting that everyone be like himself" — the barren saline land where "as far as the eye can see, there is nothing but yellow reeds and white grasses — this is Wang's uniformity."

"The decline of literature has never been as severe as today. Its source truly lies with Wang. Wang's writing is not necessarily bad. The fault is in his insistence that everyone be like himself." — Su Dongpo to Zhang Lei

04
REFORM

Abolishing the Green Sprout Law: Fighting for the Poor

Su fought alone against government corruption and incompetence, demanding the complete abolition of the Green Sprout Law and debt forgiveness for the poor — "This is the people's wealth and should be used for the people's benefit."

In April 1086, the court half-abolished the Green Sprout Law. Su argued this wasn't enough and petitioned in August for complete abolition. He wrote: "Over twenty years, because of Green Sprout debts, people sold their fields and houses, sold their wives and daughters, threw themselves into the water and hanged themselves — too numerous to count." He asked: how can a sovereign lend money to the people and make a profit from the interest? He proposed forgiving debts of the poorest households. The Green Sprout Law was abolished the next month, but debt forgiveness was only accepted six years later after Su's repeated efforts.

He also exposed examination fraud and the proliferation of superfluous officials. While 300-400 people earned office through examination, 800-900 obtained posts through recommendations without exams.

05
POSTURE

Four Petitions to Resign: Self-Exile Under Imperial Favor

Facing his enemies' attacks, Su did not counterattack — he submitted four petitions to leave the capital.

The Dowager supported him and even wanted to punish his accusers. Su was trapped — with the Dowager's backing, and his enemies having lost face without achieving their goal, he had to stay. But he resolved to "be more frank, more direct, and tell her what others dared not say." This was Su's way of "repaying" — not through compliance, but through more honest self-expression. Yet it was precisely this posture that made him intolerable to all factions. His story reveals a truth: sometimes a person's greatest political crime is refusing to commit any political crime at all.

"I have submitted several petitions requesting a provincial post without approval... I am not tolerated by the censors. The gentlemen of the past took only Jing as their master. The gentlemen of today follow only Wen. What they follow differs, but the act of following is the same." — Su Dongpo to Yang Gui

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

Su Dongpo's political tragedy reveals an eternal paradox in the arena of power: a person who seeks no power, cultivates no relationships, and refuses to follow the crowd becomes the most dangerous target precisely because of his independence. He belonged to no faction — neither Wang Anshi's, nor Sima Guang's, nor Cheng Yi's. He had only one faction: being himself. And "being himself," in a political game that demands everyone pick a side, was itself the greatest crime.
