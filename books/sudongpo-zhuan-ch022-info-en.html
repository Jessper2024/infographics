The Gay Genius · Ch.22 「The Art of Retreat」

No following, no flattering, no shutting up — why a man who refused to play politics became the prime target

There are ways to advance in the world, and there are ways to retreat. Su Tungpo specialized in the latter. He was never a good party man — his "character was too fine." When his own party was in power, the Empress Dowager admired him, and he enjoyed fame and high rank, he continuously sought to resign. He did not chase politics, but politics chased him. He broke every rule of official success: he refused to speak empty words, to be agreeable, or to stop thinking independently. Within months, dozens of memorials attacked him. The Empress Dowager supported him — so he petitioned four times to leave the capital.

01
Political Rules
Su Tungpo: An Anti-Rulebook for Political Success
Lin Yutang summarized the rules of Song Dynasty political success: A good politician must talk at length while revealing nothing; must grant favors to friends; must carefully avoid giving offense. "If a man is not careless in his speech, speaks in a cultured, soft, pleasant whisper, and likes to do small favors, even if he does not reach the highest rank, he will never lose power." Su Tungpo did exactly the opposite: outspoken, offensive, never cultivating relationships. When Chaoyun's son was born, he wrote: "I only wish the child to be stupid and dull, to reach high office without calamity or difficulty."

02
Independent Stance
Neither Wang Anshi's Man Nor Sima Guang's Follower
In a letter to his friend Yang Gui, Su struck at the heart of the matter: "Formerly, the gentlemen followed only Jing (Wang Anshi). Now, the gentlemen follow only Wen (Sima Guang). Different followers, but the act of following is identical. I am deeply acquainted with Wen and there has never been anything between us. But on many things I do not follow." This independent stance offended both factions simultaneously: former Wang supporters saw him as an enemy; current Sima followers saw him as disloyal. He had only one party: himself.

03
Free Speech
Fighting for Open Channels of Speech
At the peak of his power, Su chose the most dangerous battle: fighting for free speech. He memorialized the Empress Dowager: fewer than a dozen people could see her — "How can these dozen people know what is happening throughout the empire?" He criticized Wang Anshi's "sickness of wanting everyone to think alike." In a letter to his student Zhang Lei, he wrote: "Good soil gives life to all plants, and they are not all alike. Only on barren salt-soiled land do we see nothing but yellow reeds and white grasses for miles around. That is the kind of uniformity Wang wanted."

"The decline of literature has never been as bad as it is today. Its source is Wang Anshi. Wang's writing is not necessarily bad — but his sickness is wanting everyone to be like himself." — Su Tungpo to Zhang Lei

04
Advocate for the Poor
Abolishing the Green Sprout Law Alone Against the System
In April 1086, the court half-abolished the Green Sprout Law. Su thought half-reform was insufficient and petitioned for complete abolition in August. He wrote: "In twenty years, those who owed Green Sprout debts and were forced to sell land and houses, sell wives and children, or throw themselves into rivers and hang themselves are beyond number." He asked: How can a ruler lend money to the people and earn interest? He proposed canceling all debts for the poorest four classes. The law was repealed the following month — but debt cancellation took six more years.

05
Self-Exile
Four Petitions to Leave: Voluntary Exile with Imperial Protection
Facing his enemies' attacks, Su did not fight back — he submitted four petitions requesting to leave the capital. The Empress Dowager supported him so strongly she even offered to punish his accusers. Su was trapped: her support meant he had to stay. In response, he resolved to be "even more frank and direct, telling her what others dared not say." This was Su's style of "repaying" — not through compliance, but through even greater honesty. It made him intolerable to all factions. Sometimes a man's greatest political error is refusing to make any political errors.

"I have submitted several petitions requesting provincial assignment, but have not been granted it… It is because the censors will not tolerate me." — Su Tungpo to Yang Gui

🔑 Key Takeaway
Su Tungpo's political tragedy reveals an eternal paradox of power: a person who does not pursue power, cultivate connections, or follow the crowd becomes the most dangerous target precisely because of his independence. He belonged to no faction — not Wang Anshi's, not Sima Guang's, not Cheng Yi's. He had only one faction: being himself. And in a political game that demands everyone pick a side, "being yourself" is the greatest crime of all.
