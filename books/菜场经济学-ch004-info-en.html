Chapter 4 · The Real Economy Suffers

Elegy for Small Business — Who's Footing the Bill for the Printing Feast?
SCQA Analysis
S (Situation): The real economy embodies social productive forces. China has 40 million SMEs supporting 500–600 million jobs.
C (Complication): Money printing → infrastructure siphons labor → labor costs explode → SME profits zero out. The new Labor Contract Law raises exit costs; 48% social insurance is a second tax; e-commerce exterminates brick-and-mortar retail through tax evasion. Enterprises are squeezed at both ends: labor disputes and soaring wages on one side; exploding distribution costs on the other.
Q (Question): Why is "running a factory worse than buying a house"? How much longer can SMEs survive? Why is innovation empty talk?
A (Answer): Real estate strangles everything — annual profit can't match one inner-ring apartment's yearly appreciation. Small bosses are on their knees. Shut down or become a landlord — those are the only two exits. In nearly 100 years, China produced one genuine original innovation — artemisinin. No R&D, no talent pipeline — just buy property with surplus cash. The only self-rescue: shrink quietly, keep a skeleton crew, survive on fumes, and wait for a bailout. Not being the first to collapse — that's victory.

🔴 The Real Economy's Plight: Productivity Hollowed Out

Domestic sales: severe overcapacity — clothing, footwear, household appliances; every household is saturated, products last for years. Exports: cost advantage eroding; Yiwu small commodities shift to India/Southeast Asia; home appliances migrate to Mexico/Latin America. Squeezed at both ends: upstream SOEs inflate basic costs (utilities, fuel, freight), while construction sites drain labor and bid up wages; downstream, Taobao's below-cost pricing strangles retail channels. The new Labor Contract Law and high housing costs are the two main culprits — costs soar while consumption withers. Central revenue depends on real-economy taxation (growth slowing); local revenue depends on real estate (growth accelerating). The diverging trends say everything. Current policy systematically drains funds from the real economy to build zero-tech-content skyscrapers. In 2013, 400 listed companies used IPO proceeds to buy wealth management products — even industry leaders have lost interest in their core business. Logistics costs are absurd: Shanghai to Chengdu, point-to-point transport alone eats 6–7% of cargo value; add packing and handling to approach 8% — swallowing all commercial profit.

🟠 Small Bosses On Their Knees: The Darkest Hour

Small-business owners are a peculiar tribe — low sophistication, many flaws, but the most dynamic. Over 30 years of reform and opening, they were the market's foundation. Now the "restrictive approach" will crush most of them within three more years. The small boss's daily misery: facing upward, monthly payment notices for taxes, fees, fines, utilities, tolls; facing downward, annual wage hikes and rising social insurance contributions for employees. Workers can resign; he can't fire anyone without the inspector immediately showing up. At economic policy roundtables, you can spot the private business owner instantly — he's the most fawning one. Everyone else is from foreign firms, SOEs, banks; he alone wears a face of cringing humility, nodding obsequiously even to the tea lady. Permits and licenses are the SME's worst nightmare — trying to get a proper license for a small recycling business? Years of running around, impossible. Meanwhile, the guy with a pushcart collecting scrap in dirty clothes pretending to be poor? He's already bought a house and no inspector ever talks to him. Inspectors treat enterprises like traffic cops pulling over trucks. The law-abiding SME? Every intersection has someone waiting to "care" for you. The cruelest part: you're clearly losing money, but your workers still believe you're raking in black-hearted profits, demanding raises every year.

🟡 The Financial Butcher's Knife: Credit Discrimination Kills

Bank loans come in two flavors: to local government platforms — principal unrecoverable, just roll it over, branch managers feel zero pressure; to small private firms — personal asset collateral mandatory, default triggers immediate asset seizure and criminal referral. The Wenzhou lesson: in 2009, banks proactively visited enterprises offering loans with unimaginably good service; two years later, when businesses had just started production with the borrowed money, banks flipped instantly, demanding immediate repayment, destroying swaths of Wenzhou companies. Actual SME borrowing costs: nominal 12%, plus a 2% guarantee shave, plus discount fees — real cost exceeds 16%. Legitimate enterprises can afford at most 5% annual interest. SOEs building subways barely think about repayment, utterly indifferent to interest rates — where does that leave small firms? Monetary policy blows hot and cold; macro controls appear and vanish without warning. SMEs can only pursue "short, flat, fast" projects. The root cause of the real economy's capital drought is no profit: "Without raising private-sector profitability, monetary idle-circulation is unavoidable." Private entrepreneurs pushing for private banks under the banner of serving SMEs are actually seeking financial-institution privileges and convenient self-financing.

🟣 The Innovation Mirage: China Has Only One Artemisinin

In nearly 100 years, China has produced one globally recognized original innovation — artemisinin, the malaria cure discovered by young pharmaceutical technicians in Yunnan and Sichuan in the 1970s. Nothing since. The core reason: intellectual property gets no respect. Countless small enterprises finally innovate; then a technician sells the secret for a few thousand yuan. Small firms end up competing only on price; when that fails, they speculate in real estate — "only a fool innovates." The total output value of every washing machine factory in China equals one Beijing/Shanghai residential compound. Real estate is the root of China's century of humiliation — the Qing dynasty lost the Sino-Japanese War because technology funds went to build the Summer Palace instead. Google develops real-time translation; what are China's internet companies doing? BAT are innovation destroyers — anything novel gets ruthlessly copied, improved, then offered free. Small companies can only accept acquisition or bankruptcy. China's "high-tech" products are substitutes for Western products, never disruptive breakthroughs. Cut housing prices, cut taxes, streamline government, let enterprises make money — then a fraction will voluntarily invest in R&D. The connected got rich (real estate); engineers and entrepreneurs? Sorry, take a number.

🩷 E-commerce's Impact: Foreign Capital Strangles Retail

China's retail sector has been beaten down three times: Gome crushed department stores; Walmart/Carrefour crushed mom-and-pop shops; foreign-capital-backed e-commerce is now exterminating all physical retail. The foreign-capital signature: zero concern for five-year profitability, only market share. Chinese retail enterprises lack the capital to bleed for five years. Taobao's cheapness doesn't come from efficiency — it comes from: foreign VC subsidies (foreign雷锋), tax evasion, zero social insurance contributions, labor exploitation. Taobao's profit model is vicious — bleed money up front to capture the market; once it monopolizes all standardized-product manufacturers, recoup everything and more. Taobao concentrates retail tax revenue in coastal regions, devastating 3rd/4th-tier city commercial real estate and retail — a giant vacuum sucking away local capital. 90% of China's department stores are spiritually broken. Taobao stole the business of both retailers and the postal service. Retailers have no political voice and can't fight back; the State Post Bureau has begun counterattacking, demanding express delivery companies pay into a "universal service fund." Retail is a nation's economic lifeline — in which Western country is retail controlled by outsiders? We handed ours away.

💡 Key Takeaway

The real economy is "the only place in China that can still produce anything" — yet it's being systematically drained. Four nooses tighten simultaneously: real estate's crowding-out effect, the new Labor Contract Law's cost explosion, discriminatory credit allocation, and e-commerce's tax-evading strangulation. SME exit isn't competitive elimination — it's "wholesale extinction." Store-opening booms, export booms, stock-market booms, small-commodity booms, steel-trading booms, photovoltaic booms — each wave extinguished wholesale. The small boss's optimal strategy: if business is bad, quietly shed staff without drawing attention; keep a skeleton crew; shrink and wait. Don't be in the first wave to collapse — that's winning. In 1998, SOEs laid off workers; now it's private enterprise's turn. "The foundation of an economy is commercial and industrial prosperity" — but who cares? Small bosses are already on their knees. Whose turn next year? The only way out is restoring SMEs to vibrant health — but does that path survive the interests blocking it?
