The World of Yesterday · Ch.4 "University Years"

University Gave Me the Only Thing I Wanted: The Most Generous Free Time of My Life After entering university, Zweig chose philosophy — not out of passion but to exchange minimal coursework for maximum freedom. He published his first poetry collection, appeared in the Neue Freie Presse, met Theodor Herzl, father of Zionism, and eventually entered Berlin's bohemian world.

This chapter records Zweig's transition from school to university. The absurd medieval privileges of Austrian universities disgusted him; he chose philosophy — only needing to pass exams in the final year, with three years devoted entirely to literature and art. At nineteen he published his first poetry collection, earning praise from Liliencron, Rilke, and Max Reger. His article in the Neue Freie Presse brought him to the attention of editor Theodor Herzl. Herzl's turn toward Zionism after the Dreyfus affair, and his tragic fate, left a deep impression. Zweig then traveled to Berlin, entered bohemian circles, translated Baudelaire and Verlaine, and established his lifelong creed: "Observe more, learn more, then create."

01
University Choice
Choosing Philosophy: Not from Passion, but for Freedom
Zweig felt indifferent toward any field; his heart had long been devoted to literature. He chose philosophy simply because it was easiest to get through — only a thesis and exam in the final year. Austrian universities retained absurd medieval privileges: students had their own "honor" code, wore red armbands seeking duel opponents, and considered facial scars from swords the mark of true manhood. Zweig was deeply disgusted. What he truly gained from university was three years of free time for literature and art. "A good book surpasses a good university" — a conviction he never abandoned.

02
Literary Debut
The First Poetry Collection at Ninete9: The Smell of Printer's Ink Sweeter Than Rose Oil
For a nineteen-year-old fresh graduate, the smell of printer's ink was the sweetest scent in the world. He sent his manuscript to Schuster & Loeffler, a prestigious publisher of German lyric poetry, and it was quickly accepted. Liliencron and Dehmel acknowledged him as a peer; Rilke sent a new poetry collection in return; Max Reger selected six poems to set to music. These accolades bolstered his fragile confidence, but he soon realized these poems "arose from uncertain premonitions and unconscious imitation" rather than personal experience — it would be six years before his second collection.

03
Fateful Encounter
Herzl: From Literary Editor to Father of Zionism
Zweig met Theodor Herzl at the Neue Freie Presse literary supplement — the first figure of world-historical significance he would encounter. Herzl had been Vienna's most beloved intellectual, but witnessing the Dreyfus degradation transformed him utterly. He wrote "The Jewish State," only to be mocked and reviled by Vienna's Jewish bourgeoisie. Zweig witnessed Herzl's isolation and suffering firsthand. When Herzl died, mourners from across the globe flooded Vienna — Zweig felt for the first time "how much passion and hope a single person's ideas could leave in the world."
Herzl told me with a bitter smile: "Please don't forget that for centuries our discussions on this question have not been serious. We must now learn the spirit of unconditional devotion."
04
Berlin Years
Berlin: A New World Beyond Bourgeois Comfort
Zweig went to Berlin not for university but to escape the confines of Vienna's Jewish bourgeoisie. Berlin was transforming from an ordinary capital into a world city; lacking Vienna's cultural traditions, it actually encouraged young people's new experiments. He joined the "Nachwuchs" (successors) society, encountering all manner of characters — the poor, drunkards, swindlers, Eastern Jews, Russian students. These experiences vastly broadened his horizons but also revealed the immaturity of his schoolboy judgment. He burned the novel manuscript he had brought from Vienna.

05
Life Creed
Translation and Patience: Establishing the Creed of "Observe, Learn, Then Create"
Berlin taught Zweig that premature work should not be shown to the world — one must first understand the world's essence. Following Dehmel's advice, he turned to translation — Baudelaire, Verlaine, Keats, William Morris. Translation refined his command of his mother tongue and cultivated patience and perseverance. From Vienna to Berlin, from poetry publication to self-negation, Zweig transformed from a "precocious literary youth" into a "measured observer." This was Rilke's path — step by step toward maturity.

🔑 Core Conclusion
University gave Zweig its most precious gift: not knowledge but freedom.
