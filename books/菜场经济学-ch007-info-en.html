Chapter 7 · The Logic of Reform

Everybody Knows What to Fix — and Why It Can't Be Fixed
SCQA Analysis
S (Situation): China's economy is declining, the reform moment has arrived. The Third Plenum declared "markets decide resource allocation." A reform consensus has formed.
C (Complication): Every reform touches entrenched interests — streamlining government faces personnel reassignment; healthcare reform is locked by doctor kickback chains; hukou reform hits the "reform others OK, reform me NO" wall; the civil service is bloated and underpaid, breeding inefficiency with zero motivation to change. Fix one surface problem, create three new ones — so nobody bothers.
Q (Question): Where exactly is reform stuck? Why does everyone know the solution yet nothing moves?
A (Answer): "Legal gridlock" — every side can cite laws to block change. The only entities that can yield are state organs and SOEs, but the will to "work harder for fewer perks" is uncertain. The core path: leaders go first, halve headcount/double salaries, external oversight — but who wants to be watched? True reform is monks with umbrellas — lawless and fearless — not bureaucrats writing papers behind closed doors.

🔴 Reform Paralysis: "Reform by Law" Means No Reform

A reform consensus has formed — reform others, so you benefit. If it's yourself getting reformed, drag the authorities in: "You failed to manage before — compensate me." Terms too short (2–3 years) breed slash-and-burn; too long breeds entrenched fiefdoms. Everyone knows what to fix, but you don't know until you're in charge — government agencies and functions lack statutory definition; what's cut today can be added tomorrow. Past reform logic: "new rules for new people, old rules for old people" — only reform the increment, never the stock. What is real reform? Cai's answer: monks with umbrellas — lawless and fearless. Not top-down design but spontaneous order. Not bureaucrats writing papers for the masses to follow, but the people charging ahead, risking it all.

🟠 Streamlining Government: Same People, Same Stamps

The core of streamlining is accelerating economic flows — ¥1 circulating twice creates ¥2 GDP, six times creates ¥6. But with all those section chiefs, fee notices, and highway toll stations — try it! Cut approvals? Same number of people; trim one procedure, they add intricacy to the remaining ones and issue new implementation rules. Approval changed to "filing"? Junior officials always find nitpicks — your filing materials aren't compliant, you're illegal. Merge three approvals into one? The running around doesn't decrease by one step. Power lists, sealed stamps? Outside every window, crowds of fixers still operate. Any approval: 20% must be approved, 20% must be denied, the remaining 60% — you understand! This is the secret of China's economy.

🟡 The Healthcare Trap: The Doctor Kickback Chain

90% of poor families Cai knows fell into poverty from illness. An ordinary person's savings last one to two weeks at a top-tier hospital. Doctors order every test, every expensive drug, every pricey surgical device — collect big kickbacks, then find excuses to deflect. Patient switches hospitals, does it all again — wiped out, dead. Annual central fiscal revenue: ¥10T. ¥3K/person healthcare for 1.4B people = 40% of budget. And ¥3K disappears after a few big tests. Zhong Nanshan: "Chinese doctors survive by selling drugs, using equipment, and ordering tests." The Health Bureau's control is simple: you have nowhere else to go (private Fujian clinics are shameful), promotions require sucking up, and since they can't solve compensation, they tacitly allow doctors to prey on patients. For decades, not a single doctor has been fired for taking red envelopes. The reform is simple: separate public hospitals from paid hospitals. But then those freeloading on public hospitals lose out.

🟣 The Hukou System: The Toughest Fortress

The greatest achievement of hukou reform: continued strict control of BJ/SH residency. The non-local playbook: kids need education fairness, work needs employment fairness, marriage needs lower housing prices, illness needs healthcare fairness, retirement needs pension fairness. They call grabbing benefits "fighting for fairness" — while their own land benefits are never mentioned. In 1952, no one cared about hukou; 1955 brought grain ration coupons, and everything locked in place. BJ/SH hukou is naked interest: the rich want identity, the poor want security, the middle class want vanity. Low-income BJ/SH natives cling tightly to residency status. Cai's prediction: hukou will transition to "property deed + continuous work record" — governance by housing. This is the bedrock of BJ/SH's long-term housing outlook. Those outsiders who finally got Shanghai hukou are now the fiercest opponents of opening it up.

🩷 Civil Service: Too Many People, Too Little Pay

The optimal state: halve the workforce, double salaries, crush any misconduct ruthlessly — in three years, mostly fixed. Today, nominal income is lower-middle class, so procedures are made excruciatingly complex — nominal fees are low, but real costs (through connections) are sky-high. Window staff are surly because at that salary, doing more or less doesn't change income — being nice attracts more applicants, making your own life harder. File a complaint? Offend him today, face endless "you know what" tomorrow. A department head's salary is less than a bricklayer's; Heshen's annual salary was one jade bracelet. But don't believe the "¥3K salary" line — salary ≠ income. In nice Inner Ring apartments, state employees vastly outnumber private: 8:2. As long as the civil service headcount doesn't drop 20%, housing market confidence is rock-solid. Reforming your own salary — what bliss. Cai suggests: let private-sector employees draft civil service pay scales.

💡 Key Takeaway

The logic of reform is brutally simple — revenue and expenditure, two lines. Zhu Yuanzhang with an elementary education founded the Ming Dynasty. But today, every reform enters the dead loop of "fix one surface problem, create three new ones." The crux: authorities believe ordinary people can't choose for themselves, so they must choose everything for them — and a swarm of section chiefs descends. If reform can't achieve "leaders go first," forget it. Real reform isn't writing papers behind closed doors; it's trusting and respecting the masses. Streamlining government isn't about process — it's about cutting staff and removing chairs. Hukou reform's endpoint is governance by housing — own a home and you have a foothold. Healthcare reform is blocked by doctor kickback chains. Civil service reform is blocked by self-reform. Bottom line: reform depends on resolve — farmers, workers, white-collar workers, small bosses have all made their sacrifices. Civil servants need to put themselves in the game too.
