The Great Compound Interest · Ch.4 "The County Town Store-Opening Playbook"

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer Brick-and-mortar stores are the oldest business model, and capital is once again chasing offline retail. But offline has always been saturated—MUJI cut prices repeatedly, IKEA's net profit collapsed. What is the correct playbook for opening offline stores in today's China? Answer: forget "downward expansion" as a concept—only tiered markets exist; research must go deep into real scenarios, not surveys; success hinges on 5 factors—location, naming, logo, content, and markup rate; and above all, it requires the spirit of one who was born, raised, and fights in the soil.

This chapter is the product of an in-depth dialogue between the author and Ma Yingyao, founder of Sunmei Lifestyle Group (6,000+ stores). The business world is wonderfully cyclical—a few years ago, telling investors you wanted to open physical stores would get you branded insane; today, capital is frantically hunting for people and projects that know how to open stores. But the author warns: offline has never stopped being saturated, and the reason for going offline must never be "online is too hard." This chapter systematically deconstructs store-opening principles: the trap of consumer research (they say they love healthy drinks but eat fried chicken at 4 pm), the essence of tiered markets rather than "downward expansion," the five core factors determining store success (location, naming, logo, content, markup rate), and Ma Yingyao's "audacious" spirit—aim high, target ¥1 trillion.

01
Offline Revival
The Cycle: From Abandoned to Chased
The business world is wonderfully cyclical: a few years ago, telling investors about opening physical stores would get you branded insane; today, the investment world is frantically hunting for people and projects that can open stores. But offline has never stopped being saturated—it was saturated 10 years ago, which is why people turned to e-commerce. Most who return to offline today come back defeated, because online is even bloodier and harder. The so-called "cross-boundary disruption" only works if, in the industry you enter, you know everything others know and everything they don't. The reason for going offline must never be "online is too hard." Over the past decade, Huazhu Group's market cap rose from a few billion to a hundred billion, Anta achieved super-growth—the hundred-billion-yuan offline life-and-death evolutions have been happening all along, just ignored by the mainstream.

02
Research Trap
Consumers Never Tell the Truth: Why Traditional Research Doomed to Fail
The author spent a million yuan in tuition to learn one lesson: traditional linear consumer research is almost entirely a failure. He asked hundreds of women across different regions, professions, and ages what beverages they liked—all answered "healthy drinks." So he opened a healthy beverage shop—only to discover their real dietary pattern: occasionally having a salad and juice, taking a selfie and posting it with "Fighting, another healthy day," then eating milk tea and fried chicken at 4 pm. The key to obtaining real data is not what you ask but in what scenario you ask, and to whom the respondent tells the truth. When Ma Yingyao researches hotel occupancy rates, he always takes the latest bus and arrives at midnight, posing as a tour guide to ask the front desk "How many rooms do you have left?"—at which point the front desk will carefully calculate real figures. One night walking through every hotel in the county yields the true occupancy rate.

03
Tiered Markets
There Is No "Downward Market"—Only Tiered Markets
"Downward expansion" is, from a certain perspective, a false concept—there are no downward markets, only tiered markets. The essential difference between tier-2 and tier-3 cities: tier-2 cities more or less have industrial clusters and scaled talent inflow; tier-3 cities mostly lack scaled industries, build new towns on the outskirts with local buyers. Applying tier-1 brand models to tier-4 cities will end disastrously—in tier-3 and tier-4 markets, higher markup rates and stronger sales communication are indispensable. The consumption psychology of ordinary women in small counties also differs: luxury lingerie stores are empty, while stores with baskets on the doorstep marked "3 for ¥100" are buzzing—they feel nervous in particularly luxurious stores with more staff than customers.

Five Essentials
The Five Factors: Location · Naming · Logo · Content · Markup Rate
Store success isn't determined by a single factor—at least five are at play. Location is always primary: location, location, location—no shortcuts, no secret formulas. Brand naming: Sunkyo sounds like "thank you"—a brand you can't even pronounce already fails. Logo is the core memory point: like MUJI and IKEA, a large red sign visible from afar and instantly memorable. Store content means controlling the consumption flow: a coffee shop can sell sandwiches but not socks—three-star hotels collapsed nationwide because of redundant gyms and meeting rooms. Pricing must flex with foot traffic: tier-1 cities can host large low-price stores (high frequency, high flow), while tier-4/5 must raise per-customer spending to protect margins—the more standardized, the harder to adapt downstream.

05
Mud-Stomping Spirit
Born Here, Fight Here: Who Can Bloom in the Mud
Doing ground-level business requires extreme grit, resilience, and being foolish enough yet wise enough. The author confesses many of his own stores failed—the moment the power and water were cut, his intellectual pride collapsed, and he rebuilt a value system: the world does not necessarily belong to the so-called elite. He has no confidence in office veterans doing offline for the first time, nor in lavish spenders, nor in armchair theorists—they must pay enough tuition until it hurts to the bone and transforms them. Ma Yingyao's "audacity" (setting a 200-store target when the market said 50 was impressive—they ended up opening 100+), and his "I'll sell my house to keep going as long as I'm alive" conviction, embody the swashbuckling archetype: starting from the dust yet seeing a wholly different set of business operating principles with Chinese characteristics in that very dust. Perhaps the world truly belongs to the practical, the grounded, the persistent, and those with expansive personalities.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Brick-and-mortar retail is a millennia-old business model, but in today's Chinese offline market it must be solved with new-era methods. Understanding tiered markets rather than "downward expansion" is the first prerequisite—each tier from first to fifth has completely different consumption logic, trust mechanisms, and foot-traffic characteristics. Consumer research must go deep into real scenarios, not surveys, because people never tell the truth. Store success hinges on five factors: location always comes first, naming determines information efficiency, logo creates memory, content defines consumption scenarios, and markup rate must match foot traffic. More importantly—ground-level business requires the "mud-stomping spirit": extreme grit, foolish enough yet smart enough, born, raised, and fighting in the soil. Perhaps the world truly belongs to the practical, the grounded, the persistent, and those with expansive, visionary personalities.
