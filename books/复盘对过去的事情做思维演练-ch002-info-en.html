Fupan · Chapter 2 「Why Fupan」

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer Winners like Liu Chuanzhi, Feng Lun, Zhou Hongyi, and Sun Taoran are all masters of fupan (retrospection). Look before you leap is their habit. Yet most people never fupan — crediting luck for success, blaming external factors for failure, endlessly repeating the same mistakes.
Why fupan? Better yet: Why not fupan?
The answer: Fupan is the only means by which we truly improve our capabilities, elevating individuals and organizations from mediocrity to excellence through five core dimensions.

Fupan is the only means of true capability improvement. This chapter systematically explains six fundamental reasons: avoid repeating mistakes (Keith Ferrazzi's office politics lesson with Mary, the Ah Jiu wall-hitting case), solidify processes (from pre-flight checklists to The Checklist Manifesto to Huawei's "Rigidify → Optimize → Solidify"), course correction (Lenovo's mobile phone sale-and-repurchase strategy pivot, the painful lesson of 3721 forgetting users), see problems behind problems (TCL's failed Thomson acquisition — the root cause was "wrong motivation"), discover new knowledge (the birth of Commander's Intent, the three types of knowledge), know yourself and elevate character (Vantone's 20+ year tradition of annual reflection days). Fupan is not just a method — it is a forge for personal and organizational character.

01
Error Prevention
The First Value of Fupan: Avoid Repeating Mistakes
No one trips over the same stone twice — fupan lets us extract experience from failure, block risk at the source, and build self-warning mechanisms. Keith Ferrazzi's dealings with the CEO's assistant Mary is a classic case: In Round 1, he won Mary's support through respect and casual chats. In Round 2, he made the fatal mistake of complaining to the CEO — seen by Mary as an act of war, turning his work life into a nightmare. After fupan, he completely changed his approach: let Mary hand-pick the new assistant, and told the new hire to "obey Mary unconditionally" — never falling into the same office politics trap again.
"If Ah Jiu had fupan'd his 2008 wall-hitting incident, every future Mid-Autumn Festival he would control his gluttony, blocking the cause at the source. Even if he accidentally overate again, he would stay warm and stable — perhaps even sitting on the ground so that if he fainted, the low center of gravity would prevent injury."
02
Process Solidification
The Core Output of Fupan: Solidify Processes & Checklists
Pre-flight checklists are the archetype of fupan-solidified processes — countless flight experiences distilled into a fixed-action checklist ensuring safe takeoff. The Checklist Manifesto reveals: a tiny checklist drove Johns Hopkins' central-line infection rate from 11% to zero, preventing 45 infections and 8 deaths in 15 months, saving $2 million. Across 8 pilot hospitals using checklists, 4,000 patients saw serious post-surgery complications drop 36 percentage points, with mortality falling 47 points. When Huawei adopted IBM's system, Ren Zhengfei prescribed "first rigidify, then optimize, finally solidify" — the classic methodology for organizational progress driven by fupan.

03
Course Correction
From "Shooting Blind" to "Shooting Aimed": Fupan Corrects Direction
Fupan lets us examine progress at every step, checking whether we've deviated from the original direction. Liu Chuanzhi often says "step back from the painting to see it" — constantly analyzing the relationship between the parts and the whole. Lenovo sold its mobile division for $100M in 2008 to refocus on core business, then bought it back for $200M in 2009 — seemingly a losing deal, but in truth two rounds of fupan-driven strategic recalibration. Zhou Hongyi's fupan of 3721's battle with CNNIC and Baidu yielded a painful realization: "Competitors didn't defeat us — our users did." The essence of competition isn't crushing rivals but winning customers. And the secretary who only knew how to dial the phone is the textbook example of substituting means for the goal.
🔫

Shooting Blind

No plan, no steps, no aim. Reactive and improvised. Easy to drift off target without noticing. Means substituted for goals.

🎯

Shooting Aimed

Planned, methodical, on-target, effective. Fupan as you go — constantly reflecting, timely course correcting, making past outcomes valuable for the future.

04
Root Cause Analysis
See Problems Behind Problems: Fupan Reaches Essence
TCL's failed acquisition of Thomson's TV business had many surface reasons: misjudged TV technology trends, unaware of French labor dismissal laws, cultural clash, failure to adapt operating habits. But deeper fupan probing revealed — these surface issues weren't the real problem. TCL had hired Morgan Stanley and Boston Consulting for this deal; how could they miss obvious risks anyone could "figure out with their heel"? The real answer: Li Dongsheng's "faxin" (motivation) was wrong — his goal was to become world #1 and crush competitors, not to deliver better products to consumers. Steve Jobs on why Microsoft's Zune failed: "They don't love music and art the way we do." Motivation determines everything. When the motivation is wrong, everything that follows is wrong.
⚠️ The Core Fupan Probe
Surface reason → Why wasn't it anticipated? → What is the deeper motive?
Li Dongsheng later said: "Don't let opportunity sway you — missing an opportunity is better than doing something wrong." His most painful post-fupan conclusion.
05
Knowledge & Character
Discover New Knowledge & New Ideas, Know Yourself & Elevate Character
The U.S. Army, after fupan'ing countless failed battle plans, created the concept of Commander's Intent (CI) — stating only the end goal and desired state, not specific details. Because "you may lose the ability to execute the original plan, but you never lose the responsibility to execute the ultimate intent." Human knowledge divides into three types — genetic knowledge (instinct), cultural knowledge (absorbed unconsciously), and atomic knowledge (actively acquired). The last has only three pathways: personal experience, observing others, learning others' summaries — and all three ARE fupan. Liu Chuanzhi: "Learning from books and others is only 30%, the remaining 70% is learning from yourself. The key to self-learning is fupan." Since 1992, Vantone has held an annual Reflection Day. Feng Lun says: "Every reflection reminds us we're truly not good enough, with so much left undone — after reflection, we know the gap, know the direction, know the motivation, know the effort."

🔑 Key Takeaway
Fupan is the accelerator for individual and organizational capability, the perspective lens for seeing problems clearly, the probe for discovering answers, the generator of new knowledge and insights, the terminator of arrogance, and the corrector of wrong thoughts and actions. Every fupan is a training session for your thinking. Look before you leap — an individual or enterprise that habitually practices fupan develops a character that is more grounded, more solid, avoiding the hazards of impetuosity and recklessness. Master fupan, and you master the most powerful engine of your own growth.
