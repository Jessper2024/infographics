The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.14 "The Shocking Coup"

Part IV: The Jingkang Incident · Emperor Detained → Zhao Clan Deposed → Retired Emperor Exits → Crown Prince Exits → Entire Imperial Clan Relocated North On the 10th of the 1st month, Emperor Qinzong left the city a second time — ostensibly to confer an honorific title on the Jin Emperor. He assumed he'd return in three days, like before. But the Jin Emperor's "Edict Deposing the Ruler" was already en route. On the 6th of the 2nd month, Qinzong was stripped of his dragon robe and yellow canopy. Li Ruoshui embraced the emperor and cursed the Jin — the sole Song minister to die for his loyalty. Then the Retired Emperor, Empress, Crown Prince, princes, princesses, and consorts were all marched out. The Zhao dynasty was finished.

This chapter records the complete termination of the Northern Song Zhao dynasty — the beginning of what history calls the "Jingkang Incident." On the 10th of the 1st month, Emperor Qinzong left the city to confer an honorific title on the Jin Emperor and was detained at Qingcheng. The Jin Emperor's deposition edict arrived on the 6th of the 2nd month. Li Ruoshui, the sole Song official to die for his loyalty, embraced the emperor shouting "This is the true Son of Heaven of the Great Dynasty! You dog-spawn dare not be insolent!" — he was beaten to death after a hunger strike. The Retired Emperor Huizong was tricked into leaving: told he only needed to submit a petition at the Nanxun Gate. He arrived to find tens of thousands of Jin cavalry. Then the Empress, Crown Prince, all princes, princesses, and consorts — the Jin demanded every direct-line Zhao family member. Wolibu and Nianhan clashed fiercely over whether to depose the Zhao entirely — Nianhan's view prevailed.

01
Leaving Forever
The 10th of the First Month: The Emperor Left the City a Second Time — and Never Returned
At dawn on the 10th, Emperor Qinzong secretly departed for Qingcheng. Fan Qiong had to cut off the fingers of citizens clinging to his carriage to get through. Zhang Shuye grabbed the horse weeping: "Jizhong, be strong!" The emperor addressed him by his courtesy name, looking back. The Jin sent back 734 of his guards as too many; only 300 remained. He was housed in the eastern wing of Duancheng Hall, sleeping on an earthen bed with military mats, surrounded by iron chains. On Lantern Festival night, the Jin invited him to view 20,000 lamps at Liujia Temple — nine banquet tables, Jin dignitaries all around. The emperor wrote to the city: just waiting for clear weather to play polo, then I'll return. But the snow never stopped, and the game was never played. He didn't know the Jin Emperor's deposition edict was en route — and that his detention was Nianhan's preemptive move.

02
Capital Out of Control
Women as Debt, Student Petitions, General's Suicide: Bianjing's Final Struggles
With the emperor detained, the Kaifeng Prefecture intensified extraction. From the 25th of the 1st month, Jin demands expanded from precious metals to everything. Jade tablets, imperial carriages, coronets, 600 young girls, hundreds of court musicians and entertainers — priced as debt: palace lady 500 silver ingots, singer 200, common woman 100. Forty-seven female family members of Cai Jing, Tong Guan, and Wang Fu were marched out; streets echoed with wailing. Imperial Academy student Xu Kui went alone to the Nanxun Gate citing the Spring and Autumn Annals — killed. General Liu Ge, whom the Jin wanted as their Bureau of Military Affairs chief — he pretended to accept, wrote a farewell letter to his son, locked the door, and hanged himself. By the 19th, only 160,000 taels of gold and 6 million of silver had been collected.

03
Deposing the Zhao
The Sudden Change on the Polo Field: "Where Do You Think You're Going?!"
On the 5th of the 2nd month, the emperor finally played that long-awaited polo match with the two marshals. The emperor sat facing west; the marshals east. As Wolibu entered the field to play, General Puluhu arrived with the Jin Emperor's deposition edict. Nianhan's demeanor turned icy: "Where do you think you're going?!" Wolibu escorted the emperor back to the Fasting Palace, saying in Jurchen on horseback: "This is Heaven's mandate." On the 6th, the emperor was ordered to remove his yellow canopy, forbidden to ride, forbidden to ascend the steps — Xiao Qing read the deposition edict. Nianhan ordered his dragon robe torn off. Li Ruoshui rushed forward embracing the emperor: "This is the true Son of Heaven of the Great Dynasty! You dog-spawn dare not be insolent!" He was imprisoned, went on hunger strike, and Nianhan eventually ordered him beaten to death. The Jin lamented: "When Liao fell, a dozen ministers died for loyalty; when Song fell, only Li Ruoshui."

04
Retired Emperor Exits
"Papa, Mama, Please Come Quickly": A Gentle Kidnapping Disguised as Family Reunion
On the 7th of the 2nd month, four officials came to Huizong's residence claiming the Jin had relented — the Retired Emperor just needed to submit a petition at the Nanxun Gate complex to get the emperor back. "Papa, Mama, please come quickly — don't delay, or the opportunity will be lost." Huizong, suspicious, changed into Daoist robes and departed. At the Nanxun Gate, he found over 10,000 Jin cavalry lined up from the gate to Qingcheng and Liujia Temple. The Jin marshals mocked him: "You refused peace marriages — now everyone is a captive. What face do you have left?" Father and son embraced and wept at Qingcheng. That same day, Prince Ying and 3,000+ others — consorts, princesses, and their husbands — were marched out. On the 8th, the remaining princes exited. On the 11th, the Empress and Crown Prince departed. Sun Fu wept following the Crown Prince until Fan Qiong forcibly held him back.

05
The Logic of Replacement
"East Is Eating, West Is Eating": Why the Jin Chose Deposition Over Annexation
The Jin Emperor's decision was a cold calculation of governance costs. Hebei and Shanxi, adjacent to Jin territory, could and must be controlled. But Shaanxi, Sichuan, the Huai River basin, Jiangnan, and Lingnan were too vast for direct rule — occupation would only provoke endless resistance. The optimal solution: replace the emperor with a compliant puppet. Nianhan argued deposing the Zhao would weaken the south and leave room for future annexation. Wolibu wanted to keep Qinzong as a counterweight to the more formidable Prince Kang. Nianhan's view prevailed, and the Jin ordered Song officials to choose their own new ruler. Fan Qiong's explanation to the people was brutally pragmatic: "You've merely lost a master. East is eating, west is eating. It's like a military camp — if Zhang takes charge, call him Commander Zhang; if Li takes charge, call him Commander Li."

🔑 Key Takeaway
The essence of the Jingkang Incident was not Jin brutality but the total systemic collapse of an empire in crisis. Qinzong's naivety during his second exit — "just wait for clear weather to play polo and I'll be back" — formed the sharpest contrast with the deposition edict already on its way. The Zhao father and son's repeated betrayals of trust, military incompetence, bureaucratic evasion, gold and silver shortfalls, hopelessly delayed territorial handovers — all convinced the Jin to cut their losses: replace the ruling house. Li Ruoshui's martyrdom was the sole bright spot in this catastrophe, yet ironically his advice (that the emperor should go out) had helped precipitate the disaster. When the Retired Emperor was tricked out, when 3,000 royals were marched north, when Fan Qiong declared "East is eating, west is eating," a dynasty's dignity had evaporated entirely. And Prince Kang Zhao Gou, far away in Xiangzhou — he was a faint lamp, but the only hope left after the Northern Song's extinction.
