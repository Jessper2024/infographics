The Elements of Style · Ch5「Words and Expressions Commonly Misused」

Precision Over Eloquence · Language truly works only when the writer resolves to express individual thought accurately Strunk catalogs dozens of commonly misused words — from outright unacceptable errors (like I did) to contested constructions (split infinitive) to words innocent in themselves but often misapplied (case, factor, feature). The cardinal rule: when you spot a problem, rewrite the entire sentence, never merely substitute one word.

This is the most practically useful chapter in The Elements of Style. Strunk dissects common misuses alphabetically — roughly 60 entries from All right to Worth while. He classifies them into three tiers: ① outright unacceptable usage (like for as, Different than); ② constructions with some support but generally inadvisable (split infinitive); ③ words innocent in themselves but frequently misapplied (case, factor, feature). The critical repair strategy: do not merely substitute a word — rewrite the entire sentence. This extends Rule 12 ("Use definite, specific, concrete language") and Rule 13 ("Omit needless words") into real-world practice.

01
Filler Words
Delete Case, Character, Nature, Factor, Feature — they contribute nothing new
Many words are pure structural fillers; delete them and the sentence grows stronger. Case means "an instance of something occurring," but in "In many cases the rooms were poorly ventilated," striking out "in many cases" yields "Many of the rooms were poorly ventilated" — same meaning, sharper delivery. Character appears in "acts of a hostile character," which is simply "hostile acts." Nature is equally superfluous. Factor and Feature are termed "hackneyed words": Heavy artillery has become an increasingly important factor → Heavy artillery has played a constantly larger part. System, too, is overused: Dayton has adopted the commission system of government → Dayton has adopted government by commission. Student body is "needless and awkward" — a member of the student body is just a student.

02
Confusable Pairs
Less/Fewer, Like/As, Compare to/with — the correct usage of commonly confused pairs
English contains numerous paired words whose subtle distinctions determine a sentence's accuracy. Less refers to quantity, Fewer to number: His troubles are less than mine (not as great) vs. His troubles are fewer than mine (not as numerous). Like governs nouns and pronouns; As governs phrases and clauses: never "He thought like I did" but "He thought as I did." Compare to points out resemblances between different classes (life compared to a pilgrimage); compare with points out differences between similar classes (Congress compared with Parliament). Effect (noun = result; verb = to bring about) must not be confused with affect (verb = to influence). Data is plural — These data were tabulated.
❌

Less misused / Like misused

He had less men → fewer men. He thought like I did → as I did. Different than is never permissible — use different from.

✅

Compare to vs with / Effect vs Affect

Life may be compared to a pilgrimage (analogy). Congress may be compared with Parliament (difference). Effect = result; Affect = influence.

03
Grammar Traps
Split infinitive, participle for verbal noun, Whom misuse — three persistent grammar errors
The split infinitive has existed since the fourteenth century, but the careful writer should avoid it. "to diligently inquire" should become "to inquire diligently." A subtler error is using a participle instead of a gerund with possessive: "Do you mind me asking a question?" should be "Do you mind my asking a question?" — the possessive is needed because we mean "a prospect of asking," not "a prospect of me." Whom is frequently misused as the subject of a following verb: "His brother, whom he said would send him the money" should be "who he said." Avoid using While as a mere substitute for and, but, or although — when in doubt, use a semicolon.

04
Cliché Traps
One of the most, Interesting, He is a man who — cliché expressions that drain all vitality
Avoid opening paragraphs with "One of the most..." — the sentence is not wrong, but it is stale and feeble. "He is a man who is very ambitious" is a classic redundancy; simply say "He is very ambitious." Likewise, "Spain is a country which I have always wanted to visit" becomes "I have always wanted to visit Spain." Using "Interesting" as an opener is among the worst habits: "An interesting story is told of..." — you should make people find what follows interesting, not tell them it is. Tell the story without preamble. Along these lines, Worth while, and Respective/Respectively should also be discarded or used with extreme restraint.
⚠️

Expressions to avoid entirely

One of the most… as opener. An interesting story is told of… (tell it!). Along these lines. He is a man who…

📝

Correct approach

State directly and concisely: I have always wanted to visit Spain. Rewrite wordy constructions. Replace vague "worth while" with specifics.

05
Overused Intensifiers
Very, Certainly, So, Literal — overused intensifiers weaken, not strengthen, your expression
Use the word Very sparingly! Where emphasis is necessary, use words strong in themselves. Strunk's verdict on Very is one word: "sparingly!" Some writers abuse Certainly to emphasize every sentence — a habit that is bad in speech and worse in writing. In formal prose, avoid using So as an intensifier (so good, so warm). Literal/Literally is frequently misused in extravagant figures of speech — "a literal flood of abuse" and "literally dead with fatigue" are not literal at all. Most must not be used for almost — "most everybody" → "almost everybody." Likewise, excessive But leads to the loose-sentence fault discussed in Rule 14 — "one But after another" is the feeblest construction.
🛑
Very
"Sparingly!" — Replace with words inherently strong. Don't write "very good"; find a word that means "excellent."
🛑
Certainly
"Abusing certainly to emphasize every sentence" is a bad habit — worse in writing than in speech.
🛑
Literal / Most / But
Literal ≠ exaggeration. Most ≠ almost. Avoid stringing But after But. Simplify to strengthen prose.
🔑 Core Takeaway
Precision in English writing comes not from knowing which words you "may" use but from knowing which you should not. Cut filler words — case, character, nature, factor, feature, system. Master the distinctions: less vs. fewer, like vs. as, compare to vs. with. Fix split infinitives. Abandon the stale clichés like "One of the most…" and "An interesting story…" Stop leaning on very and certainly for empty emphasis. And above all: when you discover a sentence has gone wrong, rewrite the whole thing — never just swap out a single word. This is Rules 12 and 13 in live combat.
