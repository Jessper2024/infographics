大腦減壓的子彈筆記術 · Chapter 6 "How to Optimize the Time Management System"

Context Labels + Auto-Perspective + Notebook Architecture: Choice Over Management

After the system is built, what truly needs optimization is not "managing more" but "choosing less." We always want, need, and must do more than available time allows. This chapter's core question: How can the system help you make better "goal choices" and "action choices"? The author answers through three optimizations: ① "Progress labels" replace abstract importance judgment with process stages (Want → Need → Satisfied); ② "Context labels" match actions to real situations rather than fixed clock times; ③ "Auto-perspective" uses Evernote search syntax to quickly focus on the few tasks suited to the current context. The chapter also reveals how just 8 notebooks can manage everything — notebooks are for "workflow management" not "category storage."

01
Only Two Levers
You Can Truly Control Only Two Things
The project system already houses all tasks and actions, but the ultimate executor is human. What we can truly control is not the number of projects, the occurrence of surprises, or the passage of time — but two things: ① The brave "goal choice" we make for ourselves — what is truly most important right now? ② The "action" we decide to take under various time contexts — what best suits this moment? The ultimate value of time management is not a perfect schedule, but "doing what best fits this moment, while steadily walking toward your goals."

02
Progress Thinking
Replace 'Abstract Importance' with 'Process Progress' Judgment
We often struggle to judge 'what is important' because importance is a vague concept. But if we convert importance into 'process progress,' it becomes clear. The author uses a shopping wishlist example with three progress labels: ① 'Want' — still evaluating pros/cons, judging needs; ② 'Need' — confirmed purchase, awaiting the right moment; ③ 'Satisfied' — completed, occasionally checking utility. When you want to reward yourself, open the 'Need' label directly, exclude all distractions, and only buy what you've already judged worthy. This is 'replacing importance thinking with progress thinking.'

03
Goal Progress Labels
Design Process Labels for Projects: Important / Planning / Tracking
Applying progress thinking to project management, the author designed three core labels: ① 'Important' — projects due in coming months or currently most important life projects; only 'project master directory notes' get this label, not scattered tasks — daily attention to just ~20 notes; ② 'Planning' — projects still in conception, not yet with timelines — check occasionally, push progress; ③ 'Tracking' — completed project results — review periodically for extended benefits. Three labels together filter the ~20 projects down to the most critical targets.

04
Gap-time Contexts
Design Your Gap Time: From Passive Waiting to Active Utilization
You can't schedule when gap time appears, but you can 'design it.' List your recurring daily contexts — early morning, breakfast, commute, lunch break, dinner, family time, bedtime — then design dedicated labels and task banks for each. Example: 'Solo Free' label for games and reading, 'Couple Time' for movies with your spouse, 'With Kids' for family activities. When the context arises, open the corresponding label and know what to prioritize. Don't be pushed by time — let context guide action.

05
Perspective + Notebooks
Auto-Perspective + 8 Notebooks: Radically Streamlined Management
Above label classification, use Evernote search syntax to create 'auto-perspectives' — one-click filtering of tasks for a specific context. For example, searching 'any: tag:!Important tag:!Planning tag:!Tracking' shows all active projects. For notebooks, the author uses only 8: Inbox, Work Tasks, Life Tasks, Blog Tasks, Second Brain, Important Abandoned, Work Archive, Life Archive. Notebooks aren't for classification (that's labels' job) but for 'workflow management' — organized by action context: open Work Tasks at the office, Life Tasks at home, Blog Tasks when writing. 8 notebooks manage everything.

💡 核心要点
The essence of time management optimization is not managing more, but choosing less. When you convert "importance" into "process progress" (Important → Planning → Tracking), "schedules" into "context matching" (gap-time context labels), and "classification" into "workflow management" (8 notebooks), the system ceases to be a warehouse — it becomes a second brain that auto-filters noise, pushes focal points, and guides action. All you need to do is open the corresponding label or perspective in each moment, and execute with focus.
