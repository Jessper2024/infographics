Part III · Chapter 6 · Journey into the Microcosmos From Democritus' atomic philosophy to modern quantum mechanics, humanity descended a staircase into the inner structure of matter. The Greeks believed in four elements; alchemists dreamed of turning iron into gold; today we know 92 natural elements, each precisely assembled from nucleons and electrons. The oil-film experiment, molecular beams, X-ray photography—every experiment says the same thing: atoms and molecules truly exist.

This chapter traces the cognitive staircase humanity descended in probing the inner structure of matter. From the Greek four-element theory to Democritus' atomic hypothesis, from the oil-film experiment measuring molecular size to Stern's molecular beam measuring molecular velocity, from Bragg's X-ray photography "seeing" molecules to Rutherford's discovery of the nucleus, and Bohr-Heisenberg's quantum mechanics—each step deeper into the foundations of matter.

01
Conceptual Evolution
From Four Elements to 92: Alchemy's Right and Wrong
Democritus proposed the "atom" (indivisible unit); Empedocles believed the world was made of four elements: earth, water, air, and fire. Medieval alchemists tried to add more "fire atoms" to iron to produce gold—from their perspective, as reasonable as modern chemists synthesizing rubber. Their error was viewing metals as compounds, not elements. Yet without these futile attempts, we might never know metals are elemental. In reality, smelting iron is not combining earth and fire atoms but separating oxygen atoms from iron oxide molecules—atom splitting, not combining.

02
Simple Experiment
Oil Film: Anyone Can See Evidence of Molecules
1 mm³ of oil can cover ~1 m² of water; stretched to breaking point = a single molecular layer. Fill a shallow container with water, add one drop of pure oil, and push the film with a wire. The film will first show rainbow colors (interference of light reflected from top and bottom surfaces), shifting from red→yellow→green→blue→violet as thickness decreases, then colors vanish but the surface remains "dimmer" than clean water. Continue until rupture—this thickness is the oil molecule diameter: ~10⁻⁷ cm. A method Democritus could have devised 2,300 years ago. Each oil molecule contains multiple atoms, making atoms even smaller.

03
Experimental Evidence
Molecular Beams & Atomic Photography: Seeing Is Believing
Stern's molecular beam device proved: at 200°C, sodium atoms travel at 1.5 km/s, speed increasing with temperature. This provided the most direct basis for thermodynamics—heat is simply intensified random thermal motion of molecules. Even more convincing: Bragg's X-ray atomic photography. Since molecular spacing is merely 10⁻⁸ cm and visible light wavelengths are far too long, X-rays must be used. Based on Abbe's microscope imaging theory, Bragg captured numerous X-ray band patterns from different angles and superimposed them on photographic paper—like multi-color plate printing to synthesize a molecular image. The carbon ring structure of hexamethylbenzene molecules is clearly visible.

04
Anatomy of the Atom
Dissecting the Atom: From Watermelon to Micro Solar System
Thomson discovered the electron (1/1840 hydrogen mass) and proposed the "watermelon model": positive charge uniformly distributed, electrons embedded like seeds. In 1911, Rutherford bombarded aluminum foil with α particles and found occasional large deflections—meaning the atom's positive charge and mass are concentrated in a "nucleus" thousands of times smaller than the atom. New picture: atom = micro solar system, nucleus = sun (99.97% mass), electrons = planets. Electric attraction, like gravity, follows the inverse-square law. Hydrogen has 1 electron, helium 2, lithium 3…uranium 92. Mendeleev's periodicity arises from stable electron shell structures: 2, 8, 8, 18, 18…

05
Uncertainty
Micro-Mechanics: The Vanishing Trajectory & Uncertainty Principle
If electrons followed planetary mechanics, they would lose all energy through electromagnetic radiation and crash into the nucleus within 0.01 microseconds—yet atoms are extremely stable. The root problem: classical mechanical concepts of "trajectory" and "velocity" break down when applied to particles billions of times lighter than cannonballs. Quantum mechanics reveals: any interaction between material bodies has a lower limit—the "quantum of action." Observation becomes part of the motion itself; precise mathematical trajectories must be replaced by diffuse bands of finite thickness. This parallels geometric optics where infinitely thin light rays, upon encountering a pinhole (≈wavelength), no longer travel straight but fan out in diffraction. De Broglie and Schrödinger's wave mechanics treats particle motion as continuous wave-like distributions, perfectly explaining Stern's atomic diffraction experiments.

🔑 Key Takeaway
From Democritus' philosophical atom to Rutherford's micro solar system, to Heisenberg's uncertainty principle, material science descended a staircase probing ever deeper. Each step overturned part of prior knowledge—elements grew from 4 to 92, atoms from indivisible to dissectible, Newtonian mechanics from absolute truth to macroscopic approximation. But one thing never changed: science arrives at truth through experiment, not prior reasoning. Oil films, molecular beams, X-rays, α-particle scattering—each hands-on experiment answers the same question: what is matter made of?
