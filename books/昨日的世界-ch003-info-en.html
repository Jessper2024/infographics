The World of Yesterday · Ch.3 "The First Stirrings of Desire"

That "Moral" Era Created the Deepest Hypocrisy Through Silence and Concealment Zweig reveals Victorian sexual morality: a society that locked the devil out the front door, only to let him in through the chimney. Women were imprisoned in ignorant greenhouses; men ventured in the underworld; prostitution formed the dark basement of a grand façade.

This chapter offers a profound analysis of late 19th-century bourgeois sexual morality. Zweig reveals a deeply contradictory society: absolute silence in public, turbulent undercurrents in private. Women were shielded from reality from birth to marriage; men groped through fear and guilt. Clothing became morality's external symbol — corsets, high collars, and layers of fabric actually accentuated what they tried to conceal. The army of prostitutes formed the dark basement of a magnificent society, and the terror of venereal disease haunted every young person's青春. Compared with the free post-war generation, Zweig felt both relief and a faint nostalgia for the mysterious allure born of prohibition.

Moral Dilemma
Victorian Hypocrisy: Lock the Devil Out, He Enters Through the Chimney
19th-century bourgeois morality invented a compromise: it didn't forbid young people's sexual lives but demanded they be handled invisibly. Society formed a united front of secrecy — schools, churches, salons, courts, press, and books all avoided the topic. Even science shamefully participated in the cover-up. Charcot, Freud's mentor, knew the true causes of certain patients' conditions but never dared reveal them. Yet the more society concealed, the more curiosity it激发 — underground pornography flourished.

Women's Prison
Hothouse Flowers: From Birth to Marriage, Entirely Cut Off from Reality
A girl from an upper-class family had to live in a completely sterile atmosphere from birth, forbidden any knowledge of the male body. A governess watched her constantly; every book was inspected; her days were filled with piano, singing, painting, and languages — yet she knew nothing of the most natural things. One aunt fled back to her parents on her wedding night, calling her husband a "madman" — because he seriously tried to undress her. An unmarried woman of twenty-five became an "old maid," mocked by satirical magazines — yet it was society that had suppressed all her natural needs.

03
Fashion Code
Clothing as Morality: How Corsets and High Collars Exposed What They Concealed
Every century's clothing naturally reflects its moral values — 1900s fashion made future audiences roar with laughter. Women wore whale-bone corsets that cinched waists like wasps, collars buttoned to the chin, hair piled into towering structures. The true meaning: conceal every curve so the groom couldn't predict his bride's real figure. Yet the deliberate emphasis on "masculine strength" and "feminine fragility" actually increased sexual attraction — this psychologically counterproductive approach backfired completely.

04
Underworld
The Prostitute Army: The Dark Basement of Bourgeois Grandeur
Prostitution formed the ceiling of the dark basement beneath bourgeois society's magnificent edifice, upon which stood the pure, luxurious, immaculate façade. On the street, a woman could be bought for a few crowns, "as simple as buying a pack of cigarettes." Prostitutes were divided into "fortress artillery" (fixed streets), "cavalry and infantry" (street-walkers), and high-class brothels. The law classified prostitution as a state-licensed profession yet placed prostitutes outside ordinary legal rights — if a man refused to pay after, she couldn't sue. It was precisely these women, punished and restricted, who upheld the old morality against free love.
A society that has sinned by dishonestly suppressing human nature always treats most cruelly those who expose its secrets to the light of day.
05
Generational Contrast
From Fear to Freedom: The Enormous Gulf Between Two Generations
Post-war youth walked with heads held high and bright eyes, boys and girls together naturally — to Zweig's generation, it seemed like a thousand years had passed. Yet Zweig also faintly felt that the shyness born of prohibition had its own mysterious charm. "Only what is withheld creates strong desire; the more forbidden, the more fiercely one craves it." Young people no longer needed to lie; they could express natural feelings honestly — the greatest happiness of all. But the inexplicable thrill of forbidden encounters had vanished forever.

🔑 Core Conclusion
19th-century sexual morality was collective hypocrisy: absolute silence in public, turbulent undercurrents in private. Women were locked in ignorant greenhouses; men groped through fear and guilt; the prostitute army upheld the grand façade's respectability. The more society concealed, the more it激发 curiosity and rebellion. Zweig was both glad that a new generation gained freedom, and faintly nostalgic for the mysterious allure born of prohibition — "the more forbidden something is, the more fiercely one craves it."
