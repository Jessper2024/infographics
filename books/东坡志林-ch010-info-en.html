Dongpo Zhilin · Ch.10「Portraits of a Chaotic Age—The Spiritual Predicament of Wei-Jin Elites」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer From late Han through Wei-Jin to the Northern and Southern Dynasties, Chinese literati faced unprecedented existential dilemmas. How to balance loyalty and filial piety? Which matters more—honor or survival? Is \"reclusion\" genuine liberation or mere escape? Is \"unbridled behavior\" personal liberation or moral decay? In the second half of his \"Figures\" section, Su Shi turns his gaze toward this most turbulent era. He sees Xi Chao's complex humanity—betraying the court while devising a posthumous \"heart-rescue plan\" for his father. He sees the tragedy of Huan Fan and Chen Gong serving mediocre masters. He sees Guo Wen's transcendence—\"no memories, therefore no feelings\"—alongside Wang Ji's depravity—\"steaming pork in human milk.\" How does Su Shi understand these souls caught in the cracks of chaos? His judgment is not simple right-or-wrong, but a deeper \"situational ethics\"—place the person in his time, then weigh his choices.

This chapter collects the second half of Su Shi's \"Figures\" section—fourteen character studies: Zhu Bo & Xunzhi, Liu Cong & the Wu Recluse, Xi Chao's Posthumous Rescue, On Huan Fan and Chen Gong, Wen Qiao Questions Guo Wen, Liu Ling, Fang Guan at Chentao, Zhang Hua's Wren Rhapsody, Wang Ji & Wang Kai, Wang Yifu, Wei Guan's Failed Coup, Pei Wei Faces Emperor Wu, Liu Ningzhi & Shen Linshi, and Liu Zongyuan's Reckless Falsehoods. From Jin dynasty recluses to Tang dynasty historians, Su Shi applies \"situational ethics\" to pierce through the fog of turbulent times.

01
Reading Character
One Word Reveals the Man—Sketches of Zhu Bo, Liu Cong, and the Wu Recluse
Meeting Zhu Bo and Xunzhi at Yingzhou, the conversation turns to Luoyang's skill at grafting flowers—\"new branches every year, chrysanthemums most numerous.\" Xunzhi says: \"Chrysanthemums should be yellow as the orthodox color; all others are base.\" Su Shi instantly cites an ancient precedent: \"Long ago, Shuxiang heard one word from Zong Mie and understood his character. For Xunzhi, I say the same.\" Respecting \"yellow as orthodox\" reveals a value system prioritizing canonical authority. On Liu Cong and the Wu recluse, Su Shi's brush is colder: Liu Cong, hearing he would become king of the Zhe'xu Kingdom after death, \"no longer feared dying\"—\"A man's love of wealth and rank can exceed his love of life.\" Conversely, when the moon encroached on the Shaowei constellation, the Wu recluse \"sought death but could not attain it\"—\"A man's love of fame can exceed his love of life.\" Two strokes capture humanity's most absurd dimensions.

02
Loyalty vs. Filiality
Xi Chao's Posthumous Rescue & the Tragedy of Wrong Masters—Impossible Choices
Xi Chao is the most complex figure here. He was Huan Wen's trusted confidant, party to usurpation plots—yet his father Xi Yin was loyal to the Jin throne and knew nothing. On his deathbed, Xi Chao handed a box of secret letters to a disciple with instructions: \"I'd wanted to burn these, but feared my father, at his age, would harm himself in grief over losing me. If after my death he cannot eat or sleep, show him this box. If not, burn it.\" As predicted, Xi Yin was devastated—until he read the treasonous letters. \"That boy died too late!\" he raged, ceasing to mourn. Su Shi's verdict is exquisitely nuanced: \"Had Xi Chao understood the filiality of a gentleman, he would never have followed Huan Wen. I say: Xi Chao's was the filiality of a petty man.\" A lesser filiality—not none. Huan Fan and Chen Gong represent another tragedy: Huan Fan's brilliance wasted on Cao Shuang's short-sightedness; Chen Gong's strategies ignored by Lü Bu. Su Shi's judgment: \"Cao Shuang and Lü Bu—what kind of men were they? To serve such wretches—what wisdom can one speak of?\"
That boy died too late!
—Xi Yin upon reading his son's treasonous letters · Su Shi: \"the filiality of a petty man\"
03
Reclusion & Transcendence
Guo Wen's \"No Feelings\" & Liu Ling's \"Bury Me\"—The Inner Life of Recluses
Wen Qiao asks the hermit Guo Wen: \"You have abandoned parents, siblings, wife, children—what joy is there?\" Guo Wen's answer is devastatingly honest: \"I originally set out to study the Way. I did not expect the world to fall into chaos, and now there is no road home.\" Reclusion was not a choice—but homelessness. Wen Qiao presses: \"Do you alone have no feelings?\" Guo Wen's reply defines emotion with surgical precision: \"Feelings arise from memory. Without memory, there are no feelings.\" This is the most incisive psychological definition of detachment ever recorded. Su Shi personally visited Guo Wen's hermitage at Dadong Cave Heaven—\"a vast abyss, depth unfathomable.\" As for Liu Ling—the world's exemplar of nonchalance, ordering a servant with a shovel to follow him: \"When I die, bury me right there.\" Su Shi rejects this: \"Liu Ling was not a transcendent man. Coffins and burial clothes do not impede true transcendence. If not, dead is dead—why must one be buried?\" The very command \"bury me\" reveals a lingering concern with posthumous affairs. True transcendence needs no burial.

04
The Dark Side of Elites
Zhang Hua's Paradox & Wang Ji's Depravity—The Other Jin Dynasty
Su Shi's critique of Jin dynasty elites is more ferocious than any predecessor. Zhang Hua wrote the \"Wren Rhapsody\" expressing a philosophy of avoiding calamity through non-ambition—Ruan Ji praised it as \"the talent of a king's counselor!\" But Zhang Hua couldn't even protect himself—ignoring Liu Bian's warnings, he was caught in the Jia clan's disaster, and despite fearing the Eight Princes' rebellion, died at Sima Lun and Sun Xiu's hands. \"This is precisely the fault of seeking perfect safety—betraying the 'Wren Rhapsody's' true meaning.\" The more one seeks safety, the less one finds it. Wang Ji and Wang Kai's extravagance is pure horror: Wang Ji \"steamed pork in human milk.\" Wang Kai \"ordered a female musician flogged to death for a minor pitch error; ordered a beauty to serve wine, and if a guest didn't finish, killed the beauty too.\" Su Shi's judgment chills: \"At the time, Emperor Wu was still on the throne—yet nobles dared such acts. One knows Jin's chaos was already long brewing.\"

05
Historical Judgment
From Wang Yifu to Liu Zongyuan—Virtue, Conspiracy, and the Truth of History
Wang Yifu surrendered to Shi Le and urged him to proclaim himself emperor. Su Shi's single cold line: \"Thus I know Wang Yifu's death—it was not only shame before Jin ministers, but shame before his own daughter.\" Wang's daughter Huifeng, consort to Crown Prince Minhuai, faced a general who wanted to marry her—she \"grasped a sword and died cursing him.\" The daughter's steel exposes the father's clay. Wei Guan wanted to depose the idiot Crown Prince (later Emperor Hui) but lacked courage. At a banquet, drunk, he patted the throne and said \"This seat is a pity!\"—Su Shi criticizes: \"What words were these, spoken in public? Is this not the 'loose tongue that costs a life'?\" The secret should have been kept; spoken publicly, it invited death. Pei Wei flattered Emperor Wu by interpreting a divination—and the histories recorded this as praiseworthy. Su Shi erupts: \"Gentlemen considered it shameful, yet history treats it as splendid! Despicable!\" In the contrast between Liu Ningzhi and Shen Linshi, Su Shi offers a golden rule of conduct: Liu, when his shoes were mistaken for another's, gave them away and refused their return. Shen, when the same happened, laughed and said: \"Not your shoes?\" and accepted them back with a smile. \"Though a small matter, in conduct one should be like Linshi, not Ningzhi.\" Refusing too absolutely is inhuman rigidity; accepting an apology gracefully is the higher realm. Finally, Liu Zongyuan: Su Shi flatly declares \"Liu Zongyuan dared reckless falsehoods.\" Liu claimed the people of two prefectures wept for months at Lü Wen's death—more than for the sage prime minister Zi Chan. He praised Lü Gong as a worthy hero, then noted Lü Gong's wife was Pei Yanling's daughter. \"What gentleman would willingly be Pei Yanling's son-in-law?\"—Su Shi implies Liu's own associational guilt with Wang Pi and Wang Shuwen.
Though a small matter, in conduct one should be like Linshi, not Ningzhi.
—Su Shi · Life philosophy: gracefully accept an apology rather than rigidly refuse
TAKEAWAY · Core Insight
If the first half of Su Shi's \"Figures\" section systematically demolishes every sage-king myth with skeptical intelligence, this second half is his deep inquiry into \"situational ethics in times of chaos.\" He sees the human contradiction in Xi Chao's \"petty man's filiality\"—a man who politically betrayed but emotionally protected. He finds Guo Wen's most precise definition of emotion: \"Feelings arise from memory.\" Through Shen Linshi's \"laughing and accepting\" the returned shoes, he delivers one of the subtlest lessons in Chinese life philosophy—not steadfast refusal, but soft acceptance of apology. Su Shi's character studies never offer simple moral answers. He gives us real human reactions in complex situations—and reality is more powerful than morality.
