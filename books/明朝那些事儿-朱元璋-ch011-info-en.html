Chapter 10 "Waiting for the Right Moment"

Forging the Perfect Trap — Chen Youliang Builds Unmatched Warships; Zhu Yuanzhang Waits Patiently for the Decisive Hour

Situation: After his defeat at Longwan, Chen Youliang built a three-layer iron-armored flagship and dominated the Yangtze with 600,000 troops from 1361 onward. Yet by killing Zhao Pusheng, he lost his army's soul — soldiers now fought only for pay, not for ideals. Conflict: In February 1363, Zhang Shicheng attacked Anfeng, trapping Han Lin'er and Liu Futong. Zhu faced a brutal choice. Question: Should he risk dividing his forces to rescue Anfeng while Chen prepared his final assault? Answer: Against Liu Bowen's fierce objections, Zhu marched north — exactly the opening Chen had been waiting for. Chen mobilized 600,000 troops and prepared his iron-fleet armada for the final strike.

01
Arms Race
Chen Youliang's "Aircraft Carriers"

After Longwan's defeat, Chen Youliang built towering three-deck iron-armored warships — the most powerful naval force of the era — believing technology alone could crush Zhu Yuanzhang.

Each warship stood several stories tall with three fighting decks. The lower deck powered oars uninterrupted by fighting above; iron plating wrapped the entire hull, making it nearly invulnerable. Chen also pioneered soundproofing — a single wooden floor separated decks, so sounds from battle above never reached rowers below. This prevented panic and maintained discipline. He placed absolute faith in firepower and scale, unaware that the strongest weapon was never material — it was the hearts of men.

02
Loss of Morale
Killing Zhao Pusheng: Chen's Army Lost Its Soul

By murdering Zhao Pusheng to secure total control of Tianwan, Chen Youliang destroyed the loyalty and ideals of his own army — soldiers now fought only for wages.

Zhao Pusheng was a legendary dual-blade commander who always led from the front. Chen had him executed on trumped-up charges of treason, eliminating his last rival. From that moment, Tianwan's soldiers no longer fought for a cause — they fought for pay. An army without ideals is fundamentally fragile. Chen achieved total control but paid with his army's fighting spirit. He would pay the full price for this bargain at the decisive battle.

03
Fatal Decision
The Anfeng Trap: Zhu Yuanzhang's Costly Mistake

Ignoring Liu Bowen's urgent warnings, Zhu marched to rescue Anfeng — walking directly into Chen Youliang's meticulously laid trap.

Zhu's reasoning seemed flawless: he owed loyalty to Han Lin'er as nominal emperor, and Anfeng was Ying Tian's northern shield. But Liu Bowen demolished both arguments in turn: Han Lin'er had served his purpose and could not be managed even if rescued; losing Anfeng was minor compared to the disaster if Chen attacked Ying Tian while Zhu's main force was away. Liu even physically grabbed Zhu's sleeve to stop him. Zhu trusted his instincts and pressed on. The error was about to cost everything.

04
Perfect Ambush
Chen's Grand Design: Exhaust Zhu Before the Final Strike

Chen Youliang did not attack Ying Tian while Zhu was away — he was preparing something far larger: to let Zhu exhaust himself completely, then crush him in one decisive battle.

Hongdu's (Nanchang) defection enraged Chen, but he restrained himself and methodically prepared. When Zhu returned from Anfeng burdened with Han Lin'er, Chen still did not strike. He waited until Zhu, harassed by Zhang Shicheng's guerrilla tactics, made his worst error: attacking Luzhou — walking into the trap. Chen had mobilized 600,000 elite troops and his invincible iron fleet. The net was closing.

05
Eve of Battle
Two Armies March Toward Their Destined Battlefield

In April 1363, Chen Youliang led 600,000 troops from Jiangzhou toward Hongdu; Zhu Yuanzhang rushed back to Ying Tian — both armies heading for their fated confrontation.

Zhu returned from Anfeng with disappointment and an unwanted burden. Chen stood in his palace making final preparations. Two armies were moving toward the same inevitable collision. Zhu had 200,000 elite troops and the finest generals. Chen had 600,000 men, an unbeatable fleet, and a vow of no retreat. Both men understood: this battle would decide who held the Mandate of Heaven. The greatest gamble in Chinese history was about to begin.

🔑 Core Conclusion
Waiting for the perfect moment requires both strategic patience and the willingness to absorb setbacks. Chen Youliang spent three months building history's most powerful naval armada — 600,000 troops and iron-armored three-deck warships. Zhu Yuanzhang, meanwhile, made a costly error at Anfeng against Liu Bowen's counsel, playing directly into Chen's trap. History shows: timing is harder than courage — recklessly moving when an opponent sharpens their blade usually means walking into their trap. As both armies converged in mid-1363, China's fate hung on a single decisive battle.
