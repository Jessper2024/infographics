01
Introduction Notes
The Reliability of Analogy as a Mode of Thought—From Classical to Modern
The Introduction's notes sketch the philosophical lineage of analogical thinking from antiquity to modernity. Plato in The Sophist discusses the elusiveness of similarity; Nietzsche defines intelligence as "a mobile army of metaphors"; Hobbes locates "the light of human minds" in "perspicuous words"—these classical formulations lay the theoretical foundations for the entire book. Bachelard's The Formation of the Scientific Mind and Bouveresse's Prodiges et vertiges de l'analogie discuss, from modern perspectives, the reliability of analogy as a mode of thinking. Meanwhile, Danto's and Samaras's studies of chairs reveal the astonishing diversity of categories—how many forms can a single chair take? This question goes straight to the heart of categorization.
📜

Classical Thinkers on Analogy

Plato: similarity is an elusive thing. Nietzsche: a mobile army of metaphors. Hobbes: the light of human minds lies in perspicuous words. Ancient thinkers already perceived analogy's profound influence on thought.

🪑

How Many Forms Can a Chair Take?

Danto's 397 Chairs, Fiell's 1000 Chairs—these studies demonstrate the astonishing diversity a single category can accommodate, while people effortlessly classify them all as "chairs."

02
Chapters 1–3 Notes
Words · Phrases · Hidden Analogies—Core Literature of Cognitive Science
The notes for the first three chapters focus on concept development, lexical access, and the unconscious nature of analogy. Wittgenstein's "family resemblance" concept launched modern categorization research; the work of Barsalou, Medin, Goldstone, and others established contemporary categorization theory; Boroditsky, Lakoff, and Johnson revealed metaphor's systematic role in thought; Gentner, Holyoak, and Thagard established analogy's central place in cognitive science. Notably, La Fontaine's fables (the fox and the grapes, etc.) are cited repeatedly, showing how the same "category" can be expressed in different forms across different cultures and languages—a vivid demonstration that analogy is everywhere.

03
Chapters 4–6 Notes
Abstract Slippage · Manipulation by Analogy · Two-Way Perspective—Experiments and Historical Cases
The middle chapters' notes cover abstraction processes, analogy's unconscious manipulation of thought, and cases of humans actively deploying analogy for persuasion. Borges's short story "Funes the Memorious" demonstrates what happens when a person cannot abstract—cannot build analogies between concrete instances—their mind is drowned by the flood of specificity. Edward Teller's biography reveals how metaphor becomes a scientist's everyday thinking tool ("as an exile, also a physicist…"). Khong's classic study of the "Munich analogy" and "domino theory" in Vietnam War decision-making shows how analogies exert enormous influence at the highest levels of political decision-making—often without the decision-makers themselves being aware.
📖

Borges: The Man Who Couldn't Abstract

Funes remembered every detail of every leaf, yet could not recognize the category "dog"—because he couldn't build analogies between instances. No abstraction = no thought.

🏛️

Khong: The Munich Analogy

In Vietnam War decision-making, the "Munich analogy" (appeasement = disaster) and "domino theory" dominated American policymakers' thinking, almost none of whom realized they were being manipulated by analogy.

04
Chapters 7–8 Notes
Naive Analogies and Earth-Shaking Analogies—From Math Education to the Physics Revolution
The notes for the final two chapters are the most academically dense in the book. Chapter 7 cites historical documents such as Recorde (the mathematician who invented the equals sign in 1557) and Bezout (19th-century textbook definitions of division and multiplication as naive operations), showing how naive analogies are passed down through generations of textbooks, lodged deep in the public mind. Chapter 8 traces Einstein's original 1905 light-quantum paper, alongside Poincaré's classic statement that "the mathematician's guide is chiefly analogy." The contrast between the two carries enormous tension: the same cognitive mechanism—analogy—is both the naive trap that ensnares non-mathematicians for life, and the earth-shaking force that guided Einstein to the discovery of light quanta.
1
Recorde (1557): The historical document inventing the "=" sign—the equals sign itself is a naive analogy: "two parallel lines, because no two things can be more equal."
2
Bezout (1833): 19th-century textbook definition of "division = equal sharing"—this naive analogy still circulates in classrooms worldwide today.
3
Einstein (1905, 1920): Original light-quantum paper and subsequent reflections—making analogies "with an intuition of the unity of the universe."
4
Ulam (1976): "Good mathematicians see analogies between theorems and theories; great mathematicians see analogies between analogies"—nested layers of analogical thinking.
05
Epilogue Notes
Categorization = Analogy-Making—The Epilogue's Documentary Support
The Epilogue's notes, though concise, pinpoint the book's most critical documentary support. Spalding and Murphy's "Categories allow people to treat new things as familiar" and Gick and Holyoak's "Analogy allows us to see new things as familiar"—these two sentences respectively define categorization and analogy-making, but read side by side, they describe the exact same function. Polya's "Analogy pervades our thinking," Oppenheimer's "Faced with novelty, we inevitably compare it with the familiar"—these statements from the finest minds in different fields all point to the same conclusion: categorization and analogy-making are fundamentally the same thing.
🎯

Two Sentences Define One Function

Spalding & Murphy: "Categories allow people to treat new things as familiar." Gick & Holyoak: "Analogy allows us to see new things as familiar."—Different terms, the same thing.

🌐

Cross-Disciplinary Consensus

Polya (mathematician), Oppenheimer (physicist), Gentner (psychologist), Holyoak (cognitive scientist)—top scholars from different disciplines pointing, in different languages, toward the same conclusion.

🔑 Core Takeaway
These notes are not a mere list of references but an interdisciplinary, cross-era intellectual map. From Plato's "similarity is an elusive thing" (4th century BCE) to Einstein's "intuition of the unity of the universe" (1905), to the experimental evidence of contemporary cognitive science—over two millennia of intellectual exploration converge on a single point: analogy is the core mechanism of human cognition. Every page of notes reminds the reader that this book is not the personal conjecture of two authors, but a systematic summation of centuries of global academic dialogue. The notes are the book's "source code"—through them, you can trace the academic roots of every argument and see the trajectory of ideas flowing from one mind to another.
