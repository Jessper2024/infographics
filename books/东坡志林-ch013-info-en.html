Dongpo Zhilin · Ch.13「The Art of Rule—Su Shi's Anatomy of Political Legitimacy」

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer If the previous twelve chapters were Su Shi roaming through landscapes and profiling historical figures, this chapter is his ultimate interrogation of what makes a regime legitimate. In Volume V, "Discussions of Antiquity," thirteen essays tear open truths that emperors preferred to bury: Was King Wu of Zhou a sage or a regicide? Was the Zhou dynasty's eastward relocation a strategic necessity or an act of self-destruction? Did Qin unify China through Heaven's mandate or through sheer luck? Was the abolition of feudalism a stroke of genius or simply the tide of history? Su Shi never accepts ready-made answers—he judges King Wu by Confucius's own standards, contrasts King Ping of Zhou with Wang Dao of Eastern Jin, and dissects Qin's conquest strategy with a tooth-pulling metaphor. Four essays, each a trial against "orthodox historical narrative."

01
THE SAGE DECONSTRUCTED
King Wu Was Not a Sage—Detonating the Confucian Orthodoxy
Su Shi opens with a thunderbolt: "King Wu was not a sage." He cites Confucius's silence as evidence—Confucius praised Yao and Shun lavishly, found nothing to fault in Yu, but on Tang and Wu merely said "perfectly beautiful, yet not perfectly good." Boyi and Shuqi refused to eat Zhou grain, and Confucius approved of them—this itself is a judgment against King Wu. Su Shi goes further, comparing the ideal ruler to King Wen: "To save the world through benevolence and righteousness, so that when the world is pacified, the sacred vessel comes of its own accord... if it does not come, do not take it." Had King Wen been alive, he would never have attacked King Zhou. What did King Wu do? "Kill the father, enfeoff the son. If the son is not human, fine; if he proves human, he must seek death." Enfeoffing Wu Geng after killing his father King Zhou—if Wu Geng doesn't rebel, he's not human; if he does, King Wu launched an enterprise doomed from the start. Mencius claimed, "I've heard of executing the tyrant Zhou, never of regicide." Su Shi pronounces: Mencius opened a Pandora's box—henceforth scholars accepted Tang and Wu as archetypal sages, "all criminals against the Confucian school."

02
RELOCATION = RUIN
The Zhou's Fatal Move East—How One Relocation Sealed Eight Centuries of Fate
Su Shi slices through history with a real estate metaphor: "A wealthy household bequeaths to its descendants only land and houses. If misfortune strikes, one may resort to begging, but one must never sell the ancestral estate." King Ping relocating to Luoyi—"abandoning the enterprise of Wen, Wu, Cheng, and Kang, this is one defeat that sold the family property." Then comes a cascade of historical evidence: Pangeng's relocation restored Yin's old capital; Gugong moved to Qi following pastureland; Duke Wen of Wei relocated relying on Qi—all "in times of strength, not out of fear." Every relocation to escape enemies led to ruin: "None did not perish; even those not immediately destroyed never recovered." When Chu, beset by famine, proposed relocation, it was vetoed; when Jin, ravaged by the Su Jun rebellion, proposed relocation, Wang Dao blocked it: "Jinling is the capital of kings. A king does not move his capital for prosperity or poverty." Returning to King Ping: "Had King Ping had one Wang Dao, he would have decided against relocation, gathered the remnant people of Feng and Hao, restored the policies of Wen, Wu, Cheng, and Kang... Qi and Jin, strong as they were, would not have dared to rebel—and how could Qin have become hegemon?" One relocation decision handed eight centuries of dominance to Qin.

03
STRATEGIC AUTOPSY
Qin's Clumsy Conquest of Chu—Luck, Not Destiny, Unified China
Su Shi delivers a startling verdict: "Qin's unification of the world did not come through the Way, merely through cunning; nor was it luck." Then he flips: "I believe they were cunning against Qi but clumsy against Chu—and that they were not defeated by Chu was sheer luck." He performs a frame-by-frame dissection of Qin's conquest strategy. Against Qi: "With Zhang Fa's talent, Qin attacked; with Jian's lack of talent, Qin did not attack—why?" For forty-plus years, Qin left Qi alone—not out of fear, but to use Qi as a wedge to dissolve the Three Jin alliance. Once the Three Jin fell, Qi was isolated—"The Qi people, blind to this, aligned with Qin, giving Qin the window to seize the Three Jin." Against Chu, it was a disaster: "Qin first sent Li Xin with 200,000 men to take Chu and failed; then sent Wang Jian with 600,000—essentially emptying the state to fight." Su Shi deploys a tooth-pulling metaphor: "Qin dismissed Chu as a baby tooth, ready to be pulled; they pried open the mouth and yanked—the child must be wounded, the fingers must be bitten." Had Qi possessed a competent ruler, it could have attacked while Qin's forces were drained in Chu—destroying Qin would have been as easy as turning over one's palm. Wu attacked Chu for three years before entering Ying; Jin subdued Wu step by step—only Fu Jian and Qin Shi Huang share the same pathology. "From this I deduce the two Qins share the same law: Shi Huang won through luck; Fu Jian was merely unlucky."

04
INSTITUTIONAL CHOICE
Qin Abolished Feudalism—Genius or the Tide of History?
Su Shi dismantles the "feudalism versus commandery-county" debate with breathtaking dialectical sophistication. He opens by citing Li Si: "The many enfeoffed relatives of King Wen and Wu, once their bloodlines grew distant, attacked one another like mortal enemies." Qin Shi Huang resolved: "The world has suffered constant warfare because of feudal lords." Su Shi's conclusion operates on two levels. Level one: "The sage cannot create the moment, but must not miss it." During the Three Dynasties, feudal lords could not be stripped of their lands without cause—this is "unable to create the moment." After the Zhou declined, abolishing fiefdoms was timely—Shi Huang and Li Si "did not miss it." Level two: "Feudalism is the source of conflict and the beginning of chaos. Since the age of writing, when has a minister killing his lord, a son killing his father, brothers slaughtering each other, ever NOT arisen from hereditary fiefdoms?" He caps it with Liu Zongyuan's On Feudalism: "Feudalism was never the sage's intent—it was the momentum of the age." The commandery-county system wasn't a stroke of genius; history arrived at that point, and the sage's task was merely to recognize the moment and act. Qin Shi Huang and Li Si, for all their flaws, did exactly that.
The sage cannot create the moment, but must not miss it. ... Once Shi Huang had unified the world, dividing it into commanderies and counties, appointing governors—this was as natural as winter furs and summer linen, fitting for the season, not the private insight of a single mind.
— Su Shi on Qin's abolition of feudalism
05
THE HIDDEN CHAIN
Connecting the Four Essays—The Master Key to Su Shi's Political Philosophy
These four essays are not isolated commentary but a rigorous logical chain. Essay one, King Wu Was Not a Sage, establishes Su Shi's first principle of political philosophy: seizing power through military conquest and executing the previous sovereign lacks moral legitimacy—the true sage follows King Wen's path, allowing the world to submit through virtue. Essay two, The Zhou's Fatal Eastward Relocation, demonstrates how a legitimate dynasty destroys its own foundation through relocation—legitimacy requires a geographic anchor (Feng-Hao); abandoning the ancestral seat equals abandoning the mandate. Essay three, Qin's Clumsy Conquest of Chu, exposes the fatal flaw in the Qin legitimacy myth—not the Way, not Heaven's mandate, but luck and the stupidity of opponents. Essay four, Qin Abolished Feudalism, answers the constructive question: if revolutionary seizure is illegitimate, how should institutions be designed?—seize the historical moment, replace feudalism with commanderies. Together they form Su Shi's complete answer to the ultimate question: what makes a regime legitimate, how is legitimacy maintained, and through what means is it lost?

KEY INSIGHT
Su Shi's philosophy of history is not moral sermonizing—it is a cold analysis of political legitimacy. He refuses the "winner writes history" narrative: King Wu's successful conquest does not make him a sage. He refuses fatalistic "Heaven's mandate" thinking: Qin's unification was cunning and luck, not the Way. The only historical law he accepts is the momentum of the times: "The sage cannot create the moment, but must not miss it." Doing the right thing at the right time—this is the entirety of sagely wisdom. Qin Shi Huang abolishing feudalism and instituting commanderies was "as natural as winter furs and summer linen"—not one man's genius but the necessity of an entire era. And King Ping's eastward relocation was a "miscalculation" not because he lacked virtue, but because he made an irreversible decision at the wrong moment. The ultimate question of governance is not morality, not destiny, but judgment of timing—miss that window, and an eight-century foundation can be reduced to ashes in a single relocation decree.
