1 Economic Analysis

The author uses a "car market" analogy for contemporary male-female relationships: new cars (bride prices) grow increasingly expensive; at new-car prices you might get a wreck with hidden damage; after purchase the car may "drive itself away" (divorce asset seizure). Renting or ride-hailing (prostitution) is punished by law. In this dilemma, dating married women is likened to "driving away the neighbor's car" — purchase cost, mortgage, and insurance all paid by someone else, and entirely legal.

Traditional Marriage Path

High bride price + high housing + divorce risk + one-sided loyalty obligation

Author's Alternative

Zero cost + legal + no support obligation + access to "wealthy men's wives"

3 The "Iron Cuckold Law"

The author introduces the concept of the "Iron Cuckold Law": in France and China, husbands are legally obligated to support children born from their wives' extramarital affairs — like a Qing dynasty "Iron Hat Prince," it can never be removed. The US allows husbands to disclaim paternity through DNA testing. China defaults to the Iron Cuckold system with supplementary compensation mechanisms.

4 Moral Analysis

The author argues moral legitimacy from three angles: single men's right to satisfy physiological needs when costs are prohibitive; women's suffering within marriage; and the husband's supposedly damaged rights lacking legal or moral support. The chapter cites Pew Research Center data showing Americans under 35 hold a historically low share of national wealth, arguing that "redistribution" through relationships with wealthy men's wives has moral justification.

5 Modern Chinese Values Critique

The author characterizes China's mainstream values as "conservative progressivism" — radically conserving century-old progressivism, essentially a "diluted Prussian-total-war value system." After ideals collapsed, it devolved into materialism. The author uses a Beijing-based Hunan sex worker's home-buying story and Guo Juyang's mansion-bragging as specific case studies.

"Looking at her once-familiar yet now strange face, like in The Great Gatsby, I felt her voice is full of money."

Key Takeaways

This chapter constructs a complete argumentation framework from four dimensions: economics (cost-benefit analysis), legal theory (legality of affairs, sexual autonomy, Iron Cuckold Law), moral philosophy (resource redistribution legitimacy), and value-system critique ("conservative progressivism" and materialism). Drawing on the French Declaration of Rights, New China's first Marriage Law, and Pew Research data, the conclusion points to: in the current social structure, dating married women is legal, morally defensible, and economically rational.
