The Gay Genius · Ch.16 "The Eastern Slope Recluse"

After escaping death, how a poet became farmer, hermit, and philosopher

Banishment to Huangzhou, paradoxically, allowed Su Dongpo to find his true self. Stripped from being a capital minister to a disgraced farmer, he tilled wasteland east of the city and called himself "Dongpo Jushi" — the Recluse of the Eastern Slope. He watched the great river flow past his cottage, sat in silent meditation at the Anguo Temple, and divided his monthly expenses into thirty packets hung from the roof beam. Core question: What remains of a person when every social identity is stripped away?

01
IDENTITY
Shedding the Armor: From Minister to Farmer
Social identity is a "trap" — remove it, and the true self emerges. As Lin Yutang wrote: "Society, culture, classical studies, external duties and responsibilities hide a person's true nature. Remove these traps of circumstance and tradition, and the genuine self appears." Su in Huangzhou was like a seal returning to water — a seal flopping on land is only half a seal.
🏛️

Past Identity

Capital minister, literary titan, top-ranked scholar. All external glory, rank and fame — the "social trap" of the scholar-official.

🌾

Present Being

Farmer on wasteland east of the city, self-named "Dongpo Jushi." Donning farmer's clothes on the Yellow Mud Path, he became unrecognizable — his true self emerged.

02
METHOD
Elegant Poverty: The Roof-Beam Budgeting System
4,500 cash divided into thirty portions hanging from the roof beam, retrieved daily with a painting fork — Su Dongpo's unique approach to financial planning. Daily expenses capped at 150 cash; leftovers saved for entertaining guests. He calculated his savings could last over a year, and stopped worrying. He even named a friend's noodle dish "Weishensu" (Why-So-Crisp).

DUALITY
Two Faces of Su: Different "Repentance" for Different Audiences
Su Dongpo displayed radically different attitudes depending on his audience — defiant integrity to close friends, feigned contrition to political enemies. A survival strategy, and a mirror of human nature under political persecution.
To confidant Li Chang
→
"Principles run through our hearts, loyalty fills our very marrow — we can face life and death with a laugh." Unrepentant defiance.

04
BUILD
Snow Hall: An Exile's Architectural Dream
"Architecture was Su Dongpo's instinct." He built a five-room Snow Hall (constructed during snowfall), its walls decorated with his own paintings. Behind it stood the Prospect Pavilion with panoramic views. He built dams, dug fish ponds, planted trees from neighbors, flowers from friends, vegetables from home. The western neighbor had giant bamboo stalks seven inches in circumference, so dense they blocked the sky.

05
PHILOSOPHY
The River, the Wind, and the Moon Have No Fixed Owner
In impoverished Huangzhou, Su discovered a startling truth: the most precious things in the world — the river, the wind, the moon — belong to anyone with a leisurely heart. Living at Lingao Pavilion just ten paces from the Yangtze, he wrote: "Half the river below is melted snow from Mount Emei. I drink it, I bathe in it — why would I need to return home?" The freedom of being forgotten by the world was precisely what he treasured most.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
"Dongpo Jushi" is not a self-deprecating joke but a declaration. When a man loses his office, wealth, and reputation, and builds a home on wasteland with his own hands, what he gains is not failure but a more authentic mode of existence. As he wrote: "The river, the wind, and the moon have no fixed owner. The leisurely man is their master."
