Self-Study for High Scores · Ch.3 · Learning Principles: The Brain's User Manual

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Learning is the process of storing external stimuli as long-term memory — the brain has a fixed memory-storage procedure, much like a complex machine needs a manual. How can we study in accordance with the brain's operating mode? The answer: complete all four stages of the learning cycle, and use precise repetition to thicken myelin — this is the fundamental reason self-study is essential.

01
Brain Science
Memory Is Physical: Three Memory Types and Neuronal Transformation
Memory is not an abstract concept — it is a physical substance that literally occupies space in the brain. Brain science classifies memory into three types: sensory memory (visual fades in ~1 second; auditory lasts a few seconds), short-term memory (minutes to hours, limited capacity like a cutting board), and long-term memory (lasting over a day). Storing long-term memory is a physical phenomenon — when neurons receive stimulation, they change shape, extending branches or sprouting new ones, connecting via synapses. Like the glass marbles in the movie Inside Out — every memory is a real, physical presence in the brain.

02
Core Process
The Four-Stage Memory Cycle: Understanding Brain Rules via Instant Noodles
The brain stores memories only through four complete stages — miss any one, and your learning is like undercooked instant noodles. Neuroscientist James Zull identified the four-stage cycle: ① Concrete Experience (reading, listening — receiving visual/auditory stimuli) → ② Reflective Observation (using prior knowledge to understand new content) → ③ Abstract Hypothesis (mental simulation, questioning, verifying) → ④ Active Experimentation (solving problems, writing reports, taking exams). Repeated cycling strengthens and connects the relevant neurons, consolidating content into long-term memory. If you only read without solving problems (skip Stage 4), or only listen without thinking (skip Stage 2), your learning is like instant noodles that taste like toothpaste — a disaster.

03
Physical Basis
Myelin: Why Precise Repetition Outweighs Long Study Hours
The difference in "excellence" between people is fundamentally a difference in myelin thickness — and myelin thickens only when identical signals flow repeatedly through neurons. Myelin is an insulating substance that wraps around neuronal axons. When the same precise signal flows through a neuron repeatedly, myelin thickens layer by layer — like wrapping communication cables in insulation to prevent signal leakage. Thicker myelin means faster, stronger signal transmission. This explains why some people calculate mentally at lightning speed or read English at incredible speeds — not because they were born with "dedicated neurons," but because specific pathways were repeatedly and precisely used, thickening their myelin. The key is "precise": half-hearted study, no matter how many hours, won't thicken myelin. Only understanding that matches the textbook with exact precision counts as truly effective learning.

04
Critical Bottleneck
Reflective Observation: True Focus Is Not Just Staring at a Book
The real reason most people fail at learning — they skip "reflective observation," letting information pass in one ear and out the other, never entering the memory cycle. Focus is not sitting up straight and staring at a page, nor photographing every word into memory. True focus means "constantly asking yourself while reading: Am I understanding this correctly?" — actively connecting new information with what you already know. Mayu Yamaguchi (graduated first from University of Tokyo, passed bar exam) used "7-time reading" — not mechanical repetition, but each read involves "consciously activating thinking and continuously organizing information." A bar exam top scorer's secret: while reading, constantly flipping back and forth to connect related content — progress is turtle-slow, but this "troublesome" focus is what makes a study god. There is absolutely no such thing as "naturally memorizing without thinking, just by looking long enough."

05
Balance
Learn and Practice: Balancing Knowledge Intake with Familiarization — Confucius's 2,500-Year Warning
The brain naturally tends to split "learning" and "familiarization" into separate halves — but excelling requires both. Brain research reveals: the first two stages of the memory cycle (Concrete Experience + Reflective Observation) constitute "learning," while the last two (Abstract Hypothesis + Active Experimentation) constitute "familiarization" — and the brain's structure makes switching between them difficult. This creates two typical learner types: Type 1 excels at learning but neglects familiarization (knowledge remains loose, lacking application ability); Type 2 passionately asks questions but lacks foundational accumulation (reports lack core content). Confucius pinpointed this 2,500 years ago: "Learn and frequently practice — is this not a joy?" encapsulates the full cycle; "Learning without thinking leads to confusion; thinking without learning is dangerous" warns against leaning to one side. The simplest and most powerful familiarization method: "Close the book and test yourself" — try to recite what you read. That's the strongest instant feedback.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Learning is a physical phenomenon — memory literally occupies brain space. The brain stores memory through four stages: Concrete Experience → Reflective Observation → Abstract Hypothesis → Active Experimentation. Most people fail because they skip "reflective observation" — passively receiving without actively thinking. True focus means actively connecting new and old knowledge, constantly asking and answering yourself. Myelin only thickens through "precise" repetition — half-hearted study, no matter how long, is useless. Confucius said it all 2,500 years ago: "Learn and frequently practice" — both learning and familiarization are indispensable. Increase your solo study time and operate according to the brain's user manual — that's the only way forward.
