Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 18

Caution Without Paralysis, Decisive Action—Forging Professional Instinct

A · Answer: Professional instinct comes from continuous study + accumulated experience + historical memory → instantaneous judgment when market signals appear → decisive action. It's like a doctor's diagnosis—outsiders call it intuition, but it's actually a battle-hardened professional reflex.
01
Clarification

Not Intuition—The Subconscious at Work

Everything sent to his mill was grist—all market signals were automatically processed by experience, ultimately forming "feel for the tape."

Livermore's friends credited a perfect top liquidation to "inspiration from a black cat." Livermore clarified: the truth was he noticed many small warning signals—none individually clear enough to provide a definite reason, but all together they formed his "feel." That morning he found he could sell 72,000 shares of U.S. Steel in a highly liquid market without difficulty, just 1 point off the day's high.

At the same time, when he liquidated his 5,000 shares of Utah Copper, the price dropped 5 points—proving his judgment of only holding the initial 5,000 shares without adding was equally wise. He wasn't fooled by the illusion of paper profits but followed the answers the market itself provided.

02
Principle

The Law of Group Behavior: Follow the Leader, Beware Laggards

When buying a stock that refuses to follow the group leader, exercise extreme caution—insiders not wanting to buy their own stock must have reasons you don't know.

Livermore demonstrated with two cases: Chester Auto, while the entire auto sector was red-hot, kept falling instead—insiders were selling. The subsequent crash confirmed the company's dire condition. Guyana Gold was the same: pushed to $47 by a banking syndicate during a bull market, then persistently weakened. The tape was warning long before the news of "drilling into barren rock" was disclosed.

"I shorted Chester Auto. Not on intuition or inspiration—I just knew something had to be wrong. The warning signal came before the market decline. I wasn't looking for a crash; I was looking for warning signals."

"If you're walking along a railroad track and see an express train barreling toward you at 60 miles an hour, do you keep walking on the track? Buddy, I step aside."—Refusing to sell a stock that's lagging behind its group is like standing on the tracks waiting for the train to hit you.

03
Real Trade

Cotton Reversal: From Million-Dollar Loss to Two-Day Turnaround

The way the market absorbs your selling is the best inside information—it tells you the direction of the line of least resistance.

After losing $1 million in cotton, Livermore finally admitted defeat and closed his position. The next day he observed that after declining, cotton did not bounce back as it had for weeks—meaning the line of least resistance had shifted from up to down. He immediately sold 10,000 bales as a test: the price dropped 50 points. Confirmed, he jumped from the lunch table and added another 10,000 bales. After arriving in Washington, he sold 40,000 more—the market offered no support whatsoever.

"You never know if you're right until you bet." Livermore immediately covered all shorts in Philadelphia. This trade perfectly illustrated "yesterday's mistake is today's opportunity"—if the reason you closed yesterday no longer holds today, act in the opposite direction immediately.

04
Analogy

Professional Instinct Like Medicine: Diagnose → Act → Verify

Training a stock trader is much like training a doctor—both require years of theoretical study and a lifetime of practice.

🩺 Doctor

Study anatomy, physiology, pharmacology → years of internship → observe pathology → diagnose → treat by experience → refine through continuous practice

📈 Trader

Study market laws, historical cases → years of trading → observe the tape → determine line of least resistance → establish positions by experience → verify with the market

A trader relies on four pillars: observation, experience, memory, and mathematics. He can transfer knowledge (specific facts on index cards), but he cannot transfer experience. Someone may know what to do, but if they don't act fast enough, they'll still lose money—"decisive action" is the final link in professional instinct.

Livermore's example: winter wheat had been delayed by strikes, while spring wheat would soon be harvested and flood the market simultaneously → two seasons of wheat converging → price must fall. From forming the judgment to selling, he didn't waste a second testing it—"you never know if you're right until you bet."

05
Ultimate

The Tape Is the Best Inside Information

Observing the market can give you the best inside information in the world—the tape never lies; it only speaks to those who know how to read it.

Livermore tracked the wheat market: sold 250,000 bushels as a test, and the price dropped only ¼ cent—the signal wasn't clear enough. Sold another 250,000 bushels, and the price fell 1¼ cents, with buy orders coming in tiny fragments—the market had no real buying. So he decisively sold 2 million bushels. A few days later, the market "forced" him to sell another 2 million bushels. Eventually, wheat plunged 6 cents.

"Acting on the dictates of experience may occasionally make you look foolish; but if you don't follow them, then every single time, without exception, you will turn into a jackass."

"Momentary inspiration and the mysterious so-called feel for the tape don't have much to do with trading success... When I say a trader must follow the latest minute-by-minute developments, I only intend to emphasize once more—all reasons are built on a foundation of facts accumulated over years of work and thinking." This is the entire secret of the professional attitude.

💡
Key Takeaway

The market has no mysterious "sixth sense"—only battle-hardened professional instinct. Observation accumulates experience, experience forms memory, memory combined with mathematics produces judgment, and judgment drives decisive action. When all this is absorbed into your blood, what outsiders call "intuition" is simply the automatic response of professional mastery. The tape is always the best inside information—provided you know how to read it.
