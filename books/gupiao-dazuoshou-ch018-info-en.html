Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 18
"History Repeats—and So Do Trades"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Everything sent to his mill was grist — all market signals were automatically processed by experience, ultimately forming a "feel for the tape." Through two great campaigns — liquidating U.S. Steel at the top and reversing his cotton position — Livermore proves: the market has no mysterious "sixth sense," only battle-hardened professional instinct built on four pillars: observation, experience, memory, and mathematics.

This chapter reveals the core secret of speculative success: professional instinct comes from continuous study + accumulated experience + historical memory → instant judgment when market signals appear → decisive action. Livermore clarifies his success was not "inspiration from a black cat" but the synthesis of countless small warning signals — just like a doctor's diagnosis, which outsiders call intuition but is actually a battle-hardened professional reflex. Through the law of group behavior, the cotton reversal campaign, and reading the wheat tape, Livermore presents the complete operating system of a professional trader's mind.
01
Clarification
Not Intuition — The Subconscious at Work
Livermore's friends credited a perfect top liquidation of 72,000 U.S. Steel shares to "inspiration from a black cat." Livermore clarified: he noticed many small warning signals — none individually clear enough, but together forming his "feel." He liquidated Utah Copper at the same time, causing a 5-point drop — proving his judgment to hold only the initial position was equally wise. He was not fooled by paper-profit illusions but followed the answers the market itself provided.

02
Core Principle
The Law of Group Behavior: Follow the Leader, Beware the Laggard
When buying a stock that refuses to follow its group leader, exercise extreme caution — insiders not buying their own stock must have reasons you don't know. Livermore demonstrates with Chester Auto and Guyana Gold: the tape warned long before the news of "drilling into barren rock" was disclosed. "If you're walking along a railroad track and see an express train barreling toward you at 60 miles an hour, do you keep walking? Buddy, I step aside."

03
Real Trade
Cotton Turnaround: From Million-Dollar Loss to Two-Day Reversal
After losing $1 million in cotton, Livermore finally admitted defeat and closed his position. The next day he observed that after declining, cotton did not bounce back as it had for weeks — the line of least resistance had shifted from up to down. "You never know if you're right until you bet." He shorted 10,000 bales as a test: the price dropped 50 points. Confirmed, he jumped from the lunch table and added more. By Washington he had sold 40,000 more bales — the market offered no support whatsoever.

04
Professionalism
Professional Instinct Like Medicine: Four Pillars
Training a stock trader is much like training a doctor — both require years of theoretical study and a lifetime of practice. A trader relies on four pillars: observation, experience, memory, and mathematics. He can transfer knowledge (specific facts on index cards) but cannot transfer experience. Someone may know what to do, but if they don't act fast enough, they'll still lose — "decisive action" is the final link. Example: winter wheat delayed by strikes + spring wheat harvest ahead → two crops converging → price must fall → sell immediately.

05
Ultimate Truth
The Tape Is the Best Inside Information
Livermore tracked the wheat market: sold 250,000 bushels as a test, price dropped only ¼ cent — signal unclear. Sold another 250,000 bushels, price fell 1¼ cents, buy orders came in tiny fragments — the market had no real buying. So he decisively sold 2 million bushels. Days later, the market "forced" him to sell another 2 million bushels. Eventually, wheat plunged 6 cents. The tape never lies; it only speaks to those who know how to read it.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The market has no mysterious "sixth sense" — only battle-hardened professional instinct. Observation accumulates experience, experience forms memory, memory combined with mathematics produces judgment, and judgment drives decisive action. When all this is absorbed into your blood, what outsiders call "intuition" is simply the automatic response of professional mastery. The tape is always the best inside information — it never lies, but only those who know how to read it can hear its language.
