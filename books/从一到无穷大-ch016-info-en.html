Elementary Particles
The Ultimate Recipe: Nucleons, Electrons & Neutrinos
In the 19th century, Prout proposed all atoms are built from hydrogen, dismissed because atomic weights weren't all integers—until Aston's 1919 discovery of isotopes proved him right. In modern terms: different nuclei consist of varying numbers of protons (hydrogen nuclei) and neutrons (chargeless protons). Oxygen = 8p+8n, iodine = 53p+74n, uranium = 92p+146n. Additionally: positrons (free positive charges) and neutrinos (chargeless, nearly massless, can pass through light-years of lead). Neutrinos were discovered via "reductio ad absurdum"—energy went missing in β-decay; a thief must have taken it. The elementary particle family stops here: nucleons, electrons, neutrinos, electromagnetic waves—four players performing the infinite drama of the universe.

Liquid-Drop Model
Heart of the Atom: Nuclear Fluid & the Tug-of-War for Stability
Gamow's own liquid-drop model: the nucleus is like a tiny droplet of "nuclear fluid," with surface tension (cohesion) counteracting electric repulsion among protons. Nuclear fluid density is 2.4×10¹⁴ times water; surface tension 10¹⁸ times water—a nuclear soap film would weigh 50 million tons and support a trillion-ton weight. Bohr and Wheeler's 1939 calculations yielded a decisive conclusion: in the first half of the periodic table (up to silver), surface tension dominates → fusion releases energy; beyond silver, electric repulsion dominates → fission releases energy. All nuclei heavier than silver are unstable—only the prohibitively high activation energy prevents spontaneous decay. We live in a world of nuclear explosives—thankfully, ignition is extremely difficult.

03
Atom Smashers
Particle Accelerators & Cloud Chambers: Seeing Nuclear Reactions
In 1919, Rutherford bombarded nitrogen with natural α particles, achieving the first artificial nuclear transmutation—aluminum into silicon. Three main acceleration devices followed: the electrostatic generator (repeatedly charging from inside a sphere, theoretically unlimited voltage), the cyclotron (two semicircular boxes in a magnetic field alternately charged, particles spiral-accelerated), and the linear accelerator (cylinders of increasing length, particles accelerated stepwise). The cloud chamber (Wilson chamber) makes high-speed particle tracks visible—particles passing through supersaturated water vapor leave mist trails, allowing direct photography of nuclear transmutations: a nitrogen nucleus struck by an α particle splits into oxygen and a proton; a boron nucleus shattered by a proton into three helium nuclei…

04
Chain Reaction
Nucleonics: The "Rabbit Multiplication" Logic of Chain Reactions
Charged particle bombardment is extremely inefficient—only 1 in 10,000 particles smashes a nucleus; converting 1g of boron would take 20,000 years. The solution: neutrons—uncharged, not slowed by electron shells, but free neutrons don't exist in nature. Nuclear fission provides the answer—when a uranium nucleus fissions, each fragment, vibrating violently, ejects neutrons (average ~2 per fission). This builds a rabbit-breeding chain reaction: 100 neutrons → 35 fissions → 56 second-generation neutrons (decaying) OR 100 → 65 fissions → 143 second-generation (progressive explosion). Key parameters: neutron fission efficiency × neutron yield per fission. Only 0.7% of natural uranium (U-235) can sustain chain reactions, requiring separation via diffusion, magnetic methods, or moderator technology.

05
Future Energy
The Nuclear Future: From Uranium Ore to Granite
Known U-235 reserves could power global industry (fully nuclear) for several years; converting U-238 to plutonium extends this to centuries; adding thorium (4× uranium reserves) converted to U-233 extends to at least a millennium. When all rich ores are exhausted, each ton of ordinary granite contains 4g uranium and 12g thorium—converted to fissile material, yielding energy equivalent to 320 tons of gasoline. For fusion: deuterium (hydrogen isotope in heavy water)—two deuteron collisions produce either helium+neutron or tritium+proton, requiring temperatures above 100 million degrees. The H-bomb achieved uncontrolled fusion; controlled thermonuclear reactions require strong magnetic fields to confine super-hot plasma—the core challenge of "artificial suns." Physicists have opened Pandora's box; using its contents depends on human wisdom, not physical law.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The alchemists' dream was realized in the 20th century in a form far beyond their imagination—not only transmuting elements but releasing stellar-level energy locked within matter. From the "cosmic recipe" of elementary particles (nucleons + electrons + neutrinos + EM waves) to the elegant liquid-drop explanation of nuclear stability, from accelerators shattering nuclei to chain reactions multiplying like rabbits—all this constitutes "nucleonics," the science that changed human destiny. The only true limit is the speed of light; and the single element "silver" sits exactly at the balance point between stability and rupture. The physical laws are laid out; how to use them is a question humanity must answer for itself.
