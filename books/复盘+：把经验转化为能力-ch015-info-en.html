01
BP Practice
BP's After-Action Review: 20 Minutes to Make Everyone Better Than Before
BP adapted the U.S. Army's AAR, calling it \"After-Action Review\" — a \"learning by doing\" mechanism: An honest, pragmatic review takes no more than 20 minutes, held immediately after any definable event during the project (not after project completion). A long-term project may hold 10–15 reviews. Core guidelines: meet immediately → create an open climate (not criticism/blame) → appoint a facilitator → revisit expected goals → reconstruct what actually happened → compare differences → document and share.

Operational Secrets
The 12 Secrets of Project Review: BP's Practice Crystallized Over Years
12 proven operational guidelines: ① Convene face-to-face (within 2 weeks of project end) ② Invite the right people (clients, similar new project members) ③ Appoint an independent facilitator ④ Revisit project goals and deliverables ⑤ Revisit the project plan/process ⑥ Start with what went well ⑦ Analyze reasons for success, convert to recommendations ⑧ Ask what could have been better ⑨ Identify obstacles and improvement suggestions ⑩ Ensure nothing is missed (scoring method) ⑪ Ask \"what do we do next?\" ⑫ Compile and distribute the meeting record.

03
Multi-Iteration
Layered + Phased: The Multi-Iteration Project Review Model
One review at the end of a large project is never enough — too much time passes (facts blur), too many personnel changes (new members don't know prior context), too many problems accumulate (intimidation and resistance). Hence the multi-iteration approach: layered (team/functional line → project group level) and phased, embedding fupan into the project management rhythm itself.
📐

Layered Reviews

Team/functional line periodic reviews (every 1–2 weeks) → Project-level reviews (team leads + key members). Follow Ch4 team review model.

📊

Phased Reviews

Key events → Periodic progress (weekly/monthly meetings) → Stage reviews (at each phase end) → Overall project review (before/after completion).

04
Review Timings
Four Review Timings: A Learning Rhythm Throughout the Project Lifecycle
Four moments during a project deserve a review: ① Key activities or events — new, important, valuable, or underperforming nodes, reviewed immediately; ② Periodic progress reviews — integrate into weekly/monthly meetings, adding fupan alongside regular reporting; ③ Stage-based reviews — at the end of each project phase, uncovering hidden risks and weak points; ④ Overall project review — before or after project completion, a mechanism used by Google, Lenovo, Walmart, and others.

Success Factors
Four Critical Success Factors: Making Project Review Stick
Four keys to implementing project reviews thoroughly and sustaining them long-term: ① Leadership commitment — Lenovo's ERP project had two successive directors personally participating; Liu Chuanzhi personally attended Legend Capital project reviews. ② Clear accountability — investing dedicated time and energy is worth it; Lenovo's ERP fupan produced tens of thousands of words of reports, which later SCM/CRM projects effectively leveraged. ③ Accumulate small to big — periodic reviews generate written materials, daily accumulation provides raw material for the overall review. ④ Embed fupan in work — don't treat it as an extra task; tightly integrate it with actual work discussions and execution, promptly adjust goals and strategies based on findings.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The essence of project fupan is making \"every repetition better than the last\" into organizational muscle memory. BP's 12 secrets boil down to one principle: don't wait until the project ends — embed fupan into the project rhythm, layered (team to project level) and phased (key events to overall), iterating repeatedly. Lenovo's ERP project used countless stage reviews to produce a report tens of thousands of words; subsequent SCM and CRM projects leveraged this to shorten cycles and cut costs. Fupan isn't extra work — it is the work itself.
