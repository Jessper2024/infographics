The New Sell & Sell Short · Chapter 5
"Stop Losses"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Every trade must have a stop loss — this is the baseline of trading integrity. A system without stops is like racing without a seatbelt. Most traders either refuse to stop out due to the "endowment effect," or place stops at the most obvious level below prior lows — exactly where professional traders hunt. Where to set the stop? How to execute? When to move? Answers span the Iron Triangle, Nick's Stop, hard-stop principles, and trailing strategies.

A stop loss connects the trader to reality — you must answer a fundamental question: is the potential profit worth the risk? Trading without a stop is gambling. Holding a losing position not only costs money but consumes attention, preventing you from taking better trades. Like a toothache that steals more focus than expected, a losing position shackles the trader. Stops are the best tool we have — they're not perfect, but without them, you have nothing.

Foundation
The Essence of Stop Losses & the Iron Triangle
A stop connects the trader to reality. Before entering, complete the Iron Triangle: per-share risk (entry − stop) → max loss per trade (account × 2%) → max shares. Never take a trade with less than 2:1 reward/risk. You trade money, not stocks — you trade risk. The core rule: know your entry price and your stop price at the same time.

02
Execution
Market Orders vs. Limit Orders for Stops
All orders fall into two categories: market (guarantees fill, not price) and limit (guarantees price, not fill). Elder's approach: use limit orders for entries and targets ("trade at my price or walk the plank"), but always use MIT market orders for protective stops — never negotiate when running for the exit. Saving a few dollars on slippage with a limit stop can lead to catastrophic gap losses.
✅

Market Stop (MIT)

Guarantees immediate exit, accepting slippage. When trouble arrives, don't haggle — get out. Protective stops must use market orders.

⚠️

Limit Order as Stop

Saving pennies on slippage may cost major losses. In fast declines, limit orders won't fill — price can gap far below your stop.

03
Discipline
Hard Stops vs. Soft Stops
A hard stop is an order placed in the market, executed by your broker. A soft stop is a price you keep in mind and manually act on. Elder is blunt: "For most beginners, a soft stop is equivalent to having no stop at all." Hard stops eliminate hesitation — mandatory for beginners. Soft stops are only for traders profitable over one year, demanding iron discipline and real-time monitoring.

04
Placement
The Art of Stop Placement: Don't Be the Pros' Prey
Setting a stop one tick below the prior low is the worst approach — that's the market's most crowded stop zone. Pros wait to trigger those stops and scoop discounted shares. Nick's Stop Method: find the bar with the lowest low, take the two adjacent bars, and place your stop just below the lower of their lows. This tight-trailing stop says: hit my stop if you must (it's small), or give me my gains.

Trend Amplifier
Trailing Stops: The One-Way Street to Bigger Profits
A stop is a one-way street — you can only tighten it, never loosen. When long, move stops only up; when short, only down. If price falls and you lower your stop, you are indulging your mistake. After reaching targets with no bearish signals, switch to a trailing stop: Parabolic SAR, SafeZone, or Volatility Reduction — three tools that let the market decide when to take you out.

🔑 Key Takeaway
A stop loss is an inseparable part of any trading system — complete the Iron Triangle before entering. Never place stops at the market's most crowded obvious levels — use Nick's Stop to dramatically reduce shake-out risk. For execution: market orders for stops, hard stops for beginners. In trends, the stop is a one-way street — master trailing strategies to let profits run, and let the market tell you when it's time to leave.
