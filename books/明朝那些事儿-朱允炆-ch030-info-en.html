"Zhu Di's Opponents"

Situation After Baigou River, Zhu Di's Northern Army had destroyed the Southern main force and marched on Jinan, expecting an easy victory over a demoralized remnant.
Conflict Tie Xuan — a scholar-official with zero military training — rallied scattered troops and held Jinan for three months against the era's finest commander.
Question How could a civilian scholar with no battlefield experience hold a walled city against Zhu Di, widely considered China's best military mind?
Answer Moral conviction + psychological warfare (the Ancestral Tablet gambit) + firepower tactics by Sheng Yong — belief can become a weapon.

01
Tie Xuan: Scholar Saves the State with a Tablet Gambit
Tie Xuan of Dengzhou was a pure civilian official — no military training, no battlefield experience — yet he became the first person to stop Zhu Di in his tracks. After Li Jinglong's collapse, Tie Xuan gathered scattered soldiers and marched for Jinan. Zhu Di breached dikes to flood the city; Tie Xuan counter-deceived with a 1,000-man feigned surrender, nearly trapping Zhu Di inside Jinan's gates. When cannon fire threatened to breach the walls, Tie Xuan hung giant plaques inscribed "Great Ming Imperial Ancestor's Spirit Tablet" along the ramparts. Zhu Di could not fire — bombing his own father's spirit tablet was politically unthinkable. After three failed months, Zhu Di withdrew in frustration — the most humiliating day of his military career.

02
Sheng Yong: From Perpetual Defeat to Breaking the Invincibility Myth
Sheng Yong's biography in the Ming History begins: "Unknown origin" — a man who had only known military defeat, chasing from Zhen Ding to Beiping, Dezhou, and Jinan. Yet each retreat taught him something: he mapped Zhu Di's flank-charging pattern and devised firepower-counter-cavalry tactics. At Dongchang, he lured Zhu Di deep and encircled him — the first commander to truly defeat Zhu Di in open battle. Zhu Di's elite Northern Army was shattered; his best general Zhang Yu died charging the encirclement to save Zhu Di. Sheng Yong shattered the myth of Zhu Di's invincibility.

03
Dongchang Decisive Battle: Sheng Yong's Encirclement Trap
Sheng Yong chose to fight with back to the city walls — no retreat, live or die. Zhu Di attacked the left flank, then the center; the center feigned collapse and drew him deep into a killing zone. Zhu Di was surrounded, fighting with bow, sword, and three successive horses — arrows gone, sword shattered. Zhu Neng broke through with personal bodyguard to rescue Zhu Di; Zhang Yu followed but was too late — hacked to pieces by vengeful Southern soldiers. The Northern Army shattered. Remarkably, Zhu Di himself survived unscathed — Southern soldiers, bound by the "no-harm" edict, refused to target him directly.

04
Creative Thinking: Breaking Through the Jinan Mental Trap
Zhu Di had fallen into a mental trap: he believed capturing the capital required taking Jinan first. Monk Da Yan — the era's most brilliant strategist — shattered this assumption with one insight: your goal is to enter the house, not to open that particular lock — the door right in front of you is wood. In December 1401, Zhu Di committed to an all-or-nothing final campaign: bypassing Jinan, crossing at Guantao, sweeping through Dong'a and Dongping, striking directly at Xuzhou. Sheng Yong and Ping An could not catch up. At Chao River, Zhu Di ambushed Ping An after two days of preparation — the Southern vanguard was annihilated. Zhu Di rallied his troops with: "This is our final battle — no retreat, no surrender."

05
Jiahe & Hutuo River: Sandstorms Rewrite History Twice
March 1401: Battle of Jiahe — Zhu Di personally led a night raid across the river, but Xu Huizu's timely reinforcement turned it into a costly failure for Zhu Di. Zhu Di used his "bulletproof vest" status to boldly survey the Southern lines — inside the rules, but outside any soldier's ability to respond. At the second Battle of Jiahe (夹河), northeast winds suddenly arose, blinding the Southern Army — Zhu Di's second "jackpot." At Hutuo River, Wu Jie's forces were defeated the same way: sandstorm blowing into Southern soldiers' eyes. The Jingnan Campaign was unique: Zhu Di, leading charges personally, never suffered serious injury; two sandstorms struck precisely when Southern morale was highest. Luck or mandate of heaven?

🔑 Key Takeaway
From 1400–1401, Zhu Yunwen finally found the right commanders: Tie Xuan held Jinan for three months; Sheng Yong became the first general to defeat Zhu Di in open battle. But Zhu Di's creative bypass of Jinan — directly targeting the capital via Xuzhou — shattered Southern strategic plans. Three sandstorms then tipped the scales decisively at Jiahe and Hutuo River. The chapter's core lesson: belief and ingenuity can compensate for material disadvantage, but war's outcome often hinges on uncontrollable environmental factors — true historical dialectics in action.
