Dongpo Zhilin · Ch.16「Regency and Warnings—Institutional Dilemmas in Power Transition and History's Lessons」

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer The closing movement of Volume V, "Discussions of Antiquity." Having traversed the grand narratives of governance legitimacy, the dissections of ruler-minister power dynamics, and the institutional absorption of elites, Su Shi narrows his focus to the most fragile and institutionally demanding moment: power transition. Three essays—Regents, The Misfortune of Duke Yin of Lu, and Seven Virtues and Eight Warnings—address, respectively, the institutional arrangements during power vacuums, the fatal cost of political naivety, and the curation and transmission of historical lessons. The thirteen essays of "Discussions of Antiquity," having started with the thunderclap that "King Wu was not a sage," now arrive at the quiet culmination of "Seven Virtues and Eight Warnings"—from demolishing historical falsehoods to building a code of conduct for posterity. This is no accidental structure: Su Shi's "discussions of antiquity" are not mere revisionist essays; they are the quarrying of new building materials from the rubble of old histories.

From Dongpo Zhilin Volume V, the final three essays: Regents traces the institutional evolution of power transition (from the Three Dynasties regent system to Qin-Han matriarchal regency), The Misfortune of Duke Yin of Lu reveals the fatal cost of political innocence through five historical figures' fates, and Seven Virtues and Eight Warnings constructs a complete code of sovereign conduct through seven positive and eight negative case studies.

01
INSTITUTIONAL ARCHAEOLOGY
The Regent—Why Should a Forgotten Institution of Power Transition Be Restored?
Su Shi opens with the debate over whether Duke Yin of Lu was a "regent" or a "duke." Ouyang Xiu argued that the Spring and Autumn Annals recording him as "Duke" proves he was not a regent. Su Shi flatly rejects this logic: "Yin was regent and Huan committed regicide—this is recorded in detail in the histories." He traces an almost-forgotten institution: the Regent. The Duke of Zhou offered the paradigm of regency with restoration; Ji Kangzi established the norm of yielding when a male heir was born—"In ancient times, when the heir of the Son of Heaven, a feudal lord, or a high minister died before the heir was born, the younger brother or the son of a brother next in line would be established as Regent. If the child born was female, the Regent succeeded; if male, the Regent withdrew." This is an exquisitely engineered institution: it ensures the smooth operation of government while the heir is unborn or too young, and through the "if male, the Regent withdraws" mechanism, guarantees the eventual return of the direct bloodline. Yet since the Qin-Han, this institution was abandoned: "Since the Qin and Han, this ritual has not been practiced; instead, the Empress Dowager governs as regent." Su Shi's critique of matriarchal regency is devastating: "Confucius said: 'Only women and petty men are difficult to nurture.' Even involving them in external affairs is inadmissible—as the saying goes, 'when the hen crows at dawn, the household is in ruins'—how much less can they be allowed to occupy the regent's position and oversee the world?" Yet he lists successful exceptions: the Queen Dowager of Qi, and the Empresses Cao, Gao, and Xiang of the Song—"perhaps one in a thousand." The failures far outnumber successes: "The Empress Lü of Han, Hu Wu of Wei, Wu of Tang—whose chaos could not be overcome—Wang Mang and Yang Jian thereby changed the ruling surname." His final verdict: the Regent system was "the canonical institution of the Former Kings and the orthodox doctrine of Confucius"—"yet the world does not know it, accustomed to seeing matriarchal regency and taking it as natural. Therefore I cannot leave this undiscussed, awaiting the gentlemen of later ages."

02
REGENT VS. MATRIARCH
Regent System vs. Matriarchal Regency—A Historical Test of Two Power Transition Models
Su Shi performs a cold cost-benefit analysis of two power transition models. The advantage of the Regent system: the Regent and the heir share the same surname and lineage, being "descendants of our former lords"—"If matriarchal regents could be trusted, so could Regents. If neither could be trusted, then having the Regent seize power still means the realm remains in the hands of the same surname—would that not be better than its seizure by a different surname?" Even if the Regent usurps, the dynasty remains in the family bloodline—vastly preferable to usurpation by outsiders (Wang Mang, Yang Jian). The institutional hazards of matriarchal regency go beyond the moral problem of "the hen crowing at dawn"—Su Shi traces a clear causal chain: matriarchal regency → expansion of maternal-relative power → usurpation of the dynasty by a different surname. From Empress Lü of Han to Empress Wu of Tang, to the dynastic usurpations of Wang Mang and Yang Jian, every instance of matriarchal regency created an institutional channel for outsiders to seize power. The Regent system was entirely different—its "if male, withdraw" clause was a self-limiting mechanism; the Regent's power had an explicit expiration date. Su Shi's final call is to "the gentlemen of later ages": reintroduce the Regent institution into public discourse. This is a quintessentially "Su Shi-style" reform proposal—not forcibly pushing a new institution in reality, but resurrecting a forgotten old one from history.

03
FATES IN CONTRAST
The Misfortune of Duke Yin—How Political Innocence Proved Fatal in Five Lives
Su Shi opens with the tragedy of Duke Yin of Lu. Prince Hui requested permission to kill Duke Huan in exchange for the Grand Steward position. Duke Yin refused—but his reason was "he is still young; I shall hand over the state to him"—he planned to voluntarily cede power. Prince Hui, fearing retribution from Duke Huan after Duke Yin's abdication, slandered Duke Yin to Duke Huan and assassinated him. Su Shi delivers a devastating comparison: "When a bandit threatens someone with a weapon, that person must kill him—and not only the one threatened, but all passersby will strike at him. The passerby has no enmity with the bandit, but believes that if he doesn't strike, the bandit will kill him too. Duke Yin's wisdom did not even reach that of this passerby—how lamentable!"—Even a random passerby knows: if you don't kill someone who wants to kill you, he will kill you. Duke Yin's political intelligence was beneath that of a stranger on the street. Su Shi then arrays five fates: Li Ke (drawn into the Li Ji conspiracy, later executed by Duke Hui), Li Si (heeded Zhao Gao's scheme, forged an edict, ultimately subjected to the Five Punishments in the marketplace), Zheng Xiaotong (poisoned by Sima Shi merely on suspicion of having seen a secret memorial), Wang Yunzhi (feigned drunken vomiting to escape suspicion during Wang Dun's conspiracy—narrowly survived). Su Shi connects them with a single logic: in the arena of power, political innocence is not a virtue but a fatal weakness. Li Si's tragedy is particularly instructive—had he, upon hearing Zhao Gao's conspiracy, "summoned all officials, deployed the Six Armies, and beheaded him," he would have been the savior of Fusu. Instead he chose collaboration with the conspiracy—"What is this if not the lowest form of stupidity!"

04
SEVEN VIRTUES
Seven Virtues—Why Seven Rulers' Restraint from Killing Deserves to Be an Eternal Law
Starting from Guan Zhong's "splendid virtue" in declining Prince Hua's request while honoring the Cao Mo covenant, Su Shi enumerates seven instances of rulers exercising restraint: King Cheng of Chu, knowing Jin would become hegemon, did not kill Chong'er; Han Gaozu, knowing the southeast would be turbulent, did not kill King Bi of Wu; Emperor Wu of Jin, hearing Prince You of Qi's words, did not kill Liu Yuanhai; Fu Jian, trusting Wang Meng, did not kill Murong Chui; Emperor Ming of Tang, employing Zhang Jiuling, did not kill An Lushan—together with Guan Zhong and Duke Huan, seven cases. The world's consensus was "these seven all erred in not killing, thereby sowing chaos." Su Shi's response is the culminating thesis of his entire Discourse on Antiquity: "These seven each had their own causes for failure and destruction—it was not the fault of not killing." Duke Jing of Qi, had he not multiplied punishments and taxes, would not have fallen to the Tian clan despite their existence; King Cheng of Chu, had he not employed Ziyu, would not have been defeated despite Jin's strength; Emperor Jing of Han, had he not harmed the Wu Crown Prince and not employed Chao Cuo, would not have faced rebellion from King Bi of Wu; Emperor Wu of Jin, had he not installed Emperor Hui, would not have seen chaos from Liu Yuanhai; Fu Jian, had he not coveted Jiangzuo, would not have faced rebellion from Murong Chui despite his existence; Emperor Ming of Tang, had he not employed Li Linfu and Yang Guozhong, what could An Lushan have done?—In every case, the restraint from killing was not the cause of disaster; the collapse had separate roots. This argument forms the apex of Su Shi's Discourse on Antiquity: true statecraft is not eliminating potential threats (killing) but denying threats the conditions to erupt (governing).

05
EIGHT WARNINGS
Eight Warnings—The Millennium Fallacy of Judging by Results and the Fatal Logic of Taking Medicine Before Illness
After the Seven Virtues, Su Shi lists the Eight Warnings—eight historical figures who killed innocents out of paranoia, and the consequences. Emperor Jing of Han killed Zhou Yafu out of displeasure; Cao Cao killed Kong Rong for his great reputation; Emperor Wen of Jin killed Ji Kang for the nickname "Sleeping Dragon"; Emperor Jing of Jin killed Xiahou Xuan for his great reputation; Emperor Ming of Song killed Wang Yu for his powerful clan; the Latter Ruler of Qi killed Hulü Guang based on rumors; Emperor Taizong of Tang killed Li Junxian based on a prophecy; Empress Wu killed Pei Yan based on rumors—"the world considers all of these to have been wrong." Then Su Shi cross-references the Eight Warnings with the Seven Virtues: "Were the concerns of these eight, at the time, not 'worry for the state and preparation against chaos,' identical to the concerns about Liu Yuanhai and An Lushan? How long the world has judged right and wrong by results alone!"—These eight killed their targets with exactly the same motivation as those who worried about Liu Yuanhai and An Lushan: "concern for the state against chaos." But the world judges motives by outcomes: the seven didn't kill and their states didn't fall, so restraint was right; the eight killed and still couldn't prevent chaos, so killing was wrong. Su Shi refuses this retrospective logic. He delivers the most beautiful metaphor in the entire book: "Governing the world is like nurturing life; worrying for the state against chaos is like taking medicine. Nurturing life is nothing more than being careful with rest, food, and drink, moderating sensual pleasures—moderation and caution before illness, medicine taken only after sickness has arrived. Now, if I worry about a cold ailment and preemptively take monkshood, or worry about a heat ailment and preemptively take kansui, then the illness has not yet arisen but the medicine has already killed the person. Those eight all took medicine before illness had appeared." True governance is daily moderation and regulation, not preemptive killing to "cure" a rebellion that has not yet happened. Preemptive killing does not prevent rebellion—it only kills innocents and strips the regime of legitimacy.

KEY INSIGHT
The nurturing-life metaphor in "Seven Virtues and Eight Warnings" is the ultimate cipher to Su Shi's entire Discourse on Antiquity. He compares governing to nurturing life—not through strong medicine (violence and purges) but through daily "care in rest, food, and drink, moderation in sensual pleasures." King Cheng of Chu did not kill Chong'er; Han Gaozu did not kill King Bi of Wu—not because they foresaw the future, but because they did not substitute preemptive killing for daily governance. Shang Yang's "bloody carving" was inferior to Duke Xiao's "devotion to agriculture"; Shi Huang's "extreme authority and trust" brewed the Sand Dune Coup—all share the same logic: "taking medicine before illness." Su Shi spent thirteen essays—from "King Wu Was Not a Sage," demolishing the sage-king narrative, to "Seven Virtues and Eight Warnings," the ultimate exposition of the wellness metaphor—constructing a complete philosophy of history: the best power is not the strongest power but the most restrained power. The best ruler is not the one who reverses catastrophe in crisis but the one who dissolves crisis imperceptibly in daily life. If Chapter 13's "King Wu Was Not a Sage" was Su Shi's first detonation of old history, then this chapter's "taking medicine before illness" is the last lighthouse he built upon the ruins.
