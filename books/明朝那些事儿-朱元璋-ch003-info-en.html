1344 saw twin catastrophes strike—the Yellow River burst its banks and the Huai River brought plague and drought. Within sixteen days, Zhu Chongba lost four family members. From the ashes of total loss, a warrior was forged.

Twin Disasters of 1344: Yellow River and Huai River
At the start of 1344, the Yuan Empire received two devastating blows: the Yellow River burst its banks, flooding Shandong and Henan and creating hundreds of thousands of refugees; plague and drought simultaneously devastated Huai River communities. The court split over whether to repair the dikes. Tuituo, the famous chancellor who had compiled the Song History, pushed for repair—unaware he was lighting the fuse on a massive powder keg.

Six-Layer Corruption: Relief Grain Becomes Chaff
Relief grain was siphoned at six administrative levels—Central Secretariat → Circuit → Province → County → Township → Village. By the time it reached the people, only chaff remained. Officials memorialized the emperor with gratitude, comparing him to sage-kings of antiquity. The emperor read these reports and congratulated himself. "Everyone was satisfied, everyone was satisfied"—except the people, who starved.

Sixteen Dark Days: The World's Saddest Diary
April 6–22, 1344: father starved; elder brother starved; elder brother's son starved; mother starved. Sixteen days, four deaths, one seventeen-year-old boy powerless to stop any of it. This is "one of the world's saddest diaries." Zhu Chongba's wish was modest: a home, children, a peaceful old age for his hardworking parents. Instead, he had nothing. Landlord Liu De refused burial land cleanly—"Your parents died; what's it to me? I gave them food while they worked for me."

04
No Land for the Dead: Refused by Liu De
Zhu Chongba begged landlord Liu De for burial ground—Liu De refused. Zhu and his elder brother carried the bodies on a door board, searching everywhere. "The world is vast, land everywhere, yet none of it belonged to them." Finally a kind stranger gave them a plot. "Souls drifting, searching for parents in vain"—this is how the future emperor Zhu Yuanzhang remembered his youth. His parents worked the land a lifetime; in death they could not even rest. Landlords never worked yet lived comfortably. He could not understand—but he knew one thing: no one would save him but himself.

05
Begging Monk: Dignity vs Survival — The Ultimate Choice
Fifty days after entering the temple, famine forced all monks to beg. Zhu Chongba was sent to the worst-hit Huai West and Henan—"who would give to him?" Every door he knocked on brought contempt. "To beg is to lose dignity." But he was different from other beggars: while walking, he studied Huai West's geography and folkways, met local heroes, embraced Maitreya faith. The three-year wandering forged him from "a crying boy in a haystack" into "a warrior armed to the heart."

🔑 Key Takeaway
The 1344 twin disasters destroyed not just Zhu Chongba's family but his last shred of naive trust in the world. "Parents worked the land a lifetime, yet in death could not rest"—one sentence that explains every choice he would ever make. From "a begging beggar who lost all dignity" to "a warrior armed to the heart," the three-year wandering forged not skills but "the power of the heart"—true strength is what you become after losing everything.
