The Wrath of the Dragon King · Chapter 3

Folk Faith, Modernity, and the Cultural Interpretation of Disaster During the 1931 flood, Wuhan erupted in Dragon King worship — people gathered at the ruins of the demolished Dragon King Temple, held parades, set up altars, and officials kowtowed begging the Dragon King's forgiveness. This was not ignorant superstition but the continuation of a millennia-old disaster culture. When the anti-superstition movement destroyed this psychological toolkit in the name of science and modernity without providing any replacement, people returned to their most familiar explanatory framework — not regression, but cultural inertia in disaster response.

Dragons evolved from wild rain-bringing creatures into bureaucratized Dragon Kings, reflecting the evolution of the disaster regime. The 1930s anti-superstition movement sought to eradicate folk religion, only to expose its own emptiness when disaster struck — when science could provide no comfort, people returned to the explanatory frameworks they knew best. Disasters test not only engineering but faith.

01
Evolution
Taming the Dragon: From Wetland Beast to Celestial Official
For millennia, middle Yangtze residents lived in symbiosis with dragons. As early as 1000 BC, rice farmers of the Chu kingdom worshipped dragons — wild creatures dwelling in rivers and lakes that brought rain through their breath. Drumming on alligator-skin drums to mimic the dragon's mating call was believed to summon clouds. With the rise of hydraulic agriculture, the dragon's nature transformed: water-control engineers like Yu the Great and Li Bing were deified for their ability to tame dragons. The dragon evolved from a wild natural force into a bureaucratized "Dragon King" — enshrined in temples as a celestial official receiving solemn worship.

02
Ideological Clash
Questioning the Dragon: The Rise of Anti-Superstition
In the early 20th century, intellectuals launched a comprehensive assault on folk religion. In 1915, Xu Huandou labeled Dragon King worship as "superstition" (mixin) — a newly pejorative term. In the 1920s, Yun Daiying condemned both "the nonsense of Christian superstition" and folk belief's "gods and ghosts." Hydrologist Zhang Hanying believed education could eradicate weather superstitions. Historian Deng Tuo attributed fatalism to socioeconomic backwardness. The true turning point was the entry of "superstition" into the Chinese lexicon: it demoted folk belief from "heterodoxy" to "nonexistence," negating its philosophical foundations entirely.

03
Violent Suppression
Dethroning the Dragon King: Revolutionary Violence and Political Purge
When Wuhan became the revolutionary capital in 1927, anti-superstition escalated into violent campaigns. 1928 legislation authorized secular activists to "ruthlessly attack" folk religion: Earth God temples were closed and converted into public toilets; wooden deity statues were "arrested" and publicly "decapitated" at police stations; fortune-tellers were ordered to change professions within three months. That same year, the Dragon King was officially classified as superstition. In 1930, the Dragon King Temple was demolished by municipal planners to make way for a new road. Ironically, the campaign was itself highly ritualized — the "arrest" and "decapitation" of statues carried unmistakable traces of the religious sensibility it sought to eradicate.

04
Faith's Comeback
The Flood Sweeps the Dragon King Temple: Faith Returns in Crisis
As the river rose in July 1931, rumors spread that the Dragon King was taking revenge for his destroyed temple. Crowds gathered at the old temple site by the river, crafting paper shrimp and crabs to guide the "Dragon King's army" back to the sea. Taoist priests performed rituals; spirit-mediums chanted incantations. The climax was a grand parade — garrison commander Xia Douyin and other senior officials set up an altar at the temple ruins and kowtowed, begging the Dragon King's forgiveness. Ironically, former mayor Liu Wendao, who had ordered the temple's demolition, reportedly saw dragons in the floodwaters. Shanghai suffered only minor flooding — locals claimed they discovered the Dragon King (transformed into three snakes) at a hotel and promptly burned incense.

05
Accountability
The Dragon at the Breach: Who Was Responsible for the Flood?
On August 1, Wuhan's last line of defense — the railway embankment — breached after workers stopped. Accounts diverged: Shanghai journalists blamed an incompetent engineer; British media reported a coolie strike demanding higher wages; Xie Qianmao revealed a shocking version — the embankment supervisor fabricated a Dragon King sighting to frighten workers away and flee with their wages. Historian Fang Qiumei noted that municipal managers had diverted all funds to road construction rather than dike maintenance. Road building, urban renewal, and temple demolition — these acts of "modernization" may themselves have exacerbated the flooding.

🔑
Core Paradox: Dragon King belief was not fatalism — Hubei residents always built temples alongside dikes, integrating ritual with engineering. The anti-superstition movement equated folk belief with passive resignation, ignoring its role in community solidarity and charitable relief. During the 1931 flood, temples became refugee shelters and monks distributed food on the front lines.
🔑 Key Takeaway
Dragon King worship was not a vestige of ignorance but an integral component of disaster culture — the psychological toolkit communities used to understand calamity, forge solidarity, and sustain hope in the face of uncontrollable natural forces. The anti-superstition movement destroyed this toolkit in the name of science and modernity without providing any alternative. When the 1931 flood struck, people returned to their most familiar explanatory framework — the Dragon King's wrath — not as regression but as the cultural inertia of millennia of disaster coping. Disasters test not only engineering but faith.
