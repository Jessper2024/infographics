Core Proposition

For millennia, residents of the middle Yangtze lived in symbiosis with dragons — the Dragon King was water official, rain master, and community guardian. Yet in 1930, the municipal government demolished the Dragon King Temple to build a road. When catastrophic floods struck the following year, countless people believed it was the Dragon King's revenge. Dragon King worship was not simple "superstition" — it was a disaster-culture mechanism that integrated religious ritual with hydraulic engineering. The battle over its existence exposes the deep fractures between modern governance, folk religion, and political legitimacy in Republican China.

01
Evolution

Taming the Dragon: From Wetland Beast to Celestial Water Official

In the belief system of Chu rice farmers, dragons began as wild creatures feared for their rain-making power. The rise of hydraulic agriculture fundamentally transformed the dragon's nature — as communities built dikes and polders to enter water-control systems, dragons were "tamed" into the Dragon King (Longwang), absorbed into the celestial bureaucracy as "environmental officials" who managed rainfall within their jurisdiction. Temples were nearly always built alongside dikes: ritual and engineering were never contradictory — together they formed a holistic approach to flood management.

02
Critique

Questioning the Dragon: Elites, Missionaries, and a New Conceptual Weapon

Attacks on Dragon King worship did not begin in the 20th century. By the 19th century, Confucian elites already despised the Dragon Boat Festival's "rowdy excesses," while Christian missionaries insisted the Chinese were "praying to the wrong god" — Justus Doolittle lamented that villagers "knew nothing of the Lord who sends rain from heaven." But the true turning point came with the entry of the word mixin (superstition) into the Chinese lexicon: it demoted folk belief from "heterodoxy" to "nonexistence," negating its philosophical foundations entirely. Yun Daiying went further, condemning both "gods and ghosts" and "the nonsense of Christian superstition" with equal contempt.

03
Suppression

Dethroning the Dragon King: Revolutionary Violence and De-Sacralization

When Wuhan became the revolutionary capital in 1927, political radicalism and the anti-superstition campaign entered an uncanny resonance. Protesters borrowed ritual forms — dragon parades, drumming, firecrackers — for anti-imperialist demonstrations: folk religion's symbolic power was unwittingly weaponized for political mobilization. When the Nationalists later suppressed the revolution, the anti-superstition campaign paradoxically escalated: 1928 legislation authorized ruthless attacks, Earth God temples were converted into public toilets, deity statues were "arrested" and publicly "decapitated." The Dragon King was officially classified as superstition — while Yu the Great survived as "paragon of Chinese engineering."

04
Revival

The Flood Sweeps the Dragon King Temple: Faith's Comeback Under Disaster

As the river rose in July 1931, Wuhan residents desperately sought explanations. A local charity invited a "spirit" to perform fuji (spirit-writing); the cryptic result read "Old Han, no worries" — interpreted as reassurance that Old Hankou would remain dry. Soon the entire city was submerged. This was widely understood as the Dragon King's revenge. Mayor He Baohua personally presided over appeasement rituals at the temple ruins; garrison commander Xia Douyin led parades, set up an altar, and kowtowed. Journalist Chen Hesong mocked this as "temporarily embracing the Buddha's feet" — suddenly devout amid disaster, faithless the rest of the year. Yet the irony: Xia Douyin suffered no political damage from kowtowing to the Dragon King; he was promoted to provincial governor in 1932.

05
Reflection

The Dragon at the Breach: Two "Ethno-Meteorologies" in Final Confrontation

Historian Fang Qiumei attributes the flood defense collapse to years of municipal neglect — road construction had drained dike maintenance funds. The bitter irony: road building (including demolishing the Dragon King Temple) appears to have genuinely exacerbated the city's flooding. As for the final dike breach, witnesses gave starkly different accounts: some blamed an incompetent engineer, others a coolie strike demanding higher wages, while Xie Qianmao reported that the supervisor fabricated a Dragon King sighting to frighten workers away and flee with their wages. It seems the dragon — whether as belief, excuse, or cultural symbol — truly did cause Wuhan's flood. Meanwhile, British journalist William Bruce Lockhart deployed an Old Testament variant of moral meteorology, declaring the flood "divine punishment" for Wuhan's defiance against the British concession — proof that secularists had their own "ethno-meteorology" too.

Key Takeaway

Dragon King worship was not simple "superstition" — it was the cultural crystallization of millennia of environmental adaptation in the middle Yangtze, weaving religious ritual and hydraulic engineering into a single fabric. Though the anti-superstition movement legally dethroned the Dragon King, it could not replace his social function when the flood arrived: as their homes were submerged, people still turned to the Dragon King for explanation and solace. The Dragon King's dual nature — simultaneously tamed celestial official and wild subversive force — reveals the deepest truth of folk religion as "disaster culture": it provided ordinary people battered by environmental risk with frameworks for understanding catastrophe and scripts for action, while also furnishing a symbolic language of collective grievance that could be activated at any moment.
