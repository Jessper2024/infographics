Wenhua Kulv · Ch.19 "The Distant Sea"

Chinese culture has long feared and forbidden the sea—but countless living Chinese have always entered and embraced it. Culture and life are, after all, very different

Above the Pacific's deepest Mariana Trench, the author experienced life at its limit in a small boat—dark brown seawater with a purple tint, waves semi-solid rather than liquid, the boat oscillating between extremes in suffocating silence. Here, humanity's artistic dream of being "above nature" must be abandoned; one must become eternal rock sculpture. From Hemudu canoes to Chinese "home-wherever-they-roam" across Micronesian islands—including a Chinese couple who sailed for 25 years—the sea reveals a long-buried dimension of Chinese culture: freedom, challenge, symbiosis with nature.

01
Raging Sea
Becoming eternal rock sculpture above the Mariana Trench
In the deepest waters of the Western Pacific, the author was drenched by waves, mouth full of bitter salt, eyes stinging. The seawater was dark brown with a purple tint; the waves felt semi-solid rather than liquid—as if just propelled by solid force, or about to solidify in the next instant. The boat oscillated between extreme heights and depths in absolute, suffocating silence. When the largest waves strike, even flinching is forbidden—to flinch is to become soft, alive, struggling, crying out, and here struggle and outcry equal death. One must focus every fiber and become eternal rock sculpture. "Even if cracked, even if broken—still eternal rock sculpture." The author pressed his whole body against the stern, turning entirely black—as if "the dark-iron and black-jade energy from life's depths had been fully mobilized and instantly released."

02
Maritime Gene
From Hemudu to canoes: the buried maritime gene
The indigenous boatman said elders believed their ancestors also came from China. The author, researching the ultimate fate of Hemudu and Liangzhu peoples, had consistently entertained the possibility of distant sea voyages driven by catastrophic flooding. Even today, a rare few islanders can navigate by the stars alone, paddling canoes from Micronesia to Hawaii. "One person, paddling a canoe, can reach Hawaii?" "Yes." "Then could they reach China?" "Yes." Yet where did the great navigator Zheng He die? No clear record exists. Rigid Chinese characters have no affinity with the sea—half of China's history sank beneath the waves. Lazy armchair scholars only search dusty books—books that never contained the real ocean.

03
Home Everywhere
Another form of Chinese "home": the farther, the more essential
The author's wife fell ill on the island with no doctor or medicine. Yang Gang—a former Beijing diplomat who quit his career, entranced by the sea—appeared with a pot of rice porridge with greens and Chinese herbal pills. "Rice porridge with greens, plus huo xiang zheng qi wan—one taste brings you back to life. This is what it means to be Chinese." The island's only Chinese resident, Chen Mingcan from Guangdong, lived in a tin shack with poultry and a satellite dish for Phoenix TV. There was also a Chinese diving couple—Li Mingxue and Zhang Xin—who traveled the world's dive sites. "Diving couples depend on each other for life and death. They generally have no children, no house. Their minds hold only distant dive sites they must visit." Their "home" can drift anywhere on Earth.

04
Maritime Life
Twenty-five years at sea: a couple rewrites anthropology and aesthetics
The husband, Luc, was Belgian; the wife, Jackie, was Chinese-American. They lived continuously on sailing boats for 25 years. Each voyage typically lasted under two weeks, with the two taking night watches. Any woman would find such a life unbearable, but this Chinese woman endured. Her face looked older than her years—the solemn imprint that ferocious space and time left on a Chinese woman. Of those 25 years, day and night in that cramped vessel, Yu Qiuyu writes: anthropology, ethics, literature, aesthetics—all have been quietly rewritten by such a couple in the dawn and dusk.

05
Life Awakens
The gap between culture and life: finally, someone disturbs the sea and sky
Chinese culture may have long watched, suspected, feared, and forbidden the sea—but for countless living Chinese, this has never been true. They enter, embrace, and depend on the sea—culture and life are, after all, very different. From Hemudu canoes to Zheng He's treasure ships, from distant ancestors on Pacific islands to today's sailing couples, that long-buried life within Chinese culture has finally been awakened in an era when people can step beyond texts and beyond the small family. During those Micronesian days and nights, the author's wife looked at him several times: "We should have had a boat long ago..." He said: "It must be a sea-going boat." She smiled: "Of course." Behind this simple exchange lies a solemn affirmation of the maritime dimension missing from Chinese culture.
🔑 Key Takeaway
"The Distant Sea" is the most embodied chapter in Wenhua Kulv—the author no longer observes as a scholar but confronts the raging sea with flesh and blood. This embodiment reveals the deeper meaning of the "cultural bitter journey": Chinese culture needs not only to reflect in the study, but to rediscover its vital dimension at sea, at the limit. From Hemudu canoes to today's Chinese who disturb the sea and sky, the maritime gene suppressed by a "sea-fearing" culture has always flowed in the depths—a sea-going boat is not a choice, but a necessary cultural supplement.
