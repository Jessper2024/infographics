🔴 Banks That Never Lose: The Art of Lying Down to Profit

The bank profit formula is brutally simple: a developer offers 12% interest; Bank A packages the loan as a "wealth product" and sells it to Bank B at 10%, pocketing a 2% risk-free spread. Bank B does the same. Depositors get 3.5%. Banks have become mere "capital middlemen." Loans come in two flavors: to local government platforms — principal unrecoverable, just roll it over, branch managers feel zero pressure; to small private firms — personal asset collateral mandatory, default triggers immediate asset seizure and criminal referral. SOE loans operate in a closed loop: "government credit = central bank credit." Commercial bank funds come from the PBOC, get lent to public entities, and circle right back to PBOC deposits earning interest — simple and comfortable. China has no bankers, only branch managers — they read KPIs off A4 sheets from above, underperform and just rotate to a retirement post. After 2003, real estate's explosive rise made commercial banks soar, leaving other sectors in the dust. But oversupply in 3rd/4th/5th-tier cities is eroding the profit foundation.

🟠 The Inside Story of Wealth Products: Legal Scams Disguised as Innovation

Walk past the savings counter (cold, bulletproof glass, prison-visit vibes) to the wealth-management counter — now that's "the beautiful life": tea served by smiling ladies in black silk. Wealth products are essentially grassroots fundraising — high-interest deposit-gathering in legal drag. Financial workers and lawyers exhaust themselves designing "capital pools": pooled funds go to infrastructure and real estate; repayment in 5 years is impossible. Solution: issue new products to repay old ones. As long as the rollover machine runs, risk stays invisible. How are yields calculated? The security guard writes a number on the blackboard outside the post office; take 60%, the rest is "yield." Where does the money actually go? Nearly everything traces back to housing prices and land values. Primary buyers: the elderly — raised in the planned-economy era, naive and cash-rich. "First half: everyone makes money. Second half: concentrated slaughter." Watch the elderly in your family — they're hiding six-figure savings and are prime targets. A 10% drop in housing prices triggers mass wealth-product defaults; bank lobbies fill with furious seniors.

🟡 The Stock Market Graveyard: A Pit Retail Investors Can Never Fill

The essential difference between Chinese and Western stock markets: Western companies treat IPO as a new starting line; Chinese companies treat IPO as the finish line — sell shares fast and emigrate. Stocks are electronic symbols with infinite supply: nearly 1,000 companies have passed IPO review awaiting their turn to extract money. How much capital can you pour in? The rich have all moved into housing. "A market where only poor people participate — how can it rally significantly?" The housing market is fairer than stocks — no insider information, no price manipulation. When prices move, everyone moves together. "This is China's last patch of clean land." Stock trading has no entry barrier — fulfilling every poor person's fantasy of getting rich. Old retail investors trapped for years have wronged their families in countless ways: down payments evaporated into stocks, creating endless personal tragedies. Brokerages profit from license rents — ordinary people never will. Textbooks say rate cuts boost stocks? Nonsense. When rates drop, dump stocks and borrow to buy property. The path to wealth: stay away from stocks, from Bitcoin, from high-yield "wealth products."

🟣 Baby's Got Muscle: Yu'e Bao's Siege on Big Banks

Alipay launched in 2003 — banks were too busy lying down and profiting to care about product innovation. E-commerce desperately needed payment rails; Alipay won everything. Yu'e Bao (Alipay's money-market fund) turned dead Alipay balances into live money, essentially "online banking U-shield operations + free deposits and withdrawals." The catch: free in/out creates large volatility risk — big banks avoided it. Yu'e Bao pays term-deposit rates for demand-deposit rights — hard to sustain high yields long-term. The banks' counterstrike was masterful: tighten mortgage lending, holding the entire economy hostage. Yu'e Bao pushes up economy-wide funding costs — ordinary people earn an extra ¥200/month in Yu'e Bao interest, then pay ¥100K extra in mortgage interest. Banks don't fear Yu'e Bao; they fear having to work for a living. Jack Ma's tactic: bet on banks being spoiled rich kids, use foreign VC backing to sustain unprofitability and harass them. A showdown is inevitable. Once massive printing resumes, Yu'e Bao loses its raison d'être instantly — ¥400B in assets with nobody to take the other side; paying interest alone will kill it.

🩷 China Can't Have a Financial Crisis — Printing Is the Ultimate Antidote

What defines a financial crisis? Bank runs, capital flight, currency devaluation. None can happen in China: you can only take $5,000 abroad, the exchange rate is set by the PBOC, and a bank run is laughable — with your bank card in your hand, what exactly are you running on? The only way to resolve bad debt: keep pouring water. In 1995, owing ¥100K was astronomical; now it's 1–2 years' salary. Printing is the best method for dissolving debt. All financial risk dissolves before the printing press. Credit asset securitization means taking unrecoverable infrastructure loans, blending them with 20 calculus formulas, slapping on a bank label, and selling to the public. Banks borrow at 2%, lend at 12%, sell to the public at 7% — pocketing a steady 5–6% risk-free spread. But risks in 3rd/4th/5th-tier cities are surfacing. Two endings: keep printing, or cough up the profits. The answer is obvious.

💡 Key Takeaway

China's financial system is a closed-loop trinity: "printing → SOEs → real estate." Banks lie on this loop collecting rent; "financial innovation" is just legalized circumvention to keep pumping credit into housing. Wealth products are traps designed for the elderly; the stock market is a casino designed for the poor. The Yu'e Bao vs. banks battle isn't market competition — it's a collision of two monopoly models. The ordinary person's survival rule: stay away from all "high yield" financial products, stay away from stocks. Small savings → sub-5% products. Large savings → Beijing/Shanghai property. Remember the Wenzhou Formula: Year 1: smile. Year 2: cry. Year 3: run. First half: everyone profits. Second half: concentrated slaughter. Which half are you in?
