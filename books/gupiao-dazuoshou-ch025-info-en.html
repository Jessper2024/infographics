In the tone of an elder, Livermore offers his most sincere advice to ordinary investors: don't blindly trust broker recommendations — first ask whose side the recommender's interests are on; never accept "free gifts" from insiders — gifted stock is the most expensive present; see through the essence of stock-split games — when a stock needs price-cut promotions to attract buyers, ask "why." Your most reliable ally is only your own independent thinking.
01
Advice One
Broker Recommendations: First Ask Who's Paying
It is entirely proper for brokers to provide trading advice. But Livermore exposes a fatal conflict of interest: the stocks brokers induce the public to buy through market letters are often the very same stocks for which they have already received sell orders from insiders. Every time a retail investor "follows a recommendation," they are paying for the dual profits of both the broker and the insider.
📉

Broker Tells Retail Investors: "Buy"

Market letters, verbal advice, authoritative analysis — each tells you what to buy. The broker cares about earning commissions right now.

📈

Broker Executes for Insiders: "Sell"

For the same stock, insiders ask brokers to sell 50,000 shares. With a large following, brokers earn both commissions and option profits.

Advice Two
Insider "Free Gifts": The Most Expensive Presents
Livermore portrays the insider's SOP: visit a large brokerage firm's customer manager, express gratitude — "I'm going to give you a chance to really make some money" — then "let" them have stock at below market price, creating instant "paper profit." This bait is used to have the entire following crowd take over the insider's position.

Advice Three
The Stock-Split Game: Old Wine in New Bottles
Livermore exposes the most popular and thoroughly legal sales gimmick — the stock split: issuing 2, 4, or even 10 "new" shares to replace 1 "old" share — often just changing the certificate color. Why doesn't the public ask why the stock needs subdivision to be promoted? This alone constitutes the complete warning signal.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The most precious legacy Livermore leaves to ordinary investors is this crystal-clear advice: in a market where the law only punishes bearish rumors but not bullish lies, your wallet can only be guarded by yourself. Three iron rules: never blindly trust any recommendation (first trace the interest relationship), never accept free gifts (what's given free is the most expensive), never worship packaging magic (splits and color changes do not equal value creation). Independent thinking is your only talisman on this asymmetric battlefield.
