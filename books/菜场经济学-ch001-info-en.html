🔴 Paper Money Never Sleeps: The Over-issuance Engine

Modern paper currency is barely a century old. China's monetary regime jumped from "fiscal transfers" to "direct printing" — Premier Zhu Rongji took the helm of the PBOC and pressed print. To contain inflation, a reservoir was dug: the housing market. Trade surplus → forex inflows → mandatory FX settlement → passive RMB expansion → all flowing into real estate — an unstoppable flywheel. The PBOC Monetary Committee has said only one thing for 18 years: "Continue prudent monetary policy." M2 grew nearly 8× anyway. Paper money's greatest power: rapidly funneling resources wherever the state wants. Get the direction right, develop fast; get it wrong, irreparable. The trend is irreversible: more money, faster.

🟠 The ¥4 Trillion Watershed

The 2008 financial crisis coincided with China's new Labor Contract Law — export factories shuttered en masse, migrant workers returned home. The ¥4T stimulus concentrated on infrastructure, the ideal sector for low-skilled rural labor. In three years, the money was gone; Europe's debt crisis demanded more printing. Result: a real estate bonanza. Steel, coal, shipbuilding boomed. But "doing 10 years' work in 3" created massive overcapacity while inflating labor costs in every other sector. Biggest winners: first recipients of new money, migrant workers, landlords, banks. Biggest losers: business owners, white-collar workers, ordinary families with just one home. The ¥4T permanently rewired China's social structure and shattered the post-80s generation's dream of "going to Beijing/Shanghai and buying a home."

🟡 Winners and Losers: The Class Divide of Money

Printing is a choice of interests: print, and the rich see assets soar while migrants earn high wages on construction sites; stop, and only then can the middle class afford homes. Migrant workers are the biggest beneficiaries — daily wages jumped from tens to 300 yuan. Yet they have little savings (all spent on building village homes and weddings), so inflation doesn't bother them. Urban white-collar workers save 5–10 years for a down payment, watching their bank deposits "slowly evaporate." Over the last decade, the richest and poorest benefited (developers, migrants); the sole losers: non-state-sector white-collar workers and their parents. In ten years, average monthly wages may reach ¥10,000 — but eggs will cost ¥12 each, and a Shanghai lane-house will rent for ¥7,000/month. Save houses, not cash — it's the only escape route.

🟣 The Exchange Rate Bubble: The Biggest Bubble of All

China's biggest bubble isn't real estate — it's the RMB exchange rate, "like subsidized housing, restricted and artificial." US QE3 pours $40B/month into global markets; China is forced to print in response or lose the exchange rate peg. RMB inflates internally (wages double in 3 years, housing prices go wild) yet appreciates externally — strangling exports. Capital controls create a pressure cooker: money flows in but can't flow out; M2 surpasses America's. When Japan QEs, yen flows abroad for yield; RMB stays trapped, flooding into housing. The eurozone and Japan join the printing party — "all ugly brides meet their in-laws together." RMB will catch up to the New Taiwan Dollar in 5 years, the yen in 10. The ultimate lesson: Beijing/Shanghai property is the currency anchor. Holding property is holding foreign exchange.

🩷 Distorted Interest Rates: Western Economics Is Garbage Here

Western monetary policy centers on price signals — interest rates. But China's SOEs don't care about rates. Hike to 10%? City investment companies shrug: "The mayor will be transferred, here's the subway as collateral." Cheap loans are hoarded by public entities; private firms scramble like "mad dogs" for leftovers, bidding rates up to 15–18%. Deposit rate liberalization won't help — small firms can't outbid mega-SOEs. The only result of interest rate marketization: SOEs keep getting capital advantages disconnected from performance. SMEs: good luck. Rate cuts benefit local government debt; RRR cuts are just fancier words for printing. Monetary control must be "crude and direct, quantity only" — because price signals don't work at all.

💡 Key Takeaway

The printing press has no off switch. As long as the power stays on, paper money multiplies — this is interest, not rationality. In the age of fiat, saving houses instead of cash is the only reliable hedge. Trace the money path: PBOC prints → buys up 3rd/4th-tier city housing as "affordable housing" → contractors and developers take the cash to Beijing/Shanghai → prime BJ/SH apartments become "large-denomination CDs," disconnected from wage-earners entirely. Each year of delay shrinks ¥600K in savings from a small apartment to half a kitchen. Ordinary people should study macro direction, not penny-pinching techniques. Right direction, outsized returns; wrong direction, harder work leads to deeper poverty.
