Chapter 6 · Beijing & Shanghai Forever Rising

In the Age of Fiat, Save Houses Not Cash — BJ/SH Real Estate Is China's Ultimate Anchor
SCQA Analysis
S (Situation): ~50% of China's GDP is housing-related, over 50% of local government revenue comes from real estate, and 80% of household wealth is property.
C (Complication): M2 grows 12–20% annually, a tsunami of money seeking the only reservoir — but manufacturing loses money, the stock market traps everyone, gold is illiquid, and capital controls block overseas investment. Society has nowhere to park money except housing. The worse the real economy, the higher BJ/SH housing rises — a self-reinforcing death spiral.
Q (Question): Will Beijing and Shanghai housing prices ever fall? If not, what choices do ordinary people have?
A (Answer): Beijing and Shanghai will rise forever. Not by economic law, but by institutional inevitability — printing never stops, inward migration never stops, land supply is precision-engineered scarcity, and there are no alternative investment channels. A BJ/SH deed is a large-denomination CD, more reliable than RMB itself. The poor stay poor for three generations — just because they bought housing too late.

🔴 The Monetary Reservoir: Housing Prices Are Printed

M2 rises 14–28% annually. Freshly printed money must go somewhere or prices explode everywhere — real estate became the monetary reservoir. The logic is brutally simple: print money → build roads → every tier pockets cash → everyone rushes to sales offices → housing prices rise. China has no other store of value. Overseas investment is blocked by capital controls. What else can you buy? Currency and housing are ironclad-linked: RMB is a dammed lake, housing prices are the dam. The higher the water, the higher the dam must be built. The basis for RMB issuance is not foreign exchange reserves — forex dropped 18% in 8 months of 2015 while M2 rose — the real basis is the national average housing price growth rate. To suppress housing you must stop printing. But without printing, how do you pay for social spending and ongoing projects? So printing never stops. BJ/SH housing always rises.

🟠 The Worse the Real Economy, the Higher BJ/SH Housing

Cai's decade-long observation of Wenzhou. Private capital must go somewhere. When business is profitable, who speculates on housing? When business sours, production capital seeks an exit — scan the landscape, and BJ/SH forever rising is the only answer. The more factories close, the higher housing climbs. A Zhejiang SME with 50 workers, sweating all year for ¥1M profit — can't buy a Shanghai toilet. All of society labors for real estate. A major manufacturer doing ¥200M in revenue struggles to net ¥10M after tax — while one Pudong Inner Ring three-bedroom apartment costs ¥10M. The agony of industrial capital is the fuel for housing. If the real economy were thriving, capital would invest in production and housing speculation would subside. The key to reviving manufacturing is in the housing market — but it's a key that can't turn the lock.

🟡 BJ/SH Deeds = Large-Denomination CDs

The credibility of a BJ/SH Inner Ring deed far exceeds that of RMB purchasing power. Hauling a sack of cash to propose marriage — that's country folk. City people bring one thin deed — the mother-in-law serves tea immediately. Buffett? Please. Buying housing five years ago beat Buffett's returns. Three years ago matched him. BJ/SH housing is quasi-currency: different brands of pants are incomparable, but money is perfectly comparable. A 30% discount in Shunyi hits Chaoyang in one minute, Shanghai housing in three days. In the fiat era, storing RMB is worse than storing a BJ/SH apartment. Shanghainese have never gone bankrupt from mortgage payments; those whose lives are ruined for lack of a mortgage are everywhere. Bought at ¥4M, unemployed three years later? Don't panic — it's now ¥7M. Net ¥3M gain. Head back to Chengdu for a townhouse. A triumphant return.

🟣 Supply & Demand: Manufactured Scarcity + Population Influx

BJ/SH hold 50M people combined — 1.3B more outside, 7.3M new graduates annually. Housing can never be enough because it must bundle land, transport, utilities, employment, and public services. Beijing's water and transport are already at breaking point. Authorities run a hunger-marketing scheme — deliberately drip-feeding land supply to keep pushing prices up. Release three years of land supply in one year, oversupply would cut prices instantly — but that will never happen. High housing prices are a penalty for disorderly population movement. If China fully freed migration, BJ/SH would need ~150M people, Shanghai 80M. The short-term way to cut BJ/SH prices: ruthlessly expel people. But when nine-year compulsory education is free for migrant children, how do you expel? The long-term answer: develop other core cities. But that's a long road.

🩷 Prices Can't Fall: The Interest Structure Is Locked

A 5% drop is tolerable. 15% means ¥500–600K losses per buyer — sales offices get smashed everywhere. US and Japan housing can fall because their oligarchs aren't developers; China's can't — when market funds are drained, the printing factory does a night shift and new money surges back. Purchase restrictions aren't for making housing affordable — they prevent industrial capital from flooding in too fast and triggering mass unemployment. Without limits, Inner Ring could hit ¥300K/sqm. Property tax won't cut prices — in BJ/SH undersupply, any tax is passed to tenants. After property tax, you still can't afford BJ/SH — but your hometown county apartment now gets taxed. Two slaps in the face. Housing prices are the convergence of all economic and social problems, involving 80% of people's interests. To truly make prices fall, 80% of people would want to beat you up.

💡 Key Takeaway

Beijing and Shanghai will rise forever — not a slogan, but the inevitable outcome of institutional structure. The printing press has no off switch, population inflow has no stop valve, land supply is precision-engineered scarcity. Tier-1 housing is hard currency, Tier-2 is somewhere in between, Tier-3 housing is a consumer good, county-level housing has use value but zero investment value. The national housing market looks to BJ/SH: if BJ/SH holds, other cities have hope; if BJ/SH falls, total collapse. For ordinary people: grab a spot if you can — buying in BJ/SH isn't for yourself, it's holding a spot for the next generation. Today's BJ/SH prices, seen ten years from now, are cabbage prices. The poor stay poor for three generations — just because they bought housing too late.
