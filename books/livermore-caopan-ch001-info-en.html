01
Mindset

Speculation Is a Serious Business, Never Gambling

Livermore opens with a hard line: if you have a speculative temperament, treat speculation as a serious business. Be diligent, and never lower yourself to the level of the outsider.

Outsiders dismiss speculation as mere gambling — and they have paid dearly for this delusion. "Your success will be in direct proportion to the honesty and sincerity of your own efforts." You reap what you sow.

This is a guide for those "willing to put in the sweat and study to earn a reasonable return." You must keep your own records, think with your own brain, and reach your own conclusions — just as you wouldn't read a fitness book and delegate the exercise to someone else.

❌ Should Not Speculate
The foolish, the lazy-minded, the psychologically unfit, the get-rich-quick gambler
✅ Suited For
Those with speculative instinct who are willing to study and treat it as serious business
🔑 Core Requirement
Keep your own records, think independently, build and follow your own rules
02
Entry Discipline

Wait for Market Confirmation — Act Only at the Pivotal Psychological Moment

Speculation is anticipating future market movements — but you must wait until the market itself validates your opinion before signing off on your judgment.

Livermore repeatedly warns: even if your judgment is ultimately correct, acting too early can still cause losses. For a stock at $25 he expects to reach $50, he patiently waits until it becomes active and makes a new high (e.g., $30). Only then has the market confirmed your thesis through its own behavior.

Livermore's core experience: the real profits from speculation come from trades that are profitable from the start. He always commits his first trade at a moment when the market's momentum is so powerful "it simply has to go through, and indeed it is going through."

03
Psychology

Let Profits Run, Cut Losses Fast — Taming Hope and Fear

The speculator's greatest enemy resides within: being fearful when you should be hopeful, and hopeful when you should be fearful.

❌ The Wrong Emotional Response

Up 2 points → fear of losing the gain → sell immediately
Down 2 points → hope it's "just a temporary reaction"
Result: You cut profits short and let losses run

✅ The Right Approach

When winning: hold firm and let profits ride the trend
When losing: cut decisively before a small loss becomes a big hole
"Profits take care of themselves. Losses never do."

Livermore admits he himself fell prey to impatience and did foolish things. Speculation is like poker — everyone is tempted to "play every hand," and this common human weakness is the No.1 cause of ruin. The successful speculator must act as his own insurance broker, vigilantly guarding his capital account against losses large enough to threaten future operations.

04
Framework

Fusing Time and Price — Your Records Are Your Compass

The core of Livermore's trading system: only after incorporating the time element can your records predict major market movements with reliable accuracy.

Over 40 years, he spent countless nights reflecting, waking at dawn with new ideas, and immediately testing them against historical data. Gradually, he developed a mature new method of keeping records that served as his compass.

⏱ The Time Element
Wait for the right moment to follow the trend. Never buy on reactions, never sell on rallies.
💰 The Price Element
Re-examine every stock through the Livermore formula combining time and price — not by whether it "looks high" or "looks low."

He emphasizes that his recording method clearly reveals the evolution of the current market movement. By combining familiarity with stock groups and the time element, you can determine the timing of major moves and pick the leader of any sector. The key: fill in the figures yourself — "in doing the work yourself, you will be amazed at how many ideas come to you."

05
Iron Rules

Five Trading Commandments You Must Never Violate

Livermore explicitly lists several iron rules that must be "burned into your mind" — lessons bought with real money over 40 years of speculation.

1
Never sell because a stock seems too high — a stock can rise from $50 to $150 with good earnings and sound management
2
Never buy because a stock has fallen sharply — deep declines usually have solid reasons; it may still be at an extreme high on a fair-value basis
3
Never buy on reactions / Never sell on rallies — buy only when prices make new highs in an uptrend; sell short only on new lows in a downtrend
4
Never average down a losing trade — if your first trade is in the red, never follow up. Do not attempt to reduce the cost of a losing position
5
Never confuse speculation with investment — many "investors" paid dearly for buying stocks with speculative intent. Today's safe stock could be tomorrow's pure speculation — or even worthless.

"You can win a horse race, but you can't win all the horse races. If we are in the market day after day and week after week, we cannot consistently profit."

💡
Key Takeaway

Speculation is not gambling — it is a serious business requiring independent judgment, patient waiting, and strict discipline. Success comes not from predicting the market, but from: keeping your own records, waiting for the market to confirm your judgment before acting, letting profits run while cutting losses decisively, fusing time and price into a decision framework, and obeying the iron rules — never sell because a stock looks high, never buy because it has fallen sharply, and never average down.
