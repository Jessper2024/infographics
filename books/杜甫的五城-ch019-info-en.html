Five Cities of Du Fu · Ch19 "The Yellow Earth of Northern Shaanxi"

Sancha · Yulin · Yan'an · Pucheng

Leaving Ying County for northern Shaanxi, the bus broke down in the middle of nowhere — the passengers disembarked silently as if they all knew what to do. Stranded in the obscure town of Sancha, he slept on a heated kang for the first time, waking at midnight to a full moon hanging above the open door — "I may not have seen moonlight and stars like this for over a decade." He recuperated in Yulin, climbed the Ming-era Zhenbeitai fortress. In Yan'an, he saw jujube trees for the first time and rode a tricycle through the city hunting for fried millet cakes. Then to Pucheng to pay homage at Tang imperial tombs — Emperor Ruizong's Qiaoling with its elegant stone ostrich, Emperor Xuanzong's Tailing rough and forlorn, the decline of the High Tang carved in stone.

01Stranded in Sancha: Heated Kang and Midnight Moon
The bus to Baode broke down in an anonymous wheat field. The driver said, "It's dead, everyone off." Passengers waited aimlessly beside the road — "like 'Waiting for Godot.'" A Zhejiang salesman led them to Sancha. The inn was a family courtyard with five added rooms. He rented a room with a heated kang. Waking at midnight, "a full moon hung right above the open door. The moonlight streamed in faintly… I may not have seen moonlight and stars like this for over a decade."
02Yulin: Recuperation by the Ancient Great Wall
From Sancha through Baode, ferrying across the Yellow River into Shaanxi, skirting the Mu Us Desert to Yulin. "I arrived in Yulin with a 'recuperating' frame of mind." He visited Zhenbeitai — "Looking back, I saw it still standing on a small hill, solitary against the vast sky." He took a rare long afternoon nap. At dusk, walking on the surviving sections of the ancient Great Wall, he saw students reading on the crumbling ramparts in the fading light, thinking of Shi Tiesheng's stories about this land — "I felt both happiness and sadness welling up."
03Yan'an Food Hunt: Jujube Trees and Buckwheat Noodles
A ten-hour bus ride to Yan'an. "I never understand why travelers read books on buses instead of watching the scenery. Scenery, once passed, is gone; books can be read at home." In the jujube orchard, he saw jujube trees for the first time — "their leaves so tiny, branches so slender, drooping softly." The young tricycle driver became his guide. Asked what's best to eat: fried millet cakes. But they were seasonal. The driver took him around town searching, finally settling for buckwheat noodles with mutton soup that made him sweat. Recalling Shi Tiesheng's fiction: "They too spent all day thinking about food, looking for food."
04Pucheng: Qiaoling's Splendor and Tailing's Decay
He took a hand tractor to visit the tombs — the same kind of vehicle featured in Zhang Xianliang's and Wang Anyi's novels. Ruizong's Qiaoling exuded royal presence: "My favorite was the stone ostrich, its long neck curled in such a graceful arc suspended in midair." Du Fu once stood on this very spirit way, writing his "Thirty Rhymes on Qiaoling." Xuanzong's Tailing told a different story — "Everything seemed carved in haste, the figures weak, all shrunk to a smaller scale." Xuanzong had personally chosen this site for its "dragon-coiling, phoenix-soaring" terrain, yet after death, no one cared to build him a worthy tomb.
05Yaowang Mountain: An Unexpected Trove of Northern Wei Stelae
From Yan'an through Huangling to Tongchuan: "The highest and most majestic loess plateaus lay on the road between Fu County and Huangling." He failed to reach the Yuhua Palace ruins where Xuanzang once translated sutras, but unexpectedly found a wealth of Northern Wei stone inscriptions on Yaowang Mountain — "compensating for the Yuhua Palace regret." He also visited the Tang-Song Yaozhou kiln site. Two full days of yellow earth. He recalled the most desolate loess landscape he'd ever seen, in Lintao, southern Gansu — "uniformly parched yellow, so barren there wasn't even farmland, so arid even grass couldn't grow."
💡 Key Insight
Northern Shaanxi's yellow earth reveals itself in two forms: the natural plateau and the imperial tombs. From the moonlight over a heated kang in Sancha to the 1,200-year-old stone ostrich on Qiaoling's spirit way, Lai Ruihe traveled between the most humble folk life (heated kangs, steamed buns, tricycles, tractors) and the most magnificent Tang civilization remnants. Xuanzong chose his own tomb site for its auspicious terrain, yet after his death no one bothered to build it with care — the High Tang's decline etched in stone. And the traveler gazing at the midnight moon in Sancha, the man hunting fried millet cakes through Yan'an's streets, was now approaching middle age with a newfound equanimity, taking this landscape's beauty as simply the way things are.
