The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.9 "Blitzkrieg"

Pincer Offensive · Deep Strike · Last-Minute Abdication In the winter of 1125, the Jurchen launched a two-pronged assault on the Song — Nianhan's western army through Shanxi, Wolibu's eastern army across the Hebei plain. Emperor Huizong abdicated in panic; his 26-year-old crown prince was forcibly dragged to the throne. Within a single month: Yanjing surrendered, Shiling Pass fell, the Yellow River defense was abandoned — the Song empire shuddered.

This chapter marks the turning point — the Jurchen launched their blitzkrieg. Two armies formed a pincer: General Nianhan crossed the treacherous Shanxi passes on the western route, while Crown Prince Wolibu raced across the Hebei plain on the east. Song defenses crumbled under a cascade of mutinies by the Yisheng Army: Shuozhou, Wuzhou, Daizhou, Shiling Pass, and Xinzhou all fell from within. Yanjing, recovered from the Jurchen at enormous cost just two years earlier, was lost again when General Guo Yaoshi was betrayed by his own colleagues. In Bianjing, Emperor Huizong hastily abdicated to his 26-year-old son Qinzong and fled south. Li Gang made his debut, arguing that only a true emperor could rally the nation. The Yellow River garrison fled without a fight, and the student Chen Dong denounced the "Six Traitors." The Jingkang era had begun.

01
Strategy
The Jurchen Pincer: Two Arms Converging, Eight Taihang Passes as Coordination Points
The Jurchen offensive was like the two jaws of a pincer — the western arm through Shanxi's mountain passes, the eastern arm across the Hebei plain, with the Taihang mountain passes serving as coordination links. The western route descended from Datong through Yanmen Pass into the Taiyuan basin; the eastern route swept from Yanjing across the North China Plain toward Bianjing. Ma Kuo identified the key weakness: hold Zhending to prevent the two arms from linking up. But Tong Guan, the supreme commander, had only one thought — flee back to Bianjing. His dereliction of duty doomed the defensive plan from the start.

02
Defense Collapse
The Yisheng Army's Chain Mutiny: Five Prefectures Fell from Within
The Song's northern defense line was not breached by Jurchen assault — it was dismantled from within by the very Yisheng Army they had created. This Han-Chinese auxiliary force, raised from Liao territories, was well-equipped but undisciplined and rife with grievances against regular Song troops. When the Jurchen approached Shuozhou, Yisheng troops inside opened the gates. Wuzhou fell the same way. In Daizhou, they seized the commander Li Siben and surrendered. At Shiling Pass, Yisheng commander Geng Shouzhong drove off Song general Ji Jing before defecting. Xinzhou's commander He Quan threw open the gates with celebratory music. By December 17, all territory north of Taiyuan was lost — the Jurchen advanced to Taiyuan's walls in under twenty days.

03
Eastern Collapse
Guo Yaoshi's Surrender: Betrayed by Colleagues, Not by Choice
Yanjing, recovered from the Jurchen at enormous cost just two years earlier, was permanently lost — and Guo Yaoshi surrendered not out of disloyalty, but because his own comrades planned to deliver his head to the enemy. On December 6, Guo's Ever-Victorious Army fought the Jurchen for over thirty li at the Bai River, driving back the enemy. But the northern wing under Zhang Linghui and Liu Shunren fled first. That night, the guard commander of Yanjing's northeast gate decided to surrender, asking the Jurchen: "Do you want Guo Yaoshi alive or dead?" The masterminds were the very commanders who had fled the battlefield. Guo, discovering the plot, chose to deliver his entire army rather than have his head delivered alone. Yanjing fell. Guo promptly became the Jurchen guide, revealing every weakness of the Song.

04
Power Transfer
Huizong's Abdication: Self-Criticism Edict, Forced Coronation, and Li Gang's Debut
In terror of the approaching Jurchen, Emperor Huizong executed the most humiliating power transfer in three steps. December 20: appointed his 26-year-old crown prince as Governor of Kaifeng — a prelude to his own escape. December 22: issued a devastating self-criticism edict written by Yuwen Xuzhong, confessing to blocking remonstrance, exhausting state wealth, ignoring disasters, and being blind to popular grievances. December 23: Huizong, feigning illness, ordered servants to dress the crown prince in imperial robes. The prince wept, resisted until he nearly suffocated, was revived by physicians, then dragged to Funing Hall to complete the ceremony. That same day, Li Gang proposed via Wu Min: follow the precedent of Tang Emperor Minghuang — a full emperor, not a mere crown prince, could rally the nation. Chen Dong denounced the "Six Traitors": Cai Jing, Wang Fu, Tong Guan, Liang Shicheng, Li Yan, and Zhu Mian.

05
Fatal Blow
Yellow River Abandoned: 7,000 Guards Burned the Bridge and Fled; Huizong Escaped by Night
On New Year's Day 1126, the Jurchen reached the Yellow River's north bank. Song commander Liang Fangping fled without fighting, burning the pontoon bridge — thousands of Song soldiers drowned, unable to cross. Across the river, He Guan with 30,000 troops fled the moment he saw Liang retreat. From January 3 to 6, the Jurchen crossed leisurely in a dozen small boats, completely unopposed — the most unforgivable failure of the entire war. That night, Huizong fled Bianjing by the water gate: a boat was too slow, a sedan chair too slow, so he commandeered a brick transport vessel. Hungry, he begged flatbread from the boatmen. One hundred li in a single night to Shangqiu. Chen Dong renewed his call to execute the Six Traitors. Li Gang stepped forward demanding a fight — but the Yellow River breach had already placed Bianjing under the Jurchen blade.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Song collapse erupted in a single month between December 1125 and January 1126. The Jurchen pincer was not unstoppable — Ma Kuo's Zhending strategy and Zhang Xiaochun's Taiyuan defense proved that. But five cascading failures overlapped: Tong Guan's desertion, the Yisheng Army's chain mutiny, Guo Yaoshi's betrayal by his own side, the Yellow River garrison's flight, and Huizong's abdication-as-escape. In thirty days, the empire fell from apparent peace to the brink of annihilation. The root cause lay not on the battlefield but in the systemic rot of the decision-making class.
