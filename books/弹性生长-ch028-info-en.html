📖 Chapter Overview

"Cycle guru" Zhou Jintao famously said: life's fortune is made by riding Kondratiev waves. Kondratiev's theory found that capitalism experiences 40-60 year cycles driven by technological revolutions. The author personally experienced two complete sub-cycles—the PC internet era and mobile internet era—those who caught them earned 1-2x or even 10x+ more than peers. This chapter traverses four Kondratiev waves: water-powered textiles (1770s) → steam engine and railways (1825-1920) → automobiles, oil, and electricity (1920-1975) → computing and internet (1975-present), revealing a brutal truth: everyone is in a cycle—in prosperity, opportunities are at your fingertips; in decline, the path keeps narrowing.

🔍 In-Depth Analysis
What Are Kondratiev Waves?
Nikolai Kondratiev examined 200+ years of capitalist history and discovered clear cyclical patterns: each 40-60 year wave is technology-driven, with distinct boom and bust phases. Within big waves are small waves: computing drove a 50+ year cycle containing sub-waves like operating systems, browsers, internet, and mobile internet. The author lived through the PC and mobile internet waves—those who caught them earned 1-2x more than peers, extremes 10x+.
Waves 1-2: Textiles and Steam
Around 1770, Britain invented the spinning jenny—clothing is a hard necessity. Every later rising power started with textiles: America, Germany, Japan, Korea, China. 1825 brought the steam engine age: diffusing from coal mines to factories, then to ships and trains. Financial innovations emerged—joint-stock companies and bonds pooled idle money for mega-projects. Britain became the empire where the sun never set, but its railways ultimately empowered Germany and America—Britain built for others.
Wave 3: Railways Reshaped the World
1875-1920 was the railway era. Germany used British tech to build railways → connected hundreds of states → trade flourished → metallurgy and chemistry revolution → financial capital grew → unification consciousness → Bismarck's three wars unified Germany. After the Civil War, America went railway-mad—10,000 miles per year. Sun Yat-sen, awed, dreamed of 100,000 miles of Chinese railways—but Europe and America built railways on borrowed money and repaid later; "save first, build later" can never work.
Wave 4 and Beyond: From Automobiles to the Internet
1920-1975 was the auto-and-oil era. America and Germany raced neck-and-neck—both heavy industry, electrification, automobiles, all borrowed madly for expansion. After the 1929 crash, Roosevelt and Hitler both used public works to employ the masses. Post-WWII, European financial capital and talent flooded to America, fueling postwar prosperity. The US created national tech policy pushing military tech to civilian use—computing and internet came from this. The Soviet Union, fearing tech leakage, was cautious about military-to-civilian conversion, causing massive investments to go unrecovered.
Where Is Your Kondratiev Wave?
Everyone exists in multiple cycles of different scales—in boom phases, opportunities come easily; in decline, the path keeps narrowing. Innovation's three conditions: middle class with purchasing power (ranked first), capital, and talent. China has gradually formed all three at scale, with self-reinforcing capacity that's hard to kill. With such a massive economy, even 2-3% quality growth is impressive. The key: recognize where you are in the cycle, aggressively seize opportunities in booms, and defend your base while waiting for the next wave in downturns.
💡 Key Takeaway

Fortune is made by riding Kondratiev waves—this isn't motivational fluff, it's two centuries of economic history. From textiles to steam, railways, automobiles, and computing, every technological revolution created countless wealth opportunities. Those who caught them soared; those who missed them struggled. The key isn't predicting the next wave—it's continuous learning and maintaining an open mindset, so when the wave arrives, you're already near the launch point.
