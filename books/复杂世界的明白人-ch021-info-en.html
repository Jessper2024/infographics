Clear Thinker in a Complex World · Ch.21 "Why I Believe in China, Written on New Year's Day 2021"

From Three Traps to Dual Circulation · Iterative Evolution Will Break Through 2020 was a grim economic year. The blogger surveyed 14,000 people—nearly half responded "very bad" or "so-so." Yet on New Year's Day 2021, the author chose to believe. This letter offers not blind optimism but a logically framed, rational case: China has passed capital accumulation and entered internal integration. Cross the three traps and deepen the domestic market, and the future shines bright.

This chapter presents the author's core worldview. It uses a two-stage model of great-power rise (capital accumulation → internal integration) as its framework, traces the full path of developed nations from colonial trade to domestic demand-driven growth, examines China's current position through the "three traps" lens (low-income trap, middle-income trap, high-income trap), and arrives at one central thesis—demand is the first engine of development. China's 1.4-billion-strong domestic market is its true weapon. Deepen domestic demand, curb wealth polarization, and persist in iterative evolution—that is how to cross the high-income trap.

Development Law
The Two Stages of Great-Power Rise: After Capital Accumulation Comes Internal Integration
Every great power's rise divides into capital accumulation and internal integration. Britain took centuries to accumulate capital; America took over a hundred years. The West (including Japan) got its first fortune almost entirely from overseas plunder—Britain's colonial trade, America's territorial expansion. But money alone cannot sustain a nation. Anglo-American societies suffered severe wealth polarization and turmoil. Britain completed its "poverty alleviation" by devolving power to the grassroots and improving workers' conditions. America, under Roosevelt, broke up monopolies and curbed polarization on a massive scale. These two steps built enormous domestic markets, making America "the first nation to achieve internal-external dual circulation." Latecomers develop faster thanks to technology—an excavator moves dirt far faster than a shovel.

02
Development Traps
The Three Traps: Each Step from Low-Income to High-Income Can Be Fatal
Three traps lie on the road from developing nation to developed nation. First, the low-income trap: no money, no resources, locked out of growth without external help (loans or sweatshop factories). Second, the middle-income trap: rising labor costs drive foreign capital away. Without a large domestic market, you stall halfway—Mexico and Brazil are textbook cases. Third, the high-income trap: businesses and nations chase quick money and ship out slow-earning R&D. Only finance thrives while everyone else slides downhill—Britain is already deep in this trap. China faces the third trap now, but the author has confidence. China stays cautious about finance, encourages R&D, and government can steer banking and industrial direction.

03
Core Engine
Demand Is the First Engine of Development: Whoever Has Purchasing Power Owns the Future
The first key factor for any society's development is "demand"—that is, purchasing power. Export-led growth makes ordinary people wealthier. Expanding domestic demand makes them wealthier too. The point is the same: put money in people's hands. Money fuels consumption. Chips, batteries, and EVs find buyers. Companies recoup funds and iterate their technology. Countless shelved inventions prove useless because nobody can buy them. China's greatest advantage is its 1.4-billion-strong domestic market—"the real weapon." The rule of super-enterprises: products for the rich alone stay small; products everyone can afford grow big and strong.

04
Institutional Choice
Balancing Fairness and Efficiency: Great Nations Must Eventually Curb Wealth Polarization
The biggest benefit of curbing wealth polarization is creating an enormous domestic market. Economists often criticize this as harming efficiency, but reality shows otherwise. Every great nation at a certain stage must do this or stall. American wealth polarization peaked in 1929, eased under the New Deal, and hit a historic high again by the 2008 financial crisis. Dalio points out that America's current division matches its 1930 peak. Curb polarization → people have money → buy washing machines and travel → inventions spread and costs drop → prices fall → businesses grow and earn more → invest in new R&D. This chain proves fairness and efficiency do not conflict—fairness is the foundation of efficiency.

05
Mindset
Think Through "Iteration" and "Evolution"—Hold the Direction and Iterate Repeatedly
Fighting corruption, alleviating poverty, advancing chips and technology—even daily work—all fit the iterative evolution framework. Build software: version one is simple; add features gradually; after several iterations it looks refined and impressive. That is what "keeping the beginner's mind" means. After setting out, do not forget the direction. Iterate toward one goal several times and everything improves. Meanwhile, under a landscape where super-corporations control key industries (chips, internet, telecom, oil), traditional "perfect competition" no longer exists. The state must intervene moderately to stop monopoly giants from coasting on subsidies without R&D. Deepen the domestic market, raise tech standards, curb polarization, and improve market order—sustain these approaches and the future is bright.
🔑 Key Takeaway
Believing in China is not blind optimism—it is a structural judgment grounded in historical patterns. Great-power rise inevitably follows two stages: capital accumulation then internal integration. Three traps (low-income, middle-income, high-income) are hurdles that must be crossed. China's confidence rests on: ① its 1.4-billion domestic market is the core weapon; ② prudent financial oversight avoids the hallmark of the high-income trap—financialization; ③ curbing polarization is not a moral slogan but an economic tool to expand domestic demand. Sustain iterative evolution over the long term and push repeatedly in the right direction—everything will get better.
