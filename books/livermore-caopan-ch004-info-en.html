Livermore's Trading Method · Ch.4 "Money in Hand"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → AnswerAfter making profits, speculators' money sits in brokerage accounts — Livermore himself once wired $1,000,000 in profits to his bank while vacationing at Palm Beach, a sight the telegraph operator had never seen in 20 years. Most speculators never actually touch their profits — they either leave the money as margin for further risk, or attempt 500% returns in 2-3 months, only to lose everything through overtrading. How can a speculator turn paper profits into real, tangible wealth and keep it? Answer: withdraw half of every winning trade's profit; never average down on losing positions; treat speculation as a serious business.

This chapter contains Livermore's most essential insight on wealth. He reveals a truth overlooked by the vast majority of speculators: money in a brokerage account is not the same as money in your hand — money your fingers can actually feel. Feeling it gives it meaning. He recalls wiring his broker to deposit $1,000,000 into his bank account. The telegraph operator, in 20 years on the job, had never handled a client's request for a broker to deposit money — usually the wires went the other way: demanding more margin from clients. Livermore's only regret was not following this "withdraw half" principle throughout his entire career.

01
Rule One
Never Average Down — the Most Common Fatal Mistake
Livermore strongly advises: never buy more at lower prices to average down a losing position. Buy 100 at $50, then 100 more at $47 to "average down to $48½" — on what grounds do you double your worry? If it drops to $44, you're down $600 on the first lot and $300 on the second. If you insist on this path, you must follow through: 200 at $44, 400 at $41, 800 at $38 — but how many can withstand that pressure?
❌

Wrong: Average Down

50→100 shares, 47→100 more, 44→200 more... losses multiply, pressure mounts, eventually total loss of control

✅

Right: Cut Losses

If the first trade is in the red, admit the mistake and close immediately. Preserve capital for the next real opportunity.

02
Rule Two
Margin Call = Close Immediately
The only infallible "tip" Livermore ever received from brokers was the margin call. When such a notice arrives, close your position at once. You are on the wrong side of the market. Why throw good money after bad? Hold onto those good dollars a little longer — take them to a more attractive venture instead of pouring them into a trade that is clearly losing.

03
Rule Three
Withdraw Half Your Profits — Let Money Truly Come to Hand
Livermore's core strategy: every time you close out a successful trade, take half the profit and stash it in a safe place. The only money a speculator ever truly makes from Wall Street is the cash withdrawn from the account after a winning trade. "Money in a brokerage account is not the same as money in your hand — money your fingers can actually feel. Feeling it gives it meaning. There is a sense of possession in it that slightly dampens the impulse to make reckless trading decisions."

04
Core Philosophy
Speculation Is a Serious Business, Not Gambling
The common failing of most speculators is impatience — they want to get rich in a very short time. They are not prepared to spend two or three years growing capital by 500%, but instead attempt this in two or three months. Occasionally they succeed. But do these bold traders ultimately hold onto their winnings? No. The money comes too easily — easy come, easy go. Such speculators vanish like shooting stars.

05
Ultimate Lesson
Stay in Your Lane — Don't Lose Money Outside Your Competence
Every one of Livermore's investments outside Wall Street failed — Florida real estate bubbles, oil wells, aircraft manufacturing, high-tech ventures. In all these, he lost every cent. A friend's words snapped him awake: "Livermore, you can never succeed in any business outside your own. If you want money to speculate, you can have it — free of charge. But please, stick to speculation and stay far away from that business." The next morning, a check arrived by mail.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Paper profits are not real wealth — only cash withdrawn from your account, money you can actually hold, counts. The three iron rules of preserving wealth — never average down, close on margin calls, withdraw half of every profit — appear simple, yet require transcending the human instincts of greed and fear. Speculation is a serious business. Only by operating like a merchant, and staying strictly within your circle of competence, can you truly make money "in hand" your own.
