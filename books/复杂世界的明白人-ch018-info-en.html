Understanding the Complex World · Chapter 18 "The Small-Town Grinder's Dilemma: Why Talented People Struggle to Rise"

Position Shapes Thinking and Destiny · Break Through by Breaking Into New Circles — The Gaokao Only Breaks the First Layer "Small-town grinders" crack the gaokao and enter elite universities, only to find their lives slipping increasingly out of their control. The "high score, low ability" explanation is far too simplistic. Ability is fluid — position impacts people far more than ability ever can. Environment changes thinking; thinking changes circumstances. The lower you go, the more it's free competition; the higher you climb, the more it's a society of connections. The gaokao breaks through the first layer. Every layer after that must be broken by breaking into new circles. Choose the stability of your comfort zone, and you must accept the stable, mediocre results that come with it.

This chapter fundamentally deconstructs the sharp question "Why do talented people struggle to rise?" The core conclusion: Positional Determinism — the environment you're in (city, industry, circle, boss relationship) is far more decisive than your personal ability. A high-challenge environment can transform a B-tier ordinary person into an A-tier master; a shrinking, sinking environment can turn an A-tier genius into a mediocrity. The gaokao is a single-axis game — gather the equipment, beat the boss. The workplace is a multi-dimensional arena — you need industry momentum + boss recognition + luck + ability all lined up. The key to breaking through is not grinding more exam-style problems — it's breaking into new circles.

01
Core Finding
Position Changes Destiny: "Position" Impacts a Person More Than Ability, Even More Than Background
Resource endowments, transport links, agricultural capacity — these factors directly determine a society's development path. The same holds for people. Port cities rise when international trade booms; desert outposts thrive when they sit at critical crossroads. People are the same: similar backgrounds yield completely different outcomes depending on whether you're in a small city or a metropolis, a private company or civil service. "Position" doesn't just influence your prospects — it influences your worldview and your attitude toward life. "Rich mindset" vs. "poor mindset" is simply different mindsets emerging from different positions. The gaokao breaks your first layer. Everything after that depends on whether you can enter better "positions" — better environments, better circles, better tracks.

02
Dynamic Ability
Ability Is Fluid: Environment Continuously Reshapes People — B-Tier Can Become A-Tier, A-Tier Can Degrade Into Mediocrity
Drop an ordinary person into a high-challenge, high-pressure environment for several years — if they can withstand it, they'll become shockingly capable. Conversely, put a naturally brilliant elite-school graduate in a role where they do nothing but fill out forms every day, and a few years later they'll be a "form-filling expert" and nothing more. Someone with English proficiency at the highest certification level who doesn't use it for ten years degrades to high-school level; someone who couldn't string together a sentence in a foreign language moves abroad for a decade and blows past the certified expert. Environment changes thinking; thinking further changes circumstances — forming a positive or negative feedback flywheel. In a top-tier city, the ceiling stretches to the sky; every day feels like the gaokao, squeezing every drop of potential out of you. But in a shrinking, sinking department, the best-case outcome isn't very good either.
⬆️

Ascending Environment: B Becomes A or Even S

High challenge + high pressure → withstand it for years → complete transformation. The anxiety of a top-tier city is like gaokao every year.

⬇️

Descending Environment: A Degrades to B

Form-filling · office politics · declining salary → lose all edge after a few years. If you don't proactively change, the environment consumes you.

03
Upward Passage
The Higher You Climb, the More It's a World of Connections — Everyone Exists Within a Circle; Breakthrough Begins With Breaking Into New Circles
The lower you are, the more it's free competition and rules-based. The higher you go, the more it's a society of personal connections. This is how the Matthew Effect operates: the more capable you are, the more capable the people helping you become, and your overall strength multiplies exponentially. Every circle has its cross-layer members and its low-ceiling members. Individual effort only has meaning within a circle — the circle amplifies your effort. The small-town grinder broke the first layer through the gaokao. If they don't enter a "layer-breaking circle" after that, their ceiling is set. Many people know the upside in their comfort zone is limited, but the comfort zone itself is the problem — they're too afraid to break out. The result: hamster on a wheel, running hard and going nowhere.
🏛️
Bottom Layer
Free competition, rules-based. The gaokao breaks this layer relatively fairly — just gather the gear to clear the level.
🔗
Middle Layer
Increasingly connection-driven. Requires boss recognition + circle entry ticket. Going solo hits a ceiling.
👥
Top Layer
Pure connection society. Everyone at the top tier knows each other. Your circle determines how far you go.
04
Mindset Upgrade
The Workplace Is Not the Gaokao — The Game Shifts From Single-Axis to Multi-Dimensional Random; You Need All Four Dimensions to Clear Each Level
The gaokao is a single-axis game — gather a few pieces of gear, beat the final boss, collect the prize. Once you start working, the game rules turn strange — there are no clear levels, no clear bosses. You can't win by doing just one thing the way you could in high school. You have to simultaneously assemble: entry into a relatively ascending industry + a boss who recognizes you and is capable themselves + decent luck + your ability being genuinely strong. Get all four together, and you can clear a level now and then. Miss any one, and you don't even get the chance to try. This is why people you once far surpassed are now thriving — they assembled the four pieces; you didn't.
🏫 Gaokao Mode
Single-axis · One action: study · Clear levels and bosses · Rules are transparent · Effort and reward are roughly proportional
🌍 Workplace Mode
Multi-dimensional random · Must assemble four pieces: Industry + Boss + Luck + Ability · No clear levels · Missing one means zero chance
05
Breakthrough Paths
Four Paths to Upward Breakthrough: Circle Leap, Industry Boom, Luck, Special Talent — Choosing the Right Track Matters More Than Grinding Hard
Those who manage to climb upward almost always lean on one of the following four elements. ① Consciously or unconsciously joined a powerful circle, then used that circle to enter the next one — the most common path upward. ② Your industry is exploding, and part of the industry floats up with it — like joining a major tech company early on; the company expands, departments become business units, everyone becomes a core member. ③ Pure luck — this one is hard to explain, but it definitely plays a role. ④ Special talent — not math or physics brilliance, but performance talent like live-streaming, telling stories, comedy. In a mature society, these entertainment professions can explode insanely fast, surpassing most people around you in a year or two. The small-town grinder's dilemma isn't lack of ability — it's a general inclination toward safety, a craving for stability, a love for certainty. Choose stability, and you must accept mediocrity.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The core of the small-town grinder's dilemma is not "low ability" — it's a miscalibrated choice of position and environment. The gaokao only broke the first layer — that single-axis game you played for 18 years and won. But society's game rules are entirely different: the higher you climb, the more it's a world of connections. Individual effort only has meaning within the right circle. You're not lacking in ability — you accidentally entered a track unsuited to you or a descending track, ran into a terrible boss, and were controlled by fear into staying in the comfort zone. The first step to breaking through is not grinding more "exam problems" — it's breaking into new circles. Once you see this clearly, even if your present situation doesn't improve overnight, your path forward will no longer keep narrowing.
