Layout of the World · Chapter 1
「The Chessboard Pattern of Military Geography」

Nine strategic regions on China's topographical chessboard determined the rise and fall of dynasties

Rao Shengwen uses the game of Go as a metaphor for China's ancient military geography. On this irregular chessboard, Guanzhong, Hebei, the Southeast, and Sichuan occupy the four corners; Shanxi, Shandong, Hubei, and Hanzhong sit on the four sides; and the Central Plains form the heartland. Mountains block while rivers connect — these nine regions, distributed across China's second and third topographic tiers, constituted the decisive strategic stage throughout two millennia of unification and fragmentation.

1
Go Analogy
Nine Regions on the Chessboard

Ancient strategists likened warfare to Go. On this irregular board, Guanzhong, Hebei, the Southeast, and Sichuan form the four corners; Shanxi, Shandong, Hubei, and Hanzhong the four sides; and the Central Plains the heartland. Each region boasts mountain barriers and river arteries, playing decisive roles across dynastic history.

2
Four Corners
Corners: Fortress Bastions for Building Power

Guanzhong — "a hundred passes and two Qin," shielded by Qinling, Longshan, Hangu Pass, and Xiao Pass. Hebei — embraced by sea and mountains, with Yanshan and Taihang as wings. The Southeast — defended by the Yangtze-Huaihe dual barrier. Sichuan — a basin ringed by towering peaks, accessible only through the Three Gorges and Sword Gate. These four corners served as bases for accumulating strength before contesting the empire.

3
Four Sides
Sides: Strategic Corridors Between Corners

Shanxi overlooks three directions from its commanding plateau. Shandong commands the junction of the Yellow River and Grand Canal. Hubei anchors the middle Yangtze — "judging by the realm, Xiangyang is paramount." Hanzhong, wedged between Guanzhong and Sichuan, is a pivot where north-south interests clash. The four sides are both corridors and contested belts essential for expansion.

4
Central Plains
The Central Plains: Ultimate Arena of Struggle

The Central Plains connect all directions — when the realm is united, power concentrates here; when fragmented, it disperses. It is the arena where "deer are hunted" and the tripod contested. Yet in times of chaos, holding the exposed center proves harder than holding the corners. All unification dynasties arose from corners before converging on the Central Plains.

5
Historical Arc
From Corners to Center: The Classic Path

Qin, Han, Sui, and Tang unified from Guanzhong; Eastern Han from Hebei; the Ming from the Southeast. The path: seize a corner, build strength, expand to both wings, then contest the Central Plains. Sichuan, despite its natural wealth, never produced a unifying dynasty — its "basin mentality" encouraged complacency. Shanxi, perched above the plains, repeatedly toppled Central Plains regimes.

📌 Key Takeaways

China's ancient military geography resembled a Go board: four corners as fortress bases, four sides as pivotal corridors, and the Central Plains as the decisive arena. Understanding this chessboard pattern unlocks the geographic logic behind two millennia of dynastic rise and fall.
