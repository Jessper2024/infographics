Zen and the Art of Motorcycle Maintenance · Chapter 10: The Tyranny of Hypotheses

Infinite Hypotheses — When the Scientific Method Destroys Its Own Purpose

The canyon narrows as they approach the river's source — and the author approaches the source of Phaedrus's philosophical quest. Starting from Einstein's 1918 speech about the temple of science, Phaedrus asks: where do hypotheses come from? He discovers a terrifying fact — there are an infinite number of rational hypotheses that can explain any given phenomenon. If hypotheses multiply faster than experiments can process them, the scientific method cannot select truth. Reason itself has a congenital defect that leaves modern life emotionally empty, aesthetically barren, and spiritually blank. At seventeen, Phaedrus is expelled from university, beginning a long spiritual wandering.

TEMPLE Einstein's Temple of Science
"In the temple of science are many mansions... and various indeed are they that dwell therein and the motives that have led them thither." Some are drawn by superior intellect, others by purely utilitarian purposes. But if an angel were to drive out the first two types, the temple would not be empty — there would still remain a strange, silent, and solitary group, escaping the weariness of ordinary life to come to the quiet mountains, "tracing with delighted vision the serene features of eternal nature." Phaedrus belonged to this third type.

02
SOURCE Where Do Hypotheses Come From? Intuition & Epiphany
The most mysterious part of all scientific methods is the formation of hypotheses. No one knows where they come from. A person sits thinking, and suddenly — in a flash — an insight arrives. Einstein said: "There is no logical path to these fundamental laws; only intuition, resting on a sympathetic understanding of experience, can reach them." Intuition? Sympathy? Strange words for the source of science. Nature does not supply hypotheses — nature only supplies the materials for experiment.

03
THEOREM A Parkinson-Like Brutal Theorem: Infinite Hypotheses
Phaedrus wrote his own theorem, modeled after Parkinson's Law: the number of rational hypotheses that can explain any given phenomenon is infinite. If this theorem is true, it's not a minor flaw in scientific thinking — it completely destroys everything, because it denies the effectiveness of the entire scientific method. Hypotheses multiply far faster than experiments can handle them, making any experimental result unreliable. The entire scientific method lacks a foundation for establishing empirical knowledge.

04
CRISIS The Congenital Defect of Reason: Root of Modern Crisis
Phaedrus believed the cause of our current social crises is a congenital defect of reason. These rational models have existed since the Renaissance and will continue to function as long as people's primary needs are food, clothing, and shelter. But for most people today, these basic needs are no longer the main problem — the ancient rational structures no longer meet our needs, revealing their true nature: emotionally empty, aesthetically unexpressive, and spiritually blank.

05
FALL Expelled at Seventeen: Phaedrus's Spiritual Wandering
Having completed his freshman science courses at age fifteen, Phaedrus was expelled at seventeen for failing grades. "They thought he was immature and inattentive in class." He couldn't understand why no one cared about the question that troubled him — "We know the scientific method works, why ask such questions?" This attitude brought him to a complete halt. Like the serene mountain Einstein described, suddenly a chasm opened in it, with nothing inside. Thus began a long spiritual wandering.

💡 Key Insight

Reason is not all-powerful. When hypotheses multiply at infinite speed, the scientific method loses its ability to select truth. What Phaedrus discovered was not a minor flaw but the meta-problem of modern civilization — reason itself has a congenital defect that leaves us emotionally empty and spiritually barren after material abundance has been achieved. Expulsion at seventeen was not the end, but the beginning of a deeper quest.
