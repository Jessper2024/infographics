Great Country, Big City · Ch.5「Great Cities Never Die」

Scale Economies · Population Density · Wages · Inclusive Employment · Skill Complementarity Nearly all arguments against urban growth severely underestimate its benefits — benefits that are often invisible. The vitality of great cities comes down to human nature: most people, most of the time, are afraid of being alone.

This is Book Chapter 5. Core thesis: great cities will never die. Scale economies, human capital externalities, and skill complementarity make large cities irreplaceable in a modern knowledge economy. The chapter opens with the story of Dr. Chen and his wife choosing to stay in Beijing, illustrating two key logics: human capital accumulation and specialized job matching. It demonstrates empirically that urban expansion creates inclusive employment — especially for low-skilled workers (each 1M increase in city population raises individual employment probability by 0.66pp). It contrasts "living in the city" vs. "existing in the city" — narrow-street, high-density vs. wide-boulevard, low-density — using a Shanghai neighborhood comparison. Finally, it criticizes "controlling people through industry" policies and reveals the complementarity between high-skilled and low-skilled workers: one high-tech job creates five additional service-sector jobs.

01
Vitality
The Vitality of Great Cities: Human Nature, Interaction, and Diversity
Two types of people: those who long for a solitary life like Tao Yuanming's "picking chrysanthemums under the eastern fence," and those who thrive on bustle and diversity. Most people are the latter. In modern economies, knowledge-intensive industries dominate — more and more people need interaction to spread and acquire knowledge. Even the most introverted office worker must communicate and attend meetings. Diversity is another human need — only big cities offer diverse consumer services. Producers can serve niche markets because even niche preferences have enough consumers in a large city. High housing prices and "urban ills" are the price of entry to a diverse urban life. Today, the "consumer city" is increasingly important — quality of urban life is becoming the main driver of migration.
02
Technology
Digital Won't Replace Face-to-Face: Complementarity, Not Substitution
Some worry that IT will reduce the need to cluster in cities. The opposite is true: online and offline interaction are complementary, not substitutes. WeChat friends are people we've met in person; online conversations naturally lead to face-to-face meetings. IT may reduce meetings with a particular friend but generates the need to meet more people. The richer people get, the more they purchase "experiences" — concerts, theater, gallery shows, cafés — which cannot be delivered online. If online could replace offline, no one would pay to attend business school classes in person instead of watching videos online. Japan's total population is declining, but Tokyo's keeps rising. Information technology has not — and in the foreseeable future will not — replace the need for face-to-face interaction.
03
Human Capital
Human Capital Externalities: Greater Benefits for the Less Educated
"Human capital externality" — when one person's education rises, not only does their income increase, but through interaction and knowledge sharing, others' incomes rise too. Research by the author and Harvard's Edward Glaeser using Chinese data confirms: each additional year of average city education raises average wages by 21%. Crucially, low-skilled workers benefit more from human capital externalities. Why? High-skilled workers compete with each other, offsetting some externality gains, while low-skilled workers are complementary to high-skilled ones. Financial districts need cleaners; engineers need technicians; high-income earners need restaurant servers and domestic help. This is skill complementarity through division of labor. City governments should not think they don't need low-skilled workers — consumer service workers are largely less educated, and their jobs cannot be automated.
04
Employment
Inclusive Employment Creation: Empirical Evidence
Using CHIPS 2002 urban data, the author, Gao Hong, and Sato Hiroshi found: each 1M increase in city population raises individual employment probability by 0.66 percentage points. Each 1M increase in college graduates raises employment probability by 4.3–6.6pp. Most importantly, the employment effect is larger for less-skilled workers: for those with ≤9 years of education, +1M graduates → employment probability +8.58pp; for 9–12 years → +3.57pp; for >12 years → only +2.12pp. Why do low-skilled workers gain more? Under the hukou system, local residency policies favor the highly educated, causing oversupply of high-skilled labor in cities — intensified competition and underemployment. But the concentration of high-skilled workers creates complementary demand for low-skilled workers — high demand with institutionally constrained supply means low-skilled workers find jobs more easily.

05
Consumer City
The Consumer City: Key to Service Sector Development
China is entering a post-industrial phase where cities — especially large ones — become even more important. Richer countries have higher service-sector GDP shares, higher urbanization rates, and continuing population concentration in big cities. Why? Population density benefits service development. Unlike agriculture and manufacturing, services are non-transportable and require face-to-face delivery. Consumer services follow people and money: dense areas better share fixed costs (convenience stores, restaurants); higher-productivity cities have more wealthy residents demanding more services. The more a modern city develops high-tech industries and producer services, the more consumer services it generates. China's tertiary sector rose from 40.5% of GDP in 2001 to 48.2% in 2014, but remains ~10pp below comparable-stage countries. Low-density urban development is a major factor constraining service sector growth.
06
Urban Form
"Existing" vs. "Living" in the City: Narrow Streets vs. Wide Boulevards
Comparing Shanghai's Puxi Tianzifang (narrow streets, high density) and Pudong Lianyang (wide streets, low density) reveals the fundamental difference between two urban development models. Tianzifang: high intersection density, abundant street-level commerce, walkable, walking/subway commutes, fresher and cheaper fruit. Lianyang: wide roads, low density, inadequate service amenities, car-dependent, less fresh and more expensive fruit. A new urban studies term: "walkability." Narrow streets encourage walking, higher pedestrian density means more "foot traffic" for shops, supporting fixed investment. European cities (Paris, Barcelona, Tokyo's Ginza) have 100–200 intersections per km²; Shanghai Pudong and Beijing's north have fewer than 20. Wider roads don't mean less congestion — high density with small streets reduces commuting needs and boosts service diversity and living convenience.

07
Industry Control
"Controlling People Through Industry" Is Counterproductive
Some cities intuitively think they should "control people through industry" — eliminate low-end industries, restrict low-skilled migrants. This violates economic logic. Migrant workers come to cities because cities need them — their unemployment rates are far below those of local residents. Does industrial upgrading eliminate the need for low-skilled labor? That's a huge misconception. A city's vitality lies precisely in its low-end service sector. High-skilled and low-skilled workers are complementary, for three reasons: division of labor (every workplace needs both), human capital externalities (educated workers spill knowledge to the less educated), and consumption externalities (high-skilled workers generate demand for services). Larger cities do not have fewer low-skilled workers — precisely because high-skilled workers drive demand for them, larger cities have higher shares of manual service workers.
08
Complementarity
The High-Tech Multiplier: One Job Creates Five
US research shows: one high-tech job in a city creates 5 additional jobs in other sectors — 2 relatively high-end (doctors, lawyers) + 3 in consumer services (retail, restaurants). Manufacturing has a smaller multiplier but still creates 1.6 local service jobs per manufacturing job. The ratio of high-skilled to low-skilled is roughly 1:1. In China, the same pattern holds — larger cities have higher proportions of manual service workers. However, because every city gives hukou priority to college graduates, high-skilled workers are in "oversupply" — college graduates doing bank teller work (underemployment). Restrictions on low-skilled workers drive up service prices (Shanghai live-in nanny wages now exceed Hong Kong's Filipino maids), paradoxically reducing the productivity and quality of life of high-skilled workers. A city aspiring to be a global hub attracts talent through its service environment, not hardware like talent apartments.

09
Consequences
The Consequences of Selecting Workers by Skill
When city governments use hukou to "select" high-skilled workers and exclude low-skilled ones, severe consequences follow. Reducing low-skilled labor supply in manual services drives up service prices. High-skilled talent earns high incomes and consumes many services — if a city's service prices are too high, it loses global talent competitiveness. Within a single country, cities should not imitate national immigration policies. Restricting low-skilled entry while attracting high-skilled talent violates citizens' freedom of movement and creates paradoxical labor shortages and rising wages for low-skilled services. Shanghai's nanny wages exceeding Hong Kong's is a stark signal of institutional distortion. Cities should mitigate "urban ills" through technology and management (increasing infrastructure and public service supply), not by increasing discrimination against migrants to raise their cost of living.
10
Policy
Respecting Markets: The Right Way to Manage City Size
The 18th CPC Central Committee Third Plenum report first said "let the market play the decisive role in resource allocation and better play the government's role," then said "strictly control mega-city population." This ordering has meaning — any population control measures must respect market principles, not reverse them. Population controls can only be short-term emergency measures (since infrastructure cannot be built overnight), and can at most slow population growth — not reverse it in mega-cities. Increasing public service discrimination against migrants to raise their living costs is absolutely wrong. In the long run, the correct policy is: adapt to population growth trends, continuously increase infrastructure and public service supply, alleviate congestion and pollution without hindering urban development. In the context of sluggish growth, these very investments in mitigating urban ills are the ones with real returns.

🔑 Core Conclusion
Great cities never die. Scale economies (sharing, matching, learning) and human capital externalities make large cities irreplaceable in the knowledge economy. Empirical evidence shows urban expansion creates inclusive employment — especially for low-skilled workers — because high-skilled and low-skilled workers are naturally complementary in production and consumption. One high-tech job creates five service-sector jobs. "Controlling people through industry" and "selecting by skill" violate economic laws, driving up service prices and reducing a city's talent attractiveness. The correct urban form is high-density, narrow-street, walkable — the "living city" — not low-density, wide-road "existing city." Population control can only be a short-term measure. The long-term correct policy is reducing "urban ills" through technology and management while expanding infrastructure and public services. The vitality of great cities comes down to human nature.
