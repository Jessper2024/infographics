Review+ · Ch.9「Beyond Review」

From single-method review to a multi-dimensional organizational learning system — building the closed loop toward a learning organization

Review (复盘) is a vital method for learning from past experience, but in an era of rapid change, it is far from sufficient. The author introduces the Organizational Learning Matrix framework, classifying four learning mechanisms by source (past/present) × channel (internal/external). Four complementary approaches are systematically introduced: Theory U, Scenario Planning, Benchmarking, and Open Innovation. Using the U.S. Army CALL system and BP's knowledge management practice as canonical case studies, the author proposes four closed-loop elements — Collective Wisdom → Coordinated Execution → Review → Knowledge Management — to build a complete organizational learning system.

01
Premise
Review Is Essential, But Review Alone Is Not Enough
Review (复盘) is fundamentally learning from one's own past experience. While Peter Senge called it the only pathway for human learning, in an era of rapid disruption, it is far from sufficient. When disruptive innovation becomes the norm, past winners carry the heaviest baggage and face the lowest odds of adapting. The author takes a dialectical view: when done properly, review can uncover patterns, expose inherent limitations, and even catalyze innovation — as the ancient philosopher Xunzi said, "From near, know the far; from one, know all; from the subtle, discern the bright." Yet we cannot learn solely through introspection and self-practice. There is no such thing as a "universal remedy." The manager's duty is to understand each method's strengths and limitations, then select and execute the right one at the right time.
💡

Value of Review

Uncovers intrinsic patterns and limitations. Can spark and even lead innovation when executed deeply. Wisdom through empathy.

⚠️

Limits of Review

Narrow source and coverage. Insufficient depth and adaptability for disruptive innovation. Cannot work in isolation.

Core Framework
The Organizational Learning Matrix: Four Mechanisms, Full Coverage
Organizational learning can be classified across two dimensions: source (past/present) and channel (internal/external). Review occupies the Internal × Past quadrant — foundational since learning from practice is the primary mode for adults. But review is just one piece of the puzzle.

03
Future-Oriented Learning
Theory U & Scenario Planning: Learning Toward the Future
Theory U, developed by MIT's Otto Scharmer, emphasizes "learning from the emerging future" — complementing review's backward-looking orientation. The core is a shift in the structure of attention: stop downloading patterns, suspend judgment, sense in the field, let go of assumptions, connect with one's highest potential and Source, experience presencing, then let the desired future crystallize and manifest. The seven-step U-shaped journey: Suspending → Redirecting → Letting-go → Presencing → Letting-come → Crystallizing → Prototyping → Institutionalizing.
Scenario Planning is a method for foresight in highly uncertain environments. Shell used it to leap from the bottom to the top of the seven oil majors — by pre-simulating multiple futures, they adjusted rapidly during the 1973 oil crisis. Scenario planning is heavier than review, requiring substantial resources and external expertise, but it is essential for long-term strategy and high-uncertainty innovation.

04
External Learning
Benchmarking & Open Innovation: Harnessing External Wisdom
Benchmarking — the power of learning from exemplars is boundless. Xerox dispatched a study team to Japan, benchmarked world-class manufacturing practices, and recaptured market share lost to Japanese competitors. Benchmarking systematically compares one's products, services, and management with the best practices of excellent firms, identifies gaps, and drives continuous improvement. By 2005, 80% of US companies had adopted benchmarking (Bain survey).
Open Innovation — the smartest people don't all work for you. P&G CEO Lafley set a goal of sourcing 50% of innovations externally, transforming R&D into C&D (Connect & Develop). External creative input rose from 20% to ~55%. R&D capability increased nearly 60%, innovation success rate more than doubled, and innovation costs fell 20%.

05
System Building
Build a Closed-Loop Learning System with Review as the Cornerstone
Review is an indispensable link in the organizational learning system. Combine review with collective wisdom, coordinated execution, and knowledge management to form a closed loop. The U.S. Army CALL system operates three reinforcing loops: AAR for instant reflection (R1) → the Center for Army Lessons Learned for collection and dissemination (R2-1) → proactive research and knowledge creation (R2-2). BP integrates expert yellow pages, knowledge bases, peer assists, communities of practice, AARs, and project reviews to achieve "Learn Before → Learn During → Learn After." In 1998 alone, BP saved $7 billion through knowledge management.

Key Takeaway
There is no single "universal remedy." Review is vital but not sufficient. An organization's sustained competitiveness comes from the organic integration of diverse learning methods — from self-review (Internal × Past) to Theory U (Internal × Future), from benchmarking (External × Past) to scenario planning (External × Future), built upon the closed-loop system of "Collective Wisdom → Coordinated Execution → Review → Knowledge Management" — turning the organization into a genuine learning organization.
