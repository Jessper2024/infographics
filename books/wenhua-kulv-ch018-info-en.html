Wenhua Kulv · Ch. 18 "Arctic Cold Night"

On a Nordic night train, Yu Qiuyu condensed his European journey into seven comparative images — his final conclusion: the most fundamental conflict of the 21st century lies between civilization and non-civilization

At the end of his European expedition, on a Nordic night train, Yu Qiuyu distilled seven images for comparison with Chinese civilization: a line of letters (Medici), a cemetery (German scholars), a chart (French inventions), a castle (Windsor and Eton), a crowd of idlers (European leisure culture), a great rock (Iceland's Law Rock), and a blue flag (the EU). From the healthy convergence of power and culture, the purity of intellectuals, the spirit of innovation, gradual reform, the recovery of humanity through leisure, the legal transformation of the warrior ethos, to civilizational union beyond the nation-state — these seven comparisons reveal deep deficiencies in Chinese civilization. His final warning: much evil hides behind the banners of "nation" and "state"; the most fundamental conflict of the 21st century lies between civilization and non-civilization.

01
Seven Images
Seven Civilizational Comparisons Distilled on a Nordic Night Train
This European expedition was meant to find comparative coordinates for Chinese civilization. But Europe proved too captivating — "every step into it is intoxicating." Only on the Nordic night train — "outside the window stretched endless snowfields; the road so long, the night so long — a vast strangeness in motion, nearly causing the self to disappear" — did Yu Qiuyu finally gather his thoughts. Pulling two sheets of paper from his luggage, he wrote over thirty comparative images, then cut and cut until only seven remained: a line of letters (M-E-D-I-C-I in Florence), a cemetery (Fichte and Hegel's graves in Berlin), a chart (Lyon's 19th-century inventions), a castle (Windsor Castle and Eton College), a crowd of idlers (Europe's leisurely street-side sitters), a great rock (Iceland's Law Rock at Þingvellir), and a blue flag (the EU flag in Brussels).

02
Power & Thought
The Medici and German Graves: The Healthy Convergence of Cultural Resources
In Florence, the letters M-E-D-I-C-I appear everywhere — on streets, doorways, walls. By conventional Chinese thinking, a wealthy aristocratic family should be an obstacle to historical progress. Yet the Medici family, at a critical historical juncture, built a center of new culture, "using wealth and power as the backdrop upon which to gather the great masters of humanism and art." In China's historical transitions, it has always been difficult to see the healthy convergence of power, wealth, and cultural resources — reformers have always preferred expropriation. In Berlin, the graves of Fichte and Hegel represent the purity of German intellectuals. "The finest Chinese literati across the ages have all been closely tied to the framework of power — even their escapes and rebellions were forms of intimate reverse connection." Kant proposed the intellectual's standard of conduct: "Have the courage to use your own reason in all public spheres" — precisely the fatal weakness of Chinese intellectuals.

03
Innovation & Reform
Lyon's Chart and Windsor Castle: Don't Rest on the Four Great Inventions
A chart in a Lyon museum lists over a dozen inventions and creations from 19th-century Lyon alone — from textile machinery to film technology — each directly advancing the modernization of all humanity. Yet at the century's end, the French still soberly reflected that they lagged far behind America and Germany. "This chart reminds us: Chinese people must no longer rest complacently on the distant 'Four Great Inventions.'" Almost none of the key components of modern life were invented by Chinese people. Meanwhile, Britain's Windsor Castle and Eton College demonstrate a different path of social change — gentle and gradual. "Britain may be criticized as not profound enough because of its gentle gradualism. But think carefully: they've done everything that social development requires, crossed every threshold that civilizational progress demands." By contrast, modern China has been too deeply influenced by French radicalism, even worshiping violence in peacetime.

04
Leisure & Law
Street-Side Idlers and Iceland's Law Rock: Restoring Humanity, Completing the Transformation
Across Europe, people sit leisurely at street-side tables with beer or coffee. "Chinese people work hard and endure; occasionally they take holidays, but only to work better. Europeans reverse this: they believe the main point of working hard on ordinary days is largely for holidays — because only in holidays can distractions be interrupted, anxiety frozen, the body returned to itself, family bonds restored. That is, to temporarily leave the state of alienation and recover one's humanity." Sweden's welfare logic — social security through common welfare, common welfare through economic development, economic development through market competition, competition through normal order, order through social responsibility, responsibility through civic duty — "property must manifest as obligation; freedom must manifest as responsibility." At Iceland's Law Rock amid the volcanic rocks of Þingvellir, Nordic warriors spent centuries painfully replacing their coordinates of honor and rewriting their heroic sentiments — laying down swords and blood oaths to listen to legal judgments and church bells. This stands in stark contrast to Chinese civilization's hidden vein of martial chivalry — the "heroic personality" can easily transform into "mob personality."

European Idlers

Leisure is a form of clarity; busyness may be a form of confusion. Wealth is infinite but life is finite — when the twilight of life descends, where will you be?

⚖️

Iceland's Law Rock

Nordic warriors were "recruited" by law and religion — this is the spiritual pass humanity must struggle through to truly set foot on the path of civilization.

05
Civilizational Union
A Blue Flag: Civilizational Direction Beyond the Nation-State
The EU's blue flag flies before the headquarters in Brussels — just forty kilometers from the battlefield of Waterloo. "Immortal feats, victorious and defeated heroes, are always tied to Waterloo and countless other battlefields. The eternal gaze is always fixed upon the flag of the final victor rising through the smoke of cannon fire. Yet Europe has finally told us: the flag that rises last has nothing to do with victory or defeat, with nation or ethnicity — it is the flag of union, the same color as the blue sky." Kant believed in human reason, convinced that humanity would overcome antagonism and move toward harmony — this vision became the theoretical foundation of the European unification movement. "National sovereignty has partial rationality, but Europe's bloody history has long proven that excessive assertion of it is humanity's calamity." Yu Qiuyu disagrees with Huntington's clash-of-civilizations thesis: "The most fundamental conflict of the 21st century lies between civilization and non-civilization." Much evil hides behind the banners of "nation" and "state" — terrorism, nuclear competition, environmental pollution, natural disasters no longer respect national boundaries.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Europe's seven comparative images reveal deep deficiencies in Chinese civilization: the healthy convergence of power and culture, the public rationality of intellectuals, sustained innovative spirit, gentle and gradual social reform, the recovery of humanity through leisure, the legal transformation of the warrior ethos, and civilizational union beyond the nation-state. The most fundamental conflict of the 21st century lies between civilization and non-civilization — safeguarding the collective civilization of all humanity is the urgent contemporary path.
