The Elements of Style · Ch6「Spelling」

Spelling depends on usage, not authority · Over-simplification distracts the reader Strunk reveals that English spelling is fundamentally conventional, not dictated by any authority. This chapter provides a list of 56 commonly misspelled words and three practical spelling rules. The core insight: over-simplified spellings like tho for though fail not because they are wrong, but because they force the reader to mentally supply missing letters, diverting attention and reducing reading efficiency.

This is the shortest chapter in the book, yet it captures the fundamental principle of English spelling. Strunk addresses three levels: ① the nature of spelling — English word spelling rests on convention, not authority; ② why over-simplification fails — tho is inferior to though not because it is "wrong," but because tho forces mental letter-completion while though passes without spelling-consciousness; ③ practical toolkit — a 56-word list of common errors plus three rules (consonant doubling, hyphenation, compound-word splitting) form the writer's most essential spelling toolkit.

01
Spelling Nature
Spelling rests on convention, not on any authority's decree
The spelling of English words is not fixed and invariable, nor does it depend on authority, but on general agreement. Today, most words have settled spellings. At any given period, a small group of words has more than one form — one gradually gaining favor, the others falling into disuse and eventual abandonment. New spellings (mostly simplifications) are coined from time to time, some winning acceptance, others fading. This process is entirely spontaneous, democratic, driven by the collective choices of language users.

02
Simplification Trap
Over-simplified spellings fail — not because they are wrong, but because they distract
A spelling not readily accepted or too greatly simplified offends the reader — this is the real reason for rejection. Strunk provides a brilliant example: reading though, the reader's thought takes in the word instantly without awareness of its spelling; reading tho, the reader must mentally supply the missing letters, causing distraction and reducing reading efficiency. Simplified spellings fail not because of "spelling rules" but because of cognitive psychology — they increase the reader's cognitive load.
🚫

tho (over-simplified)

The reader must supply missing letters mentally. Attention is diverted, reading is interrupted. Good spelling is invisible.

✅

though (conventional)

The reader's thought takes the word at once without spelling-consciousness. Reading flows effortlessly. This is the standard.

03
Error Checklist
56 high-frequency misspelled words — a complete guard list from accidentally to villain
Strunk's list of 56 commonly misspelled words covers the most frequent traps in English writing. These fall into several categories: ① double-letter traps (accidentally, occurred, disappoint, dissipate); ② ie/ei confusion (believe, deceive, siege, seize, relieve); ③ silent-letter words (fascinate, rhythm, privilege, undoubtedly); ④ confusable suffixes (preceding, pursue, repetition, separate, similar); ⑤ proper-name special cases (Philip, playwright whose wright is not write); ⑥ common function words (until, too, tries, led as past tense of lead). Memorize these and you will avoid the vast majority of spelling errors.

04
Doubling Rule
Short vowel + single consonant: double the final consonant before -ed or -ing
When a monosyllabic word or a word accented on the last syllable ends in a single consonant preceded by a single vowel, double the final consonant before adding -ed or -ing. For example: planned (plan → planned), letting (let → letting), beginning (begin → beginning). The sole exception is coming (come → coming, because the final e is dropped). This rule is elegantly simple, yet countless writers violate it through carelessness. Internalize it as muscle memory to avoid errors like writing → writting.

05
Hyphen & Split Rule
Hyphenation and splitting rules: to-day needs a hyphen, any one is two words
Write to-day, to-night, to-morrow with a hyphen (together is an exception). Write any one, every one, some one, some time (meaning "a period of time") as two words — but sometime meaning "formerly" is one word. These rules, though partly dated today, were formal standards in Strunk's era. They remind us: spelling rules evolve. The key is understanding the logic behind the rule — hyphens and spaces are not decoration; they are tools for eliminating ambiguity for the reader.
📝

Need Hyphenation (traditional)

to-day · to-night · to-morrow. Exception: together. Hyphens are tools for eliminating visual ambiguity.

📝

Write as Two Words

any one · every one · some one · some time ("a period"). But sometime ("formerly") is one word. Splitting depends on meaning.

🔑 Core Takeaway
Spelling serves not "correctness" but "invisibility." though beats tho not because a rule says so, but because though lets the reader absorb meaning effortlessly, while tho interrupts flow with cognitive friction. Memorize the 56 high-frequency error words, master the consonant-doubling rule, understand hyphenation and splitting — these are not tedious trivialities. They are the fundamentals that make your writing invisible: good spelling never draws attention to you; it keeps the reader focused on your thought.
