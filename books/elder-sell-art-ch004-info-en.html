Trading for a Living II: The Art of Selling · Chapter 4
"Taking Profits"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Before every trade, a trader must answer three questions: where to take profits, where to place stops, and what's the risk/reward ratio. Professionals constantly ask; gamblers never do. Amateurs avoid setting profit targets, believing they cap upside — greed drives them to hold too long until profits evaporate. How to systematically set and execute profit targets across market phases? Three tools match three phases, united by "enough" (zhī zú) — contentment.

Have a target, have a method, know "enough." Profit-taking is not about predicting tops — it is about executing a plan. Three technical tools match three phases: MAs for bear-to-bull transitions, channels for established trends, support/resistance for long-term positioning. "Enough" runs through them all: decide what makes you happy, set your target, execute without regret. Every trade has two goals: profit, and becoming a better trader — the second you can always achieve.

01
Technical Tool
Moving Average Profit-Taking: Value Regression
When a market transitions from bear to bull, prices typically trade below moving averages. Buy below the weekly value zone (between two EMAs), set profit targets near that value zone or the slow EMA. The Triple Screen: weekly for strategy, daily for tactics. MAs represent consensus on long-term value — price regressing to value drives the first phase of a bull market.

02
Technical Tool
Channel Profit-Taking: Selling Overbought Zones
Once the bull trend is established, prices stay above MAs, making them poor exit targets. Switch to channels (envelopes): buy at the value zone, sell above the upper band. Channels auto-adjust with volatility — targets rise as swings widen. This "riding the invisible rail" pattern repeats mid-trend. But Elder warns: it may feel like an ATM, but the market is never that simple.

Channel Method

03
Technical Tool
Support & Resistance: Long-Term Targeting
Deer hunters need bigger guns. Long-term trades require larger targets via support and resistance zones. S/R is built on two emotions: pain and regret. Buyers who suffered drawdowns wait painfully for breakeven; those who missed shorting regret it. Elder emphasizes: S/R is not glass — it's more like a wire fence both bulls and bears can lean against.

04
Mental Discipline
Contentment: Greed Is Profit's Worst Enemy
The most important word in trading — and in life — is "enough" (zhī zú). Greedy traders want more after a win, furious at missing the exact bottom or top. Elder: you must decide how much will make you happy, and set targets accordingly. Those who demand too much become slaves to greed; those who decide what is enough gain freedom. Those willing to accept smaller profits often earn more in the long run.

05
Case Review
Trade Review: Continuous Evolution Through Your Journal
Elder highlights three core journal functions: grade every trade on entry, exit, and total, keeping averages above 50%; monthly reviews with hindsight but without regret — regret poisons your mindset; track P&L by idea source to know whose advice to follow. Learn continuously from experience, and every day you become a better trader than the day before.
