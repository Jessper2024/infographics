Five Cities of Du Fu · Ch.13 "Leaving Western Sichuan"

Nanping · Jiuzhaigou · Songpan · Zoigê

A 200-kilometer journey from Zhaohua to Nanping takes nearly twelve hours, crossing southern Gansu and the Baishui River—white as milk from sulfur deposits. At Jiuzhaigou, landslides keep tourists away, leaving Pearl Shoal and Mirror Lake in pristine silence. Songpan, a Tang Dynasty frontier garrison, preserves Ming-Qing city gates and Tibetan charm; here Lai threads a needle, wields a calligraphy brush, and visits the post office three times to mail a package—at what may be the world’s latest-closing post office (11 PM). The journey ends at Zoigê Grassland, 4,000 meters above sea level, where summer wildflowers bloom and yaks roam: ‘Ah, Zoigê, the most beautiful pasture on earth!’

01Nanping & Jiuzhaigou: Emerald Lakes Under Green Mountains
Crossing from Zhaohua through southern Gansu into Sichuan, the Baishui River flows ‘white as milk.’ Nanping, surrounded by mountains on three sides, blends Han and Tibetan culture. At Jiuzhaigou, landslides block the main road—tourists vanish, leaving Mirror Lake and Pearl Shoal in solitude. ‘My guest room window faced yet another green mountain.’ Water droplets scatter like pearls at Pearl Shoal; a Tibetan water mill hides among dark green trees. Riding a Tibetan donkey cart to Long Lake and Five-Color Pond, then descending by tractor in the afternoon—‘These green mountains and lakes are utterly unlike those of Jiangnan or North China.’
02Songpan: A Medieval Border Town & The World’s Latest-Closing Post Office
Songpan feels like a medieval Tibetan town, with surviving Ming-Qing city gates and ‘thin horses and mournful donkeys everywhere.’ Mailing a package to Hong Kong becomes an adventure: a Wenzhou tailor sews cloth bags, Lai borrows a calligraphy brush to write the address, and visits the post office three times. The young female director covers the bags with small-denomination stamps. The post office stays open until 11 PM—‘likely the world’s latest-closing post office, located in a remote town in western Sichuan.’ Tang poet Chen Zi’ang once petitioned to withdraw the garrison here to spare civilians the ordeal of ‘transporting grain a thousand li.’
03Zoigê Grassland: A Breathtaking Alpine Pasture
Heading northwest from Songpan, the bus crosses the great grassland—‘the road was narrow but incredibly flat.’ At over 4,000 meters, Zoigê remains cold even in midsummer. The grassland is ‘like a thick, lovely, furry carpet,’ dotted with small yellow wildflowers. Venturing alone deep into the grassland, climbing a small hill, Lai watches the sunset dye the sky orange-red. Strong winds blow; distant peaks lie silent in golden dusk. Yak dung, herders’ tents, a silver stream winding through—‘Ah, Zoigê, the most beautiful pasture on earth!’
04Western Sichuan Flavors & Travel Trivia
Braised pork knuckle with cucumber in Nanping; camphor-tea duck in Songpan; sour tofu and gritty rice in Zoigê. The joy of an afternoon nap: ‘Having time to nap during a journey is, I often feel, one of life’s great pleasures.’ A red-haired foreign woman sits on the post office floor waiting for an international call. The Zoigê restaurant’s walls are papered with Hainan Daily—the owners likely came from distant southern China.
05Afterglow of the Western Sichuan Journey
From grassland sunsets to post office lamps burning late into night, from Tibetan water mills by emerald lakes to the thin horses of Songpan—this journey through western Sichuan is a continuous ascent. Wearing a cashmere sweater in midsummer, standing on a 4,000-meter hilltop in the vastness of heaven and earth, riding a tractor downhill from Jiuzhaigou—every moment becomes the Tang historian’s most vivid memory of Sichuan’s western frontier.
💡 Key Insight
From the green mountains’ watchful gaze to the boundless grassland, from Jiuzhaigou’s emerald lakes to Songpan’s late-night post office lights—this western Sichuan journey climbs from physical heights to spiritual ones. Lai Ruihe addresses a package with a calligraphy brush, measures the Zoigê grassland on foot, and feels the fate of Tang garrison soldiers across a thousand years. The most remote places yield the warmest encounters; the simplest conditions spark the most genuine life wisdom.
