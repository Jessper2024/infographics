Meishan lies in western Sichuan, beneath the shadow of Mount Emei and by the Glass River. The Sus, known as the "Three Sus" — father Su Xun and sons Su Shi and Su Zhe — are among the "Eight Great Masters." Grandfather Su Xu was illiterate yet extraordinarily wise and generous; father Su Xun only began serious study at 27 but achieved supreme literary fame; mother, née Cheng, was well-educated and encouraged her husband. The Sichuan people's argumentative spirit, confidence, and refusal to accept defeat were perfectly embodied in Su Dongpo.

01
Geography
Beneath Mount Emei: Meishan's Geography and Spirit
Meishan was not a large city but a very comfortable place to live — famous for lotus flowers in May and June, with lotus ponds everywhere filling the air with fragrance. A perfect water control system, functioning for over a thousand years, made western Sichuan a fertile plain in all four seasons. The Su residence had a tall pear tree, a pond, vegetable gardens, and a dense bamboo grove.

Mount Emei's Shelter

The Great Buddha of Leshan — 360 feet carved from the cliff. Meishan lies 40 miles north, nestled between mountain and river.

Rice Paddies & Lotus Ponds

02
Family Genes
The Wisdom of an Illiterate Old Man: Grandfather Su Xu
Grandfather Su Xu could not read, yet possessed extraordinary vitality and a broad mind. He exchanged rice for unhusked grain and stored 4,000 measures — when famine came, he opened his granary and distributed grain. Only then did people understand his foresight: grain can be preserved for years, while milled rice would spoil. On the day his son passed the exams, he was dead drunk, stuffed beef and the official notice into a bundle, and rode a donkey into town — the townspeople burst out laughing.

Father's Example
Late Bloomer: Father Su Xun's Comeback
Su Xun only began serious study at 27, yet achieved supreme literary fame — his reputation was not eclipsed by his outstanding sons, a rarity in Chinese literary history. Taciturn, highly intelligent, hot-tempered, he taught his sons to study the laws of historical rise and fall, to pursue a simple style, and to oppose the ornate fashion of the day.

04
Regional Spirit
The Argumentative Spirit and Confidence of Sichuan People
Sichuan people are hardy, argumentative, confident, and largely self-governing — Sichuan natives, ancient and modern, love to argue and produce powerful essays. Su Dongpo shared this innate eloquence and refusal to accept defeat. Meizhou natives earned a reputation for being "hard to govern": every gentry household kept a law code and freely discussed legal provisions. If a local official was fair, people would hang his portrait and worship it for fifty years.

05
Cultural Soil
Home of Printing: Fertile Ground for Knowledge
Printing was invented in Sichuan a hundred years earlier, sparking sudden enthusiasm for learning — before Su Dongpo's birth, this land had already prepared the intellectual ground. Mother Cheng came from a landed aristocratic family and was well-educated. Su Xun noted in his wife's epitaph that she had constantly encouraged him. At Dongpo's birth, his grandfather was 63 and still alive; his father was undergoing the greatest spiritual crisis of his life — seven years of daily worship before a portrait of Zhang Xian, praying for a son.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Su Dongpo's birth was no accident. Four forces converged on that crying, kicking baby boy on December 19, 1036: the cultural spirit of Sichuan as an independent kingdom, his grandfather's wisdom and vitality, his father's late-blooming dedication, and his mother's fine education. As Lin Yutang put it: the illiterate old man's wisdom lay dormant in his blood, only to bloom into extraordinary flowers and fruits in his grandson.
