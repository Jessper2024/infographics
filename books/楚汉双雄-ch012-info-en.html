The Chu-Han Contention · Chapter Twelve 「The Founding of Han: From Chu-Han Strife to the Naming of a Civilization」

Liu Bang mourns Xiang Yu, strips Han Xin of command, the vassal kings urge enthronement, crowned Emperor at Dingtao — the finale of eight years of contention and the birth of a people

After Xiang Yu's death, Liu Bang buried him with the rites of Duke of Lu, personally weeping in mourning, and pardoned the entire Xiang clan, granting them the imperial surname Liu. Immediately after the burial, Liu Bang staged his classic trick — suddenly riding into Han Xin's camp and stripping him of military command. In 202 BC, the vassal kings urged enthronement, and Liu Bang was crowned Emperor on the north bank of the Si River at Dingtao. The Great Han Dynasty would last over four centuries, giving a people their name after its state. The idioms born from these eight years — “Rising in Revolt,” “Burning the Boats,” “Secretly Crossing at Chencang,” “Hongmen Banquet,” “Last Stand” — still course through every Chinese person's blood. The most spectacular eight years in ancient Chinese history drew to a close.

01
惺惺相惜
Behind a Lifetime of Enmity: Liu Bang Buries Xiang Yu with Duke of Lu Rites
After Xiang Yu's death, Liu Bang selected Gucheng as his burial site and interred him with the rites and ceremony befitting the Duke of Lu (the title King Huai of Chu had granted Xiang Yu). On burial day, Liu Bang personally conducted the funeral for his former sworn brother — weeping, mourning, grieving. The entire Xiang clan was pardoned without exception — Xiang Bo was enfeoffed as Marquis of Sheyang, Xiang Tuo as Marquis of Pinggao, Xiang Xiang as Marquis of Tao; they were later granted the imperial surname Liu and incorporated into the royal clan. Many say Liu Bang's generosity toward the Xiang clan stemmed from Xiang Bo having saved his life repeatedly. In truth, Liu Bang had always regarded this young man over twenty years his junior as his personal idol — though he hated Xiang Yu, deep in his heart was more admiration. Behind a lifetime of mortal enmity lay a bone-deep mutual recognition.
02
帝王手腕
One Always Tames Another: Liu Bang's Lightning Disarmament of Han Xin
After burying Xiang Yu, Liu Bang once again performed his most adept human-nature magic — launching a surprise strike, riding straight into Han Xin's camp and stripping him of military command, taking full control of the army. Han Xin could command a million troops, attack and surely conquer, fight and surely win — yet compared to Liu Bang's political acumen, he was always one move behind. Kuai Tong's prophecy began to materialize. Liu Bang reassigned Han Xin as King of Chu, ruling the region north of the Huai, with his capital at Xiapi — removed from his power base in Qi. Peng Yue was enfeoffed as King of Liang, capital at Dingtao. Both major partners in the years-long campaign against Chu received their due reward — but the form and location of these rewards had undergone precise political calculation. Liu Bang's mastery of human nature, from the art of enfeoffment to the skill of disarming, marked him as the supreme political animal in Chinese history.
03
帝国诞生
Enthroned at Dingtao: The Birth of a Great Dynasty
In 202 BC, the first month of Han's fifth year, Liu Bang declared a general amnesty. The King of Chu, Han Xin; the King of Huainan, Ying Bu; the King of Zhao, Zhang Ao (son of Zhang Er) — all the vassal kings urged enthronement: Liu Bang's military achievements were supreme, his virtue the deepest — the title King of Han was no longer sufficient. On the third day of the second month, Liu Bang was crowned Emperor on the north bank of the Si River at Dingtao. A great dynasty formally ascended the stage of history. The greatness of this dynasty lay in this — it caused a people to be named thereafter by its state name. “Han” henceforth became another synonym for Chinese civilization. To this day, the Han people remain the most widely distributed and most numerous ethnic group on earth. The Great Han founded by Liu Bang endured over four hundred years, becoming one of the few long-duration unified dynasties in Chinese history.
04
历史回响
The Legacy of Eight Years of Strife: Idioms and Stories Fused into a Nation's Blood
From Chen Sheng's uprising in 209 BC to Liu Bang's unification in 202 BC, within merely eight years the divine realm erupted with heroes in dazzling profusion. Countless valiant champions burned through the accumulated momentum of a century, ignited by the great fire of the Qin's collapse, burning blazingly bright and thoroughly clear. These eight years are the most spectacular in ancient Chinese history — when pushed beyond endurance, we think of “Rising in Revolt”; when divine mandate is invoked, we recall the white serpent beheaded at Mangdang; when feinting east and striking west, we smile — “Openly Repair the Plank Road, Secretly Cross at Chencang”; when an uncertain perilous invitation arrives, we wonder — “Could this be a Hongmen Banquet?”; when driven to a dead end, we roar — “With Our Backs to the River, Why Not Fight with All Our Might?”; when staking everything on a desperate gamble, only four characters fill the mind — “Burn the Boats, Smash the Cauldrons.” These instantly recognizable words and stories have fused into this people and this land — even after a thousand more years, they will still be passed down.
05
终章启示
The Ultimate Lesson of the Chu-Han Contention: System vs. Individual Genius
Xiang Yu was a genius among geniuses — Julu's burning of the boats and smashing of cauldrons, Pengcheng's 30,000 routing 560,000, three years of stalemate without a single defeat, twenty-eight riders still fighting like a thousand. But no matter how strong Xiang Yu was as an individual, he could never overcome Liu Bang's system. Liu Bang built a complete organizational system: Xiao He's logistics, Zhang Liang's strategy, Han Xin's military command, Chen Ping's intelligence. The ever-victorious Xiang Yu was ultimately ground down by systemic attrition. Han Xin's values made him abandon the chance to divide the realm into three; Xiang Yu's pride made him refuse to cross the river — both military geniuses fell to being “personal rather than principled.” Liu Bang, on the other hand, was “principled rather than personal” — correcting mistakes, executing what worked. The decisive factor in the Chu-Han contest was not cavalry charges on the battlefield but the fundamental divergence between two organizational philosophies. These eight years teach us: individual brilliance can win battles, but only a system can win the war.
📌 KEY TAKEAWAY
The founding of Han marks the close of the most spectacular eight years in ancient Chinese history. Liu Bang burying Xiang Yu with the rites of Duke of Lu demonstrated the breadth of a top-tier politician — the highest respect for an enemy is the most effective political integration. Liu Bang immediately stripping Han Xin of command after Xiang Yu's death proves he never relaxed his vigilance over power — the imperial art of “one always tames another” ran through everything. The deepest lesson of the Chu-Han contention: individual genius can never defeat a system. Xiang Yu and Han Xin were both ranked among the top five military prodigies in Chinese history, yet one fell to pride and emotional volatility, the other to values and misplaced compassion. Liu Bang had no single ability that was supreme under heaven — but he built a system that made every genius work for him. That system ultimately caused an entire people to be named after its state. Over two thousand years later, we are still the “Han” people.
