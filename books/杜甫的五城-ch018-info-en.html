Five Cities of Du Fu · Ch18 "The Public Office Fields of Shahe"

Mount Wutai · Shahe · Hunyuan · Ying County

Taking a long-distance bus from Baoding to Mount Wutai — a sea of green covering hills and valleys. At Fuping, mistaking a brief stop for lunch break, he disembarked with a can of French foie gras only to see the bus leaving. He sprinted 200 meters in panic, all passengers laughing. Stranded in the desolate town of Shahe, he met an elderly caretaker tending a vegetable garden, which evoked the Tang Dynasty institution of "public office fields" (公廨田). Visited the 1,400-year-old Hanging Temple on a cliff, realizing Xu Xiake once took the same route. At dusk, explored the Liao Dynasty wooden pagoda in Ying County as swallows circled home.

01Mount Wutai: Emerald Waves of Green
Only one bus per day from Baoding. The driver drove eight hours without eating. Wutai's greenery cascaded down every slope — "like a plush carpet draped from the heavens." The white stupa of Tayuan Temple gleamed against this sea of jade. He savored local oat noodles steamed in tiny bamboo baskets, "golden as a honeycomb, fragrant with the scent of naked oats." That night, his pillow was stuffed with millet husks — "more natural and breathable than any down or latex pillow, adjusting delicately to every movement of the head."
02Fuping Panic: French Foie Gras Nearly Cost Everything
At Fuping, he thought the bus had stopped for lunch. Jumped off with only his camera bag and a can of French foie gras. Under a tree at a roadside tea stall, he opened the treasured can, paired it with "Hong Kong-style bread" sold even in this remote town — "a beautiful encounter in my life, enjoying French delicacies under the shade of a tree in a desolate Hebei town." Lifting his head, he saw the bus already 200 meters away. He sprinted. The bus stopped — someone inside had heard his shout. "I gasped my way aboard. The entire bus of Hebei farmers grinned at me as if something novel had happened under the sun."
03Shahe: The Old Caretaker and His Tang-Era Field
Stranded in Shahe at the foot of Wutai. The inn's yard was immaculately swept. His room looked out onto a willow and a datura tree — "pink blossoms against green, as if fallen from a Ming-Qing painting album." He bathed crouching under an outdoor tap, drank sorghum liquor alone. At dawn, the elderly caretaker was hoeing his vegetable plot. "His smiling face in the garden suddenly made me think of a technical term from Tang economic history — gongxietian (public office fields). In the Tang Dynasty, every government office had its own farmland, the income subsidizing operations. A thousand years later, here was this system still in operation, in a remote town called Shahe."
04Hanging Temple: On Xu Xiake's Ancient Path
The 1,400-year-old Hanging Temple clung to a sheer cliff face — "exquisite, yet dwarfed by the colossal precipice." Inside, every chamber was surprisingly small. Later, reading Xu Xiake's travel diaries, he discovered a coincidence: Xu had taken the exact same route — from Baoding through Fuping to Wutai, then north to Mount Heng. "Xu Xiake's journeys were almost entirely south of the Yangtze. Hunyuan was the most remote northern county he ever visited."
05Ying County: The Swallows of the Liao Pagoda
Through Hunyuan to Ying County, the bus crawled along footpaths between farmlands. The driver stopped under a poplar tree, everyone squatting by an irrigation ditch to wash. At Ying County, China's oldest and tallest wooden pagoda stood frozen in the Liao Dynasty — "while everything around it transformed, leaving it stranded in an alley of humble dwellings." At dusk, "a great flock of swallows circled the pagoda in the sunset glow, chattering as in a ritual of returning to roost. The pagoda had become their home."
💡 Key Insight
A forgotten vegetable garden in Shahe — a living fossil of the Tang Dynasty's gongxietian system. Lai Ruihe's historian's eye finds micro-histories in the most mundane travel moments: sprinting after a bus while clutching French foie gras; a willow and datura framed by a window like a painting from an old album; swallows circling a thousand-year-old pagoda as they have for centuries. Travel is not about checking off landmarks, but about discovering, in accidental detentions, the thin, hidden thread connecting history to the present.
