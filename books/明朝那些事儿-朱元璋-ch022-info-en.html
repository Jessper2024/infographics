明朝那些事儿 · 朱元璋 · Chapter 22「The Fall of Lan Yu」

From founding general to political casualty: The Lan Yu Case was the final chapter of Hongwu's Four Great Cases, implicating 15,000 people and sweeping away almost all of the dynasty's meritorious officials.

Lan Yu was the younger brother-in-law of Chang Yuchun and the architect of the decisive victory at the Battle of Buyur Lake. He was a sharp blade in Zhu Yuanzhang's hand. But this blade was too sharp—it wounded enemies and nearly wounded its master. After Crown Prince Zhu Biao died in the 25th year of Hongwu, Zhu Yuanzhang needed to clear obstacles for the young heir Zhu Yunwen. Lan Yu, who was close to the late crown prince and had warned about the "imperial bearing" of Prince of Yan Zhu Di, became a liability. In February of the 26th year of Hongwu (1393), the Embroidered Uniform Guard accused Lan Yu of treason. This political purge, known as the "Lan Yu Case," implicated 15,000 people, resulting in the execution of one duke, thirteen marquises, and two earls.

01
Arrogance
Lan Yu's Three Offenses
After leaving the battlefield, Lan Yu became inseparable from the word "arrogance." Offense one: He forcibly took the Yuan emperor's consort on the return journey, driving her to suicide. Offense two: At Jifeng Pass, he ordered an attack on the checkpoint despite officials already opening the gates. Offense three: He appointed personal allies in the army without imperial authorization, reaching into military power. These acts infuriated Zhu Yuanzhang, who downgraded Lan Yu's title from "Duke of Liang" to "Duke of Liang" (same characters but with "cool" instead of "beam" — a subtle warning). But Lan Yu only grew bolder.

Lan Yu's Conduct

Forced himself on the Yuan consort, attacked a friendly checkpoint, planted cronies in the army—each act was a direct challenge to imperial authority.

Zhu Yuanzhang's Bottom Line

The greater the merit, the greater the threat. Zhu could tolerate battlefield glory, but never ambition at court.

02
Escalation
From Warning to Execution
Lan Yu refused to curb his behavior. When Zhu Yuanzhang bestowed the rare honor of Grand Tutor to the Crown Prince (first-rank), Lan Yu loudly complained: "With my merit, shouldn't I be Grand Preceptor?" This was not a request for promotion—it was a death wish. In February 1393, Guard Commander Jiang Huan accused Lan Yu of treason. Arrested and imprisoned, Lan Yu "confessed" to plotting rebellion and named 15,000 "accomplices." The Lan Yu Case had begun.

03
Character
A Rough Man's Fate
Lan Yu's downfall was rooted in his character. He was a "rough man"—not uneducated, but thoughtless. He acted first and thought later, never understanding the art of knowing when to step back. From his arrogance at the Nanguch surrender banquet to his reckless attack on Jifeng Pass, Lan Yu spent his entire life proving one truth: character determines destiny. His tragedy was not history's fault, but the inevitable result of his flaws.

04
Hidden Motive
Clearing the Path for Zhu Yunwen
The Lan Yu Case unfolded in February 1393—just ten months after Crown Prince Zhu Biao died in April 1392, leaving Zhu Yunwen as the new heir. Zhu Yuanzhang's real purpose was to eliminate potential threats to the young grandnephew. Lan Yu was close to the late crown prince (as Chang Yuchun's brother-in-law) and had warned the prince that Zhu Di "had the bearing of an emperor." To secure Zhu Yunwen's throne, Zhu Yuanzhang needed to remove every general who might challenge it. The "treason" charge was merely a convenient pretext to purge 15,000 people.

05
Legacy
A Dynasty Swept Clean
The Lan Yu Case swept away nearly all the founding generals of the Hongwu era. From the Hu Weiyong Case in 1380 to this final purge, twenty years of bloodletting left the dynasty without military leadership. Lan Yu was exterminated along with his clan, and 15,000 names were recorded in the "Book of Traitors." But Zhu Yuanzhang failed to foresee the consequence: when the Mongols raided again, there were no experienced generals left—only Prince of Yan Zhu Di, the very "time bomb" he had cultivated.

KEY TAKEAWAY
Lan Yu died from his character flaws, but also from politics. Zhu Yuanzhang used the Lan Yu Case to clear the path for Zhu Yunwen—only to unknowingly prepare the way for Zhu Di's Jingnan Campaign. By eliminating one generation of generals, he created the next dynasty's greatest crisis.
