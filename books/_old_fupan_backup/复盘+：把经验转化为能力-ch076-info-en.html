FuPan · Chapter 26「Self-FuPan」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Self-FuPan is the simplest, most accessible form of review — unrestricted by time or space, achievable with a single thought. Yet precisely because it relies solely on oneself without external scrutiny, self-FuPan easily falls into two traps: letting insights slip and self-deception. How to make self-FuPan truly effective? Record immediately, apply instantly, be ruthlessly honest, make it a habit.

Self-FuPan is mind-guided, freely deployed — a flash, a recall, a reflection, a comparison, a summary — each can be FuPan. Cialdini discovered rejection-then-retreat by reviewing a Boy Scout chocolate sale; his jeweler friend uncovered expensive-equals-quality from an anomalous event. The greatest risk of self-FuPan is self-deception: without ruthless self-analysis, FuPan becomes a tool for making excuses.

02
Case Study
The Jeweler & Expensive = Quality: How an Anomaly Spawned a Business Formula
Cialdini recounts in Influence: a jeweler turquoise would not sell. She left a note to discount by 50%. The clerk misread it and doubled the price — everything sold out. Reviewing this bizarre event, she realized people automatically apply the expensive-equals-quality heuristic. From then on, her strategy was price hikes, not discounts, reaping huge profits.

03
Case Study 2
Cialdini & Rejection-Then-Retreat: From Personal Experience to Scientific Proof
Cialdini reviewed his own encounter: a Boy Scout first asked him to buy 5-dollar circus tickets (rejected), then 1-dollar chocolates (he bought). He realized the concession from the first request triggered his obligation to reciprocate. He named this rejection-then-retreat strategy and validated it through experiments.
"If someone takes an action toward us, we should reciprocate with a similar action. This produces two results: obligation to return favors; and if someone makes a concession, we feel obligated to make one."
—— Robert Cialdini, Influence

04
Two Traps
Letting It Slip & Self-Deception: Two Fatal Traps of Self-FuPan
Self-FuPan easily lets insights slip: reach a conclusion, feel satisfied, and move on — the insight fades, and the same mistake recurs. The palest ink beats the strongest memory — record immediately. The bigger problem is self-deception: failing to ruthlessly review oneself. Kodak invented the digital camera yet went bankrupt refusing to abandon film profits — a tragic lesson in organizational self-deception.
⚠️ Beware Two Traps

Trap 1: Letting It Slip — Figured it out, let it go, fail to record or apply, repeat the same mistake.
Trap 2: Self-Deception — It is everyone else fault, FuPan becomes an excuse-making tool.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Self-FuPan appears simple but success hinges on crossing two traps: letting insights slip (failing to record and apply) and self-deception (making excuses instead of finding causes). Honest FuPan must be selfless: empty yourself to find true root causes. When FuPan becomes habit — live and breathe with FuPan as Lenovo requires — any time, any place, any event becomes an opportunity to learn and elevate your capability.
