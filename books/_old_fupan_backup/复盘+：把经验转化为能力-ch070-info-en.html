Fupan · Chapter 20「Institutionalizing Processes」

Lock in the optimal response · Share lessons learned · Reduce organizational costs Pre-flight checklists, recipe steps, factory assembly lines — these mechanical-looking routines are all the crystallized results of countless rounds of fupan. Institutionalizing processes isn't rigidity; it's turning battle-tested best practices into standards so successors don't start from zero.

One of fupan's core purposes is to institutionalize repeatedly validated optimal responses. This chapter systematically demonstrates — through the Checklist Revolution, Hua Luogeng's critical-path method, Taylor's scientific management, and Huawei's "Rigidify → Optimize → Institutionalize" trilogy — that institutionalizing processes doesn't restrict creativity; it subtracts trial-and-error costs from every individual, enabling organizations to advance on the shoulders of predecessors. From aviation checklists to assembly lines, from making tea to house cleaning — efficient behavior is always backed by processes crystallized through fupan.

01
The Checklist Revolution
One checklist drove infection rates from 11% to zero — the minimalist process born from fupan
Checklists are the ultimate form of fupan-institutionalized processes: they capture only critical, error-prone points. Johns Hopkins Hospital used one small checklist to reduce central line infections from 11% to 0. Eight pilot hospitals using continuously improving checklists cut severe post-surgery complications by 36 percentage points and mortality by 47%. Serious construction accidents in the U.S. occur fewer than 20 times a year. These staggering results all come from checklists — which don't contain every step, only the most critical and error-prone nodes.

02
Life Wisdom
Making tea and cleaning house: everyday routines are all fupan-crystallized processes
Hua Luogeng used tea-making to illustrate the essence of critical-path optimization. Method A (boil water first, wash teaware while waiting) is fastest. Methods B and C both waste time. House cleaning follows the same logic: dust high → wipe surfaces → sweep floor → mop last — this sequence wasn't innate; it was solidified through countless people's repeated practice. We institutionalize processes in daily life without noticing, but its value amplifies in work settings — plan more, coordinate better, institutionalize the flow, and save enormous costs.
❌

Inefficient methods (B, C)

Prep everything before boiling water (idle waiting), or boil first then frantically search for tea and wash cups. Bad sequencing causes wasted idle time.

✅

Optimal method (A)

Boil water → use wait-time for prep → tea ready instantly. A fupan-crystallized flow: optimally combining parallel and serial tasks.

03
Scientific Management
Taylorism: decomposing actions → optimal sequence → assembly line — fupan-driven mass production
Taylor, the father of scientific management, used fupan-like analysis of production to derive standardized methods. The core steps: decompose production actions → analyze efficiency of each → derive optimal sequence → set optimal workstation → determine reasonable labor quotas → standardize operations → ultimately enabling Ford's assembly line mass production. The assembly line is the ultimate form of institutionalized process — every worker's every motion has been fupan-optimized.

04
Huawei Practice
Rigidify → Optimize → Institutionalize: Ren Zhengfei's "cutting feet to fit shoes" process evolution
IBM told Huawei: "No time to do it right the first time, yet endless time to repeat the same mistakes." Ren Zhengfei adopted IBM's system with a three-step strategy. Rigidify: copy and execute without deviation — "cut Huawei's feet to fit IBM's shoes." Optimize: once proficient, identify small improvements from practice. Institutionalize: turn validated improvements into standards, spread across the company. Then start a new cycle, continuously advancing.
🔒

05
The Complete Loop
Fupan → find best response → institutionalize → share lessons → reduce cost: the full value loop
Institutionalizing elevates fupan's value from personal insight to organizational asset. Fupan helps us find optimal responses under different conditions. Locking these responses into standard processes provides behavioral guidance for successors. From aviation checklists to recipe steps, from warm-up routines to corporate SOPs — behind every process regulation is fupan. Institutionalization ≠ rigidity; institutionalization = writing down validated best paths so no one pays the same tuition twice.
🔄

Institutionalize = stop reinventing the wheel

"No time to do it right once, yet endless time to repeat the same thing." Institutionalizing breaks this vicious cycle.

⚠️

Institutionalize ≠ rigidity

It means standardizing current best practices. When conditions change or new experience emerges → fresh fupan → re-optimize → re-institutionalize. Processes evolve.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Institutionalizing processes is fupan's most practical output. From the Checklist Revolution to Taylorism, from critical-path thinking to Huawei's "Rigidify → Optimize → Institutionalize," every high-performing organization shares one secret: they lock fupan-validated optimal responses into processes so successors needn't start from scratch. Processes are not cages — they are standard answers purchased with predecessors' failures, the stepping stones on which you stand upon giants' shoulders. Repetition without fupan is wasted life; fupan without institutionalization means reinventing the wheel every time.
