Avoid Making the Same Mistake

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer Keith Ferrazzi's relationship with the president's assistant Mary went from close to hostile—because he made a mistake most people make: going over her head with complaints. People don't trip over the same stone twice—this is review's doing. What is the first value of review? The answer: avoid making the same mistake. Through the cases of Keith and Ajie, discover how review helps us avoid failure at its source.

The relationship between Keith Ferrazzi, author of Never Eat Alone, and Mary, executive assistant to Deloitte president Pat Loconto, vividly illustrates review's first value: avoid making the same mistake. Round One: Keith did well—winning Mary's respect through small talk and praise. Round Two: he committed a fatal error—complaining to President Pat about Mary's treatment of his new assistant Jennifer, leading to nightmare days. Ultimately, through review, Keith found the solution: let Mary choose the new assistant and instruct the new assistant to obey Mary unconditionally. Meanwhile, the Ajie head-banging case shows that knowledge gained through review can prevent similar misfortune at multiple levels: source, process, and consequence.

01
Round One
Round One: Keith's Successful Opening
Keith built a good relationship with Mary through small talk and genuine praise, winning her respect. Before each call to President Pat, he spent a few extra minutes chatting with Mary, always saying, "Mary, when you talk to people you're like a 'loudspeaker.'" The main reason Keith could easily approach Pat was his good relationship with Mary—this became one of the most important relationships of his business career.
✅

Key to Success

Extra minutes of small talk · Genuine praise not instrumental use · Making her feel seen as a person, not just a channel

🤝

Relationship Outcome

Easily bypass bureaucratic hurdles · Smooth access to the president · One of the most important business relationships

02
Round Two
Round Two: Over-the-Head Complaint — Keith's Fatal Mistake
Keith made a mistake most people make: going directly to Mary's superior, Pat, to complain about Jennifer. From Mary's perspective, this was going behind her back—equivalent to declaring war. The nightmare ensued: scheduling Pat became harder, expenses were scrutinized, time was consumed, and Jennifer's stress mounted. Eventually Pat had to step in: "Keith, you handled this entire thing poorly."
⚠️

Trigger

New assistant Jennifer incompatible with Mary · Scheduling Pat became harder · Expenses scrutinized · Time consumed

💥

Fatal Mistake

Going over Mary's head to complain to the president · From Mary's view = declaration of war · Pat: you handled this whole thing poorly

03
Three Remedies
Keith's Three Remedies: How to Repair a Broken Relationship
Keith didn't let things spiral into a vicious cycle—through review, he found the solution. He first recognized: Mary had a problem with Jennifer, not with him (Mary still liked him as before). Then he did three things:
🔑
Recognize the Truth
Mary's issue was with Jennifer ≠ With Keith · "Punishing the dog to show the owner" is illusion · Mary still liked Keith
🎯
Let Mary Choose
Showed all assistant candidates to Mary for ranking · Had her rank them by preference · Hired her #1 pick
📋
Command Obedience
Told new assistant to obey Mary unconditionally · Sent a clear message: Mary is the real superior
04
Ajie's Case
Ajie's Head-Banging: Three-Level Defense Through Review
Ajie's head-banging case perfectly illustrates how review helps avoid the same mistake at multiple levels. Review's power isn't simply remembering "don't overeat"—it builds defense at source (control eating desire), process (avoid catching cold), and consequence (hold posture to minimize harm).
🛑

Source Defense

Recall the 2008 incident · Control gluttonous desire · Prevent risk at its source

⚠️

Process Defense

Even if overeating, be careful to avoid catching cold · Neutralize external unknown factors

🪑

Consequence Defense

Hold same posture until head clears · Simply sit down to lower center of gravity · Even if fainting occurs, no injury

05
First Value
First Value of Review: Avoid Making the Same Mistake
Avoiding the same mistake is review's most direct and fundamental value. People say you never trip over the same stone twice—because after the first trip you have experience to prevent the second. If someone does trip over the same stone twice, we'd call them a fool—and review is precisely how we avoid being that fool.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Review's first value is to avoid making the same mistake. Keith Ferrazzi's case demonstrates a complete three-act drama: Round One success (building a good relationship) → Round Two failure (over-the-head complaint leading to nightmare) → Round Three remedy (finding the right approach through review to restore the relationship). Meanwhile, Ajie's head-banging case shows that the defense built by review is three-dimensional—source control, process prevention, and consequence mitigation, all working together. Review ensures we need never trip over the same stone twice.
