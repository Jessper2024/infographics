The Elements of Style · Ch.10 "Begin Each Paragraph with a Topic Sentence"

Three-part structure · Topic sentence → Development → Conclusion — reader knows what you'll say, then what you said Rule 9 is Strunk's longest and most richly illustrated rule on paragraph structure. It defines the classic three-part paragraph — topic sentence, development through argument, and a concluding restatement — then provides two complete sentence-by-sentence analyses: Stevenson's 9-sentence paragraph from Walking Tours and Lecky's 6-sentence paragraph from The Political Value of History. Strunk shows how a great writer puts this principle into practice at the highest level.

This is one of the book's most thoroughly argued rules. Strunk goes far beyond simply saying "use topic sentences" — he builds a complete framework for paragraph-level internal organization: ① The golden three-part structure — Topic sentence → Development → Concluding restatement. ② Five methods of development — restatement, definition, negation of the contrary, illustration, and proof. ③ The art of transition — sometimes a single word (again, therefore, for the same reason) establishes the paragraph's connection to what precedes; sometimes an entire transitional paragraph is needed. ④ Two classic dissections — sentence-by-sentence analyses of Stevenson's 9-sentence and Lecky's 6-sentence paragraphs, revealing how a topic sentence is expanded, argued, and reinforced. The rule also distinguishes between expository/argumentative writing (which requires topic sentences) and narrative/descriptive writing (where topic sentences are optional or implied).

01
Three-Part
The classic three-part structure: Topic sentence → Development → Concluding restatement, forming a complete logical loop
The best paragraphs — especially in exposition and argument — follow a three-part structure. ① The topic sentence appears at or near the beginning, so the reader knows the paragraph's purpose from the start. ② The sentences that follow explain, argue for, or develop the idea stated in the topic sentence. ③ The final sentence either emphasizes the topic-sentence idea or states an important consequence. Never end a paragraph with a digression or an unimportant detail — the ending must reinforce the topic, not scatter the reader's attention. This structure ensures the reader knows your central claim both entering and exiting the paragraph.

02
Five Methods
Five weapons for developing a topic sentence: restate, define, negate, illustrate, prove
The writer may develop the paragraph-to-topic-sentence relationship in five distinct ways. ① Restatement — say the same thing in different words, reinforcing memory. ② Definition — precisely define key terms from the topic sentence. ③ Negation of the contrary — refute the opposite view to make the topic clearer. ④ Illustration — use concrete examples to ground abstract principles. ⑤ Proof — demonstrate the topic's validity through logical testing. In a long paragraph, the writer may use several of these methods together. Strunk's subsequent dissections of Stevenson and Lecky show exactly how these five methods combine in practice.
🔄
Restate + Define
Say the same thing in different words while precisely defining key terms. The reader understands the core idea from multiple angles.
⚔️
Negate + Illustrate
Refute the contrary view to strengthen your position, then ground abstract principles with concrete examples.
🔬
Prove (by test)
Demonstrate the topic's validity through logical testing — show its implications and consequences to further clarify.
03
Classic Dissection
Stevenson's 9-sentence paragraph dissected: from topic sentence to elevated conclusion
Strunk selects a classic paragraph from Stevenson's Walking Tours to demonstrate the three-part structure sentence by sentence. Sentence ① is the topic sentence: "Now, to be properly enjoyed, a walking tour should be gone upon alone." Sentence ② makes the meaning clearer by denying the contrary (traveling in company) — development via negation. Sentence ③ restates the topic sentence in briefer form and gives three reasons — restatement + proof. Sentences ④-⑤ state a fourth reason and restate it in different language — restatement. Sentences ⑥-⑦ state the same reason in the words of Hazlitt — illustration. Sentence ⑧ restates in paraphrase — restatement. Sentence ⑨ states the fourth reason in emphatic and elevated language, forming a strong conclusion — the perfect concluding restatement.
"Now, to be properly enjoyed, a walking tour should be gone upon alone."
— Topic sentence announcing the paragraph's central claim
— Robert Louis Stevenson, Walking Tours
04
Second Dissection
Lecky's 6-sentence paragraph dissected: Topic → Define → Expand → Contrast → Supplement → Conclude
Lecky's paragraph demonstrates a different development pattern. Sentence ① "It was chiefly in the eighteenth century that a very different conception of history grew up" — topic sentence. Sentence ② defines the new conception more clearly — definition. Sentence ③ expands the definition — definition + illustration. Sentence ④ defines by contrast ("they sought rather to write a history of peoples than a history of kings") — negation + contrast. Sentence ⑤ supplements the definition: another element of the new conception (the chain of causes and effects) — restatement. Sentence ⑥ conclusion: an important consequence of the new conception — a perfect concluding restatement. This case proves that the same three-part framework can produce entirely different textual rhythms with different subjects and styles.
"It was chiefly in the eighteenth century that a very different conception of history grew up."
— Topic sentence compressing time, direction, and change into a single sentence
— Lecky, The Political Value of History
05
Transitions & Mode Distinctions
The art of transition + flexible topic sentence use in narrative and descriptive writing
Connections between paragraphs must also be designed. If a paragraph is part of a larger whole, its relation to what precedes must be indicated. Sometimes a single word or phrase in the topic sentence suffices: again, therefore, for the same reason. But sometimes one or more introductory or transitional sentences before the topic sentence serve better; if more than one such sentence is needed, the transition is best made an independent paragraph. In narrative and descriptive writing, the rule is more flexible. Sometimes a concise, comprehensive sentence at the beginning binds together the details that follow ("The breeze served us admirably"). More often, the opening sentence simply indicates the direction ("He picked up the heavy lamp from the table and began to explore"). Vivid narrative paragraphs are often brief and lack anything resembling a topic sentence — the paragraph break serves only as a rhetorical pause.
📝

Expository/Argumentative: topic sentence required

Three-part structure applies strictly. Topic→Develop→Conclude. Transition words build logical chains between paragraphs.

📖

Narrative/Descriptive: flexible approach

A comprehensive sentence may bind details together, or the opening may simply suggest direction. Short paragraphs often lack topic sentences — the break is purely rhetorical.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
A topic sentence is not optional decoration — it is your reader's advance-notification system. Strunk provides not a rigid rule but a complete paragraph engineering kit: the three-part structure defines the paragraph's internal logical flow; five development methods provide a rich toolbox for argumentation; two sentence-by-sentence dissections demonstrate how theory translates into practice. The critical insight: whether your paragraph is 9 sentences or 6, expository or narrative, the reader must know "what this is about" upon entering and must confirm "what was just said" upon leaving. Achieve both, and your paragraph becomes not a heap of sentences but a complete, logical intellectual journey.
