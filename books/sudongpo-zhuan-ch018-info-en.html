The Gay Genius · Ch.18 "Happiness in Huangzhou"

Yoga, alchemy, longevity — how a Chinese scholar cultivated body and mind to find inner peace

During his Huangzhou years, Su Dongpo delved deeply into Buddhist and Daoist thought, transforming Indian yoga into a Chinese scholar's discipline of body and mind. He practiced breath control, silent meditation, swallowing saliva for inner alchemy — his goal not merely peace of mind but physical immortality. "Without strict discipline of the mind, there can be no enlightenment" — he believed mental self-mastery was the starting point of liberation. Su brought distinctly Chinese innovations to yoga: eliminating contorted postures while incorporating Daoist physiological practices.

01
MOTIVATION
From Peace of Mind to Physical Immortality
"Without strict discipline of the mind, there can be no enlightenment" — Su believed mental self-mastery was the starting point of liberation. Buddhist-Daoist liberation began with overcoming fear, anger, and sorrow. He grew increasingly interested in longevity, believing body and soul were inseparable — if the mind was carefully cultivated, it would one day shed its temporary shell and soar into the spiritual realm. His brother Ziyou had started yoga practice years earlier; when Su saw Ziyou's radiant complexion in Huangzhou, he was deeply moved.
😰

Before Practice

Goal of Practice

Peace of mind, mastery over emotions; further — prevent aging, extend life, and eventually let the mind soar free from its bodily shell.

02
INNOVATION
Chinese Yoga: Removing Contortions, Adding Daoist Elements
Su brought distinctly Chinese innovations to Indian yoga — he eliminated bending, twisting, and contorting postures, using only comfortable poses, and added Daoist physiological practices like timed saliva swallowing. "This can be considered the Chinese contribution to yoga." The essence of yoga is rest — planned, conscious rest. The ultimate goal: "think less and less, until finally you think of nothing at all."

DAILY ROUTINE
Su's Daily Practice Regimen
Su documented his practice in detail: rising after midnight, knocking his teeth thirty-six times, holding breath to visualize his internal organs, rinsing and refining saliva, massaging his entire body — a complete mind-body cultivation system blending yoga, Daoism, and Chinese medicine. His breath control ratio was 1:2:2 (inhale:hold:exhale). His longest breath-hold reached "one hundred and twenty before release" — approximately 144 seconds.

04
COSMOLOGY
Dragon and Tiger, Fire and Water: Su's Daoist Cosmology
Su explained his practice through Daoist five-element cosmology: Heart-fire is the "Tiger," Kidney-water is the "Dragon." The goal is to push fire toward water — reversing the natural direction that consumes life. "The Dragon emerges from fire, the Tiger is born from water." Saliva swallowing pushes heart-fire toward kidney-water. This theory brilliantly fused Daoist cosmology, Chinese medical organ theory, and yogic practice into a unified system.

05
ENLIGHTENMENT
True Seeing Is Seeing Nothing At All
In a letter to his brother, Su articulated the ultimate goal of yogic meditation: true realization is not seeing something extraordinary, but reaching a state where nothing is seen. "Be at ease, follow conditions with an open heart, exhaust your mind's efforts — there is no other sublime understanding." He compared those who mistake vacant ignorance for Buddhahood to cats and dogs sleeping off a full meal — true enlightenment is not pursuing a "state" but removing what obscures the original mind.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
Su Dongpo's spiritual practice in Huangzhou proves that a Chinese scholar could creatively fuse Indian yoga, Daoist longevity arts, Chinese medical theory, and Buddhist meditation into a unified whole. His "Sinicized yoga" aimed not at mystical experience but at practical goals — physical health and peace of mind. As he told his brother: true enlightenment is not "seeing" some supernatural vision, but the natural state that remains after removing the "film" that obscures the heart-mind.
