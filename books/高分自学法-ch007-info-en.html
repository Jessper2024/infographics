Self-Study Mastery · Ch.2「Concrete Learning Methods: Principles at the Core」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Countless learning methods exist, but most are merely "techniques" applying core principles to specific situations — not principles themselves. People desperately search for their ideal method yet never see results because they fixate on techniques while ignoring five immutable principles: exercise, goal-setting, repetition, deep focus, and spare moments.

BLUF: The core of learning is not technique, but principle. Principles surpass all techniques. Drawing from brain science, psychology, and historical cases, this chapter systematically explains five principles: ① Exercise — priming the brain for optimal performance; ② Goal-setting — activating the goal-directed mechanism; ③ Repetition — myelin formation and Ebbinghaus curves as the ultimate secret; ④ Deep Focus — Csikszentmihalyi's flow theory and three conditions; ⑤ Spare Moments — creating competitive differentiation. Each principle includes concrete practice methods. With the right direction, you will always reach the destination.

Principle 1
Exercise: The Starting Point — Key to Optimal Brain State
Learning begins with exercise. Murakami runs 10km daily, King Jeongjo scored 49/50 arrows, da Vinci single-handedly controlled a horse — they succeeded because they exercised, not while exercising. Naperville 203 District's "Zero Period" P.E. boosted reading speed and led to #1 in science globally on TIMSS. California's study of 1M+ students found fully-fit students averaged 67 in math vs. just 35 for students passing only one fitness standard. Three mechanisms: ① more oxygen & nutrients to the brain; ② neurotransmitter concentrations rise for efficient signaling; ③ neuron growth expands memory storage.

Principle 2
Goal-Setting: The Biological Force of Goal-Directed Mechanism
Once a clear goal is set, the body blindly follows it. The 1953 Yale study: 3% who wrote clear goals out-earned the remaining 97% combined 22 years later. Professor Hwang Nong-mun's son aimed for "#1 nationwide" — within one month, he voluntarily quit gaming and resisted sleeping early. Brain sets goal → neuron signals strengthen → myelin thickens → goal takes root → body follows blindly. What matters is a "clear" goal, not an "impressive" goal.
Biology Principle
→
Brain declares "this is the goal" → body blindly follows. Goal near = positive feedback (joy); goal far = negative feedback (motivation).
SMART Framework

"The Analects" · 10 pages daily · on subway during commute · finish in 1 month. Clear = Specific + Measurable + Achievable + Realistic + Time-based.
Practice Tips
→
Repeatedly recall goals (visualization · phone wallpaper · wrist band); remind at every opportunity; clarity > impressiveness.

Principle 3
Repetition: Ebbinghaus Curves & Myelin — The Ultimate Secret
Repetition is the ultimate learning method — the single secret behind all learning techniques. The Ebbinghaus forgetting curve proves: forgetting begins the moment learning ends, with no exceptions. But the memory curve proves: with each review, the forgetting curve flattens until nearly horizontal — becoming permanent long-term memory. From the myelin perspective, repetition thickens myelin, accelerating signal transmission. The student who went from 3rd-to-last to 3rd used just three repetitions: after class + end of day + end of week. The author memorized full texts by copying them just 10 times.

Principle 4
Deep Focus: Csikszentmihalyi's Flow — Three Conditions
Study volume = Study time × Focus intensity. Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi named the state of "complete loss of time awareness" as Flow. Newton skipped meals for research; mobile gamers miss their stop — the same flow mechanism. As focus rises, relevant neurons and synapses activate, and the brain releases dopamine for pleasure. Three conditions for deep flow: clear goal, fast feedback, and balanced difficulty.

Principle 5
Spare Moments: The Secret to Sleeping, Playing, and Learning Well
Using spare moments is not "nice to do" — it is "must do." Einstein always carried problems to think about. The author spent 2 hours commuting daily, using that time to review error notebooks while still sleeping 8 hours even in senior year. Two reasons: ① necessary for competitive differentiation — use time others discard; ② maintain focus without dropping to zero — like crawling in traffic rather than turning off the engine. Four tools: handbook → flash cards → smartphone apps → mental recall.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The five learning principles form the complete system for self-study: ① Exercise — exercise first, study second; prime the brain for optimal performance; ② Goal-setting — set clear goals to activate the goal-directed mechanism, and the body will follow; ③ Repetition — the single ultimate secret behind all learning methods; forgetting is inevitable but repetition converts it to long-term memory; ④ Deep Focus — enter flow state using clear goals, fast feedback, and balanced difficulty; ⑤ Spare Moments — the final weapon for creating competitive differentiation, allowing you to sleep, play, and learn well simultaneously. With the right direction, you will always reach the destination.
