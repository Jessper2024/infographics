The World of Yesterday · Ch.16 "The Agony of Peace"

"The sun of Rome is set. Our day is gone." — Shakespeare Zweig begins semi-exile in London, witnesses Austria's fall and his mother's death, spends Freud's final days; experiences the false peace of Munich and its shattering; on September 1, 1939, hears that war has returned—an era ends.

This final chapter of the book covers 1934–1939: Zweig's semi-exile in London. He rents a small room eerily identical to his Vienna student quarters from thirty years earlier—feeling fate had drawn him back to the beginning. After Austria falls, he loses his homeland and possessions, becoming stateless. In London, he witnesses the futility of appeasement: Chamberlain flies to Germany three times, Munich brings two days of elation, then shatters. He visits Freud in London during the master's final days—Freud refused painkillers, "preferring to suffer in full consciousness." On September 1, 1939, while registering his marriage in Bath, Zweig hears: "Germany has invaded Poland—war has broken out!" An era has ended.

01
Semi-Exile
The Small London Room: Fate Drawn Back to the Beginning
Zweig's rented London room was eerily identical to his Vienna student quarters of thirty years before—same size, same Blake painting on the wall. "Was this a symbol of my life shrinking back to the past?" His life unintentionally became provisional—no long-term plans possible. With his Austrian passport still valid, he was not yet stateless but already in the first stage of exile. The feeling of losing one's homeland was "like walking with open eyes through a void, disoriented." In London he began work on Mary Stuart, spending weeks in the British Museum pursuing the truth about the Scottish queen.

02
Austria Falls
The Loss of Homeland: From Stateless Person to "Enemy Alien"
On March 13, 1938, Austria fell—"All Austrians, regardless of their will, were henceforth citizens of Hitler's empire." Zweig's Austrian passport became invalid; he was forced to apply for a British "alien's registration card." For the first time he experienced the humiliation of statelessness: a person without a country "has no rights before world law." He recalled his own indifference when Merezhkovsky had complained of banned Russian books in Paris—now his own books were burned in Germany, and only then did he truly understand. His elderly mother died in Vienna; he could not even return for her funeral.

03
Appeasement Fails
The Munich Agreement: Two Days of Elation, Eternal Shame
Chamberlain flew to Germany three times, each time believing he had saved the peace—"Peace for our time." All London rejoiced; Zweig himself walked the streets faster and lighter. But two days later the truth emerged: this was total surrender, a betrayal of Czechoslovakia. Appeasement had completely failed. The British painfully awoke from their credulity—they finally understood that negotiating with a faithless dictator was futile. Zweig watched all this with an exile's clear-eyed vision, his heart filled with anguished concern.
"We rarely recall our political confusion with pride, but rather with horror at where that era led us."
04
Freud
Freud's Final Days: Holding the Beacon of Reason in Darkness
Freud, aged 83, escaped Vienna to London. Zweig spent the master's final days in long conversations with him—"To be with Sigmund Freud during those historic days was a comfort and spiritual encouragement." Freud refused painkillers, preferring to suffer in full consciousness. Salvador Dalí sketched him, capturing the image of "death" already visible. Freud said: "There is rarely 100% truth, just as there is no 100% alcohol." This great rationalist maintained astonishing clarity and dignity to the very end.

05
War Comes
September 1, 1939: An Era Has Ended
While registering his marriage in Bath, Zweig heard a young man burst in shouting: "Germany has invaded Poland—war has broken out!" An era had ended. The radio announced Britain's declaration of war against Germany. Zweig, an Austrian opponent of Nazism, was officially classified as an "enemy alien." He walked through the small town of Bath for a last glimpse of peace: "Nature, the ancient mother, once again cannot feel her creatures' suffering." The shadow of war would stretch across every day to come.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
Peace did not die in an instant—it slowly suffocated in appeasement's self-deception. Zweig understood only after losing his homeland: what perishes with a fatherland is far more than territory—it is the very foundation of existence. Freud faced death with lucid reason, becoming the last beacon of the dark age. On September 1, 1939, as the radio broadcast the declaration of war, Zweig stood under Bath's sunshine knowing that "the shadow of war will stretch across every day to come"—but as he wrote: "Only those who have experienced light and darkness, peace and war, rise and fall, have truly lived."
