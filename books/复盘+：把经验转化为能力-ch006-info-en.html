WHY — Why It Matters
The Core Significance of Business & Strategic Review

Business and strategic review is the critical mechanism that shifts an organization from "heads-down execution" to "heads-up navigation" — enabling learning from operations, adapting in real time, and responding swiftly. Great entrepreneurs are forged through practice, not lectures. Business management is a complex and nuanced system requiring knowledge, skills, experience, and even intuition and wisdom. Embedding review into operations — regularly examining internal and external changes, identifying weaknesses, and optimizing strategy — is a vital discipline for any leader and an indispensable closed-loop element of organizational operations.

🔍

Scan for Change

Regularly examine shifts in the internal and external environment to spot trends and patterns

⚡

Rapid Adaptation

Learn from trial and error, adjust strategy quickly, and achieve "fast but not chaotic" iteration

2
WHO — Who Does It
Benchmark Enterprise Practices

Google and Lenovo represent two archetypal approaches: Google builds an open, transparent closed-loop through OKR + quarterly review, while Lenovo embeds review into its "Build the Team, Set the Strategy, Lead the Execution" management framework.

🔴

Google: OKR + Quarterly Review

Quarterly company-wide meetings where everyone from CEO to individual contributors discusses and scores their OKRs, leaders candidly dissect mistakes, and next-quarter OKRs are set based on synchronized information — forming an open, transparent feedback loop

🟢

Lenovo: Review Embedded in Operations

Strategic review (major milestones and direction) + tactical review (projects and daily ops), through "blue-sky meetings," annual strategic planning, and quarterly business reviews — forming a "Plan → Execute → Review → Optimize" closed loop

"Looking back at Lenovo's critical junctures across key historical periods, Mr. Liu Chuanzhi ultimately succeeded by relying on repeated review and study." — Zhang Tao, Liu Wen

HOW — How to Excel
Four Critical Success Factors

Strategic review is the most "difficult" type of review — it spans many domains, involves many participants, and tackles complex issues. You must master four key factors: CEO leadership, clear role division, balanced internal-external focus, and skilled facilitation.

👑
CEO-Led Setting strategy is the CEO's core duty — strategic review must be personally led and driven by the top leader
⚙️
Clear Roles Establish mechanisms and sustain them long-term; each unit self-reviews first, then joins company-level discussions
🌐
Internal + External Never look inward only — you must gain insight into the industry, customers, competitors, and external environment
🧭
Skilled Facilitation Requires certified facilitators proficient in SWOT, Porter's Five Forces, Balanced Scorecard, and other tools
5
WHO ELSE — Who Else Needs It
Small Businesses & Startups Need Strategic Review Too

"Small companies don't need strategy" is completely wrong — strategy is fundamentally about the holistic, long-term, and essential design of a company's development, and no business can do without it. Startups have limited money, people, and resources, making continuous "target calibration" even more critical. Liu Chuanzhi advises entrepreneurs to progress from "shooting blind" to "shooting with intent" to "shooting with aim," gradually learning to formulate strategy. While the form need not be formal, the leader must have timely, thorough, and candid discussions with the core team about goals, strategies, progress, and changes — this is, in essence, strategic review.

"Without strategy, you won't eat tomorrow; with a bad strategy, you might starve today." — Liu Chuanzhi
💡 KEY TAKEAWAY
Business and strategic review is not a privilege reserved for large corporations — it is a foundational capability for any organization's continuous evolution. By embedding review into the "Plan → Execute → Review → Optimize" loop, led by the CEO, balancing internal and external perspectives, and facilitated with expertise, organizations can truly achieve "fast but not chaotic" and thrive in today's complex business landscape.
