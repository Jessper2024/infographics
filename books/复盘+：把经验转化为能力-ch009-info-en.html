SCQA · Situation
Retrospective Review Matters, but It Is Not Enough

Retrospective review can only learn from one's own past experience. In an era of frequent disruptive innovation, its source, scope, and depth cannot meet competitive challenges. By nature, retrospective review learns from past experience. Although trial-and-error learning is fundamental to human development, it carries three key limitations in a fast-changing era: the learning source is narrow (only one's own experience), the scope is limited (it cannot foresee entirely new approaches), and depth is constrained by the burden of past success. As the ancient Chinese philosopher Xunzi wrote, "From the near we know the far; from one we know ten thousand." Retrospective review can reveal patterns and spark innovation, but we must not work in isolation. We need to understand each method's strengths and limitations and choose the right tool at the right time.

⚠️

Three Limitations of Retrospective Review

Narrow source (only own experience), insufficient scope (cannot foresee disruptive approaches), constrained depth (heavier burden of past success lowers the odds of innovation)

💡

The Right Attitude

Understanding each method's strengths, weaknesses, and conditions. Choosing the right method when needed and executing it well reflects true managerial wisdom.

MECE · Methods
Three Learning Methods Beyond Retrospective Review

Theory U, scenario planning, and benchmarking each have unique learning mechanisms and applicable scenarios. They powerfully complement retrospective review.

🌊 Theory U — Learn from the Emerging Future

Proposed by MIT researcher Otto Scharmer, Theory U requires a "shift in the structure of attention." Practitioners stop downloading patterns, suspend judgment, and sense the field. The process moves through seven stages: suspending, redirecting, letting go, presencing, letting come, crystallizing, prototyping, and institutionalizing — enabling deep creation and transformation.

🗺️ Scenario Planning — See Clearly in Uncertainty

Shell used scenario planning to rise from last place to the top among the seven major oil companies during the 1973 oil crisis. Through systematic analysis of key uncertainties, organizations develop multiple future scenarios and map out strategic pathways. This helps leaders at all levels stay prepared and composed amid disruption.

🏆 Benchmarking & 🔓 Open Innovation

Xerox regained market share by benchmarking against Japanese manufacturers. By 2005, 80% of large U.S. companies adopted benchmarking. Procter & Gamble rebranded R&D as C&D (Connect + Develop). Over 50% of innovation now comes from outside. R&D capability improved by 60%, success rates doubled, and costs fell by 20%.

Four Core Elements of a Closed-Loop Learning System

Collective wisdom, collaborative execution, retrospective review, and a knowledge base form a closed loop. Together they drive organizations from retrospective review toward a learning organization. Based on case studies of the U.S. military, BP, and others, we can use retrospective review as one core element and combine it with three other elements to form a complete closed-loop system.

✦ Key Takeaway
Retrospective review is an indispensable part of the organizational learning system, but it alone is far from enough. Use the Organizational Learning Matrix as your framework. Integrate Theory U, scenario planning, benchmarking, and open innovation. Combine retrospective review with collective wisdom, collaborative execution, and knowledge management. Only then can you build a complete closed-loop learning system that drives sustained growth and propels your organization to become a true learning organization.
