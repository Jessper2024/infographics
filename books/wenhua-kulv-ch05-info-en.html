01
Tragic Guardian
Wang Yuanlu: Destruction by an Ignorant Custodian
Even the smallest person can cast a long shadow across the desert. Wang Yuanlu—an illiterate Daoist priest, custodian of Mogao—whitewashed exquisite murals with lime, smashed statues with iron hammers. His ignorance was itself a form of violence.
📖

Wang Yuanlu's Identity

Hubei farmer → soldier → Daoist priest → Mogao 'custodian'; raised funds through alms, hired local workmen to 'renovate' the caves

🔍

His Destructive Acts

Broke through cave walls into corridors, whitewashed thousand-year murals with lime, smashed statues with iron hammers, built crude clay altars

02
Historic Coincidence
The Day the Library Cave Opened, the Eight-Nation Alliance Decided to Attack
On June 22, 1900, Wang Yuanlu opened the Library Cave—on the same day, foreign powers in Beijing decided on a joint military intervention. Months earlier, oracle bones had been discovered. 'The Library Cave, like the oracle bones, must appear violently at the very moment that confidence is about to be lost.'

Three-Man Tragedy
The Transaction: Wang Yuanlu · Aurel Stein · Jiang Xiaowan
The Dunhuang calamity was not Wang Yuanlu's crime alone—Stein cloaked colonial arrogance in scholarly professionalism, while interpreter Jiang Xiaowan, who 'felt almost no moral anguish,' used the story of Xuanzang to trick open the cave door.

04
Cultural Plunder
Nine Thousand Scrolls and Five Hundred Paintings for Thirty Pounds
Stein acquired over 9,000 scrolls and 500 paintings for £30, packed into 29 large wooden crates. It was one of history's most unequal 'transactions'—'there, an ancient nation's wound was bleeding.'

05
Historical Judgment
Why Does Stein's Epitaph Omit Dunhuang?
Stein's tombstone inscription lists all his expeditions—India, Xinjiang, Persia, Iraq—but deliberately omits Dunhuang, the discovery that made him famous in the West. He died in Kabul in 1943, age 82. Wang Yuanlu's Daoist pagoda 'draws crowds daily, though none cast a respectful glance.'

🔑 Key Takeaway
Daily crowds visit the Daoist Pagoda, beneath which lies the wound of an ancient civilization. The Dunhuang tragedy was not one man's crime but a triple tragedy of an era: destruction by an ignorant custodian, plunder by a colonial scholar, and betrayal by a cultural comprador.
