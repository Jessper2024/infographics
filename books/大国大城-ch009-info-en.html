Great Country, Big City · Ch.2「Achieving Balance through Agglomeration」

Agglomeration · Efficiency · Balance · Free Migration · Factor Mobility · 3D Framework It is widely claimed that China's population is overly concentrated in coastal regions. But cross-country data tells a different story: China is not over-aggolomerated — it is under-agglomerated. Free migration is not just a right; it is a national development strategy.

This is Book Chapter 2. The author dismantles three popular "spatial misconceptions" using cross-country comparative data and fundamental economic logic. First, richer countries exhibit higher agglomeration; China's level is lower not only than developed nations but also than Brazil and South Africa. Second, agglomeration has natural limits — rising factor prices slow it but do not reverse it. Third, agglomeration does not widen regional gaps — "achieving balance through agglomeration" is the essence of regional economics. The chapter then examines the dual significance of free migration: protecting individual rights while narrowing urban-rural and regional gaps. The US-China comparison of GDP shares versus population shares reveals China's core problem: population agglomeration lags behind economic agglomeration. The chapter concludes with the World Development Report 2009's "3M" and "3D" frameworks.

01
Misconceptions
Three Widespread Spatial Misconceptions
Myth 1: China's population is over-concentrated in a few areas. Cross-country agglomeration indices show: the richer the country, the higher its agglomeration. China's index is well below the US, Japan, Europe — and even below Brazil and South Africa. China's secondary and tertiary industries account for 90% of GDP, yet policy thinking is stuck in an agricultural-era mindset. Myth 2: Unchecked, population will concentrate infinitely. No — the trade-off between benefits and costs (congestion, pollution, crime) sets natural limits. Tokyo's population continues to grow, but at a slower pace. Myth 3: Agglomeration widens regional gaps. Developed countries experienced an inverted-U pattern — gaps widen first, then narrow. The essence: "achieving balance through agglomeration."
02
Cross-Country
Agglomeration Across Countries: China Is Not "Over-Agglomerated"
Figure 2-1 plots per capita GDP against agglomeration index: developed countries cluster in the upper right (high agglomeration); underdeveloped countries in the lower left (low agglomeration). Why? Developed economies are dominated by industry and services — especially modern services that rely on knowledge, information, and technology, which thrive in dense urban environments. In the US, Canada, Japan, and Europe, population continues concentrating in major metro areas. By contrast, less developed countries have larger agricultural sectors, which are naturally dispersed. China sits in the lower left — not over-agglomerated at all.

Dynamics
Agglomeration Accelerates Then Stabilizes: The Price Adjustment Mechanism
Agglomeration does not continue indefinitely. Figure 2-2 shows: agglomeration rises with per capita GDP, then stabilizes at a certain stage. Rising factor prices (land, housing, wages) in the core area push some producers and residents out. But this does not mean agglomeration declines — it stabilizes at a high level. Japan's total population is shrinking, yet Tokyo's population continues to grow. That is the power of agglomeration. Factor prices act as the market's "brake," preventing unlimited inflows. The final level of agglomeration reflects the equilibrium between benefits and costs.

04
Balance
"Balance through Agglomeration": Balance in Per Capita Terms
The essence of regional economics is captured in one phrase: "achieving balance through agglomeration." People confuse two concepts: total quantity balance vs. per capita balance. Making every city the size of Shanghai violates economic laws. What matters is minimizing regional differences in real income and quality of life. The World Bank's conclusion: every country that achieved balanced regional development also has free labor mobility. No country that pursued regional balance through administrative resource allocation has succeeded. Factor mobility equalizes factor returns — a fundamental economic principle.
05
Free Migration
Free Migration: Individual Rights and National Strategy
Free migration serves a dual purpose. First, it protects individual rights — people should be able to work where they earn more. The "Zhuoma and Matsutake" story illustrates this: without reform, too many people picking mushrooms means no one's income improves. Second, it narrows urban-rural and regional income gaps. In the long run, population stability arrives only when real income gaps approach zero. Free migration enables regional specialization: some areas make cars, others develop tourism, others pick mushrooms. Economic interdependence between regions is the most effective way to maintain national unity and market integration. The "voting with one's feet" mechanism also pressures local governments to improve public services.
06
US vs China
US: GDP Share = Population Share vs. China's Divergence
Figures 2-4 and 2-5 provide the most compelling contrast. In the US, each state's GDP share and population share are highly aligned — per capita GDP is nearly identical across states. Some states concentrate population for industry and services; others are sparsely populated with large-scale farms. American farmers are wealthy precisely because there are few of them. In China, by contrast, provincial GDP shares are more uniform, but population shares lag far behind. China's problem is not that the economy is too concentrated, but that population agglomeration cannot keep pace with economic agglomeration.

07
3M Framework
The Costs of Urban Development: "3M" — Crowding, Pollution, Crime
Urban development has both economies of scale and three costs. The World Development Report 2009 uses "3M" to frame urban challenges: Time (congestion), Grime (pollution), Crime (safety). Congestion raises commuting costs; pollution harms health; crime reduces quality of life. The correct policy: reduce "3M" through technology and governance. Subways, surveillance cameras, and better management can mitigate urban problems without sacrificing the gains from agglomeration. Cities should not abandon agglomeration benefits because of "3M" — they should work to lower these costs and unleash the power of scale economies.

3D Framework
The Power of Space: Density, Distance, Division
The 3D framework is the core tool for understanding geography's impact on development. First, Density — agglomeration generates economies of scale through three effects: sharing (shared infrastructure), matching (specialized labor), and learning (knowledge spillovers). Second, Distance — distance creates trade costs; infrastructure investment can "shorten" distance. Third, Division — the most powerful dimension because it reflects politics. Between nations, political barriers prevent free migration; free trade is a substitute. China's internal provincial divisions severely undermine its advantages as a large country. Fundamental conclusion: geography matters for economic development, and administrative forces cannot reverse geographic laws.

09
Geography
"Moving People" before "Moving Money": Development Policy Choices
Regional balance can be pursued through two paths: "moving people" (labor to capital) vs. "moving money" (capital to labor). China's Western Development and industry relocation policies are "moving money" — massive land quotas and fiscal transfers to central/western regions. But the author argues: if geography matters, "moving people" must take priority. Entrepreneurs seek profit — why would they choose China's interior over Southeast Asia's deep-water ports? Many inland industrial parks lack scale to attract businesses, resulting in high investment, low output, and heavy debt burdens. Nationwide, new-city plans total a combined population of 3.4 billion. Correct policy: prioritize "moving people," with fiscal transfers built on that foundation.
10
Free Trade
From Domestic to International: Trade as a Substitute for Migration
Free migration is impossible between nations for political reasons — developed countries welcome only high-skilled labor. Developing nations must pursue free trade. By leveraging comparative advantage in cheap labor, they produce competitive goods for developed markets. China is a clear beneficiary of the global free trade system — its post-WTO growth proves it. Earning the low-end share of the "smiling curve" is not wrong; it is what China can do at this stage. Urbanization reallocates resources across regions, improving economic efficiency. China must guard against local interests overriding the national interest — market segmentation and blocked migration hurt everyone. "The world is not flat" — respecting spatial laws is the path to effective great-power governance.

🔑 Core Conclusion
China does not have a "population over-agglomeration" problem — by any international comparison, its agglomeration level is on the low side. "Over-agglomeration" is a popular but erroneous spatial misconception. People naturally move toward higher-income areas, but not infinitely — rising factor prices create a natural braking mechanism. Crucially, "achieving balance through agglomeration" is possible: economic concentration and per capita income convergence can happen simultaneously. Free migration is not just an individual right but a national development strategy. China's core problem is that population agglomeration severely lags behind economic agglomeration. Policy should prioritize "moving people" (free labor mobility), with fiscal transfers only as a supplement. Respecting spatial laws is the only viable path for a great power seeking unity, efficiency, and balance.
