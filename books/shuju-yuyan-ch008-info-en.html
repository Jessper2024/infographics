Use tangible numbers to deconstruct a complex business world, and gain true freedom through clarity.

Freedom I · Right ≠ Persist
Right Doesn’t Mean Must-Persist: Relative Choice Over Absolute Correctness
There is no absolutely correct in this world. “Right” must be discussed within a measurement system to be meaningful. A factory has 100K units monthly capacity. Product A contributes +20%, Product B +15%—both profitable. Now a client offers Product C at +30%, but it consumes half the capacity—would you persist with A and B just because they’re profitable? Pure economic modeling compares relative choices, not sunk historical investment.

02
Case · Welch’s Resolve
Jack Welch: The Courage to “Sell It Even Though It’s Profitable”
Jack Welch’s most distinctive move as GE CEO: selling “business units that are very profitable but not top-3 in their industry.” If such a simple rule were easy to execute, there wouldn’t be just one Jack Welch in decades. It’s so hard because most managers can’t escape the “sell it even though it’s profitable” mental cage—historical investment and vested interest prevent the clear decision an outsider would instantly see.
“Sell business units that are very profitable but not top-3 in their industry.”
— Jack Welch, Former GE CEO
🔒

Ordinary: Locked by “Profitable”

Profitable but not top-3 → decisively sell → free resources → focus on champions → sustained excellence

03
Freedom II · Wrong ≠ Correct
Wrong Doesn’t Mean Must-Correct: When Correction Costs Exceed the Error
There is no absolute right or wrong in finance. When the cost of correcting an error exceeds the cost of the error itself, such errors aren’t worth correcting—otherwise you’re a professional perfectionist. February’s depreciation cost isn’t treated differently from 31-day months—why? Because designing such a correction system would cost more. Many financial numbers are approximate estimates, and often we just look the other way.
“How much is this?” — “Two to three thousand a month.” — “Let’s stop here. The salary cost of a dozen executives discussing this for half an hour already far exceeds that amount.”
— From a real discussion about departing employees’ meal card balances
04
System · The 90% Rule
90% Is a Good System: Low-Cost Small-Probability Errors Should Be Let Go
A system that solves 90%+ of practical problems is basically a good system. Unless you’re building airplanes, low-cost low-probability errors—let them go. It’s part of life. The departing employee meal card shortfall is ¥2-3K per month; a dozen executives discussing it for half an hour already exceeds that in salary cost. Some suggested splitting payments in two, others proposed developing alert software—but a simple cost-benefit analysis stopped all proposals in their tracks.
💸

Error Cost

Departing employee meal card shortfall: ¥2,000-3,000/month. Small amount, low frequency, limited impact—classic low-cost small-probability error.

🧠

Correction Cost

Executives discussing for half an hour: salary cost far exceeds ¥3,000. Not to mention system and workflow costs of software development or split payments.

05
Freedom III · Don’t Worship Numbers
Don’t Worship Numbers: The Philosophical Problem in DCF Models
Anyone with basic knowledge of financial reporting knows: financial numbers are the result of various assumptions and approximate estimates. A single percentage-point assumption change can produce completely reversed conclusions. In DCF models, assuming a perpetual growth rate of 3% vs. 5% can produce a 10× valuation gap—below bid price? Don’t invest. Above? Invest. Such analysis is less valuable than dining with the founder or visiting key clients. Which company has X% perpetual compound growth? This is no longer a financial question—it’s a philosophical question only God can answer.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The ultimate purpose of studying finance is not to trap yourself in a fortress of numbers, but to gain cognitive freedom through deep understanding. Three dimensions unlock it: right need not persist—replace absolute correctness with relative choice, like Welch selling profitable but non-top-3 units; wrong need not correct—when correction cost exceeds the error, 90% perfection is a good system; don’t worship numbers—recognize that every financial conclusion rests on a pile of variable assumptions, and learn to find judgment anchors beyond the numbers. Use tangible numbers to deconstruct a complex business world, but never be imprisoned by them.
