Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 20
"The Process of Distributing Large Blocks of Stock"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Livermore systematically traces the history of market manipulation: from old-school wash sales and matched orders to modern corners. Tactics evolve with regulations, but human greed and fear remain the manipulator's ultimate weapon. As Woodlock said: "In the future, people will continue to repeat the mistakes they have made in the past."

Manipulation is a common stock-market merchandising process. Old-school wash sales, matched orders, and corners each had their own techniques and ecosystems — but they are now obsolete or illegal. Exchange rules have been transformed and tax laws have made fictitious transactions prohibitively expensive. But the human nature that makes "suckers always look for a free lunch" has never changed. Livermore's core insight: abandon the fantasy of replicating specific techniques and instead study unchanging human nature — greed, fear, and credulity — the weaknesses manipulators will always exploit. Studying human nature is ten thousand times more valuable than studying techniques.
01
Historical Review
The Old-School Trinity: Wash Sales, Matched Orders & Corners
Livermore systematically dissects three classic manipulation techniques now obsolete. Wash Sales — buying and selling to oneself to create the illusion of active trading, crude and easily detected. Matched Orders — same essence, now prohibitively expensive due to tax-code revisions. Corners — the Northern Pacific corner of 1901 is the most famous in history, but Livermore notes it wasn't intentional manipulation, just competitive buying run amok. "The originator rarely profits from a corner."

02
Core Principle
Unchanging Human Nature: The Manipulator's Eternal Weapon
Livermore uses a West Point analogy: today's operators need not study what the ancients did, just as cadets need not study ancient archery to improve their ballistics. Weapons change, but the principles of war do not. Humans seek comfort and avoid difficulty, preferring to believe what makes them feel at ease. In prosperous times the public rushes in — "at such times no attention whatsoever is paid to details" — and ultimately must pay the price for the privilege of pursuing easy money.

03
Practical Takeaway
Study Human Nature, Not Techniques: The Modern Trader's Defense
Livermore offers three core insights. Distinguish means from principles — specific techniques belong to their era, but principles never expire. Recognize today's "sucker traps" — when an investment opportunity makes you feel particularly comfortable or that "no thinking is required," ask yourself: is this real, or is it a comfort zone custom-tailored for me? Prosperity periods are the most dangerous — the public enters at the height of a boom, which is precisely when the greatest losses occur.

04
Timeless Quote
Woodlock's Law: The Future Is the Past Repeated
Livermore quotes Thomas F. Woodlock's immortal words: "The principles that guide success in stock speculation are based on the assumption that in the future, people will continue to repeat the mistakes they have made in the past." This isn't empty aphorism — it's the cornerstone of the entire speculative philosophy. When Livermore says "manipulation techniques may become obsolete, but human nature never does," he's merely providing a battlefield annotation of Woodlock's Law.

05
Ultimate Warning
The Privilege of Easy Money Simply Does Not Exist
Livermore's ultimate warning remains the most precise prophecy in the financial world: "People who seek easy money will ultimately have to pay the price for pursuing this privilege — countless facts decisively prove that such a privilege simply cannot be found in this noisy world." From 1923 to today, from Ponzi schemes to crypto scams, from bucket shops to social-media "signal calls" — the packaging changes, but human nature follows the same script.
