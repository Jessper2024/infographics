Tips are "the alcohol of hope"—they let you substitute someone else's judgment for your own thinking. Only by forging independent thought into an iron rule can you become immune to all tips. In this chapter, Livermore devotes an entire chapter to dissecting the mechanism and fatal allure of tips—through the Borneo Tin honey trap, the psychology of tip addiction, the reverse-tip "bear raid" myth, and four classic absurd tales.
01
Case
Borneo Tin: The Most Carefully Crafted Tip Trap
At a Palm Beach dinner party, Borneo Tin chairman Wisenstein deliberately sat next to Mrs. Livingston and whispered a "top secret" tip in her ear: buy Borneo Tin with everything you've got. Mrs. Livingston used the $500 pocket money her husband had just given her to buy 100 shares, which rose 3 points that day. However, Livermore was bearish on the broad market, and noticing the stock's unusual activity, decided to start his short-selling campaign with this very stock—and made a killing instead. Wisenstein made two mistakes: he miscalculated Mrs. Livingston's reluctance to tell her husband, and he mistakenly assumed Livermore was the kind of trader who listened to tips.
🎭

Wisenstein's Meticulous Plan

Using the wife to deliver a "top secret" tip, hoping Livermore would follow and buy, paving the way for insider distribution.

🃏

Livermore's Counter-Harvest

Noticed abnormal activity and shorted the stock instead, turning the tip trap into a contrarian profit opportunity.

02
Psychology
The Psychology of Tip Addiction: Wishful Thinking Syndrome
The root of tip-chasing isn't a lack of intelligence—it's laziness. Having someone tell you what to buy is far easier and more pleasant than independent research. Tip-seekers are "drunks on hope," suffering from wishful-thinking syndrome—it's less about greed blinding their eyes than about laziness breeding wishful thinking. As long as someone tells you what to do, you feel happy. Both givers and receivers of tips are relay runners in an endless chain—those who distribute tips don't care about credibility because those who seek tips never demand quality.

03
Variation
The Reverse Tip: The Myth of "Bear Raids"
A normal tip tells you what to buy or sell; the reverse tip explains a decline that's already happened. When a stock suddenly drops, brokers and the media tell you "someone is raiding it"—which spares you from having to sell at a loss or admit you were wrong. Livermore exposed this: of 100 alleged "bear raids," 99 are simply the acceleration phase of a legitimate decline. If a raid truly drove a stock below its value, insiders would immediately buy hand over fist—because it would be the deal of a lifetime.

04

Independent Thinking vs. Tip-Following: Worlds Apart
The fork in the road for two investors: a Pennsylvania German investor noticed the chairman of Atchison Railroad casually jotted numbers on fine stationery and threw it in the wastebasket—he immediately sold the stock and switched to Delaware Railroad, whose chairman tore up old envelopes for note paper. The latter stock doubled year after year. Meanwhile, J.T. Hood (an NYSE member) advised a friend not to send an angry telegram to an insider whose "tip had failed"—"If you send that telegram, you'll never get another tip from him again!"

🔑 Key Takeaway
Tips are the cheapest and most expensive narcotic in the trading world. They let you temporarily escape thinking—but permanently forfeit the ability to think for yourself. The Rothschild secret to success applies to every trader: "I never buy at the bottom, and I always sell too soon." The deeper meaning: only the judgment you make yourself is profit that truly belongs to you.
