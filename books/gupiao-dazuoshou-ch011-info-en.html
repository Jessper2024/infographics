"The Path of Least Resistance"

How to identify and follow the market's path of least resistance? Prices, like water, always follow the path of least resistance. Livermore used the breakout of a sideways range to define the path of least resistance: when price breaks above resistance, the upside path is easier; when below support, the downside path is easier. His system rests on patiently waiting for the market to make its own choice — never predicting, never anticipating, never forcing the tape to conform to his will. Pat Hearne's system was a simple embodiment: add 1% on each rise, stop out on each 1% decline. Core thesis: The speculator must submit entirely to the market to profit; never stubbornly demand that the tape submit to him.

01
Core Principle
The Path of Least Resistance: A Simple Truth as Old as Water
When the market oscillates sideways in a 10-point range (120–130), don't be fooled by appearances. At the bottom it looks weak; after rising 8–10 points it looks strong — but neither is a reason to enter. The trader must wait patiently for the market to break its limits: at 130 where buying first exceeds selling, or at 120 where selling first exceeds buying. Traders on the wrong side are forced to cover, which precisely pushes prices further along the path of least resistance. In a narrow market with no clear direction, attempting to anticipate the next big move is pointless — don't touch it until the market breaks its limits.
📈

Upside Breakout = Buy

When price breaks above 130, buying first exceeds selling. Shorts forced to cover push prices further along the path of least resistance.

📉

Downside Breakout = Sell

When price breaks below 120, selling first exceeds buying. Longs forced to stop out further drive prices along the path of least resistance.

02
Case Study
Wheat at $1.20: A Perfect Breakout Case
"If you want to make money out of wheat, just watch it. Wait until it crosses $1.20, then buy it — and you'll get a nice quick play." Livermore had watched wheat for months as it oscillated between $1.10 and $1.20. One day it closed above $1.19. Next day it opened at $1.20½ — he bought immediately. Price rose to $1.21, $1.22, $1.23, $1.25. Europeans had been quietly accumulating; large quantities had been taken out of the market. Breaking $1.20 meant upward force had finally gathered enough power to push through the ceiling — the breakout itself was the signal, needing no additional interpretation.

03
Inner Enemy
Fear and Hope: The Speculator's Greatest Resistance Isn't the Market
"A man may beat a stock or a group at a certain time, but no living man can beat the stock market!" Livermore points out: the trader's greatest resistance is not the market but himself — fear and hope, twin brothers, always dominate decisions at precisely the wrong moments. The successful trader must go against human nature: hope when one should fear, fear when one should hope.

Risk Discipline
The Risk Dimension: Breakout Signal Reliability
Not all breakouts deserve to be followed. Livermore identified different levels of breakout signals, ranked by reliability. The most reliable: the first breakout after a prolonged tight consolidation, confirmed by volume. And his key rule: trying to predict direction within a sideways range is pointless — you must wait for the market's own choice.

05
Trading System
Pat Hearne's Simple System: You Don't Know Till You Bet
"You don't know till you bet" — Pat Hearne's trading creed. His system was staggeringly simple yet powerful: buy 100 shares, if it rises 1%, buy another 100; rise another 1%, another 100. Set a stop 1 point below the last purchase price. A 1% decline triggers the stop and he exits. He didn't chase inside tips, didn't expect 20-point moves in a week — he just earned "sure money." A follower made $100,000 on Lackawanna using this system, then lost it all after abandoning it — perfectly proving Livermore's core insight: the system matters more than judgment.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The path of least resistance is the cornerstone of Livermore's trading philosophy: don't predict, don't anticipate, don't gamble — wait for the market to speak for itself. The breakout is the language; price action is the answer. The deeper insight: the trader's greatest resistance is not the market but himself — fear and hope, the twin brothers, always dominate decisions at precisely the wrong moments. The successful trader must go against human nature: hope when one should fear, fear when one should hope. This internal discipline, more than any external rule, determines success or failure. Pat Hearne's simple system proves a truth: simple rules, strictly followed, outweigh mountains of complex analysis.
