Su Shi: A Life · Ch.1
"A Young Eagle Soars Above the Age"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer In 1056, the 21-year-old Su Shi braved the treacherous Shu roads bound for Kaifeng — unaware this journey would launch him like a young eagle into the literary firmament. How did an unknown youth from Shu stun the literary world on his first attempt? The answer lies in the perfect convergence of Su Xun's classical training, Ouyang Xiu's reform movement, and a landmark exam essay.

Three forces converged perfectly: Su Xun's rigorous training in classical prose aligned exactly with Ouyang Xiu's literary reform movement; the essay "On the Utmost Loyalty in Punishment and Reward" demonstrated both political wisdom and literary brilliance; and Ouyang Xiu's discerning eye and wholehearted mentorship — declaring he must "make way for this man" — propelled young Su Shi to instant renown. Emperor Renzong himself, after the Palace Examination, joyfully told the Empress: "Today I have found two Grand Councilors of peace for my descendants!"

01
The Journey
Treacherous Roads Could Not Dim Their Ambition
The forbidding Shu roads only strengthened the Su brothers' resolve — this was no exile, but a heroic expedition full of hope. Father and sons followed the ancient plank road through Baoxie Valley, crossed the Qinling Mountains into Guanzhong. Arriving in Kaifeng in May, they threw themselves into exam preparation. By August, both brothers passed the Kaifeng prefectural exam.

02
The Exam Essay
"On Loyalty in Punishment and Reward" — An Exam Paper Became a Classic
In just over 600 characters, Su Shi distilled his lifelong philosophy of benevolent governance — leaving chief examiner Ouyang Xiu "astonished and delighted." Core argument: when you may reward or may not — reward; when you may punish or may not — do not punish. Excessive reward still leaves you a gentleman; excessive punishment makes you cruel.

03
Literary Reform
Perfect Timing: Classical Roots Meet the Reform Movement
Su Shi's triumph was no accident — his training direction aligned perfectly with Ouyang Xiu's campaign against literary decadence. The literary scene was plagued by empty, convoluted prose. Ouyang resolved to use the exams to drive reform. Su Shi, trained from childhood in pre-Qin/Han classical prose — "untouched by contemporary vogues, this exam could not have come at a better time."

04
Mentorship
Ouyang Xiu's Endorsement and the Emperor's Prophecy
Ouyang Xiu's wholehearted patronage was the most direct catalyst for Su Shi's fame — a single word from such a towering figure could make or break a young scholar's career. He publicly praised Su Shi and personally introduced him to Grand Councilors Wen Yanbo and Fu Bi, and Military Commissioner Han Qi — all received the young man as an honored guest, treating him as a talent to serve the state.

05
Turning Point
Mourning and the Second Journey — Pressing Forward Through Grief
Just as Su Shi's star was rising, his mother Lady Cheng passed. Father and sons rushed home to find the estate in ruins. Su Shi observed three years of mourning, then departed again by boat through the Three Gorges — "My homeland drifts far behind, my aspirations boundless ahead." At Qu Yuan Pagoda, he wrote of following the ancient martyr's fierce resolve. No longer a naive youth, he had fused "virtue, deeds, and lasting words" into a unified life purpose.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Su Shi's overnight fame was no accident. Su Xun's classical training laid the scholarly foundation, Ouyang Xiu's literary reform provided the historical stage, and the essay "On the Utmost Loyalty in Punishment and Reward" captured the entire literary establishment at once. More importantly, even through losing his mother, he set his course by the Confucian ideal of "virtue, deeds, and words." This young eagle had left the nest — destined to soar across ever-wider skies.
