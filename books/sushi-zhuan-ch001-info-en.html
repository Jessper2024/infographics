Su Shi: A Life · Ch.1 — A Young Eagle Soars Above the Age

In the third month of 1056, the 21-year-old Su Shi bid farewell to his homeland, braving the treacherous Shu roads bound for the capital Kaifeng — unaware that this journey would launch him like a young eagle into the literary firmament.

▎Core Question
How did an unknown young man from Shu stun the literary world and achieve nation-wide fame in his very first attempt at the imperial examinations?
Three forces converged perfectly: Su Xun's rigorous training in classical prose aligned exactly with Ouyang Xiu's literary reform movement; the essay "On the Utmost Loyalty in Punishment and Reward" demonstrated both political wisdom and literary brilliance; and Ouyang Xiu's discerning eye and wholehearted mentorship — declaring he must "make way for this man" — propelled young Su Shi to instant renown.
01
The Journey

Treacherous Roads Could Not Dim Their Ambition

The forbidding Shu roads only strengthened the Su brothers' resolve — this was no exile, but a heroic expedition full of hope.

Father and sons followed the ancient plank road through Baoxie Valley, crossed the Qinling Mountains into Guanzhong. "The road to Shu is harder than climbing to heaven," Li Bai had lamented — yet the brothers pressed on with unfailing spirits, touring historic sites along the way. They arrived in Kaifeng in May and immediately threw themselves into exam preparation.

🦅
August breakthrough: Both Su Shi and Su Zhe passed the Kaifeng prefectural exam. Under Song regulations, they still faced the Ministry of Rites exam and the Imperial Palace Examination. Neither dared relax, continuing their intensive study behind closed doors.
02
The Exam Essay

"On Loyalty in Punishment and Reward" — An Exam Paper Became a Classic

In just over 600 characters, Su Shi distilled his lifelong philosophy of benevolent governance — leaving chief examiner Ouyang Xiu "astonished and delighted."

After three drafts, Su Shi argued: "When you may reward or may not — reward, for excessive reward still leaves you a gentleman. When you may punish or may not — do not punish, for excessive punishment makes you cruel." The essay advocated that governance should always lean toward mercy, guided by "deep love and sincere concern for the people."

Ouyang Xiu wanted to rank it first but — thinking such brilliance could only come from his own disciple Zeng Gong — placed it second to avoid suspicion. Upon learning the true author, he exclaimed to Mei Yaochen: "Reading Su Shi's writing, I broke into a sweat. Wonderful! Wonderful! I, an old man, must make way for this man."

03
Literary Reform

Perfect Timing: Classical Roots Meet the Reform Movement

Su Shi's triumph was no accident — his training direction aligned perfectly with Ouyang Xiu's campaign against literary decadence.

The literary scene then was plagued by empty, convoluted prose. Ouyang Xiu had resolved to use the exams to drive reform, instructing examiners to reject all "bizarre, obscure, hollow, and ornate" papers. Su Shi, trained from childhood in pre-Qin and Han classical prose and the works of Han Yu and Liu Zongyuan — emphasizing substance and sincerity — was perfectly positioned. As the biography notes: "untouched by contemporary vogues, this examination could not have come at a better time."

✅ Ouyang's Standard

Substantive, lucid, and natural. Opposed "frivolous ornament" and "incomprehensible eccentricity." Sought to restore pre-Qin and Han plainness.

❌ Prevalent Decadence

Empty content, artificial posturing, bizarre obscurity. "Striving for depth led to pedantry; pursuing novelty produced the unreadable."

In his thank-you letter to Ouyang Xiu, Su Shi gave a masterful overview of Song literary history. Ouyang declared after reading it: "In thirty more years, no one will speak of me anymore!" — prophesying that the literary future belonged to Su Shi.

04
Mentorship

Ouyang Xiu's Endorsement and the Emperor's Prophecy

Ouyang Xiu's wholehearted patronage was the most direct catalyst for Su Shi's fame — a single word of praise from such a towering figure could make or break a young scholar's entire career.

Ouyang not only publicly praised Su Shi as a man who "reads well and uses what he reads well — his writing will one day stand alone under heaven," but also personally introduced him to the Grand Councilors Wen Yanbo and Fu Bi, and the Military Affairs Commissioner Han Qi. These were figures Su Shi had revered since childhood — now they received him as an honored guest, treating him as a talent to serve the state.

When Ouyang Xiu asked about the source of an obscure allusion in Su Shi's essay, Su Shi calmly replied: "I reasoned by analogy — just as Kong Rong did with the Duke of Zhou and Daji. I thought it plausible, and so wrote it." Ouyang Xiu struck the table in admiration.

05
Turning Point

Mourning and the Second Journey — Pressing Forward Through Grief

Just as Su Shi's star was rising, news arrived that his mother Lady Cheng had passed — the violent swing from triumph to tragedy marked Su Shi's first great life transition.

Father and sons rushed home day and night, finding the family estate in ruins — "houses collapsed, fences broken, like a fugitive's dwelling." Su Shi observed the three-year mourning period at home. In autumn 1059, the family set out again, this time by boat through the Three Gorges. He wrote: "My homeland drifts far behind, my aspirations boundless ahead" — no longer a naive youth, but a matured scholar who had fused "virtue, achievement, and lasting words" into a unified life purpose.

At the Qu Yuan Pagoda in Zhongzhou, Su Shi wrote: "Qu Yuan, that ancient hero, went to his death with fierce resolve" — channeling the ancient martyr's spirit to affirm his own determination never to compromise his ideals.

💡
Key Takeaway

Su Shi's overnight fame was no accident. Su Xun's classical training laid the scholarly foundation, Ouyang Xiu's literary reform provided the historical stage, and the essay "On the Utmost Loyalty in Punishment and Reward" — with its political wisdom and literary brilliance — captured the entire literary establishment at once. More importantly, even through the pain of losing his mother, he set his course by the Confucian ideal of "virtue, deeds, and words." This young eagle had left the nest — destined to soar across ever-wider skies.
