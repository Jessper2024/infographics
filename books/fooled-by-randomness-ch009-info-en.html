Fooled by Randomness · Chapter 9
「It Is Easier to Buy and Sell Than Fry an Egg」

Track records, survivorship bias, and randomness scams — why are “star managers” probably just lucky?

This chapter reveals a profound paradox: buying and selling securities seems easier than frying an egg — anyone can open an account and boast about a period of “profitable track records.” But Taleb uses Monte Carlo simulations, survivorship bias, and data-mining traps to demonstrate that these “track records” are most likely pure luck. From fund managers to medical research, from book blurbs to cancer cures, randomness wears the mask of “skill,” fooling everyone who refuses to look deeper.

01
Core Paradox
Buying Securities ≠ Frying an Egg: Two Fundamentally Different Capabilities
A dentist fixes your toothache, a pianist performs at Carnegie Hall, Canova brings marble to life — in these fields, success can hardly be luck. But in financial markets, when a fund manager shows you “brilliant performance,” it is very likely a product of random fluctuation. Machiavelli believed luck accounts for at least 50% of life outcomes — and that was before modern markets even existed.
🧑‍⚕️

Real-Skill Domains

Dentists, pianists, sculptors — require repeated verification to “luckily succeed.” Pure luck probability is extremely low. Skill is observable and replicable.

📈

Financial Market Domain

Thousands participate in trading — random fluctuations naturally produce “stars.” A profit record alone provides almost no useful information.

02
Monte Carlo Proof
The Fate of 10,000 Managers: Making “Stars” Out of Pure Luck
Set up 10,000 investment managers, each with a 50% chance of making $10,000 and a 50% chance of losing $10,000 per year. Eliminated after any losing year. After five years, purely by luck, 313 still have a perfect winning streak — they will be hailed as “stars,” wear designer suits, and receive impressive titles. More astonishing: even if all managers have negative expected returns (45% win / 55% lose), 184 still become “stars” after 5 years.

Survivorship Bias
The Forgotten 98%: Why You Only See the Winners
People always see the best-performing managers, not the entire population. The essence of survivorship bias: the number of successful people depends primarily on how many entered the field, not on their true ability. Taleb’s rule of thumb: if the original group has only 10 managers, he’d give half his savings to the top performer; if 10,000, he’d disregard everyone’s track record entirely.

Data-Mining Trap
Mystery Letters, the Birthday Paradox, and Overfitting
From scammers using “mystery letters” (send half bullish and half bearish predictions to 10,000 people, eliminate half who get the wrong prediction each month, those remaining think they found a prophet), to the birthday paradox (in a room of 23, the probability of two sharing a birthday is ~50%), Taleb shows how human cognition systematically misinterprets randomness. Data miners test trading rules on historical prices endlessly — like monkeys at typewriters — random sequences will always show some detectable pattern.

05
Real-World Fallacies
From Book Reviews to Cancer: Randomness Fools Every Field
Survivorship bias is everywhere. Book cover blurbs always pick only the best quote; backtesting software keeps traders up all night searching for the “perfect strategy”; cancer “spontaneous remission” is attributed to vitamin C or holy water. As Sherlock Holmes noted in Silver Blaze: the curious thing is that the dog did not bark — “finding nothing” and “not looking” are conflated, when the former may contain crucial information.

🔑 Core Takeaway
It is easier to buy and sell securities than to fry an egg — but the reverse is true for evaluating performance. With thousands of participants trading, randomness naturally produces “stars.” A profit record alone provides almost no useful information — without knowing how many people tried and failed, no track record can be evaluated. In a large enough crowd, luck is the most reliable “skill.”
