In 1352, Zhu Yuanzhang (Zhu Chongba) arrived alone at besieged Bozhou to join the rebellion. His unusual appearance drew suspicion—he was nearly executed as a spy—until commander Guo Zixing saw composure in his eyes and made him a personal guard. Within two months, Zhu rose to squad leader of nine, distinguished by courage and impartiality. He married Guo's adopted daughter, consolidating his position, yet Guo grew jealous and imprisoned him; his son attempted to starve him, but Lady Ma smuggled hot flatbread in her bosom at great personal injury. After this, Zhu resolved to leave, secured independent command, conquered Dingyuan by night assault, won four battles in succession, recruited 24 future Ming elites including Xu Da, and finally departed Bozhou with his core band—truly starting his imperial path.

Arrival at Bozhou: From "Spy" to Guard
A strange-looking man changed his fate with a single calm answer. In 1352, besieged Bozhou city saw Zhu Chongba arrive alone. Soldiers seized him as a spy. Commander Guo Zixing looked into his eyes expecting fear—found only calm. Intrigued, he questioned Zhu, learned of Tang He's referral, and made him a personal guard on the spot.

Talent & Identity
Rise and Renaming: "Destroy-Yuan Jade"
Courageous, strategic, fair: he outshone all others in two months. Zhu could read and write, analyzed problems with precision, never sought personal gain—splitting all rewards with soldiers. Elevated to squad leader in two months. Then he renamed himself: "Zhang" is a sharp jade—"Zhu Yuanzhang" meant the jade weapon to destroy Yuan, forged by Yuan's own hands.

03
Internal Strife
Factional Vortex: Guo Zixing's Internal Crisis
Complex leadership composition sowed seeds of conflict from within. Guo Zixing was a landlord; his four co-commanders were all peasants—structural class war within the rebel camp. Guo was kidnapped and beaten by Sun Deyay's men; Zhu risked his life to rescue him, only to be seized himself. Guo's son then deliberately starved Zhu; Lady Ma smuggled him burning-hot flatbread at the cost of repeated chest burns.

04
Military Rise
Conquest of Dingyuan: Four Victories
Guo sent Zhu to Dingyuan expecting him to die. Zhu had other plans. With only 4,000 men, Zhu launched a night assault against Miao Daheng's 20,000-strong garrison—victory through surprise. He then captured Huaixian, Anfeng, Hanshan, and Hongxian in rapid succession: four battles, four victories, irresistible momentum. At his hometown Zhongli, he recruited 24 future Ming elites including Xu Da and Tang He.

05
Independent Campaign
The 24 Lieutenants: Start Here
From 700 men, Zhu selected only 24 to accompany him—a 30-to-1 ratio with no written test. The rest he gave to Guo Zixing, who accepted them gladly. All 24—Xu Da, Tang He, Zhou Dexing—became senior Ming officials. Standing at Bozhou's gates, Zhu knew: one step forward meant either death in battle or founding an empire. "When others bloom, I do not; when I bloom, all are shocked"—from here, he began his solitary march to greatness.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Zhu's rise exposed Guo Zixing's factional crisis and internal betrayal. With extraordinary talent he survived, but his true turning point was leaving the old organization. Picking 24 core lieutenants with perfect judgment—all future senior officials—he launched independently at age 26. Character and vision determine the height you reach—even from rock bottom, you can rise.
