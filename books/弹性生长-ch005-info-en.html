「Severely Involutionary Traditional Industries—Where’s the Way Out?」

The Real Struggles of Civil Engineering and Possible Escapes

📖 Chapter Overview

Through short videos of real construction-site life and in-depth interviews with workers, the author reveals civil engineering’s shocking involution: stagnant wages for a decade, narrowing advancement paths, environments so harsh they cause nightmares. From 100 to 500+ schools offering civil engineering, supply explosion drives price wars below break-even. The way out? ‘When young people stop going to construction sites, the industry will have hope.’

🔍 Deep Dive
The Truth Behind ‘Postgrad Refueling Station’
Construction-site videos are called ‘postgrad refueling stations’—civil engineering students watching real site life instantly feel energized to study. Civil service hopefuls call it ‘civil service gas station,’ coders call it ‘coder gas station.’ ‘Grab-a-bucket-and-run’ is the top phrase. Shocking truth: the internet accounts are mostly real.
All-Round Hardship: Nightmares to Shack Cats
Some dream their railway bridge collapsed, remembering their prison uniform number. Dust in sun, mud in rain; 40°C air above, 10°C concrete below. Cafeteria food so bad, chefs are suspected cooking-school dropouts. A cost engineer got hit by a brick a cat knocked down—four stitches. The whole site laughed: ‘Who showers without a hard hat?’
The ‘Three-Year Chief, Five-Year Manager’ Myth
In the big infrastructure era, ‘chief engineer in 3, project manager in 5’ was possible. Now projects are increasingly remote, overseas assignments mean 2-3 years between home visits. Oversupply: civil engineering schools grew from 100 to 500+. Companies find it easy to hire grads but hard to hire anyone else.
Price Wars Below Break-Even
Too many qualified companies, bidding below profit just to keep people busy. Must front costs—clients don’t pay upfront. Collecting payment means ‘hoarse voices, smashed tables, ruined livers and stomachs, then deductions on the final payment.’ Annual checkups become competitions for most abnormal results among leaders.
The Way Out: Scarcity Creates Hope
Industry consensus: ‘The industry will have hope only when young people stop coming.’ Change is happening—recruitment gets harder, civil engineering admission scores drop, Tsinghua putting it in early admission is praised as ‘industry conscience.’ Going overseas is a path, tough but absorbs surplus capacity. Stay optimistic: humans have a strange self-fulfilling ability—the world becomes what you believe it is.
💡 Key Takeaway

Behind ‘China Speed’ and ‘Infrastructure Maniac’ are two decades of massive contributions from construction workers. But severe involution threatens future development. The way out: natural supply-demand adjustment—talent scarcity forcing better treatment, overseas expansion absorbing excess. Most importantly: stay optimistic, for optimism brings wealth and pessimism brings darkness.
