Su Shi Biography · Ch.5
"Frosty Chill at the Censorate Prison"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer En route to Huzhou, amid departed friends and unchanged landscapes, Su Shi crystallized his philosophy of "neither must one serve, nor must one retire" — unaware that a fatal political storm, the Crow Terrace Poetry Trial, was quietly brewing in the capital. Why was a poet seeking spiritual transcendence thrown into the Censorate prison less than three months after arriving in Huzhou?

In April 1079, Su Shi set out on his third journey along the Bian River to assume office. At Yangzhou's Pingshan Hall, mourning his departed mentor Ouyang Xiu, he wrote: "Say not all things turn to emptiness when you turn your head — even before you turn, all is already a dream." In the Zen stillness of a night boat, he experienced "the joy of spirit merging with nature." He distilled his ideal: "Neither must one serve, nor must one retire." Yet in the capital, Wang Gui, Cai Que, Li Ding, and Shu Dan were using Su Shi's poetry as "evidence" to weave a fatal literary inquisition.

01
Friends Departed
Three Times at Pingshan Hall: "Even Before You Turn, All Is Already a Dream"
Passing through Yangzhou on his third posting journey, Su Shi came to Pingshan Hall — the scenic landmark built thirty years earlier by his mentor Ouyang Xiu. Ouyang's hand-planted willows still stood before the hall; his calligraphy on the wall seemed freshly brushed — yet the man was gone: "Three times I have passed beneath Pingshan Hall; half a lifetime has vanished in the snap of fingers. Ten years without seeing the old immortal; his dragon-serpent brushwork still flies across the wall." Su Shi took Bai Juyi's line and pushed it further — even before the turning of the head, all is already dreamlike.

02
Night Boat Zen
Deep Night, All Creatures Leave Each Other Alone: Ecstasy of Spirit Merging with Nature
Aboard the boat on the water, Su Shi tossed and turned, unable to sleep. Deep into the silent night, all creatures slumbered — "there was no strife, no clamor, no scheming" — he experienced the joy of spirit merging seamlessly with nature: "Deep night, all creatures leave each other alone; I alone keep company with my own shadow in delight." The breeze rustled the reeds, moonlight filled the lake, the night tide silently rose — time seemed to have stopped; an instant became eternity.

03
Philosophical Summit
"Neither Must One Serve, Nor Must One Retire": The Ideal Life's Balance
In his "Record of the Zhang Family Garden at Lingbi," Su Shi crystallized years of contemplation: "The junzi of old neither felt he must serve, nor felt he must not serve" — all must follow the natural course, finding balance between the permissible and impermissible, preserving individual spiritual independence while never shirking social responsibility. "Open the door and go forth to serve — then one walks through the marketplace of court; close the door and return to reclusion — then one gazes upon mountain forests."

04
Huzhou Arrival
Calm Before the Storm: "I Have Nothing to Miss But These Clear Waters and Mountains"
On April 20, 1079, Su Shi arrived at his Huzhou post. "Huzhou's customs were peaceful and prosperous, its mountains and waters clear and distant" — he eagerly explored the city, intoxicated by scenery where "for thirty li around the city walls, every spot was exquisitely beautiful." As in Hangzhou, Mizhou, and Xuzhou, he wanted to "extend the court's benevolence above and comfort the elders' hopes below." Yet he had no inkling that in the capital, a "sudden, severe political blow" was about to shatter every one of his beautiful hopes.

05
The Trap Tightens
Political Enemies: How Petty Men Made Su Shi Their "Thorn in the Eye"
The focus of Yuánfēng-era politics was no longer reform debate but pure bureaucratic infighting. Prime Minister Wang Gui was famously called the "Three Edicts Prime Minister" — request the edict, receive the edict, transmit the edict — consummate bureaucratic cunning with zero political vision. Together with Vice Councilor Cai Que, they formed an alliance with Li Ding's faction. Li Ding and company had risen through supporting the New Policies; seeing no strongman left to lead the reform camp, they "lived in constant anxiety," terrified the Emperor might turn to opposition figures — and Su Shi became their perfect target.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Chapter 5 is Su Shi's watershed: the first half sees him achieve his philosophical summation — "neither must one serve, nor must one retire" — reaching the most harmonious balance between engagement and withdrawal; the second half reveals the capital's dark political reality, where petty men led by Wang Gui and Li Ding are weaving a net from his poetry. Philosophically, Su Shi was now prepared to face any storm life might bring — but he did not yet know that the coming "Crow Terrace Poetry Trial" would be the ultimate test of all his wisdom and courage. The frost of the Censorate was already creeping closer.
