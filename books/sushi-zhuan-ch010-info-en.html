In one line: Su Shi's "belly full of untimeliness" made court life impossible — he clashed with Sima Guang over the exemption labor law, mocked Cheng Yi, offended the Luo faction, and faced attacks from two rival cliques. Requesting external assignment, he governed Dengzhou, Hangzhou, Yingzhou, and Yangzhou in succession. Far from factional strife, he delivered monumental achievements: Su Causeway, Dongpo Pork, China's first public hospital, cancellation of exploitative festivals, and tax relief — proving that real governance happens among the people, not in the halls of power.

Core QuestionWhy couldn't Su Shi remain at court after returning to the capital? How did he transform political adversity into concrete achievements in local governance?

Core AnswerSu Shi abandoned his old prejudice to defend the exemption labor law, clashing fiercely with Sima Guang — calling him "Sima Ox" behind his back. His public mockery of Cheng Yi as a "village pedant" ignited the Luo-Shu factional war. Besieged from all sides, he requested external posting. In four prefectures, he fought famine and plague in Hangzhou, dredged West Lake, built the Su Causeway, founded the Anle Clinic; blocked a wasteful canal project in Yingzhou; abolished the Peony Festival and secured tax debt relief in Yangzhou. After Empress Dowager Gao's death, he was posted to Dingzhou to reform border defense. His record proves: a good official is measured by deeds for the people, not by rank at court.

1
Court Clashes

"Sima Ox" and Factional War: Attacked from All Sides at Court

Upon returning to court, Su Shi abandoned old biases to become a firm defender of the exemption labor law — only to meet Sima Guang's obstinate refusal. He argued face to face in the council hall, then stormed home shouting "Sima Ox! Sima Ox!" His mockery of Cheng Yi triggered the Luo-Shu factional war, drawing attacks from the Luo and Shuo cliques.

Years of local governance experience convinced Su Shi that the exemption labor law — taxing households by wealth to hire laborers — was actually fairer than the old corvée system, which let corrupt officials extort the people. But Sima Guang, who had spent 15 years in seclusion writing "Zizhi Tongjian," was determined to abolish every reform, "not weighing pros and cons, not keeping what worked."

🔴 Exemption Law Debate

Su Shi argued from practical experience; Sima Guang insisted on total abolition. Su Zhe assessed: "Sima was loyal and trustworthy, but lacked wisdom — he knew the harm of exemption labor but not its benefit."

🟠 Luo-Shu Factional War

After Sima Guang died, Cheng Yi insisted mourning and celebration couldn't mix. Su Shi mocked him — sparking the Luo (Henan), Shu (Sichuan), and Shuo (Hebei) factional conflict that dominated court politics.

Chao Yun, his concubine, captured the essence: "To my eyes, the Scholar has a belly full of untimeliness." Su Shi wrote to a friend: "Once gentlemen all followed Wang Anshi; now they all follow Sima Guang. Different men followed, but follow they do — that is the same."

2
Imperial Trust

Golden Lotus Candles: The Empress Dowager's Trust and the Young Emperor's Coldness

Empress Dowager Gao summoned Su Shi privately and revealed that his advancement was the dying wish of Emperor Shenzong — who would pause his chopsticks reading Su Shi's memorials, exclaiming "A genius! A genius!" She sent him home with golden lotus candles from the imperial desk, a signal of "heaven-sent responsibility." But Su Shi knew the young Emperor Zhezong already resented his grandmother's dominance.

"The late Emperor Shenzong would often read memorials during meals. Whenever he stopped eating, the palace attendants would say: 'That must be Su Shi's writing.' Sometimes he would blurt out: 'Genius! Genius!' But he passed away before he could promote you." — Empress Dowager Gao

The Empress Dowager entreated Su Shi to serve the young emperor faithfully. Deeply moved, Su Shi felt "this body no longer belongs to me — I can only repay with death." But Emperor Zhezong was temperamental, lazy, and resentful of his grandmother's total control of affairs. Su Shi saw the writing on the wall and repeatedly requested external posting.

⚠️ Empress Dowager Gao died on September 3, 1094. On her deathbed, she told the grand councilors: "You'd best resign early." Su Shi had already secured a posting to Dingzhou before her death, but Emperor Zhezong refused to grant him a farewell audience — an ominous sign of the coming storm.
3
Hangzhou Legacy

Su Causeway and Anle Clinic: A City Reborn

In Hangzhou, Su Shi achieved three monumental projects: using work-relief to dredge West Lake, building the 880-zhàng Su Causeway with six bridges, and founding China's first public hospital "Anle Clinic" — not a single person starved during the famine, and thousands were saved from the plague.

Facing consecutive floods and drought, with rice prices surging from 60 to 90 qian per dou, Su Shi broke the bureaucratic habit of "reporting only good news" and repeatedly petitioned for 200,000 shi of tribute rice and 300 ordination certificates to fund relief. He used all disaster funds to purchase grain, stabilizing prices, while organizing monks and doctors to distribute the "Sheng San Zi" plague remedy free to the public.

💡 The Birth of Dongpo Pork: During the Dragon Boat Festival, Hangzhou residents brought pigs and wine to thank Su Shi. He cooked the pork using the method he'd developed in Huangzhou — "slow fire, little water, patience yields perfection" — and distributed it to the laborers. Thus a timeless culinary classic was born.

Su Shi also adopted Monk Zigui's proposal to replace bamboo pipes with ceramic tubes for the city's water supply, bringing "West Lake's sweet water throughout the entire city." To prevent future silting, he leased shoreline waters to farmers for water caltrop cultivation, and erected three stone pagodas (later "Three Pools Mirroring the Moon") to mark planting boundaries.

4
Wisdom & Mercy

Painted Fans and Abolished Festivals: Governance with Heart

In Yingzhou and Yangzhou, Su Shi demonstrated governance at two scales: macro — blocking a massive wasteful canal project using 5,811 surveying poles of empirical data; micro — resolving a debt lawsuit by painting twenty fans and telling the debtor to sell them. He abolished an exploitative peony festival and lay awake all night planning relief for snow-stranded famine refugees.

🔵 The Painted Fan Case

A fan maker couldn't repay debts due to persistent rain. Su Shi took twenty fans, painted bamboo, rocks, and poems on each, and told the man: "Sell them for 1,000 qian each." They sold out instantly — debt resolved.

🔵 Abolishing the Peony Festival

Cai Jing had started a "Ten Thousand Flower Gathering" consuming 100,000+ peonies yearly, exploited by officials and merchants. Su Shi canceled it outright: "Though it spoils the scenery, it avoids creating bad karma."

In Yingzhou, he personally supervised a topographical survey proving the proposed Bázhàng Canal would bring "a hundred harms and not a single benefit," saving 180,000 laborers and 370,000 strings of cash. In Yangzhou, going incognito among villagers, he discovered that bumper harvests were worse than famine years — good harvests meant tax collectors demanded all accumulated arrears at once. His memorial compared the 200,000-strong tax-collection apparatus to "tigers and wolves scattered among the people."

"I have heard Confucius say: 'Tyranny is fiercer than a tiger.' I never believed it — until now, seeing it is even worse. Flood and drought kill a hundred times more than tigers, yet people fear tax debt collectors more than flood and drought." — Su Shi, Memorial on Six Matters of Accumulated Debt
5
Final Post

By the Snow-Wave Stone: Last Command and Eternal Homesickness

Dingzhou was Su Shi's final active post. Facing "border administration so decayed one could hardly bear to look," he disciplined troops, punished corrupt officers, repaired barracks, and revived the "Archery Society" militia plan for 30,000 local fighters. But deeper worries loomed — his wife Wang Runzhi had died, Empress Dowager Gao was gone, and Emperor Zhezong was about to unleash a political purge.

Arriving in Dingzhou, Su Shi found generals arrogant, soldiers lazy, and previous prefects too afraid to intervene. He prosecuted embezzlers, banned gambling and drinking, personally supervised drills, and inspected every dilapidated barracks. He revived the "Archery Society" — 30,000 local militia to supplement 25,000 imperial troops. Yet he sensed the approaching storm, writing in "Snow-Wave Stone": "This body itself is illusion — which is not a dream? My homeland's mountains and rivers linger in my heart."

"The parasol tree in the courtyard — three years, three times I've seen you... This year I go to Zhongshan, white-haired, no knowing when I'll return. Guest, don't sigh when leaving — the host too is a guest. Our vow of night rain, facing beds — now just empty rustling." — Su Shi, "Parting from Ziyou in Rain at the Eastern Residence"

Before leaving the capital, Su Shi bid farewell to his brother at the Eastern Residence in rain, sensing that Ziyou too would not long remain. Sure enough, in April 1094, Emperor Zhezong changed the era name to "Shaosheng" (Continuing the Sage), the reform faction returned to power, Su Zhe was demoted, and Su Shi's official career reached its end.

💡
Key Takeaway

Su Shi's four prefectures reveal a profound truth: real political achievement often happens not at the center of power, but among ordinary people. Besieged by factional warfare at court, he created enduring legacies in local governance — Su Causeway, Anle Clinic, Dongpo Pork. Even with mere days in office (Dengzhou: two memorials in five days), he acted for the people; when wielding real authority (Hangzhou: commanding six prefectures), he applied scientific rigor to flood control and famine relief. The golden lotus candles' glow had barely faded when farewell beneath Dingzhou's parasol tree became forever — Su Shi proved through deeds that an official's worth is measured by what they do for the people, not by the rank they hold.
