Chapter 5 · The Predicament of Manufacturing

End of the Golden Age — China's Factory of the World Becomes a Survival Game
SCQA Analysis
S (Situation): China's manufacturing once worked miracles — factories blanketed the Yangtze and Pearl River Deltas, clothing and footwear sold globally, "Made in China" was everywhere.
C (Complication): Labor costs soared annually at 12–15%, the ¥4T stimulus triggered runaway capacity expansion, the new Labor Contract Law crippled legitimate firms — and Taobao's race-to-the-bottom pricing destroyed all brand innovation, while unlicensed workshops dragged formal businesses into a death spiral.
Q (Question): How much longer can manufacturing hold on? Costs exceed America's, orders flee to Southeast Asia, youth refuse factory work — has Chinese manufacturing hit a dead end?
A (Answer): Capacity elimination is a brutal knockout round — only oligopoly brands and bottom-feeding workshops survive, while the middle tier of formal SMEs are collapsing en masse; industrial capital exits production and floods into Beijing/Shanghai real estate.

🔴 Cost Strangulation: "Cheap China" Is Over

Labor, rent, taxes, shipping, financing — all expensive. A product shipped from northern Jiangsu to Sichuan triples in price after 3–4 transshipments, or it loses money. Shanghai manufacturing labor costs hit $16K/year per worker (¥5K take-home + ¥2.25K social insurance + housing), versus Vietnam's $6K — a $10K gap per worker, $10M pure profit difference for a 1,000-worker plant. A Czech factory worker earns ¥8–10K/month — less than a Chinese migrant with a mortar bucket. Worse: export-sector cost surges killed competitiveness, stranded capacity turned inward, and now both export and domestic capacity are locked in mutual slaughter on the domestic market — overcapacity erupted everywhere.

🟠 The New Labor Contract Law: A Noose for Legal Businesses

Finance Minister Lou Jiwei publicly rebuked it: "The Labor Contract Law severely reduced labor market flexibility; wage growth has exceeded productivity growth and is unsustainable long-term." When orders come, bosses dare not hire — tomorrow's orders are uncertain, but once hired, workers are near-impossible to fire. Unlicensed workshops don't care; big companies can afford the costs; midsize firms are trapped. Formal enterprises shed staff and devolve into landlords, renting factory space and machines to the same unlicensed workshops doing the same work. One labor lawsuit can trigger a chain reaction that collapses an entire cluster. Zhōngtiānxìn — thousands of workers making Smartisan phones — the boss fled, and Luo Yonghao had to scramble for desks. Manufacturing cost spike → export flameout → printing-fueled infrastructure → housing boom → consumption crushed — China's self-destruct sequence.

🟡 The "Labor Shortage" Lie: Graduates Who Can't Do Real Work

90% of Shanghai urban residents descend from factory workers, yet none will set foot in a factory now. Manufacturing survives on the backs of post-60s and post-70s migrant workers, whose sole motivation is ensuring their children never enter a factory. Germany sends 75% of secondary students to vocational schools; China sends 90% to universities (mostly humanities) — four years of confinement producing arrogant dilettantes who "won't do ordinary work and can't do skilled work." Cai's "Impossible Trinity of Manufacturing": the state, entrepreneurs, and workers can never all be satisfied simultaneously. Today: the state is happy, workers are happy, small and medium bosses are not. When the middle link snaps, employment pressure bypasses enterprises and lands directly on the government.

🟣 The Taobao Meat Grinder: Killing All Brand Innovation

Taobao's greatest value is cross-border shopping — paving the way for Western products to reach every Chinese village corner. Within five years, health-related manufacturing has almost no future. Taobao set the new standard for manufacturing: how good are your Photoshop skills? Same two floral dresses — in person you judge fabric, sheen, color, tailoring; online, after retouching, it's pure price war. A new design? Copied overnight at half the price, inferior quality. Counterfeits once traveled by truck — easy to intercept. Now they're atomized into parcels; one truck carries thousands of packages, impossible to police. A brand produces 1,000 units, 30 are defects, the subcontractor secretly makes 20 more "factory extras" — zero-cost Taobao dumping, and if it sells, counterfeiting continues. The brand dies. Cai's first investment rule: if it can be sold on Taobao, reject it immediately.

🩷 The Overcapacity Death Spiral: No One Will Quit First

The ¥4T stimulus led everyone to build capacity as if ¥4T would come every year. ¥4T gone → total overcapacity → universal losses. But no one dares stop first — shutdown losses are even bigger, severance costs are enormous. The Labor Contract Law requires compensation; bosses can't pay, so they keep producing… even expanding to cut unit costs (ordering 10K units is cheaper than 1K). Absurd mutual destruction where no competitor makes money. The solar industry built 80% of global capacity in three years — Wuxi Suntech wasn't killed by the market but by the planners. After the decade-long knockout: the top 10 brands survive, everyone else exits. Private mega-conglomerates emerge, social mobility freezes.

💡 Key Takeaway

Manufacturing's golden age is over. Formal SMEs are being crushed from three sides: the Labor Contract Law, Taobao price dumping, and bottom-feeding workshops. Industrial capital is fleeing production for Beijing/Shanghai real estate — the worse the real economy, the higher BJ/SH housing rises. Cai's own business was destroyed by Taobao price wars; he had to "sell the business, buy property with the cash." Ten years from now, the formal small factory owners holding on today will either flee their debts or become landlords. For ordinary people: working a job can't beat owning property. Save houses, not cash. Direction matters more than effort.
