「Will Housing Prices Fall After the Birth Rate Declines?」

Bottom-Up Population Flow — Tier-1 Megaregions Will Only Grow Bigger

📖 Chapter Overview

Will declining population cause massive housing vacancies? The author says this is already happening—just not how you'd imagine. Population flows bottom-up: tier-1 cities absorb national talent, big cities absorb wealthy from smaller ones, small cities absorb rural youth. While villages and resource-depleted cities hollow out, tier-1 cities are growing denser. Tokyo's metropolitan area gathers 40 million people with an economy larger than Russia's. Seoul's "Gangnam Invincible" legend sees prices rising even under property taxes. Conclusion: don't count on snagging tier-1 bargains when population falls.

🔍 Deep Dive
Cascading Migration: Village to Tier-1 City
The author's hometown is a microcosm: township schools have been abolished, 70% of houses vacant. College graduates head to big cities, county wealth moves to provincial capitals, village youth buy in county seats—without a house, marriage is near-impossible. Tier-1 cities absorb national talent, big cities absorb the wealthy, small cities absorb rural youth. Even as total population declines, tier-1 and strong provincial capitals are actually growing.
Why Cram Into Crowded Cities? Economic Density
Population density creates active commerce and spawns bizarre niche industries. In tier-1 cities, "professional cat-finding" services charge 8,000–10,000 yuan a call. A mover who earned 3,000–4,000 yuan hauling 30 tons of cement daily now earns 3–4× more in Beijing. If the gap is this huge for the uneducated, imagine the chasm for professionals—aerospace engineers or programmers—between big and small cities.
Marginal Transactions & Wealthy Leverage
Housing prices are set by the most recent transaction—of 100 units in a complex, only a few high-earners can push prices up. The wealthy use bank loans to multiply wealth: mortgage→borrow→buy again→wait for money printing to inflate values. Ordinary people only earn wages. The wealthy go bankrupt; the poor are "stably poor forever." Money printing enters via credit—those with valuable homes borrow more, further driving up tier-1 prices.
Portfolio Rebalancing: Dumping Lagging Regions
Five apartments in Northeast China that aren't appreciating? The rational move is selling and buying into better-growth prospects. The wealthy abandon stagnating areas for tier-1 cities, amplifying the Matthew Effect. Many people deliberately seek Beijing jobs that offer residency permits—low salary is fine—just to "rebalance" their hometown properties into tier-1. Beijing outskirts halved, Tianjin collapsed; wealthy in declining regions keep selling and heading south.
The Mature-Country Pattern: Tier-1 Soars, Rest Stabilizes
This is the developed-country state: tier-1 city housing becomes absurdly expensive, luxury compounds are mind-boggling. But elsewhere, prices are modest—young people aren't rushing to buy. When housing doesn't rise, "must-buy" pressure evaporates. In Tianjin, youth with means still rent: "It'll drop anyway, what's the hurry?" Tier-1 megaregions grow ever larger; villages hollow out further; other cities stabilize downward—and residents feel relief.
💡 Key Takeaway

Population decline won't crash tier-1 city housing prices—population doesn't shrink evenly; it vacates the bottom while concentrating the top. Those waiting to bargain-hunt will likely be disappointed. But this isn't bad news: while tier-1 cities become outrageously expensive, everywhere else loses its investment frenzy, prices stabilize downward, and young people stop panicking about buying. Renting becomes perfectly acceptable. A mature society finds a suitable niche for everyone.
