Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch.7
"Intuition & Discipline"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer On the eve of the 1906 San Francisco earthquake, Livermore shorted Union Pacific on pure intuition, earning $250,000 while everyone questioned him. Yet at Saratoga, he abandoned his own judgment, heeded Ed Harding's advice to close out and reverse, losing $40,000 — a pivotal battle between instinct and discipline. Is trading intuition superstition or subconscious accumulation? Intuition is compressed experience built over years — far more reliable than tips from others. After this lesson, Livermore abandoned "impromptu" trading forever and began focusing on broad market conditions.

In one sentence: Livermore shorted Union Pacific on pure intuition with no fundamental basis, was vindicated the next day by the SF earthquake, earning $250,000 — only to later lose $40,000 at Saratoga listening to a friend's advice. The battle between instinct and discipline changed him forever. Key data: Pre-earthquake short profit $250K | Loss from bad advice $40K | Intuition-driven short 5,000 shares | Missed upside 3 points.

01
Intuition
Where Does Intuition Come From? A "Groundless" Short
Livermore's intuition defied logic but worked repeatedly. In spring 1906, while on vacation in Atlantic City with no position, he stared at Union Pacific's ticker and felt an overwhelming urge to short it. He couldn't explain why — he just "wanted to sell." He did, shorting 3,000 shares, then another 2,000. The next day: news of the San Francisco earthquake shook the world. Union Pacific was among the hardest-hit railroads. He pyramided to 15,000 shares and netted $250,000 within days — his largest profit to date. He reflected: over a dozen similar intuitive flashes had occurred, and each time he ignored one, he regretted it.

02
Costly Lesson
The Saratoga Mistake: When Intuition Surrendered to Others
That same summer at Saratoga, Livermore read the tape and concluded Union Pacific was being accumulated by big money, buying around 160. Ed Harding called long-distance, begging him as a friend to close out immediately — "the insiders are dumping it on you." Livermore listened, liquidated his longs, and went short 4,000 shares. Then a 10% dividend was announced, the stock soared. His conclusion was searing: "For a man who hasn't the courage to stick to his own convictions, the cost was cheap! A low-priced lesson."
❌

Wrong Approach

Listening to a well-meaning friend, abandoning one's own correct tape reading, closing longs and going short. $40,000 gone.

✅

Right Approach

Trust the tape signal and your own analysis. The tape says "up" — the insiders' true intentions are irrelevant. Price action is the answer.

03
Turning Point
No More "Impromptu" Trading: The Final Lesson
The Saratoga episode was the watershed of Livermore's trading career. He realized: despite the $40,000 loss, had he stuck to his own judgment, he would have cleaned up. This insight was worth more than money — it completed his trading education. From then on, he abandoned "impromptu" trading, focusing instead on broad market conditions rather than individual stocks. The very next day he reversed to long and recovered $55K — but the biggest gain was priceless: lifelong independent thinking.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Intuition is the subconscious rapid compression of vast market signals — not magic, but the natural product of professional training. Yet intuition must be governed by discipline: trust your own judgment, reject others' noise. This was the lifelong creed Livermore bought with $250,000 in profit and $40,000 in loss. Saratoga became his last "impromptu" trade; thereafter he turned to systematic analysis of broad market conditions.
