Su Shi Biography · Ch12
"Heaven and Earth Mourn Together"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer "To strive is already to err" — Su Shi's last words to the world. The great literary master departed in full clarity; the entire realm wept. How did a cultural titan who had experienced the highest highs and lowest lows face the end of life? How did his death and the ensuing national mourning reflect both his personal greatness and the tragedy of his era?

In one line: On his journey north, Su Shi endured the shock of Qin Guan's sudden death, agonized over where to settle, forgave Zhang Dun's son who came begging for mercy, and finally succumbed to his own fatal illness. His final words — "To strive is already to err" — encapsulated his enlightenment: no grasping for the Pure Land, no forced devotion, just serene acceptance of life's end. Li Zhi's funeral oration — "His Way too vast to be contained, his talent too high to escape envy. Heaven above and Earth below bear witness to his lifelong loyalty; the great mountains and rivers receive back his immortal spirit" — became the definitive epitaph for his life.

01
Fallen Friends
Qin Guan's Death: The Last of Su's Disciples to Fall
In June 1100, after crossing the sea, Su Shi reunited with Qin Guan at Xuwen — after seven years of separation. "Green temples and rosy cheeks? We meet again — two aging men." Qin Guan presented his own elegy as a parting gift — Su Shi, having penetrated life and death, was not alarmed. Who could know that Qin Guan would succumb to heatstroke in Tengzhou on August 12, dying at just 52? Su Shi "could not eat for two days," lamenting: "The foremost literary talent of our age — can such a man ever be found again?"
🕯️

Qin Guan: Finest of the Four Scholars

The most gifted disciple, at the golden prime of his thought and art — died suddenly at 52 just as light was breaking after long darkness. His death resulted from persecution and harsh conditions combined.

Su Shi: Two Days Without Food

"A swallow flying south, a wild goose homing north — by chance we meet, our faces gaunt with sorrow." He detoured to Tengzhou hoping to mourn — but arrived half a month too late.

02
Journey North
Re-climbing Dayu: "Old Man, How Many Exiles Have You Seen Return?"
In the first month of 1101, Su Shi crossed the Dayu Mountains heading north. For Song dynasty officials, this mountain range signified the end of political life — few who crossed south ever returned. A white-haired elder recognized him: "I've heard some have schemed a thousand ways to harm you. Today your northward return — Heaven truly protects the good!" Stranded in Qianzhou, Su Shi carried a medicine bag, prescribed remedies for the sick, and tricked the famously stern Liu Anshi into a "Jade Slab" consultation that turned out to be a bamboo-shoot picnic.

03
Life's Summation
Inscribed at Jinshan: "Ask Me My Life's Work — Huangzhou, Huizhou, Danzhou"
Journeying north, Su Shi agonized between settling in Changzhou or Yingchang — Yingchang meant reunion with his brother, but proximity to the capital meant danger; Changzhou was the second hometown of his dreams. In mid-May at Jinshan Temple, facing Li Gonglin's portrait of himself, he inscribed: "My heart — like ashes of spent wood; my body — a boat unmoored. Ask me my life's achievements — Huangzhou, Huizhou, Danzhou." These four lines are Su Shi's most distilled self-assessment.

04
Final Days
"To Strive Is Already to Err": Departing in Perfect Clarity
On June 3, Su Shi drank too much cold liquid and suffered violent dysentery through the night. Latent miasmic toxins flared. For over a month his condition seesawed. On July 28, as death approached, Abbot Weilin shouted: "Excellency, do not forget the West!" Su Shi replied: "The West is not absent — but here one cannot strive for it." When Qian Shixiong added "You must strive harder," Su Shi answered: "To strive is already to err." With that he passed away serenely — the ultimate expression of his spiritual universe.

05
National Mourning
Too Vast to Be Contained, Too Talented to Escape Envy
News of Su Shi's death spread across the realm: "The people of Wu and Yue wept together in the markets; their gentlemen mourned together at home; wherever the obituary reached, wise and foolish alike sighed and shed tears" — a spontaneous, nationwide outpouring of grief. Imperial College students gathered at Huilin Monastery; Huang Tingjian hung Su Shi's portrait and paid daily homage; Zhang Lei in white cap and robes sacrificed to his teacher.

🔑 Key Takeaway
With his final words "To strive is already to err," Su Shi achieved a breakthrough in the spiritual history of the Chinese scholar-official: he sought no Western Pure Land, performed no forced devotions, clung to no religious form — meeting death in the most natural, clearest state possible. This was of a piece with his lifelong independent spirit. Li Zhi's epitaph — "His Way too vast to be contained; his talent too high to escape envy" — captures not only Su Shi's tragedy but the deeper tragedy of the Chinese intellectual across centuries. "Heaven above and Earth below bear witness to his lifelong loyalty" declares: politics may destroy a body, but it cannot erase a great soul's radiance. The spontaneous national mourning — by students, commoners, monks, and officials alike — proves Su Shi's transcendence of faction and class. A thousand years later, Su Dongpo remains the most beloved literary figure in the Chinese imagination.
