Su Dongpo Biography · Ch. 13 "Prisoners of Conscience"

The other side of Hangzhou's paradise — prisoners, locusts, and a poet's awakening conscience, culminating in the immortal Mid-Autumn masterpiece

Beneath Su Dongpo's laughter lay profound sorrow. He witnessed the people's suffering firsthand, using poetry to plead their cause — recording the misery of canal laborers, the lament of field women without salt. On New Year's Eve, he wept as he wrote: "On this last day I should return home early, but official duties keep me. As I write, I weep — thinking of these prisoners in chains." Transferred from Hangzhou paradise to barren Mizhou, he reached his poetic maturity in despair — composing the immortal "When will the bright moon appear? Wine cup in hand, I ask the blue sky."

01
Paradise's Shadow
Seventeen Thousand Prisoners: Unease Beneath the Feasting
Hangzhou was not all lotus blossoms — seventeen thousand prisoners awaited judgment, locusts, famine, and silted waterways tormented the vice-governor. On New Year's Eve he wept as he wrote: "What I once felt ashamed of, now I feel no shame — sitting before weary people, wielding the whip." He knew most prisoners were ordinary folk who had broken new-policy laws — salt smugglers, tax debtors — laws he never approved of.

02
Poetry as Weapon
The Mosquito-Bite Effect: Protest Poems Piling into Indictments
Su Dongpo described the people's suffering in the plainest language — poems that accumulated day by day into the crime of "disregarding government authority." He wrote of canal laborers: "Men like ducks and pigs, splashing mud on one another." He penned "A Farm Wife's Lament": rice harvest comes but salt is too expensive to taste — sell the ox to pay taxes, tear down the house for firewood. "Officials now want cash, not grain."

03
Mizhou Exile
From Paradise to Wasteland: A Governor Eating Thistles
Transferred from paradise to barren Mizhou, his salary slashed, eating wolfberries and thistles to survive — this was Su Dongpo's most depressed yet creatively most brilliant period. Mizhou produced hemp, dates, and mulberries. In his "Wolfberry and Thistle Rhapsody" he wrote: "Hoping for a full meal, but the kitchen is bare — I daily wander the old city's abandoned garden with my deputy, gathering wolfberries and thistles to eat, clutching our bellies and laughing."

04
Immortal Masterpiece
The Mid-Autumn Poem: After This, No Other Moon Poem Mattered
Mid-Autumn 1076, Mizhou. Missing Ziyou whom he hadn't seen in seven years, Su Dongpo wrote the greatest Mid-Autumn poem in history: "When will the bright moon appear? Wine cup in hand, I ask the blue sky. I wonder what year it is tonight in the celestial palaces above. I wish to ride the wind back, yet fear those jade towers and crystal halls — too cold at such great height. Dancing with my own shadow — how can this compare to the human world?" From personal grief he ascended to cosmic insight.

05
Saving Lives
Gathering Abandoned Children: Protecting Life Amid Starvation
Facing children starving by the roadside, Su Dongpo did more than write poetry — he "wept as he walked the city walls, gathering abandoned children," personally saving thirty or forty orphans and placing them with families. After anger and bitterness, only profound peace remained. His admiration for Tao Qian deepened, reaching perfect harmony with nature. His poems from this period — "Western Study" and "Cloud-Gazing Tower" — achieved spiritual freedom: "Going out without purpose, returning is fine too."

🔑 Key Takeaway
Heaven and hell coexisted in the same city. Su Dongpo's greatest courage was not in indulging in pleasure, but in writing the truth for prisoners and field women, knowing full well his poems would reach the capital and invite disaster. Mizhou's barrenness did not destroy his talent — on the contrary, in his moment of deepest despair he composed "May we live long, sharing the moon's beauty though a thousand li apart." The poet's heart did not bloom in prosperity — it matured in hardship. From seventeen thousand prisoners to a diet of thistles, from gathering abandoned children in tears to questioning heaven at Mid-Autumn — this was the forging journey of Su Dongpo's soul.
