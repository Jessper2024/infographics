Layout of the World · Chapter 3
「Military Geography of the Four Sides」

Shanxi, Shandong, Hubei, and Hanzhong — strategic hinges contested between the four corners

The four sides are wedged between corners — corridors of connection and contested focal points. Shanxi, perched on a commanding plateau, controls the eight Taihang passes; Shandong commands the junction of the Yellow River and Grand Canal; Hubei anchors the middle Yangtze — 'by the realm's measure, Xiangyang is paramount'; Hanzhong, squeezed between Guanzhong and Sichuan, is a pivot of intense north-south friction with broad east-west reach. Through classic cases — the Xiongnu conquest of Western Jin, the century-long rise and fall of Northern Wei, and the rapid succession of Five Dynasties — this chapter reveals the strategic significance of each side.

1
Shanxi
Capital of Hedong, Land of Military Prowess

Shanxi's elevated terrain, flanked by the Taihang and Lüliang ranges with the Yellow River wrapping its west and south, creates an ideal interior-line position. The Eight Taihang Passes — Zhiguan, Taihang, Bai, Fukou, Jingxing, Feihu, Puyin, Jundu — control access to Hebei and Henan. Five strategic basins (Datong, Xindai, Taiyuan, Changzhi, Puzhou) face different directions, serving as springboards for external campaigns. The Xiongnu conquest of Western Jin, Northern Wei's century of power, and three of the Five Dynasties rising from Hedong all testify to Shanxi's pivotal role in the north.

2
Shandong
Hills and Rivers — Thoroughfare of War and Defense

Shandong centers on the Luzhongnan hills, surrounded by plains on three sides and the sea to the east. The Yellow River runs east-west while the Grand Canal (and the ancient Si River) connects north-south — Shandong sits at their junction. In north-south confrontations, Shandong was invariably contested. Zhu Yuanzhang's northern campaign opened the door to Dadu by seizing Shandong; Zhu Di bypassed Shandong to take Jinling in the Jingnan campaign — both reveal Shandong's pivotal role. After China's political center shifted east, the Grand Canal fell under Shandong's watch, making it the vital link between political and economic centers.

3
Hubei
Pivot of the Jiang-Han, Pillar of Jiangnan

Hubei anchors the middle Yangtze, ringed by mountains with rivers radiating outward. Upstream along the Yangtze leads to Sichuan; downstream to the Southeast; northward via the Han River enables campaigns into the Central Plains. Xiangyang, Wuchang, and Jiangling form a tripod. "By the realm — Xiangyang matters most; by the Southeast — Wuchang; by Huguang — Jingzhou." History's verdict: no power holding the Southeast ever survived losing Jing-Xiang. Western Jin conquered Wu, Sui subdued Chen, Northern Song took Southern Tang, and the Mongols destroyed Southern Song — all by breaking through from the upper Yangtze.

4
Hanzhong
North-South Stalemate, East-West Expansion

The Qinling and Daba ranges sandwich the Han River valley to form Hanzhong. San Pass, Wu Pass, and three valley roads (Baoxie, Tangluo, Ziwu) link to Guanzhong; the Jinniu and Micang roads lead to Sichuan. Wedged between two upstream powers, Hanzhong's north-south friction is intense — gains and losses often prove decisive. Yet east-west reach is broad: the Western Han River valley leads to Longxi, enabling "distant-posture competition for Longxi"; southeastern thrusts can break stalemates at Tong Pass or the Three Gorges. Shu Han's Qishan campaigns and the Mongol conquest of Jin both exploited this geography.

5
Wing Strategy
Managing Both Wings: The Key to Rise or Fall

For any power arising from a corner, success or failure in managing the two wings determines its destiny. For Guanzhong: Hanzhong and Shanxi are its wings — Shanxi is decisive. For Hebei: Shanxi and Shandong are its wings — again Shanxi is key, as northern rivalries played out in Shanxi between Guanzhong and Hebei. For the Southeast: Shandong and Hubei — Hubei, commanding the upper Yangtze, is the linchpin. For Sichuan: Hanzhong and Hubei — Jiangling to the east and Hanzhong to the north must both be held. Master the wings, and you can encircle the Central Plains and contend for the realm.

📌 Key Takeaways

Though less endowed than the corners, the four sides were the indispensable corridors of expansion. Shanxi was the hinge of the north — whoever controlled it held the initiative between Guanzhong and Hebei. Shandong was the north-south choke point — its importance grew after the political center shifted east. Hubei was the lock on the Yangtze — lose Jing-Xiang and the Southeast falls. Hanzhong was the balancer between two upstream powers. Control of the four sides determined whether a regime could break out of regional hegemony toward unification.
