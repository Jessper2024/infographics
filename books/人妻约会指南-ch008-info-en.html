1 A.1 On Middle School Students Getting Married

Written in New York in November 2020, originally published on Zhihu. The catalyst was news of an 18-year-old and a 14-year-old getting married in Guiyu Town, Chaoyang, Shantou. The essay supports the young couple — blessed by parents, married through proper ceremony, more romantic than the market-haggling blind dates of urban 30-somethings. The author notes China's high school enrollment rate is only 55%; this couple's education level already exceeds 45% of Chinese people. Critics use "changing customs" as cover for urban-rural discrimination.

The author states this was his "first essay going full force attacking the feminist cult on Zhihu," and it is considered one of the important early essays in China's anti-feminist movement. The essay points out that urban working-class women use "changing customs" as cover to vent discrimination and hatred against rural people.

2 A.2 On Zhang Guimei

Written in New York in December 2020, originally on Zhihu, gained over 10,000 likes before being deleted. The essay criticizes Zhang Guimei as a "childless, extremist feminist leader," arguing that promoting "leaving the mountains" is essentially urban-rural and gender discrimination — mountain girls are encouraged to leave home rather than build it, while mountain boys are left behind.

The author uses an analogy: imagine "Principal Li Xinye" founded a "Women's College Preparatory School for America" in Raoping's mountains, with 90%+ students getting full scholarships to American universities and marrying Americans — rural people would also feel uncomfortable seeing such promotion. The essay argues that the Zhang Guimei promotion is full of "old white male mansplaining" and "racial microaggression."

"A society with a few extreme egoists thinking this way is not a big problem, but promoting this kind of thinking as correct ideology — that's a big mistake."

3 A.3 My Father Li Songjian

Written on Father's Day, June 15, 2025 in Manhattan. The essay reveals the "three faces" of real estate developer father Li Songjian. The first face: kowtowing to masters — since failing the college entrance exam, he began "adopting fathers," worshiping figures like Shanghai's former party secretary Chen Liangyu, even decorating his rural office meeting room in Shanghai Municipal Committee style. He gifted the author "Having Officialdom in You," and when learning his son wanted to attend MIT, angrily urged him to go to Beijing instead — "maybe some leader's daughter will take a liking to you."

Second Face: Devotion to Goddesses

Lifelong pursuit of female approval, viewing women as gods. Ling Feifei divorced and took billions while he was in prison; after release he still gladly accepted it. Company's female "period leave" benefits better than Nordic countries.

Third Face: Contempt for Servants

Young men are all servants. Hates highly educated people — "Fudan and SJTU grads still work for me." In 2021 told author to sell beef balls on Taobao; in 2025 raged at unfinished buildings: "Why aren't you servants buying my houses?"

The essay went viral on WeChat, described as "an instant sensation."

4 A.4 My Sister Li Xinying

Written July 19, 2025. Tells the tragic life of sister Li Xinying: born in 1989 in Shantou, originally holding a "strong female lead script" (grandfather was town mayor, grandfather-in-law was school principal, father was worth over 100 million). After the father's affair with Ling Feifei, 3-year-old sister was sent to live with the tuberculosis-infected Ling Feifei, contracted TB, and nearly died from high fever and unconsciousness. The father rescued her, but the sister suffered permanent intellectual damage and facial scarring.

The father then sent the sister back to the mother (without disclosing the TB), causing the author, mother, and maternal grandfather to all contract TB — the grandfather later died of lung complications. After the father went to prison, Ling Feifei backstabbed and divorced, taking half the assets. The father later found Xu Jie ("Professor Chicken"), who bore a half-brother Li Xinquan. The sister was kept by the father as a "servant," at 37 years old running a Chaoyang beef hotpot restaurant alone, daily serving as "Professor Chicken's dog" accompanying her shopping.
