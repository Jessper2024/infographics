Retrospective · Chapter 6: The Content of Retrospectives

What should you discuss in a retrospective? Three core questions: What is the current situation, how did we decide, and let us re-examine our assumptions — from facts to logic to foundations, building a complete retrospective framework.

This chapter opens with a failed sales retrospective at Company H. It reveals why most organizational retrospectives become hollow rituals — they do not focus on the core content of the retrospective. The author proposes three progressive layers: ①Facts (What is the current situation — pure information, no opinions), ②Decision logic (How did we decide — decision form → rationale → execution), ③Foundations (Re-examine assumptions — motivation integrity and understanding of the essence). These three questions form a pyramid-style framework, supported by practical checklist tools.

01
FACTS

What Is the Current Situation — Pure Facts, Zero Opinions

The first question demands pure facts. Provide information only, with no opinions mixed in. Progress, completion status, participants, and obstacles — all answered as information, not decorated judgments. If you missed the target, say so directly. Do not twist logic by claiming partial success from another angle. Facts form the foundation for all subsequent discussion. A weak foundation wastes everything.

📊

No debate needed — just dig deeper

⚡ Key Principle: When debate arises at the "current situation" stage, you are stating opinions, not facts. Withdraw from the debate immediately and return to pure information. Facts do not cause arguments — I left at 7 PM, and the attendance record proves it.
02
DECISION FORM

How Did We Decide — Examining the Form and Mindset Behind the Decision

"How was this decided" is the button for self-growth. It points to the decision form and the individual's mindset within it. Retrospectives do more than find causes. They explore different possibilities and the boundaries of success and failure through reasoning. This leads to personal growth. Once you grow your own abilities, finding causes and methods becomes natural — this is where retrospectives surpass ordinary reviews.

💡 Deeper Value: By reviewing these aspects, you can recognize your own character and dig into your inner self. After repeated reviews, your character improves and your mind opens. You unite more people and achieve greater things. In the opening case, this dimension was completely absent.
03
DECISION RATIONALE

How Did We Decide — Scrutinizing the Evidence and Logic

"What was this decision based on" points to the logic and evidence chain behind the decision. What evidence supported the decision at the time? Does it still hold? Is the evidence itself sound? By reviewing the evidence, you decide whether to persist or pivot — if the evidence still stands, wait for results to come; if the evidence has changed, adjust your goals and methods.

⚠️ Classic Case: TCL Acquires Thomson
The evidence supporting the acquisition — Thomson's European network and TV technology advantage — did not exist. New obstacles also emerged (difficult layoffs, different working methods). After years of losses, TCL liquidated the European subsidiary. Evidence review is the basic audit before any action.
04
EXECUTION

How Did We Decide — Reviewing Execution: Go Beyond the Surface

Execution review must not stop at "what we did" or "where we fell short." Use the original execution plan as the reference frame. Late shipments, poor communication, weak promotion — these are the easiest things to notice. But real execution review asks: What were the original execution rules? Did we follow the plan strictly? What surprises and new situations appeared?

⚡

Common Mistake: Surface Attribution

"Too few products," "not enough promotion," "late delivery" — these name the problem but do not trace the cause or the rules. Issues get raised again and again, yet nothing changes.

🎯

Right Approach: Check Against the Rules

If the rule said "submit shipping list one week early" → ask when the list was submitted → was it late submission or late warehouse action → should the process change?

With execution rules in place, the retrospective goes beyond finding causes and problems. It can explore solutions and implementation. This is the true goal — upgrading from "knowing what is wrong" to "knowing how to fix it and who owns it."

05
ASSUMPTIONS

Re-examine Our Assumptions — The Foundation of Retrospective

If the foundation is wrong, no matter how brilliant the logic or how perfect the execution, you may still reach the wrong result. Assumptions include two aspects: ①Motivation review — pursue "public good" (act for everyone) and more importantly "righteousness" (align with the true nature of the matter); ②Essence understanding — your grasp of the essence shapes your strategy. "Doing the right thing" matters more than "doing things right."

⚠️ Classic Case: Internet Companies Making Phones
In 2012, NetEase dissolved its phone division, Shanda's hardware lead resigned, and the 360 phone drew a lukewarm response. Kai-Fu Lee commented: "They pursued embedding their own services, not serving user needs." From the start, the intentions were wrong, and the path naturally went astray. In everything you do, motives must be pure and understanding must be sound.
▎KEY TAKEAWAY

The three questions of retrospective content form a complete pyramid framework: Facts are the foundation (What is the current situation → pure information), Logic is the body (How did we decide → decision form / rationale / execution), Assumptions are the root (Re-examine thinking → motivation integrity and essence understanding). All three are essential, and their order cannot be reversed. Combined with checklist tools, any retrospective becomes systematic and actionable — upgrading from "knowing what is wrong" to "knowing how to fix it."

The core value of retrospectives beyond ordinary reviews: explore different possibilities through reasoning and achieve personal growth. Once you grow your own abilities, finding causes and methods becomes natural.
