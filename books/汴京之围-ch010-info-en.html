The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.10 "Endless Trouble"

Lone Defender · Close Combat · Diplomatic Catastrophe · The People Pay Li Gang, a mere Vice Minister of War, nearly single-handedly saved Bianjing — repelling the Jurchen assault at Suanzaomen Gate in fierce combat. But Emperor Qinzong, consumed by terror, accepted all Jurchen terms: 5 million taels of gold, 50 million taels of silver, and the cession of three strategic towns. An impossible ransom plunged the capital's population into hell.

This chapter chronicles the First Siege of Bianjing. On January 5, Li Gang almost single-handedly prevented the emperor's flight, then scrambled to organize defenses in just five days. From January 7-9, the Jurchen launched fierce assaults on the West Water Gate and the four northern gates. Li Gang countered with hooked poles against fire ships, Cai Jing's rock-garden stones to block the waterway, and Divine-Arm crossbows. The Battle of Suanzaomen Gate killed thousands of Jurchen in a single day. Yet battlefield victory brought no advantage at the negotiating table — Qinzong, in terror, accepted all Jurchen terms: 5 million taels of gold, 50 million taels of silver, cession of Taiyuan, Zhongshan, and Hejian, plus a prince as hostage. By January 20, the tally: barely 300,000+ taels of gold (6%) and 12 million+ taels of silver (24%) collected. The emperor's catastrophic ignorance of his own capital's wealth sealed the tragedy.

01
Fortifications
Li Gang's Five-Day Miracle: Arming a City That Had Not Seen War in 160 Years
Bianjing had not faced battle for over 160 years — its defensive towers had been demolished in Huizong's beautification projects. Li Gang had five days to turn a city of peace into a fortress of war. The outer wall stretched 50 li and 165 paces, with bastions every 100 paces and a moat over 10 zhang wide. But the capital works director stated bluntly: "This city cannot be defended" — the wooden towers were gone, the new wall surface incompatible with old tower designs, requiring 5,000 carpenters for a month to rebuild. Li Gang deployed 12,000 regular troops per wall section, organized five mobile armies of 8,000 each, posted the vanguard to guard the Yanfeng Granary (400,000 shi of grain), and stationed the rear guard at Fanjiagang Heights. With iron discipline, he built a defensive line from despair.

02
Battle of Bianjing
Suanzaomen Gate Bloodbath: Li Gang Executed Panic-Killers, Then Killed Thousands of Jurchen
At dawn on January 9, the Jurchen launched their fiercest assault on Suanzaomen Gate — Li Gang galloped twenty li from the palace, arriving just before the gate fell. At the height of battle, six or seven severed heads of Song soldiers were thrown from the tower — guards claimed they were spies. Li Gang recognized this as panic, not counter-espionage: soldiers, terrified, were killing anyone they suspected. Once such hysteria spread, the city would surely fall. He immediately beheaded the killers and decreed: any accusation of spying must bring the accused to an officer for questioning; unauthorized killing meant immediate execution. With panic contained, he organized layered defense: heavy crossbows and catapults at long range, Divine-Arm bows at medium range, rolling logs and hand-bombs at close range. Dare-to-die squads rappelled down the walls, burning a dozen Jurchen ladders and killing over ten Jurchen officers (identified by their golden earrings). From dawn to dusk, thousands of Jurchen fell. Suanzaomen Gate bristled with arrows like a porcupine — the First Siege of Bianjing ended in Song victory.

03
Negotiation Collapse
An Impossible Ransom: The Jurchen Demanded Ten Times Qinzong's Limit — and He Accepted
Victory on the battlefield was completely squandered at the negotiating table — Qinzong, in terror, accepted terms the Jurchen themselves might not have dared to imagine. Two fatal problems emerged. First, Jurchen envoy Xiao Sanbaonu demanded 500,000 ingots of gold and 1,000,000 ingots of silver (5 million and 50 million taels respectively) — ten times Qinzong's original ceiling — and the emperor immediately agreed. Second, chief envoy Li Zhi remained mute throughout negotiations, earning mockery as "a woman" from the Jurchen, while deputy Zheng Wangzhi alone dared to argue. Li Gang urged stalling until relief armies arrived, but Qinzong was determined to buy peace at any price: 5 million taels gold, 50 million taels silver, 10,000 each of horses, cattle, and mules, 1,000 camels, 2 million bolts of silk, plus cession of Taiyuan, Zhongshan, and Hejian — the three gateways to the Central Plains — along with Prince Kang Zhao Gou as hostage.

04
Civilian Extortion
From Eunuchs to Courtesans: The Emperor's Catastrophic Ignorance of His Capital's Gold Reserves
Qinzong made one fatal mistake — he had no idea how much gold and silver his capital actually held, yet agreed to a number that could never be met. From January 10, the requisition orders escalated mercilessly: first, the estates of Cai Jing, Tong Guan, and the "Six Traitors" were seized; then palace eunuchs, Buddhist and Taoist clergy, and physicians were forced to surrender their gold and silver; next, civilians were "requested" to loan their holdings, with informants receiving half; finally, the red-light district — including the legendary courtesan Li Shishi — was stripped bare. By January 20, only 300,000-plus taels of gold (6% of target) and 12 million-plus taels of silver (24%) had been collected. An imperial edict threatened the populace: if the ransom fell short, the Jurchen would "kill every man, abduct every woman, burn every house." In desperation, the emperor began using palace treasures — jade, rhinoceros horn, ivory — as substitute payment, laid out in open fields for Jurchen appraisal. By January 28, the total: 517,000 taels of gold, 14,302,000 taels of silver — barely a tenth of the demand.

05
Scapegoating
Li Gang Dismissed, Li Zhi Scapegoated: The Post-Treaty Purge
After the treaty was signed, Qinzong began purging the "responsible parties" — not himself, but the envoys who had carried out his orders. Li Gang, for opposing the cession of the three towns and the gold collection, was sidelined to defense duties only, while peace-party ministers controlled negotiations. Prince Kang Zhao Gou and Vice Chancellor Zhang Bangchang were sent to the Jurchen camp as hostages — Zhao Gou comported himself with dignity, and Wolibu said it was "like meeting a brother." Li Gang secretly withheld the imperial orders to cede the three towns — as long as the towns never received the order, they would keep fighting. But Taiyuan's cession order had already been delivered through Jurchen channels. On January 17, the original envoys Li Zhi and Zheng Wangzhi were dismissed as scapegoats. Yet five days later, with no one else willing to explain the gold shortfall to the Jurchen, the emperor recalled them — they had no choice but to return to the Jurchen camp, offering palace treasures as substitute payment. The fate of Song diplomats was as tragic as it was absurd.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Li Gang proved on the battlefield that Bianjing could be defended — the Jurchen had barely 60,000 troops against an impregnable capital. But Qinzong's terror-driven capitulation proved more devastating than Jurchen swords. He agreed to an impossible ransom (5M gold, 50M silver), ceded the North's last three gateways (Taiyuan, Zhongshan, Hejian), and with his own hands pushed the empire past the point of no return. More absurdly: when all Bianjing could yield was 517,000 taels of gold and 14.3 million of silver, he dared neither admit the treaty was unfulfillable nor fight to the end — merely feeding the capital's people, its palace treasures, and even his own younger brother Prince Kang, one after another, into the Jurchen maw of greed.
