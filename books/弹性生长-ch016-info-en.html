弹性生长 · Ch.16
「Population Decline Isn't Scary, Talent Waste Is」

One Genius Contributes More to a Field Than a Million Ordinary People Fumbling Around

📖 Chapter Overview

In the early 20th century, Germany had only 65 million people but 962 physicists—far more than Britain, France, or the US. The author uses this to make a core argument: we obsess over population quantity while neglecting depth of talent discovery. Scientific research doesn't follow "strength in numbers"—one genius contributes vastly more than a million ordinary people. Chip guru Jim Keller sparks tech revolutions everywhere he goes. Instead of worrying about people not having children, worry about whether talent among those already born is being wasted. China's population base is already enormous; managing existing stock matters more than fretting about additions. Technology will liberate and replace human labor, but it depends entirely on discovering geniuses.

🔍 Deep Dive
Germany's Education Code: Industrialization + Master Cultivation
In 1810, Humboldt founded the University of Berlin, combining research with teaching and emphasizing academic freedom. Prussia's military leaders recognized science's importance to defense and invested generously in education. To co-opt the academic old aristocracy, they lavishly funded universities. This system simultaneously produced industrial workers and great minds—until Hitler's racial purges drove Einstein, von Neumann, and others to America. By contrast, China's universities have been optimized for mass-producing industrial technicians, relatively utilitarian with mediocre top-talent cultivation.
Extremistan: The 0-vs-1 Chasm Between Genius and Ordinary
The author's personal case: an all-elite-school team spent four months optimizing 0.2%. A master re-wrote the core algorithm in one week—4% improvement, 20× better. Top algorithm geniuses earn 1.1M yuan straight out of school; ordinary coders start around 100K. The true gap isn't 10× but 100–1000×, or simply 0 vs. 1. Chip guru Jim Keller: Alpha chip → Apple A-series → AMD Zen → Tesla FSD—sparking revolutions wherever he goes. In hard sciences, equality doesn't exist.
Path Dependency: Abundant Labor Blocked Tech Progress
Why ancient China stagnated technologically: no need to develop, just throw manpower at problems. The Grand Canal used manual labor; the court resisted more efficient sea transport—a million boatmen's livelihoods depended on it. The West, with fewer people, kept trying machinery to replace labor, sparking the tech revolution. From Song to Qing, per-capita GDP was flat for a millennium, declining 30% in Qing due to population explosion. Even today, many "tech" companies are essentially labor-intensive operations.
The Future Already Happening: Machines Replacing 100M+ Workers
McKinsey estimates 20% of Chinese workers replaced by robots by 2030—100M+ people. Wahaha's assembly line shrank from 200–300 workers to a handful. Foxconn replaced 400,000 workers with robotic arms in 5 years. Agricultural Bank: 638 engineers improved automation by 3%, eliminating nearly 30,000 teller positions. You can't worry about both unemployment and population decline. The likeliest future crisis is tech-driven job loss. But China must go this path—if we don't, America will, profiting while we suffer. America's hedge: invest in tech stocks to enjoy the dividends.
From Demographic Dividend to Talent Dividend: One Person, Two Contributions
The demographic dividend is fundamentally debt—a sudden labor surge must eventually be repaid. Expecting more young people to support the elderly is a Ponzi scheme. The real answer: let one young person provide two people's contributions. One excavator equals thousands of workers; one programmer's social insurance payments equal twenty construction workers'; one rail line's annual transport equals six centuries of canal shipping; one genius's innovation profits match tens of thousands of workers' contributions. The key: technology, wielded by geniuses, unleashes value. China's population base is already enormous—if this isn't enough to discover talent, more won't help.
💡 Key Takeaway

Worry less about the birth rate and more about whether talent among those already born is being wasted. In science, there's no "strength in numbers"—one genius overwhelms a million ordinary people. We've exited the era of cheap, abundant labor; now it's a battle of cutting-edge capability that demands refined talent selection and cultivation. Technology will replace vast amounts of human labor (100M+ by 2030), but conversely, one top genius aided by technology can contribute "one to ten thousand." Believe in productivity gains from technological progress—that's the ultimate answer to the population question.
