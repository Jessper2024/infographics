The Gay Genius · Ch.25 「Second Exile」

His wife and the Empress Dowager both died. At 57, Su Tungpo was banished 1,500 li to Lingnan — demoted three times en route. When fate takes everything, how does a man keep his inner freedom?

In 1093, Su Tungpo's wife and the Empress Dowager died in succession. With both guardian spirits gone, his fortune plummeted. Zhang Chun became prime minister, raised the banner of "Continuing the Sacred Heritage," and purged over thirty high officials of the Yuanyou era. Su's dismissal edict was venomous: "Your writing is sufficient to delude the masses; your eloquence is sufficient to gloss over wrongs." He was banished to Huizhou in Lingnan, demoted three more times along the way. Yet he embarked on exile with "a strange sense of elevation," stood atop Dayu Ridge where the clean mountain wind swept away all worldly thoughts, and at Pine Wind Pavilion realized: "What place is there where one cannot rest?" — finding the highest point of the spirit at the lowest point of fortune.

01
Political Purge
Zhang Chun's Axe: A Precision Political Liquidation
Once Zhang Chun became prime minister, he raised the banner of "Continuing the Sacred Heritage" and banished over thirty Yuanyou officials. Su's dismissal edict read: "Your writing is sufficient to delude the masses; your eloquence is sufficient to gloss over wrongs. Having cut yourself off from sovereign and kin, against whom can you complain?" The charge was that Su had drafted dismissal orders for Wang Anshi's allies on the Empress Dowager's command — "slandering the late emperor" became the standard pretext. The irony: Zhang Chun had been Su's youthful traveling companion, and Su had once joked that Zhang "would kill people someday."

02
Journey's Miracle
Midnight Boat: A Miracle at the Dragon King Temple
The fourth demotion order arrived at midnight. Soldiers came to seize the boat. Su negotiated a noon deadline, but the harbor was still twelve li away — if the wind turned unfavorable, his entire family would be thrown off the boat with all their belongings. He went to the Dragon King Temple to pray. A fierce wind filled the sails, and by dawn they had reached their destination safely. Years later, on his return journey, Su solemnly returned to thank the Dragon King. Along 1,500 li, demoted three times — and at every stop he found a way to keep living.

03
Spiritual Liberation
Enlightenment on Dayu Ridge: Forgetting the Physical Body
Dayu Ridge was the ancient passage into Guangdong. Standing at its highest point, with clouds near his head and green-shaded stone steps stretching in both directions, Su Tungpo looked down upon the pettiness of human affairs from this land far from the political center. The clean mountain wind swept all worldly thoughts from his mind. He also visited Nanhua Temple, the sacred site of Chan Buddhism. The moment he crossed Dayu Ridge, he ceased to be an exiled criminal — he became a free man between heaven and earth.

04
Philosophy of Mind
What Place Is There Where One Cannot Rest?
After moving to Jiayou Temple, Su often lingered at Pine Wind Pavilion on the hilltop. One day, about to return home, he saw his house appearing high above the treetops — the distance was considerable, and his legs suddenly felt tired. Then a thought struck: "What place is there where one cannot rest?" Instantly his mind felt like a hooked fish suddenly freed. He returned to his natural state: closing the door, sitting silently, enjoying sandalwood incense; napping as crows and magpies interrupted his dreams; watching the river's light and shadow enter his room. In a single thought, he transformed a place of exile into a sanctuary for the soul.

"What place is there where one cannot rest? Thereupon my mind was like a hooked fish suddenly gaining release. If one understands this, then at such a moment one may also freely rest." — Su Tungpo, A Visit to Pine Wind Pavilion

05
True Freedom
Accepting Fate Without a Shadow of Doubt
Arriving in Huizhou, Su discovered that "ten thousand homes in Lingnan are all spring colors" and decided to settle there. He accepted his fate with unprecedented equanimity — not forced submission, but active reconciliation. "What place is there where one cannot rest?" is not passive resignation; it is a sage's insight into the nature of freedom: freedom is not about where you are, but how you see where you are. While Zhang Chun's axe still hung over his head, Su had already found, within his own heart, a spring that no one could take away.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Su Tungpo's second exile is the most profound interpretation of "freedom." Freedom is not where you are, but how you see your situation. When he forgot his physical body on Dayu Ridge and discovered at Pine Wind Pavilion that "what place is there where one cannot rest," he was already freer than the enemies who sat in the high halls of power. The true prison was never in Lingnan — it was in the mind. To accept fate — not as forced submission, but without a shadow of doubt — and then, on that small patch of ground you have found, to plant spring once again.
