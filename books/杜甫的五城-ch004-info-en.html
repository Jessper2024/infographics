杜甫的五城 · 第4章「By Chang'an Waters, Many Beauties」

Xi'an · Zhao Mausoleum · Cuihua Mountain

In late August 1989, Lai donned Shanghai-made 'Panda' canvas shoes and a mainland-produced shirt, boarded Train No. 272 from Guangzhou, and began the 36-hour, two-day-two-night journey to Xi'an. Crossing the great Henan plain, passing Luoyang at midnight, he finally arrived at the destination of a decade's dreams: Tang-dynasty Chang'an. Cycling across Xi'an (8 km from north to south), he climbed the Giant Wild Goose Pagoda to confirm his hypothesis: 'You cannot see Mount Zhongnan from within Xi'an.' He hired a car to the Zhao Mausoleum, hiked to the summit of Mount Jiuzong to decode 'mountain-burial,' cycled to the Daming Palace ruins to mourn the exiled minister Li Deyu, and finally climbed Cuihua Mountain—where, as clouds parted, the evergreen peak of Mount Zhongnan filled his eyes.

01Disguise: Blending into Chang'an's Scenery
During his warm-up trip, a Changsha manager identified the brand of his shirt and his tailored trousers—Lai realized he was dressed 'too well,' incongruous with the mainland landscape. Back in Hong Kong, he bought mainland-produced short-sleeve shirts and Shanghai 'Panda' canvas shoes, replacing his branded sneakers and imported luggage. He rejected the romantic 'backpacker' posture: in China, a big backpack 'tells everyone: Look! I'm a foreigner carrying dollars!' He advocated 'carrying' a small bag, like locals traveling truly light—a toothbrush, toothpaste, face towel, and enamel mug. This was the deepest form of 'blending in.'
02Train No. 272: 36 Hours into the Tang
On Platform 6 at Guangzhou station, the sign 'Guangzhou—Xi'an' hung on the dark-green train—the first time he saw 'Xi'an' on a train. All afternoon, the train crossed the great Henan plain: 'Only in Henan do you understand what distance means!' No mountains, no hills, scarcely a tree. Passing Luoyang at midnight, he jumped onto the platform: 'Luoyang—city where paper was once too expensive, Luoyang of the Record of Buddhist Monasteries.' He'd nearly chosen Sui-Tang Luoyang as his dissertation topic. On the Longhai railway, he was 'too excited to sleep': a thousand years ago, how many soldiers, scholars, and exam candidates traveled this same road to the western capital?
03The Goose Pagoda: A Geographic Discovery
First thing in Xi'an: sleep three hours. Then 'study' the map—Chang'an's streets were 'like a chessboard,' still perfectly gridded today. Renting a bicycle, he pedaled 8 km from Jiefang Hotel in the north to the Giant Wild Goose Pagoda in the south, taking nearly two hours. Under plane trees on both sides, 'many elegant modern Xi'an women cycled past in high heels'—Du Fu's 'On the third day of the third month, weather fresh, by Chang'an waters many beauties.' Ascending the pagoda, he confirmed his decade-old hypothesis: from within Xi'an, you cannot see any mountains! Not even a hint of Mount Zhongnan—it lies over 30 km away. Tang poets' verses about 'gazing toward the Zhao Mausoleum' were symbolic gestures, not literal vision. This also meant 'Chang'an has mountain defenses' was a historian's cliché—no wonder An Lushan, Huang Chao, and even Tibetan troops had all breached the city.
04The Zhao Mausoleum: Decoding History on Mount Jiuzong
Hiring a car for the day (200 yuan), driven by Master Cao in his 'bridal-red' minivan to the Zhao Mausoleum. Its museum held stelae of figures he knew from the Old Tang History: Fang Xuanling, Yuchi Jingde, Kong Yingda—'touching the cold stone with my hand, I felt as though I were touching history.' He also discovered an original Tang chiwei roof ornament—the same design atop Xi'an's railway station! Driving up Mount Jiuzong, the road ended at two-thirds height. He hiked alone for an hour to the summit. The peak held only green grass, rapeseed, and autumn chrysanthemums—'not an imperial tomb garden, but pastoral serenity.' He finally understood 'mountain-burial': Emperor Taizong's tomb chamber was built at the summit, not midway or at the base. A 400-meter wooden causeway was constructed to carry the coffin up the steep peak. When Emperor Gaozong ordered the causeway dismantled for security, he wept and refused—without it, he himself could no longer visit his father's resting place.
05Mount Zhongnan: A Millennial Rendezvous in the Clouds
Before leaving Xi'an, he rented a car again to climb Cuihua Mountain. On the winding trail, he met two elderly brothers driving cattle home barefoot. 'Master, which peak is Mount Zhongnan?'—'That one.' Wreathed in cloud, the two old men and their cattle vanished into mist, 'as if they had suddenly walked into the clouds and disappeared.' Reaching the summit, rounding a bend: an emerald alpine lake, morning sun illuminating mist rising from its surface, waterbirds taking flight. A flock of lambs filed along the right shore. Behind the lake, peaks rose—'At last I saw the highest Mount Zhongnan! Its evergreen summit, half-hidden, half-visible among white clouds.' Sunlight, lake, cloud, mountain shadows wove a shifting painting. He imagined rowing a skiff across the silent lake, rounding a bend, vanishing behind a mountain, sailing toward the eternal Mount Zhongnan.
💡 KEY INSIGHT
A decade of scholarly hypotheses, a 36-hour train, 8 km cycling, 1,188 meters of climbing—the Tang dynasty lives not in books, but unfolds inch by inch through landscapes walked with one's own feet.
