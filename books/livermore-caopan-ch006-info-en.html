How to Trade in Stocks · Ch.6 "The Million-Dollar Blunder"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Livermore formed a strongly bullish view on cotton and planned to accumulate 100,000 bales in stages once the market broke through the key point. But he rushed in five times in six weeks, losing nearly $200,000 — and worse, removed the ticker in disgust, missing the entire 500-point rally. Why would an experienced speculator miss his most certain opportunity? The answer lies in patience, emotion, and the trap of inside tips.

This chapter is Livermore's most candid confession — what he calls "the million-dollar blunder." The painful cotton lesson reveals two iron laws of speculation: first, even when your judgment is correct, acting prematurely is just as deadly — you must wait for the market to reach the pivotal psychological moment; second, when you err, never indulge in anger or disgust — admit it, study your records, and extract the lesson. He also reveals two advanced faculties: sensing danger signals through subconscious intuition developed over years of market apprenticeship, and forever being wary of every kind of inside information.
01
Position Sizing
Scale In Gradually — Never Pile Everything Into One Price
Too many speculators impulsively buy or sell everything at one price. Livermore's iron rule is the opposite: scale in gradually — buy each lot at a higher level on the long side, sell each at a lower level on the short side. This keeps you closer to the right side of the market than any other method.

02
Timing Rule
Wait for the Pivotal Psychological Moment — Timing Is Everything
Livermore's routine: Step 1, estimate the potential extent of the coming move. Step 2, study price records to determine the entry level. Step 3, wait patiently for the market to reach that key point. Step 4, make your first commitment and set your risk limit. Consistently re-enter at the pivotal point and you cannot miss the real move.

03
Case Study
The Cotton Market — The Most Expensive Six Weeks
Livermore recounts his most humiliating experience: knowing cotton would only signal its real move at 12½ cents, yet he simply could not wait — five impulsive buys in six weeks, each pushing prices up 15 points before falling back, losing nearly $200,000. Worse, he removed the ticker in disgust — two days later, cotton launched a 500-point rally and he missed it all.

04
Root Cause Analysis
Two Root Causes — Impatience and Emotional Hijack
The two root causes of the cotton disaster: first, lacking the patience to wait for the psychological moment, trying to "get a little extra" before the key point; second, letting anger at his misjudgment turn into disgust with the market, allowing emotion to displace reason. The cure: admit mistakes openly, study your records, and wait for the next big opportunity.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Impatience is the speculator's most expensive mistake. The two lessons of the cotton disaster are timeless: scale into positions gradually and wait for the pivotal psychological moment — never act before the market is ready. When you err, don't get angry or make excuses; admit it, study your records, and extract the lesson. More advanced judgment comes from long market apprenticeship — when you feel restless and unable to sleep, your subconscious may be sending a danger signal. Carry a notebook, but let its first page forever read: Beware of all inside information.
