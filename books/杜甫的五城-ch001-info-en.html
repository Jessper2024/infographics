杜甫的五城 · 第1章「Halfway Through Life's Journey」

Gongbei · Guangzhou · Changsha · Yueyang

In the summer of 1989, at age 35—halfway through life, as Dante's Divine Comedy opens—Malaysian Tang historian Lai Ruihe, teaching at Lingnan College in Hong Kong, finally set foot on the Chinese mainland. Entering via Gongbei, he took the No. 76 soft-sleeper train from Guangzhou to Changsha to visit the Mawangdui Han tomb excavation site, then a hard-seat train to Yueyang to climb the Yueyang Tower overlooking Lake Dongting. In just a few days, he sampled all three classes of China's railway system—a perfect warm-up for his grand Tang-dynasty journey ahead.

01From Princeton to Gongbei
A decade earlier, Professor Liu Zijian's sweeping gesture at Princeton—'Xi'an is ringed entirely by Mount Zhongnan'—ignited a dream. From teenage wuxia fantasies to his doctoral dissertation on Tang military and border defense, Lai built a paper Tang empire through scholarship. Du Fu's line 'How distant the Five Cities? Distant, divided by the river' crystallized his vow: one day, trace the Five Cities by train. In 1988, a teaching post in Hong Kong with three-month summers finally opened the door.
02Train No. 76: A Heavenly Blessing
Entering via Gongbei, he bought his first ticket at Guangzhou station—guided to the foreign- VIP window, paying 94.60 yuan in foreign exchange certificates (half a month's salary for ordinary citizens) for soft-sleeper berth on the newly launched No. 76. An American rail-writer had called a four-berth compartment to oneself 'a heavenly blessing.' A cigarette factory manager boarded mid-journey to upgrade, revealing China's railway micro-society: rank determines tickets, connections determine upgrades.
03Mawangdui: The Excavation Site Itself
Unsatisfied with museum exhibits behind glass, Lai hired a taxi to the eastern outskirts of Changsha to find the actual Mawangdui excavation site. Tomb No. 1 descended 20.5 meters from mound top to floor—over six stories deep—a breathtaking sight. He recalled the archaeological reports read at Princeton, and the un-looted tombs of Mancheng and Anyang that yielded spectacular treasures.
04Yueyang: Idyllic Charm vs. Pollution
A two-hour hard-seat ride from Changsha brought him to Yueyang, where he tried an 8-yuan guesthouse bed, sharing a room with an elderly man from western Hunan. Climbing the Yueyang Tower, Du Fu's verse 'Long have I heard of Dongting's waters, now I ascend the Yueyang Tower' echoed. But at the lakeshore, a red warning: 'Schistosomiasis danger zone—no entry without protective gear!' Schoolchildren on Junshan Island confirmed: classmates already infected, feet swollen.
05Railway Stations: Spiritual Landmarks
In Yueyang, what captivated Lai most was not the tower but the small clay-yellow railway station—'a special leisurely charm of small-town life.' He grew up beside a railway station: the 7:10 AM passenger train was his alarm, the midnight freight whistle the day's last lullaby. Now, this 'railway fanatic' would let his childhood trains carry him into the Tang empire of his dreams.
💡 KEY INSIGHT
The true starting point of a journey lies not in the destination but in that decade-old embracing gesture—Professor Liu Zijian's arms encircling Mount Zhongnan had already written the prologue.
