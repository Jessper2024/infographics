Understanding Our Complex World · Ch.27 "What 'Internal Circulation' Really Means"

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer Global overcapacity—capitalists' profits don't get consumed, workers' wages can't buy everything, and cyclical crises shadow every boom. China long relied on export-led growth; whenever the outside world caught a cold, the domestic economy came down with a fever—a fate eerily similar to pre-WWII Germany. How can China achieve sustainable development and shed external dependency? Answer: Internal circulation as the main engine, with dual circulation reinforcing it—short-term transfer payments to boost grassroots consumption, long-term technological breakthroughs to create new growth poles.

This chapter begins with the fundamental contradiction of "overcapacity"—as long as merchants pursue profit, some returns will never enter consumption, and the corresponding goods will go unsold. Since the Industrial Revolution, Britain solved it through colonial dumping, Germany through military-industrial external circulation, and the U.S. through Roosevelt's reforms pivoting to a large domestic market. China's current strategy of "internal circulation as primary, dual circulation for growth" is both a historical necessity and a crisis-forced transformation. The author dissects this across three dimensions: the mathematics of overcapacity → the fragility of external circulation → the roadmap for building internal circulation. The ultimate answer: short-term, transfer wealth to the grassroots (poverty alleviation); long-term, use technological breakthroughs to spawn new industries and jobs (just as mobile internet created tens of millions of jobs).

01
Root Contradiction
The Math of Overcapacity: The Capitalist Earns 100, the Worker Gets 80
As long as merchants pursue profit, some proceeds will inevitably be withheld from consumption, and the corresponding goods will go unsold. Suppose goods are worth 1 million: the capitalist takes 200K in profit, workers get 800K in wages. Workers' 800K can't buy a full 1M in goods, and capitalists won't spend all their profits either—the wealthy consume a far smaller share of their income than the poor. That 200K gap is the root of overcapacity. Since day one of the Industrial Revolution, Britain faced this dilemma: infinite productivity, but its own workers couldn't afford the output. The solution? Open foreign markets—from gunboats forcing open Qing China's doors, to seizing India as a raw materials source and dumping ground.

02
Historical Lesson
The Fatal Fragility of External Circulation: Germany, From Great Depression to Hitler
Countries dependent on external markets have zero buffer—when the outside world crashes, mass unemployment follows instantly. After WWI, Germany rebuilt production on American loans and sold goods mainly to the U.S.—a classic export-oriented economy. When the 1929 crisis hit, America couldn't buy German goods; German factories halted, workers were laid off en masse. In despair, Hitler rose to power, using state debt to expand military production as a way to absorb excess capacity, then proceeded to plunder neighboring countries through armed force—creating a new "external circulation by force." Similarly, China was overly dependent on exports; whenever external demand contracted, the domestic economy fell ill. That's why the internal market must be made large enough to create a dual-circulation system: don't abandon the external, but let the internal lead.
💀

❌ Pure External Circulation (Germany 1930s)

Export dependence → external market collapses → unemployment wave → military expansion → war circulation. No domestic buffer, one dead-end road.

🔄

✅ Dual Internal-External Circulation (China's Path)

Internal market as main engine, external as supplement. When the outside world catches a cold, the internal engine keeps running—a resilient economy.

03
Lessons from Others
America's Dual-Circulation Path: Roosevelt Tamed Inequality, "State-to-Civilian" Catalyzed Tech
Roosevelt's true brilliance was his prescient understanding that social stability requires a massive middle class. In 1929, the top 0.1% of Americans held 25% of all wealth, while 90% of the population shared just 16%. When ordinary people have no money to consume, you get an economic crisis. Roosevelt did two things right: ① broke up industrial-financial monopolies, levied heavy taxes on large corporations, implemented transfer payments, and raised workers' wages—transforming a "pyramid society" into a "football-shaped society"; ② "state-to-civilian"—atomic energy, computing, the internet, and genetic engineering were all developed by state power, then handed to the market to commoditize and democratize, creating massive high-income jobs and enormous domestic demand. That is true internal-external dual circulation.

China's Strategy
Short-Term Transfers, Long-Term Tech—China's Two-Step Internal Circulation Strategy
Internal circulation in the short term relies on fiscal transfers to the grassroots, boosting consumption capacity; in the long term, it depends on technological breakthroughs to ignite new growth engines. Why do tax cuts have completely different effects across income brackets? A household earning 1M/year getting a 100K rebate might spend it overseas → external circulation; those earning 300K are more likely to save for the next property purchase; only low-income groups are the most willing to spend locally—this is the economic rationale behind poverty alleviation. Mobile internet is the most successful "government builds the stage, enterprises perform" case: the state invested in network infrastructure → waves of internet companies emerged → tens of millions of high-paying jobs were created → technology reached remote rural areas. That is the decisive move.

05
Outlook
Crisis-Forced Transformation: Import Substitution and Retaining Wealthy Consumption at Home
China previously underinvested in its internal market; from now on, it must be treated as a national security issue—like food security. China's industrial categories are comprehensive, but high-end goods that wealthy consumers buy are heavily import-dependent: knives costing thousands of yuan, enameled cast-iron pots at two thousand, tents at ten thousand. Import substitution means domesticating these premium consumer goods. At the same time, learn from Singapore's model—gradually stabilize housing prices for the masses (non-core second/third/fourth-tier cities), while fully financializing core first- and second-tier areas, giving the wealthy a luxury residential outlet at home. In one sentence: the state supplements the poor, the rich spend domestically; technology must break through, imports must be substituted.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Internal circulation is not isolationism—it means building the domestic market into the economy's main engine: external markets as supplementary, domestic market as primary driver. Short-term, transfer payments boost grassroots consumption power (poverty alleviation); long-term, technological breakthroughs create new industries and new jobs (the mobile internet model). Three people earning 50K each contribute more to economic circulation than one person earning 300K. Every developed country needs a strong internal market to absorb productive capacity—otherwise, an external chill means no way to survive at home. Crisis is opportunity—only when the old road fails can we escape our comfort zone to do what is hard but right. That is the very source of progress.
