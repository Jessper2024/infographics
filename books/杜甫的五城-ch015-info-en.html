Five Cities of Du Fu · Ch.15 "Leisurely Journeys"

Shanghai · Hangzhou · Suzhou · Qiyang · Yongzhou · Fuzhou · Wuyishan · Huizhou · Humen

After two years of marathon travel, Lai Ruihe slows down for several short, spontaneous journeys. In 1990, he accompanies his mother back to her Meixian hometown after forty-plus years—her sister greets them with firecrackers, and the two elderly women dash through the exploding fireworks ‘with the excited, fearful gait of little girls dodging firecrackers.’ They tour Shanghai, Hangzhou, and Suzhou, savoring Dongpo Pork and West Lake Vinegar Fish. That winter, following Xu Xiake’s footsteps, Lai visits Qiyang to see Yuan Jie’s Wuxi cliff inscriptions and Yan Zhenqing’s calligraphy; then Yongzhou to walk Liu Zongyuan’s Eight Records. In summer 1991, he flies to Fuzhou specifically to eat Buddha Jumps Over the Wall—reserved by phone for ¥45. Finally, he visits Humen’s opium-destruction pools and conducts a scholarly investigation: Lin Zexu did not burn the opium but dissolved it chemically with salt and quicklime.

01Reunion Through Firecrackers: Mother’s Homecoming
Accompanying his mother back to Meixian after forty-plus years—her first return. The aunt lights a long string of firecrackers at the old family home: ‘Red paper scraps danced in the air.’ Mother and aunt dashed through the exploding fireworks ‘with the excited, fearful gait and laughter of little girls dodging firecrackers’—‘They seemed to have forgotten this was their first meeting in over forty years.’ They feast on Hakka specialties and Immortal’s Pastries, tour Beijing, Shanghai, Hangzhou, and Suzhou, sampling Dongpo Pork, West Lake Vinegar Fish, and Song Sao Fish Soup.
02Following Xu Xiake: Qiyang’s Wuxi & The Stele of Tang Revival
Winter 1990: Following Xu Xiake’s route to southern Hunan. In Qiyang, visiting Yuan Jie’s Wuxi cliff inscriptions. Wuxi—‘the character takes the water radical, but originally it was just the word for my, a character Yuan Jie invented himself. Wuxi means: my little stream.’ He sees Yan Zhenqing’s calligraphy of The Great Tang Revival Stele—seven meters tall, magnificent: ‘Over a thousand years later, it still stands at its original site.’
03Yongzhou: Walking Into Liu Zongyuan’s Landscape
Following Xu Xiake to Yongzhou, tracing Liu Zongyuan’s Eight Records of Yongzhou. Walking along Yu Stream, West Mountain on the left—‘That autumn, Liu Zongyuan had raised his cup and drunk deeply, collapsing in intoxication, unaware of the sun’s setting.’ An old farmer working his field expertly points: ‘There is Gumu Pool. That is the Little Hill. Further on is Little Rock Pool.’ The farmer seems like ‘a character stepping out of Liu Zongyuan’s collected works.’
04Flying a Thousand Miles For Buddha Jumps Over the Wall
Summer 1991: Traveling to Fuzhou specifically to eat Buddha Jumps Over the Wall. Reserving by phone—‘The first time I used a telephone in mainland China, and the purpose was to eat Buddha Jumps Over the Wall.’ One serving for ¥45, served in a delicate porcelain jar with sea cucumber, shark fin, scallops, and tendons. He visits Yan Fu’s study, Lin Zexu’s ancestral hall, Wuyi Mountain. Discovers northern Fujian’s proximity to Southern Song capital Hangzhou: ‘Zhu Xi was from Jiangxi but spent years teaching in the Wuyi mountains.’
05The Humen Opium Investigation: Not Burning but Chemistry
Visiting Lin Zexu’s opium-destruction pools at Humen. Textbooks commonly say burning the opium, but the actual method was chemical dissolution: channel river water into pools, dissolve massive amounts of salt, add opium, then quicklime for decomposition, released into the sea at low tide. The pools were deliberately built near the river—‘clearly the result of careful planning.’ With scholarly rigor, Lai corrects his poet friend’s verse about fierce flames rising from Humen: ‘The destruction of opium at Humen did not use fire.’
💡 Key Insight
From firecrackers welcoming a mother home after forty years, to following Xu Xiake’s footsteps to Yuan Jie and Liu Zongyuan’s literary landscapes, to flying a thousand miles for Buddha Jumps Over the Wall and investigating the chemistry of Humen’s opium destruction—these leisurely journeys, though short, weave together family love, literary devotion, and scholarly investigation. Lai Ruihe corrects a historical error with academic rigor, witnesses a mother-daughter reunion with a son’s tenderness, and savors the delicacies of Jiangnan and Fujian with a traveler’s curiosity. The best journeys are often the most spontaneous.
