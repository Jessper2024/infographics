The World of Yesterday · Ch.10 "Striving for a Noble Friendship"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Retreating to the suburbs could not escape the oppressive atmosphere of war. Passive non-resistance was not enough — Zweig had to act. How could he speak out for cross-border friendship and anti-war conviction under censorship? The answer: write with subtlety — praise pacifists in Austrian papers, spread his ideas through staged "rebuttals" in "enemy" papers, and ultimately pour his anti-war conviction into the play Jeremiah.

Zweig shifts from passive to active resistance: publishing "To Friends in Foreign Lands" and establishing a 25-year correspondence with Romain Rolland; witnessing the Red Cross's humanitarian feat; Rolland's earth-shaking "Above the Battle"; Zweig's failed attempt to organize an international writers' congress, followed by his frontline journey where he saw the real face of war — the horrors of hospital trains and brotherhood between enemies. Ultimately, he channels all his anti-war conviction into the play Jeremiah, finding spiritual salvation through art.

01
FRIENDSHIP
Signal Flares of Cross-Border Friendship: From "To Friends in Foreign Lands" to Rolland's Letter
Zweig published "To Friends in Foreign Lands" in the Berliner Tageblatt, openly pledging to rebuild European culture with foreign friends. Two weeks later, a letter with Swiss stamps arrived — Romain Rolland wrote: "No, I will never leave my friends." The letter was "like a white dove flying from Noah's Ark inhabited by howling, raging, maddened beasts." Their correspondence lasted 25 years until WWII severed all connections.
🕊️

Rolland's Letter

"No, I will never leave my friends." A few words confirming wartime correspondence was possible. One of Zweig's happiest moments in life.

✉️

Subtle Strategy

A French friend "rebutted" Zweig's article in Mercure de France — but published the full translation alongside, letting French readers see the original. Using "mock attacks" as signal flares.

02
HUMANITARIANISM
Romain Rolland: Practicing Humanity at the Geneva Red Cross
Following Whitman's example, Rolland refused to participate in destruction and served at the Geneva Red Cross. By December 1914, over 30,000 letters were processed daily, with 1,200 volunteers packed into the small Rath Museum. Rolland simultaneously published "Above the Battle" — just eight pages that shook the entire literary world, denouncing spiritual hatred between nations.

03
FIRSTHAND
The Frontline Journey: First Confrontation with War's True Face
Zweig traveled to the front under the pretext of collecting propaganda materials. In Tarnów, he saw Russian prisoners of war — but what struck him more was the guards sitting with prisoners "like companions," sharing cigarettes and family photos. "These rough, simple people understood war far better than professors and writers." He then endured hospital trains, witnessing horrors beyond his worst imaginings.
🤝

Brotherhood Between Enemies

Tyrolean guards and Russian POWs sharing cigarettes and photos. "All those caught in misfortune are brothers in suffering."

🚂

Hell on the Hospital Train

Windowless freight cars, one blackened oil lamp, stench of urine and iodine. Two or three corpses per car. Medicine exhausted; a priest said, "I never thought humans capable of such crimes."

04
AWAKENING
The Budapest Newspaper: War Lies Ignite the True Impulse to Write
Stepping off the hospital train, Zweig found Budapest carefree — ladies in white strolling arm-in-arm with officers, officers reeking of iodine buying violets for them. Only eight or nine hours from the front! When a waiter handed him a Viennese newspaper, "shameless war lies" made his blood boil. He found his true enemy — "those who use words to incite war" — and his true mission: to write against war.

05
CREATION
Jeremiah: Finding Spiritual Salvation Through Praising Defeat
Zweig chose Jeremiah — a futile warner — as the symbol for his anti-war play. Not a cliché pacifist drama, but a portrayal of "one despised in a feverish age who proves the only person who cannot endure defeat and can overcome it." The work touched his deep connection to Jewish fate. For the first time, he felt: "I must speak my heart and the heart of my age." Though unlikely to succeed, the play helped him survive his darkest hours.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
When the tide of hatred sweeps everything away, the true mission of intellectuals is not to join the chorus but to voice reason. Zweig and Rolland's cross-border friendship proves that even in war's darkest hour, the bonds of humanity cannot be severed. Writing — transforming pain into art — is the most enduring weapon against madness. Jeremiah is not merely an anti-war play; it is one man's spiritual salvation found in the abyss of his era.
