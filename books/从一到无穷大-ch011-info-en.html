01
Space Perception
Dimensions and Coordinates: Locating Everything with Three Numbers
We all know what space is, yet defining "space" proves surprisingly difficult. Space consists of three mutually independent and perpendicular directions — forward-back, left-right, up-down — which is why we call it three-dimensional. Any position in space can be pinpointed by these three coordinates. Cartesian coordinates (New York's grid-like streets) and polar coordinates (Moscow's radial boulevards centered on the Kremlin) are both valid positioning systems. We easily imagine 2D planes and 1D lines — but living inside 3D space makes it harder to conceive that space itself might be curved.

02
Topological Theorem
Euler's Theorem V+F=E+2: Geometry Without Measurement
Topology is the branch of geometry that studies properties without measuring lengths or angles. By stretching or squeezing — but not cutting or tearing — topological properties remain unchanged. Euler's theorem is topology's most fundamental discovery: for any polyhedron, the number of vertices (V), edges (E), and faces (F) satisfies V+F=E+2. Descartes first noticed this relationship; Euler gave a rigorous proof. For polyhedra with holes (like a donut), the formula becomes V+F=E+2-2N (N = number of holes).
Euler's Theorem:
Ordinary polyhedron: V + F = E + 2
Donut (1 hole): V + F = E
Pretzel (2 holes): V + F = E - 2
General: V + F = E + 2 - 2N (N = number of holes)
03
Five Regular Solids
Only 5 Regular Polyhedra Exist — A Classic Corollary of Euler's Theorem
Euler's theorem yields a fascinating corollary: only five regular convex polyhedra are possible (all faces with equal edges, all vertices equivalent). This is a necessary consequence derived from V+F=E+2 combined with integer constraints on face edges and vertex edges — exactly five, no more, no less.

04
Coloring Problem
Four vs. Seven Colors: From Plane to Donut
When coloring a map, adjacent regions must have different colors. Euler's theorem can prove five colors are always sufficient for any planar map — but are four enough? This is a classic case "nobody doubts in practice, but no one can prove mathematically." Ironically, more complex surfaces (donuts, pretzels) are easier to prove — a torus requires seven colors, with rigorous proof and concrete examples available.

05
Space Inversion
Inside Out: Möbius Strip · Klein Bottle · The Left-Right Mystery
A Möbius strip is made by twisting a paper strip half a turn and joining the ends. Walk around it once — a "left-facing" donkey becomes a "right-facing" donkey! Can a right-hand glove be turned into a left-hand glove in four-dimensional space? By analogy with shadow creatures on a plane, we find that in Möbius-twisted space, left and right objects can interchange. If our universe is similarly twisted, space travelers might return with their hearts on the right side.

🔑 Key Insight
Space is far richer than what we can measure with rulers. Topology shows us that simply counting vertices, edges, and faces reveals fundamental laws of space. Euler's theorem V+F=E+2 bridges everything from regular polyhedra to the four-color problem. The Möbius strip and Klein bottle remind us: space can not only be curved, but even turn itself "inside out."
