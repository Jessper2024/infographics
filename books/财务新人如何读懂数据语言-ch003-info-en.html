Public vs. Private: The Financial Eye's Core Distinction
Every discipline gives us a unique "eye" to view the world. Finance's perspective: first determine whether a company is public or private. The logic driving this eye is money, is capital. Public companies take the public's money to make money and must give shareholders an account—cash dividends and performance reports go hand in hand. Private companies operate on entirely different logic.
Three Statements as Accountability Reports
The accountability report maps to three fundamental financial statements: the Cash Flow Statement answers "where did the money go?", the Income Statement answers "how was performance?", and the Balance Sheet answers "is the company healthy right now?" Strict accounting classifications and technical processing standards exist precisely so these reports meet uniform standards and can't easily mislead investors. Some companies forgo real tax savings for prettier profits—because public companies have accountability obligations.
P/E Ratio: ¥1 Profit Leverages ¥100 Market Cap
A public company fulfilling earnings guidance positively impacts its stock price, amplified through the P/E Ratio multiplier. ¥1 of profit can leverage ¥10 or even hundreds of yuan in market cap. In China's A-share market, SMEs with P/E ratios exceeding 100 are commonplace. This means: rather than expensing through company accounts to save tax, keep profit in the company—every extra yuan of profit gets amplified 100x in the market. The math is staggering.
Business Application: Use the Financial Eye for Partners
In business development, when choosing partners, customers, or suppliers, first determine if they're listed. If so, read their financial reports for key intelligence: who's the largest shareholder? What's the executive compensation level? Which business segment is most profitable (check segment reports)? This information aids business decisions and at minimum tells you the caliber of counterparty you're negotiating with. The Financial Eye gives you an edge.
Personal Application: Use the Financial Eye for Career Choices
When choosing or switching jobs, apply the same "public or private?" lens. If you studied financial accounting or hold CPA/ACCA credentials, going to a private company is overkill—private companies' accounting purpose is tax filing, not financial auditing. Listed companies, especially those above ¥50B market cap, have robust management systems and professionalized functions. Private companies play with their own money and keep things simple. Choose the right stage to leverage your professional value.
KEY TAKEAWAY
The Financial Eye's first question is always "public or private company?"—the two operate on completely different financial management logic. This lens determines your business judgment, negotiation strategy, and career path choices.
