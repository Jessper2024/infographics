"The Financial Eye Makes You Sharper"

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → AnswerA student asks: "Some bosses put personal expenses through the company account — what do you think?" On the surface it's a tax compliance issue, but without first asking "what kind of boss," you can't give the right answer. What's the thinking tool behind this? Answer: Use the "Financial Eye" — first determine whether it's a public company or a private company; the financial management logic is completely different.

Every discipline teaches us to see the world through a unique "eye." Finance's eye looks at money and capital: first determine whether it's a public or private company. A public company has taken the public's money and must report through three financial statements; a private company plays with its own money, with tax filing as the primary goal. The same economic behavior — such as expense reimbursement — has completely different financial motivations and wealth effects under the two structures.

01
Core Framework
Public Company vs Private Company: Two Radically Different Financial Logics
Using the "Financial Eye" to examine a company's economic behavior starts with determining its capital source. Public company — took public money to make money, must give investors an account. This account takes two forms: cash dividends and performance reports. Performance reports correspond to three fundamental statements — Cash Flow Statement (where money went), Income Statement (operating performance), Balance Sheet (company health). Private company — plays with its own money, simple and practical is best, tax filing is the primary goal.
🏛️

Public Company

Took public money → must report. Three statements: where money went (Cash Flow), operating performance (Income), company health (Balance Sheet).

🏠

Private Company

Plays with own money → tax filing is primary goal. Whatever is simple and practical. No need for standardized public financial reports.

02
Value Lever
P/E Ratio: ¥1 Profit Can Leverage ¥100 Market Cap
A listed company's commitment to meeting earnings expectations positively affects its stock price, amplified through the P/E (Price-to-Earnings) Ratio. In China's A-share market, P/E ratios over 100 for small-cap companies are commonplace. ¥1 of profit can leverage ¥10 or even several hundred yuan in market cap amplification. This is why some listed companies would rather forgo tax-deductible cash benefits to preserve profit numbers — the profit → market cap → shareholder wealth transmission chain is far more attractive than tax savings.

03
Numerical Reasoning
On Books vs Off Books: Wealth Effect Comparison of ¥2M Expenses
Assume ¥2M in deductible expenses. Private company — through company accounts: ¥500K tax savings + ¥2M personal reimbursement = ¥2.5M total. Listed company — off company books: forgoes ¥2M reimbursement + pays ¥500K extra tax, but net profit increases ¥1.5M. P/E=100 → market cap increases ¥150M, 40% stake → personal wealth gain ¥60M. Even at only 10% market efficiency, that's ¥6M — far exceeding the on-books benefit. The numbers speak clearly: the wealth leverage of a listed company is incomparable to a private company.
🏠

Private Company: On Books

Tax savings ¥500K + reimbursement ¥2M = ¥2.5M total. Direct pocketing, simple and practical.

🏛️

Listed Company: Off Books

Forgo ¥2.5M cash → net profit +¥1.5M → P/E×100 = market cap +¥150M → 40% stake = personal wealth +¥60M. The leverage is stunning.

04
Business Application
Using the "Financial Eye" to Evaluate Partners and Competitors
Before engaging in business, before selecting partners, clients, or suppliers — first determine whether the counterparty is a listed company. If so, study their financial reports — who is the largest shareholder? What level are executive annual salaries (a VP earning ¥500K vs ¥2M reveals whether industry top talent is running the company)? Which business segment is most profitable (check segment reports)? At the macro level, this informs business decisions; at the micro level, it at least tells you what caliber of opponent you're negotiating with.
  • 👤Who is the largest shareholder? Understand control and decision logic
  • 💼Executive salary level? ¥500K vs ¥2M VP → gauge management quality
  • 📊Which segment is most profitable? Segment reports reveal core profit engines
  • 🎯Synthesize: know what level of opponent you're negotiating with, support business decisions
05
Personal Application
Using the "Financial Eye" for Career and Job-Hopping Decisions
When choosing careers or job-hopping, use the "Financial Eye" the same way. Those who studied financial accounting or earned CPA/ACCA — going to a private company is underutilization, because private company bookkeeping is for tax filing, not financial auditing. Even non-finance people can judge: listed companies (especially those with market cap over ¥50 billion) have sound management systems and professional departmental operations; private companies play with their own money, adopting whatever is simple and practical. First distinguish, then choose.
🏛️

Listed Company (>¥50B Cap)

Sound management systems, professional departmental operations. CPA/ACCA have room to excel — the best platform for financial accounting talent.

🏠

Private Company

Bookkeeping for tax filing, not auditing. Simple and practical above all. Finance specialists may be underutilized, but may gain broader hands-on experience.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The first question of the "Financial Eye" is always: listed company or private company? The financial management logic is completely different. The same economic behavior — whether to expense or not, whether to inflate profits or not — is a leverage game of market cap + shareholder wealth in a listed company, and a practical choice of tax + pocketing cash in a private company. Use this "eye" to evaluate partners, competitors, and career choices — you'll become sharper than your peers.
