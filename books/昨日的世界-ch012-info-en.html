The World of Yesterday · Ch.12 "Return to Austria"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer After the Austro-German collapse, returning to Austria seemed the most foolish decision — the country lay dismembered, famine spread, the currency avalanched. Yet after hesitating at Bux station, Zweig chose to go back. At the border he watched the last Emperor Karl depart into exile — the Habsburg dynasty of nearly a thousand years ended. Amid the ruins, how did a nation forced into independence yet refusing to exist survive? The answer lay in an innate goodness and a will to live that transcended money.

Zweig returned to postwar Austria and experienced the full absurdity of hyperinflation: from primitive barter to "a box of matches costing twenty times more in one shop than another," from vulture-like foreign exploitation to Bavarians crossing the border to binge-drink cheap beer in the "beer war." Yet the dismembered nation did not collapse into revolution — Social Democrats and Christian Socialists formed a coalition government, staving off disintegration through compromise. The young generation launched a total "reckoning" against their fathers: art, fashion, morality, language — every tradition was overturned. Zweig chose to withdraw to Salzburg and begin anew.

01
HISTORIC MOMENT
The Dynasty's End: Witnessing the Last Emperor's Exile
At the Feldkirch border station, Zweig watched Emperor Karl — Austria's last emperor — depart in a special luxury train. Gendarmes and police stood awkwardly, unsure whether to salute; an old woman sobbed quietly. Raised in imperial tradition, Zweig had once sworn allegiance to the old Emperor at Schönbrunn. Now he witnessed: "The splendid empire of the Habsburgs, passed down through centuries, came to an end in this final minute."
👑

Imperial Memory

Zweig had seen the old Emperor at grand ceremonies, receiving 80,000 schoolchildren's oath of loyalty. "The word 'Emperor' was the concentrated embodiment of power, the symbol of Austria's eternity."

The Last Emperor's Flight

02
ECONOMIC COLLAPSE
The Absurd Theater of Inflation: Currency to Wastepaper, Society to Barter
Austrian crowns "liquefied in your hand." Coins had less value than their metal content. Every village printed its own banknotes. Prices jumped arbitrarily — "one box of matches cost twenty times more in one shop than another." A year's rent couldn't pay for one lunch. Society regressed to primitive barter — wedding rings for bread, peasants trading food for Chinese vases. Speculators smuggled with knives and guns. Inflation destroyed all moral norms.

03
ABSURD COMEDY
The Beer War: Inflation's Absurd Microcosm and Foreign Plunder
Hordes of Bavarians crossed the border to Salzburg just to binge on cheap beer with their hard marks — "four or five liters, even ten liters more for the same money." The railway station became a nightly den of drunkenness. Later, when the mark collapsed astronomically, Austrians surged into Germany to drink cheap beer in reverse — the "beer war" vividly exposed the era's madness. Meanwhile, foreigners flocked to "devour the writhing body of the Austrian crown," buying everything from toothbrushes to estates.

04
POLITICAL MIRACLE
Why No Revolution? The Will to Survive in Weakness
Postwar Austria was absurdly positioned: Bavaria to the right had a Communist republic, Hungary to the left was Bolshevik under Béla Kun. Weapons were plentiful, starving soldiers glared at profiteers' luxury, a "Red Guard" prepared to rise — "two hundred determined men could have taken Vienna." Yet no revolution happened. The two major parties formed a coalition, staving off collapse through compromise. "The miracle became reality: this dismembered nation continued to exist."

05
CULTURAL REVOLUTION
The Young Generation's Great Reckoning and Zweig's New Beginning
The postwar generation no longer believed in parents, politicians, or teachers — they "greeted every state decree with suspicion." They overturned everything: girls cropped "boy cuts," homosexuality flourished as rebellion, Cubism and Surrealism replaced Rembrandt, telegraphic style replaced complete sentences. Old professors hastily painted cubes onto their still lifes. Zweig chose seclusion in Salzburg, writing Builders of the World and Amok: "The age of pure promise was past — it was time to fulfill promises or abandon them altogether."

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
The deepest paradox of postwar Austria was this: a nation forced into independence yet refusing to exist somehow survived. Not through a strong army or leadership, but through the innate goodness of its people and a will to live that transcended money — when currency became trash, people cherished work, love, friendship, art, and nature all the more. But the cost was heavy: the young generation's radical rebellion severed every tie with tradition, and this severance would eventually demand its price — "history proves that extreme revolutions often plant the seeds of new catastrophes."
