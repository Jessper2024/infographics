Su Shi: A Life · Ch.2
"The World's Burdens Are Hard to Bear"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer In 1068, Su Shi traveled to the capital for the third time — unaware it would be his last close encounter with his homeland. Far away, the fierce struggle between reform and conservative factions had already begun, and a storm that would reshape his life was brewing. How did the Song dynasty's crises give birth to Wang Anshi's reforms, and why did those reforms plunge Su Shi into an inescapable partisan whirlpool?

The Song dynasty's "ancestral family laws" consolidated central power but planted structural flaws: over-concentration of military authority weakened border defense; checks on civil power bloated the bureaucracy; and siphoning local revenues to the center drained the treasury. Facing internal and external crises, Emperor Shenzong and Wang Anshi launched sweeping reforms. But fierce factional conflict soon trapped the young official Su Shi in his first great political dilemma — "Seeing the times too hard to bear, yet unable to refuse imperial grace."

01
Institutional Roots
"Ancestral Laws": The Innate Flaws of Centralization
The Song founders' elaborate centralization design, while consolidating unity, sowed irreconcilable internal contradictions — worsening with time. "Relieving generals of command with cups of wine" prevented warlordism but led to soldiers who "didn't know their generals and generals who didn't know their soldiers." Prefects and vice-prefects checked each other, bloating the bureaucracy. Most local revenues went to the center, fueling upper-class extravagance while draining the treasury.

02
Era of Crisis
Impoverished and Weakened: Crisis Beneath the Calm
When Shenzong ascended the throne, the Song faced imminent danger on multiple fronts. Annual tribute to Western Xia: 70,000 taels of silver, 5,000 bolts of silk, 30,000 jin of tea. To the Liao: 200,000 taels of silver and 300,000 bolts of silk. "Benevolence to officials feared inadequacy; revenue from the people left no surplus" — the bureaucracy swelled, the treasury emptied, and the people suffered. A new wave of reform sentiment was surging among the scholar-official class.

03
Historic Encounter
Shenzong and Wang Anshi: A Once-in-a-Century Meeting
The 20-year-old emperor felt deep humiliation at the dynasty's decline and found only conservatism among veteran ministers. Fu Bi advised "twenty years without mentioning war"; Sima Guang offered vague "self-cultivation" platitudes — both had lost their reformist edge. Wang Anshi, long renowned as a "sage reborn," declared boldly at their first meeting: "The time for great deeds is today!" Shenzong once wore military attire to visit his grandmother, weeping when recalling the dynasty's humiliations.

04
Reform Panorama
Swift and Decisive: Fiscal and Military Reform on Two Fronts
In the second month of 1070, Shenzong appointed Wang Anshi as Vice Grand Councilor and established the Finance Planning Commission as the reform nerve center — launching an 18-year reform era. The New Policies split into fiscal ("enrich the state") and military ("strengthen the army") tracks. Yet controversy erupted immediately: "the court and the country were shocked, public opinion raged."

05
Personal Dilemma
Su Shi's Predicament: Between Public Service and Retreat
Facing the fierce factional conflict, Su Shi fell into deep ambivalence — yearning to serve yet dreading the political maelstrom. Traveling past Jinshan Temple, he wrote his truest feelings before the Yangtze sunset. Seeing strange torch-like flames on the river — "neither ghost nor man, what could it be?" — he interpreted this as the river god warning him: the landscape is so beautiful, yet he clings to worldly attachment and refuses to withdraw. "Seeing the times too hard to bear, yet unable to refuse imperial grace."

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Xining Reforms represent a classic "reform dilemma" in Chinese history. The Song dynasty's structural contradictions — military centralization weakening defense, governmental checks breeding inefficiency, fiscal centralization draining the treasury — gave the reforms full historical legitimacy. Yet once launched, the reforms entangled interests, ideologies, and personal grudges, rapidly becoming irreconcilable factional conflict. For Su Shi, these reforms were not abstract policy debates, but the core variable that would determine the next thirty years of his life.
