Three Prompt Types
Three Types of Prompts: Person, Context, and Action
Prompts are the decisive factor in behavior — without a prompt, behavior will never occur. ① Person Prompts: relying on your own memory or someone else's reminder — least reliable, because people forget. ② Context Prompts: sticky notes, app notifications, alarms — moderately reliable but easily ignored due to notification fatigue. ③ Action Prompts: anchoring new behaviors to existing habits — most reliable, because they leverage behaviors already happening automatically in your daily life. Don't rely on memory or sticky notes — let your life itself become the prompt system.

Anchor Selection
Three Anchor Criteria: A Good Anchor Must Pass Three Tests
Not every habit can serve as an anchor — good anchors must pass a triple test. Criterion 1: It must be something that inevitably happens every day — brushing teeth, drinking water, arriving at work, finishing a meal, lying in bed. Criterion 2: The anchor must match the new behavior in physical location — "after brushing teeth" works for triggering "do a squat" (both in the bathroom), but not for "open laptop and journal." Criterion 3: The anchor's frequency must match the new behavior — a daily anchor for a daily behavior; a twice-weekly anchor for a twice-weekly behavior.

03
Anchor Formula
The Anchor Formula: "After I [Existing Habit], I Will [New Tiny Behavior]"
This is the most practical operational formula in the Fogg Behavior Model. Amy's case: every day she drops her daughter at kindergarten — the moment her daughter closes the car door becomes her anchor. She immediately parks and writes the day's single most important task on a sticky note. The format is strict: "After my daughter closes the car door, I will park and write down today's most important thing." — precise, specific, unambiguous. This habit pulled her out of 7 years of career paralysis. The successful Anchor Formula must be: "After I [specific event], I will [tiny new behavior]" — no vagueness allowed.

04
Pearl Habits
Pearl Habits: Transform Irritations into Positive-Behavior Triggers
Pearl Habits are the most beautiful concept in the Fogg Behavior Model. How does a pearl form? A grain of sand (irritant) enters an oyster, which gradually coats it in layers of nacre, eventually forming a pearl. Similarly, the things in your life that cause anxiety, irritation, or unease (the sand) can be transformed into triggers for positive behaviors. When anxiety strikes → take 3 deep breaths. When a colleague interrupts you → smile and thank them. Negative emotions are no longer enemies — they become your prompt system. Every moment of discomfort is an opportunity to practice a positive behavior.

05
Three-Step Method
Three Steps to Designing the Right Prompt: List Anchors → Match Behaviors → Rehearse
Don't leave prompts to chance — you must design them actively. Step 1: List everything that inevitably happens every day as your "Anchor Inventory" — waking up, bathroom, brushing teeth, drinking water, arriving at work, eating lunch, coming home, lying in bed... Be as detailed as possible. Step 2: Match your new tiny behavior to the most suitable anchor — ensure location match, frequency match, and logical naturalness. Step 3: Rehearse the Anchor Formula repeatedly until it becomes automatic. After one week of practice, Amy's sticky-note habit became an automatic reflex. Right position + right frequency + right rehearsal = automatic habit.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Prompts are the ignition switch for behavior. Hundreds of prompts drive your actions every day, and you barely notice them. You don't need willpower to "remember" to do something — you just need to anchor a tiny new behavior to an old habit you already do every day. The formula is simple: "After I [existing habit], I will [new tiny behavior]." Take it further: use Pearl Habits to turn every moment of anxiety, every irritation, into a trigger for a positive behavior — sand becomes pearl. Design your prompts well, and behavior happens naturally.
