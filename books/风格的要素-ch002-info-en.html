The Elements of Style · Chapter 002「Elementary Principles of Composition」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Mastering punctuation is only the starting point — organizing words into compositions is the true craft of writing. Most writers lack awareness of paragraph structure, voice choice, conciseness, and sentence variety — their prose is loose, vague, and padded. How do you make every paragraph and every sentence serve clarity? The answer lies in 11 principles: from topic sentences to active voice, from omitting needless words to placing emphasis at the end.

This is the longest and richest chapter in The Elements of Style, covering 11 principles of composition (Rules 8–18): paragraph structure (one paragraph per topic, topic sentences), preferring active voice, stating in positive form, using definite and specific language, omitting needless words, avoiding successive loose sentences, keeping parallel structure, placing related words together, maintaining one tense in summaries, and placing emphatic words at the end. Each rule goes straight to the heart of clear English writing.

Rules 8-9
Paragraph Structure: One Topic, Topic Sentence Echo
The paragraph is the basic building block of writing. Rule 8: Make the paragraph the unit of composition — one paragraph to each topic, for the reader's convenience. Rule 9: Begin each paragraph with a topic sentence and echo it at the end — so the reader knows the purpose at once and does not forget it.

02
Rules 10-11
Active Voice & Positive Form: Inject Power into Prose
Rule 10: Use the active voice. The active voice is usually more direct and vigorous than the passive — "Dead leaves covered the ground" far surpasses "There were a great number of dead leaves lying on the ground." Rule 11: Put statements in positive form. Readers are not satisfied with being told what is not; they wish to be told what is.

03
Rules 12-13
Be Specific & Omit Needless Words: Brevity Is Power
Rule 12: Use definite, specific, concrete language — "He grinned as he pocketed the coin" is better than "He showed satisfaction as he took possession of his well-earned reward." Rule 13: Omit needless words. Vigorous writing is concise — a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts.

Cut: "the fact that" — wherever it appears, delete it. I was unaware of the fact that → I was unaware that

Cut: who is, which was — redundant. His brother, who is a member of the same firm → His brother, a member of the same firm
Spencer, Philosophy of Style: "We do not think in generals, but in particulars — when an abstract word is used, the hearer has to choose from his stock of images... by employing a specific term, an economy is achieved, and a more vivid impression produced."
04
Rules 14-15
Sentence Variety & Parallel Structure: Avoid Monotony, Keep Symmetry
Rule 14: Avoid a succession of loose sentences. Loose sentences — two clauses joined by a conjunction or relative — become monotonous in series. Intersperse simple sentences, semicolon sentences, and periodic sentences. Rule 15: Express coordinate ideas in similar form. Parallel structure requires similarity of content and function to be matched by similarity of form.
⚠️

Loose Sentence Trap

Successive and, but, so, while, who, which clauses → monotonous rhythm, weak expression. Deliberately vary your sentence structures.

✅

Parallel Structure

Both...and should link equal structures: It was both a long ceremony and very tedious → The ceremony was both long and tedious.

05
Rules 16-18
Word Order, Tense Consistency & End Emphasis
Rule 16: Keep related words together. Do not separate subject and verb with a phrase that can be moved to the beginning. Rule 17: In summaries, keep to one tense. Use present tense for dramatic plots; never switch back and forth. Rule 18: Place the emphatic words at the end. The end of a sentence is the throne of emphasis.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The 11 principles in The Elements of Style weave a network of clear writing: from paragraph organization (one topic, topic sentence echo) to voice choice (active over passive), from statement form (positive over negative) to language texture (specific over abstract, concise over padded), from sentence arrangement (vary loose with periodic, keep parallel symmetry) to word order and emphasis (keep related words together, save the best for last) — each is a critical step from readable to powerful. Good writing never wastes a single second of the reader's time.
