Su Shi Biography · Ch.11 "White-haired, Exiled to Lingnan and the Sea"

"Word came: The Master dozes sweetly in the spring. The Taoist gently taps the fifth-watch bell." — Zhang Dun read this poem and laughed: "Su Zizhan is still so happy!" So he banished him to the ends of the earth.

Lingnan Period Works
In one line: During the Shaosheng era, the reform faction returned to power and Su Shi was banished to Lingnan — the court revised his punishment five times en route, demoting him from Prefect of Dingzhou to "Huizhou Resettlement, forbidden to sign official documents." At 62, white-haired, he crossed the sea to Danzhou (Hainan), the southernmost exile ever. Yet he transformed adversity through Buddhist and Daoist cultivation: in Huizhou, he built bridges, introduced water supply, and dispensed medicine; in Danzhou, he taught students and nurtured Hainan's first imperial degree holder. When amnesty finally came, he wrote the immortal lines: "Nine deaths in the southern wilds — I bear no regret. This journey, wondrous beyond compare, crowns a lifetime."

Core QuestionWhy did Zhang Dun and his allies pursue Su Shi relentlessly? How did a 62-year-old man survive — even thrive — in the miasmic wilderness of Lingnan and Hainan, transforming "hell" into "heaven"?

Core AnswerEmperor Zhezong changed the era name to "Shaosheng" (Continuing the Sage), and Zhang Dun became Chancellor with the singular goal of destroying the Yuanyou faction. Su Shi's exile was revised five times. Crossing the Dayu Mountains, he had an awakening: "the worldly and the transcendent cannot coexist," and with the realization that "now I am truly defeated," he achieved spiritual liberation. In Huizhou, he built the Dongxin and Xixin bridges, engineered Guangzhou's water supply, and dispensed medicine. Banished to Danzhou, he educated the islanders and tutored Jiang Tangzuo, who became Hainan's first imperial degree holder — transforming from exiled official to cultural missionary.

1
Political Persecution

Five Decrees, Ten Thousand Li: From Dingzhou to Huizhou

In the leap fourth month of 1094, Su Shi was impeached for "seditious language." Edict followed edict: stripped of his dual academic titles → demoted to Left Court Gentleman → barred from routine promotion → stripped again, made Military Adjutant of Jianchang → finally, Vice Commissioner of Ningyuan Army, resettled in Huizhou, "forbidden to sign official documents." Five revisions to his punishment — from prefect to prisoner.

Huizhou resettlement

On the journey south, the sky cleared at Lincheng, revealing the majestic Taihang Mountains. Su Shi told his sons: "The ridges run north — an auspicious sign. I shall return from this southern exile." Passing Tangyin, holding a crude bowl of pea-barley porridge, he admonished them: "In the harshest conditions, still eat heartily. Always hold yourselves worthy of the people who raised you." The family was split repeatedly — he continued with only his youngest son Su Guo, concubine Chao Yun, and two maids.

"The road home grows ever more uncertain — endless green mountains, waters lapping the sky. Yet here comes a small boat selling cakes — how glad I am to see a village ahead." — Su Shi, "Detained by Wind at Cihu"
2
Huizhou Works

A Convict Who Built Bridges and Water Systems

Though "forbidden to sign official documents," Su Shi used every means to benefit Huizhou: he donated the rhinoceros-horn belt from his court robes to fund the Dongxin and Xixin bridges; wrote letters persuading Guangzhou Prefect Wang Gu to pipe spring water into the city, creating an ancient "running water" system; purchased medicines at his own expense for the poor; buried exposed bones; promoted the "rice-seedling horse" — governing without office, merging Buddhist compassion with Confucian public service.

🟠 Dongxin Bridge

Adopting Daoist Deng Shou'an's plan: 40 small boats linked as 20 pontoons, iron chains and stone anchors rising and falling with the tide. Su Shi donated his court belt and persuaded his sister-in-law to contribute thousands from her palace grants.

🟠 Guangzhou Water Supply

Channeling spring water from Pujian Mountain through five large bamboo pipes bound together, sealed with hemp and lacquer. He devised small holes with bamboo pins in each pipe section for easy blockage inspection — scientific method matched by compassionate intent.

⚠️ Su Shi and his cousin/brother-in-law Cheng Zhengfu had been estranged for 42 years after the death of Su Shi's sister in the Cheng household. Zhang Dun deliberately appointed Zhengfu as circuit inspector for Guangzhou, hoping old grievances would destroy Su Shi. Instead, the cousins reconciled in Huizhou, and Zhengfu became Su Shi's ally in implementing public works.
3
Chao Yun's Death

The Six Similes Pavilion: A Soulmate Lost Forever

In the seventh month of 1096, Chao Yun died in Huizhou at 34. She had entered the Su household at age 12 and followed Su Shi for 23 years — the only family member who accompanied him all the way south. On her deathbed she chanted the Diamond Sutra's "Six Similes Gatha": "All conditioned phenomena are like dreams, illusions, bubbles, shadows, like dew, like lightning." She passed away serenely. Su Shi buried her in the pine grove by Qichan Temple and built the "Six Similes Pavilion."

"Unlike the willow branch who left Bai Letian, just as Tongde accompanied Ling Xuan." — Su Shi, "Poem for Chao Yun." Bai Juyi's beloved concubine Fan Su eventually left him in old age; Chao Yun never wavered. She understood his "belly full of untimeliness" and reached the same spiritual plane as Su Shi himself.

In Huizhou, Chao Yun managed the household with quiet strength, read sutras, practiced calligraphy, and discussed Chan with Su Shi. Together they built a life-release pond and performed charitable works. Su Shi eulogized her as a plum blossom: "Jade bones — what fear of miasmic fog? Ice flesh — naturally of immortal grace." Legend says whenever Chao Yun sang the lines "Willow catkins on the branch blow ever fewer — at the horizon's edge, fragrant grasses grow everywhere," she wept uncontrollably — lamenting impermanence. After her death, Su Shi never listened to that song again.

The monks of Qichan Temple marveled at her clear-minded passing and built the pavilion over her grave. Centuries later, Cao Xueqin placed Chao Yun among the "lovers and fools of passion" in Dream of the Red Chamber, alongside Jia Baoyu — she had transcended the role of concubine to achieve genuine spiritual communion with Su Shi.

4
Danzhou Mission

Bamboo Hat and Clogs: From Exile to Cultural Apostle

In 1097, Su Shi was banished further to Danzhou (Hainan). At 62, believing "in old age cast into the wilds, no hope of returning alive," he wrote his will. But upon arrival, he reframed his despair through Buddhist wisdom: "Heaven and Earth float in cosmic waters, the Nine Regions in a great encircling sea — among all living things, who is NOT on an island?" In this place with "no meat, no medicine, no proper dwelling, no friends," he became the island's great teacher.

🔵 Hainan's First Imperial Graduate

Jiang Tangzuo traveled from Qiongzhou to study with Su Shi for half a year. Su Shi inscribed on his fan: "When has the vast sea ever cut the veins of the earth? From Pearl Cliff, the heavens now break open." Jiang later passed the imperial exams in 1109 — Su Zhe completed his late brother's couplet: "In brocade robes, all eyes will soon be watching — now they know Dongpo's vision was true."

🔵 The Iconic Portrait

One rainy day returning from a visit, Su Shi borrowed a bamboo hat and wooden clogs from a farm woman. The locals found this endearing; children chased him laughing. Artists later immortalized the scene as "Dongpo in Bamboo Hat and Clogs" — he had fully integrated into Li tribal life.

💡 "Three Comforts of Exile": Su Shi elevated mundane routines into poetry — "Combing Hair at Dawn," "Napping by the Window at Noon," "Washing Feet Before Bed." It seems exile was needed to experience these three comforts. He also completed his commentaries on the I Ching, the Book of Documents, and the Analects — works he considered "surpassing all other achievements."

When inspector Dong Bi drove Su Shi from government housing, locals helped him build a thatched cottage amid the palm grove — the "Guanglang Hermitage." After Emperor Zhezong died and Huizong ascended, amnesty came. As he departed, Li tribespeople wept, grasping his hands. He wrote: "I am originally a man of Hainan, who lodged temporarily in western Shu."

5
Northern Return

Nine Deaths, No Regret: The Final Gaze from Chengmai Post

In June 1100, Su Shi crossed the strait northward and composed "Crossing the Sea on the Night of June 20": "Clouds part, the moon shines bright — who adorns it? Heaven's face and the sea's hue are pure of themselves. Nine deaths in the southern wilds — I bear no regret. This journey, wondrous beyond compare, crowns a lifetime." Seven years of injustice finally cleared — looking back, he held no resentment. He had transformed bitter exile into life's most magnificent journey.

Before crossing the sea, Su Shi climbed Tongchao Pavilion at Chengmai and gazed north: "I'd meant to spend my remaining years in this Hainan village — the gods sent sorcerers to summon back my soul. Where the distant falcon vanishes at heaven's edge — a single thread of blue mountains — that is the Central Plain." He had already accepted dying in Hainan, treating Danzhou as his second home. En route, the brothers reunited in Tengzhou, sharing coarse soup at a roadside stall — Su Zhe put down his chopsticks with a sigh; Su Shi wolfed down his bowl unthinking, then looked up laughing: "You still want to savor it slowly?"

"Orion has turned, the Dipper shifted — it must be midnight. Bitter rain and ceaseless wind — but they, too, know to clear. Clouds part, the moon shines bright — who adorns it? Heaven's face and the sea's hue are pure of themselves. In vain, the Sage's wish to sail a raft on the sea — yet I've grasped, however roughly, the Yellow Emperor's music. Nine deaths in the southern wilds — I bear no regret. This journey, wondrous beyond compare, crowns a lifetime." — Su Shi, "Crossing the Sea on June 20"

In his three Danzhou years, Su Shi completed over 100 poems matching Tao Yuanming's rhymes, revised his I Ching and Analects commentaries, wrote new commentaries on the Book of Documents and "Forest of Records," and personally compiled teaching materials. As the Qiongtai Record states: "When the Venerable Su Wenzhong was exiled to Danzhou, he lectured and illuminated the Way, and education flourished daily. The flourishing of culture in Qiongzhou truly began with him."

💡
Key Takeaway

Su Shi's seven-year exile in Lingnan and Hainan stands as the most magnificent spiritual saga in Chinese cultural history. His enemies believed banishment to the miasmic wilderness would destroy him — five revisions to his exile decree, confiscation of his official boat, expulsion from government housing — but each blow became a catalyst for spiritual transcendence. In Huizhou, a "convict" forbidden to sign documents donated his court belt for bridges and wrote letters to engineer a water supply. In Danzhou, a 62-year-old wearing borrowed clogs integrated into tribal life and tutored Hainan's first imperial degree holder. He declared: "In this human realm, what is not illusion? Southward ten thousand li — truly a fine scheme." When his poem "Nine deaths in the southern wilds — I bear no regret" drifted over the Qiongzhou Strait, all political persecution became footnotes to his legend: it was not suffering that defined Su Shi — it was Su Shi who, through the height of his spirit, transcended all suffering.
