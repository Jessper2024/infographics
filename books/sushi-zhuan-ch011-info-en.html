Su Shi Biography · Ch11
"White-haired, Exiled to Lingnan and the Sea"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer "Word came: The Master dozes sweetly in the spring. The Taoist gently taps the fifth-watch bell." — Zhang Dun read this poem and laughed: "Su Zizhan is still so happy!" So he banished him to the ends of the earth. Why did Zhang Dun pursue Su Shi relentlessly? How did a 62-year-old man survive — even thrive — in the miasmic wilderness of Lingnan and Hainan, transforming "hell" into "heaven"?

In one line: During the Shaosheng era, the reform faction returned to power and Su Shi was banished to Lingnan — the court revised his punishment five times en route, demoting him from Prefect to "Huizhou Resettlement, forbidden to sign official documents." At 62, white-haired, he crossed the sea to Danzhou (Hainan). Yet he transformed adversity through Buddhist and Daoist cultivation: in Huizhou, he built bridges, introduced water supply, and dispensed medicine; in Danzhou, he taught students and nurtured Hainan's first imperial degree holder. When amnesty finally came, he wrote the immortal lines: "Nine deaths in the southern wilds — I bear no regret. This journey, wondrous beyond compare, crowns a lifetime."

01
Political Persecution
Five Decrees, Ten Thousand Li: From Dingzhou to Huizhou
In the leap fourth month of 1094, Su Shi was impeached for "seditious language." Edict followed edict: stripped of his dual academic titles → demoted to Left Court Gentleman → barred from routine promotion → stripped again, made Military Adjutant of Jianchang → finally, Vice Commissioner of Ningyuan Army, resettled in Huizhou. Five revisions to his punishment — from prefect to prisoner. The family was split repeatedly — he continued with only his youngest son Su Guo, concubine Chao Yun, and two maids.

02
Huizhou Works
A Convict Who Built Bridges and Water Systems
Though "forbidden to sign official documents," Su Shi used every means to benefit Huizhou: he donated the rhinoceros-horn belt from his court robes to fund the Dongxin and Xixin bridges; wrote letters persuading Guangzhou Prefect Wang Gu to pipe spring water into the city, creating an ancient "running water" system; purchased medicines at his own expense; buried exposed bones; promoted the "rice-seedling horse" — governing without office, merging Buddhist compassion with Confucian public service.
🟠

Dongxin Bridge: His Belt for a Bridge

Adopting Daoist Deng Shou'an's plan: 40 small boats linked as 20 pontoons, iron chains and stone anchors. Su Shi donated his court belt and persuaded his sister-in-law to contribute thousands from her palace grants.

🟠

Guangzhou Water Supply

Channeling spring water through five large bamboo pipes bound together, sealed with hemp and lacquer. He devised small holes with bamboo pins in each pipe section for easy blockage inspection.

03
Life and Death
The Six Similes Pavilion: A Soulmate Lost Forever
In the seventh month of 1096, Chao Yun died in Huizhou at 34. She had entered the Su household at age 12 and followed Su Shi for 23 years — the only family member who accompanied him all the way south. On her deathbed she chanted the Diamond Sutra's "Six Similes Gatha": "All conditioned phenomena are like dreams, illusions, bubbles, shadows, like dew, like lightning." She passed away serenely. Su Shi buried her in the pine grove by Qichan Temple and built the "Six Similes Pavilion." After her death, he never listened to "Butterflies Lingering over Flowers" again.

04
Danzhou Mission
Bamboo Hat and Clogs: From Exile to Cultural Apostle
In 1097, Su Shi was banished further to Danzhou (Hainan). At 62, believing "in old age cast into the wilds, no hope of returning alive," he wrote his will. But upon arrival, he reframed his despair: "Heaven and Earth float in cosmic waters, the Nine Regions in a great encircling sea — among all living things, who is NOT on an island?" In this place with "no meat, no medicine, no proper dwelling, no friends," he became the island's great teacher.

05
Northern Return
Nine Deaths, No Regret: The Final Gaze from Chengmai Post
In June 1100, Su Shi crossed the strait northward and composed "Crossing the Sea on the Night of June 20": "Clouds part, the moon shines bright — who adorns it? Heaven's face and the sea's hue are pure of themselves. Nine deaths in the southern wilds — I bear no regret. This journey, wondrous beyond compare, crowns a lifetime." Seven years of injustice finally cleared — looking back, he held no resentment. He had transformed bitter exile into life's most magnificent journey.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Su Shi's seven-year exile in Lingnan and Hainan stands as the most magnificent spiritual saga in Chinese cultural history. His enemies believed banishment to the miasmic wilderness would destroy him — five revisions to his decree, confiscation of his boat, expulsion from government housing — but each blow became a catalyst for transcendence. In Huizhou, a "convict" forbidden to sign documents donated his court belt for bridges and wrote letters to engineer a water supply. In Danzhou, a 62-year-old wearing borrowed clogs integrated into tribal life and tutored Hainan's first imperial graduate. When his poem "Nine deaths in the southern wilds — I bear no regret" drifted over the Qiongzhou Strait, all political persecution became footnotes to his legend: it was not suffering that defined Su Shi — it was Su Shi who, through the height of his spirit, transcended all suffering.
