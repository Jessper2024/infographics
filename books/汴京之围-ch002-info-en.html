The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.1 "After Prosperity"

42 Years Without War · The Crisis Beneath the Golden Age Song Renzong reigned 42 years and was posthumously eulogized: "To be a ruler is to rest at benevolence." When Liao Emperor Daozong heard of Renzong's death, he grasped the envoy's hand and wept: "In forty-two years we have not known war!" A poem inscribed on Renzong's tomb wall reads: "Farming undisturbed, harvests always abundant; border generals without merit, officials without ability. Forty-two years like a dream awoke; spring winds blow tears across the Zhao Mausoleum." Yet beneath this glorious surface, the "Three Excesses" — excess officials, excess soldiers, excess spending — were already consuming the empire's foundations like a festering wound.

This chapter traces the political evolution from Song Renzong's golden age to Song Huizong's era. The Northern Song's greatest problem was the "Three Excesses": the official roster swelled from under 10,000 salaried positions in Renzong's early years to 46,000 by Huizong's time; soldiers grew from 910,000 to 1,259,000; military spending devoured over three-quarters of tax revenue. Fan Zhongyan's Qingli Reforms failed after provoking bureaucratic resistance; Wang Anshi's radical New Policies intensified factional strife, transforming political differences into zero-sum partisan warfare. When Cai Jing used inflation (via jiaozi paper money and salt certificates) to fund Huizong's extravagance, the empire's last safety valve was opened.

01
Golden Age
Song Renzong's era: supreme prosperity achieved through deliberate non-interference
Song Renzong appeared to be an emperor who accomplished nothing — yet it was precisely this "non-accomplishment" that created the apex of Chinese imperial history. He executed no one arbitrarily, refrained from interfering in the private economy, and allowed ministers to openly contradict him. The founding emperor Taizu's three stone tablet injunctions — spare the deposed Zhou royal family, never execute scholars or remonstrating officials, and divine punishment for violators — were fully honored under Renzong. Commoners feared no official harassment; officials feared no punishment for speech. At Renzong's death, Bianjing spontaneously closed its markets in mourning; even beggar children burned paper money before the palace. These people wept not for one man's death, but for the passing of an era.

02
Institutional Cancer
The Three Excesses — too many officials, too many soldiers, too much spending: an empire's slow suicide
The Northern Song bureaucracy was the most interlocked administrative design in Chinese history — no one could rebel because no one could accumulate enough power, but no one could get anything done either. To prevent military strongmen, Taizu split prime ministerial power into three: Vice Chancellor, Bureau of Military Affairs, and State Finance Commission. Local powers were similarly fragmented. The Bureau controlled troop deployments; the Three Commands handled training; a separately appointed general led in battle — meaning generals went to war not knowing their own soldiers. The mercenary-army system consumed enormous funds while delivering diminished combat effectiveness. Minister Cai Xiang's statistics: military spending consumed 27% of cash tax receipts, 85% of silk, 86% of grain, and 84% of fodder.

03
The Factional Split
Conservatives vs. Pragmatists: how two opposing reform philosophies destroyed the window for renewal
Northern Song factionalism originated from two irreconcilable reform philosophies: conservatives sought moral purification, pragmatists demanded institutional change. Sima Guang and the Cheng brothers (Cheng Yi, Cheng Hao) represented conservatives who believed the problem was moral decay requiring Confucian orthodoxy to reunify hearts and minds. Fan Zhongyan and Wang Anshi represented pragmatists who believed concrete policies must solve concrete problems. Fan's Qingli Reforms attempted self-sacrifice — streamlining bureaucracy, reducing regulation, matching spending to revenue — but failed after provoking the entire official class. With the moderates sidelined, pragmatists splintered into Wang Anshi's radical interventionists, and conservatives hardened into extremists — plunging the Northern Song into irreconcilable factional warfare.

04
Fiscal Collapse
Cai Jing's printing press: how jiaozi and salt certificate inflation bled the people dry
Among all ministers, only Cai Jing's fiscal wizardry could match Huizong's spending appetite. From 1104, Cai Jing launched "fiscal reforms" — in essence, leveraging woodblock printing technology to expand jiaozi (paper money, previously limited to Sichuan) and salt certificates (previously limited to the north) nationwide, then massively over-issuing them. When the metal reserves backing jiaozi were drained and salt certificates far exceeded actual salt production, these instruments became pure inflationary tools. The Emperor gained enormous revenue for Genyue construction and the Youzhou recovery campaign; commoners suffered devastating price surges. Renzong's tradition of "non-interference in people's lives" was irrevocably broken.

05
Strategic Deadlock
When peace was needed, hawks were in power; when resistance was needed, doves held the reins
For any nation, the principle of military action should be: avoid war lightly, but once at war, fight to the death. The Northern Song did exactly the opposite. The New-Old Party struggle spilled from domestic politics into foreign affairs: the New Party (radical pragmatists) were mostly hawks seeking military glory; the Old Party (hardline conservatives) were mostly doves preferring non-resistance even facing invasion. The tragedy of the late Northern Song: in peacetime, hawks ruled and constantly dragged the nation toward war; after a couple of defeats, doves took over — but by then war had already erupted, and they sabotaged frontline commanders instead of reinforcing them. This fatal mismatch left the Northern Song with no chance of survival in the coming existential conflict.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Song Renzong's "42 years without war" was the most cherished peacetime in Chinese imperial history, but beneath the glittering surface, the Three Excesses had already sealed the empire's tragic trajectory. From Fan Zhongyan's failed moderate reforms, to Wang Anshi's radical policies that tore the bureaucracy apart, to Cai Jing's inflationary financing of an emperor's bottomless appetites — every Northern Song "reform" accelerated institutional collapse. The most fatal factor wasn't any single problem, but the structural misalignment of the political system: when the New and Old Parties stopped judging by right and wrong and judged only by factional loyalty, the nation had lost all capacity to respond to the coming storm.
