Dongpo's Forest of Jottings · Chapter 8「Historic Traces — Antiquities, Dwellings, and Leisure Pavilions」

SCQA · 情境 → 冲突 → 问题 → 回答 Su Shi's footsteps covered the length and breadth of the land. Wherever he went, he explored historical ruins, verified local chronicles, examined customs — the Iron Tomb of Chenzhou, Yong'an City of Huangzhou, the Han Dynasty lecture hall, the Red Cliffs of Fanshan, the Snow Hall of Dongpo… He was not merely a sightseeing tourist but an archaeologist-poet. When exile pushed him to geographical margins, how did he recover fragments of history from broken walls, and create — beneath the Taihang Mountains, beside the Hejiang Tower, within Lintao Pavilion — a spiritual home where "wherever the heart finds peace, there is home"? His answer: he remained always a "leisurely one" — the wind and moon over rivers and mountains have no fixed master; the leisurely one becomes the master.

This chapter collects Su Shi's essays on historic sites (The Iron Tomb and Terrace of Distress, Huangzhou as Sui Yong'an Commandery, The Han Lecture Hall, Mount Fan, Red Cliff Caves), jade and stone (Discerning True Jade, Red Vein Stone), wells and rivers (The Tube Well Water-Powered Method, Bian River Sluice Gates), choosing dwellings (Divining a Dwelling at Taihang, Fan Zhen Invites Me to Be His Neighbor, Amusements Below Hejiang Tower, Naming West Pavilion), and pavilions (Idle Notes at Lintao, Naming the Rong'an Pavilion, The Chen Family Thatched Hall, Questions at Snow Hall). Seventeen essays spanning five themes — a perfect fusion of Su Shi's observations on material culture and his philosophy of personal living.

01
Antiquarian Research
Iron Tomb·Yong'an Commandery·Han Lecture Hall——Su Shi's Archaeological Eye
Passing through Chenzhou, Su Shi stayed over seventy days, visiting every scenic spot near the city. "By Willow Lake is a mound, popularly called the Iron Tomb — said to be the tomb of Duke Hu of Chen. The city moat water erodes its base, revealing iron bindings." The tomb was bound with iron, hence "Iron Tomb" — this was field research on the burial of the founding lord of the Zhou-dynasty state of Chen. There was also a temple called "Terrace of Distress," said to be where Confucius was trapped between Chen and Cai — Su Shi maintained reservations about this legend. In Huangzhou, consulting the "Sui History — Geographical Records," he ascertained that Huangzhou was the Sui-dynasty Yong'an Commandery, while locals called it "Queen City" — Su Shi used documentary evidence to correct folk error: "The 'Illustrated Gazetteer' takes it for the ancient city of Lord Chunshen, which is also wrong. Lord Chunshen's capital was the former state of Wu; now on Huishan in Wuxi stands the Lord Chunshen Temple." The Han lecture hall's murals left Su Shi awestruck — "the paintings are truly imposing. Such ancient pigments — nothing earlier than these." This sense of shock at original Han-dynasty murals came from a literatus who truly understood visual art.

02
Landscape Exploration
Mount Fan and Red Cliffs——Tracing the Source from Battlefield to Poetic Image
Living beneath Lintao Pavilion, Su Shi had the great river right outside his door. From his residence he "crossed the currents westward, mooring at Mount Fan," investigating the triple origin of the name: "Some say Burned Mountain — in drought years they burned it to rouse the dragon and bring rain; some say the Fan family dwelled there." The mountain also held the site where Sun Quan encountered a great storm — Su Shi interwove historical legend with field survey. His visit to the Red Cliff caves is even more famous literary archaeology — he personally went to Huangzhou's Red Cliff: "Sheer cliffs stand vertical, the river water deep green, two falcons nest above, two snakes, sometimes seen. When wind and waves are calm, I take a small boat below, disembark and climb ashore, entering Lord Xu's grotto" — non-human traces of natural erosion led him to conjecture about the cliff's formation. Though he knew Huangzhou's Red Cliff might not be where Zhou Yu defeated Cao Cao ("some say this is where Zhou Yu defeated Lord Cao — I do not know if it is truly so"), this did not hinder his poetic reshaping of the natural landscape. It was precisely this attitude, at once truth-seeking and aesthetic, that gave birth to immortal works like the "Former and Latter Odes on the Red Cliff.

03
Jade, Stone, Wells, Rivers
True Jade Hard to Discern·The Tube Well Invention——Witness to Song Material Culture
Su Shi had a keen interest in the material world. "Discerning True Jade" investigates the standard for true jade — "only what Dingzhou porcelain glaze cannot scratch" is genuine jade, while the so-called true jade on the market is "merely the finest of jade-like stones." Even he admitted, "I asked the old jade craftsman of the rear garden, and he too does not know if this is reliable" — displaying a humility toward specialized knowledge. "Red Vein Stone" praised Qingzhou red-vein stone as the finest of inkstones — "now seeing what Xue'an has collected, I know the ancients did not praise it lightly." "The Tube Well Water-Powered Method" is a significant record of technological history — Sichuan, far from the sea, obtained salt from brine wells. From the Qingli and Huangyou eras, the "tube well" was invented — using a circular blade to drill wells the size of a bowl, the deepest reaching dozens of zhang, with giant bamboo, nodes removed, male and female joining as the well casing — this is precious documentation of early Chinese deep-drilling technology. "Bian River Sluice Gates" records a failed water management policy — "the court built Bian River sluice gates to silt the fields, those who understood all said it wouldn't work, yet they built them, and ultimately achieved nothing." When water was high and gates were opened, fields, graves, and houses were all harmed; when water receded in autumn and gates opened, the silt was too thin — policymakers who didn't understand natural laws wasted labor and resources.

04
Choosing a Dwelling
Taihang Divination·Beneath Hejiang Tower——Finding "Where the Heart Finds Peace" in Exile
Su Shi's life was a series of displacements, yet he never abandoned the imagination of "home." In "Divining a Dwelling at Taihang," Liu Zhongju brought fine rice to visit, "speaking of the wonders of Hundred Springs, urging me to be his neighbor" — Su Shi's heart stirred: "My heart already drifts at the foot of the Taihang range!" Yet when Fan Zhen invited him to be a neighbor at Xuxia, he politely declined — "Xuxia is full of high officials, while I, in my rain cape and bamboo hat, wander freely on the Eastern Slope — how could I still serve high officials?" Having experienced the vicissitudes of officialdom, he preferred to keep his distance from aristocratic society. There's an interesting paradox: if one has long roamed freely, one feels no illness; but once one begins "maintaining health," "a hundred illnesses erupt. Just as a neglected district, coasting on laxity, also seems trouble-free — but suddenly meeting a capable official, a hundred abuses surface at once" — this is at once self-mockery about his body and a metaphor for officialdom. Beneath Hejiang Tower, he gazed at the river and the crude thatched shops — "Is there not an inch of land to relocate to? Yet they cling and refuse to leave — always sand in others' eyes." What others saw as eyesores, he saw as the unvarnished face of life. In "Naming West Pavilion," he wanted to give Shanyang's west pavilion a good name, "pondering but finding none ideal" — a man so earnest about naming a building held deep affection for every blade of grass.

05
Pavilion Joys
Idle Notes at Lintao·Rong'an Pavilion·Snow Hall——"The Leisurely One Becomes the Master"
Su Shi's most famous declaration of life philosophy appears in "Idle Notes at Lintao" — "Eighty paces below Lintao Pavilion is the great river, half of which is melted snow from Emei. I draw my food and bathwater all from it — why must I return home! The wind and moon over river and mountain have no fixed master; the leisurely one becomes the master." He connected his hometown Emei's melted snow with his exile's Huangzhou great river — eating and bathing from the same waters, geographical distance was spiritually dissolved. This is why, banished to the ends of the earth, he could still dwell in peace. "Naming the Rong'an Pavilion" is just a few lines — Tao Yuanming said "leaning on the south window to express my pride, I find it easy to be at peace in a knee-sized room," so Su Shi "always wanted a small pavilion, naming it Rong'an" — a tiny room, enough space for one's knees, is enough for peace; this is Tao Yuanming's spiritual inheritance. "The Chen Family Thatched Hall" describes a waterfall "like suspended cloth, collapsing snow, like catkins in wind, like a flock of cranes dancing" — Canliaozi "begged this place for his retirement," and the owner agreed — Su Shi and his friends were always seeking their own Peach Blossom Spring. And in "Questions at Snow Hall," he recorded how he built Snow Hall on the Eastern Slope — "The hall was built during heavy snow, so I painted snow across all four walls, leaving no gap. Rising and reclining, gazing around and glancing sideways — nothing but snow" — this man who filled his four walls with painted snow created for himself, in his place of exile, a winter that would never melt.
The wind and moon over river and mountain have no fixed master; the leisurely one becomes the master.
— Su Shi, "Idle Notes at Lintao"
KEY INSIGHT
Su Shi's journey through historic traces follows a clear thread: from external archaeological inquiry to the internal dwelling place of the spirit. He verified the Iron Tomb, identified Yong'an City, explored the Red Cliffs — these were probings into the depths of time. He sought a dwelling at Taihang, declined the offer at Xuxia, sat idly at Lintao, built Snow Hall in a blizzard — these were declarations of spatial freedom. Examining historic traces was to understand where we came from; building pavilions was to decide where we were going. Together, they form a complete portrait of Su Shi — a perpetual traveler who discovered history in ruins and built home in exile.
