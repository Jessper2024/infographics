The Siege of Bianjing · Bibliography "A Jingkang Panorama Built from 100+ Historical Sources"

Reference Overview From official histories to eyewitness memoirs, from military geography to later scholarship — Guo Jianlong deployed four categories of sources to construct a multi-dimensional narrative of the Jingkang Incident. Behind every passage stands genuine historical documentation.

The Siege of Bianjing lists over one hundred reference works, building a complete chain of evidence from the official histories of Song, Liao, and Jin to eyewitness accounts. The bibliography is organized in four categories: Compilations (official histories, hui-bian digests, hui-yao administrative compendia) provide the macro-framework and chronological spine; Geography & Military works (such as Dushi Fangyu Jiyao) reconstruct battlefield terrain and campaign routes; Eyewitness Notes (Sanchao Beimeng Huibian, Jingkang Baishi, and dozens of personal accounts) constitute the most precious primary sources — including siege diaries, envoy travelogues, and the anguished records of captives; Reviews & Fiction (Nian'er Shi Zhaji, Song Lun, Da Song Xuanhe Yishi) provide critical perspectives from historians across eight centuries. These four categories cross-verify and triangulate with one another, giving every pivotal decision of the Jingkang years a multi-perspective evidentiary foundation.

01
Official Framework
Compilation sources: the three dynastic histories as skeleton, digests and compendia as flesh
Compilation sources supply the macro-narrative framework of the Jingkang Incident — the first layer through which to understand the entire era. Core works include the three official histories compiled under Toghto (Toqto'a) of the Yuan dynasty — the History of Song, History of Liao, and History of Jin — the foundational texts for understanding the institutions, figures, and events of all three regimes. Li Tao's Xu Zizhi Tongjian Changbian (Extended Continuation of the Comprehensive Mirror) and its supplements trace Northern Song political evolution in full. Xu Mengxin's Sanchao Beimeng Huibian (Compilation of Treaties with the North Across Three Reigns) is the single most important digest of Song-Jin diplomacy and warfare. Xu Song's Song Huiyao Jigao preserves original texts of edicts and memorials. Additionally, Song Zaifu Biannian Lu details chancellor appointments, Wenxian Tongkao offers a long-view of institutional change, and Da Jin Diaofa Lu specifically collects Jin state documents concerning the Song campaign. Together, these compilations form the "official narrative" layer of the Jingkang Incident — filtered and polished by court historians, yet the most systematic record available.

02
Battlefield Reconstruction
Geography & military sources: reconstructing the terrain of every defense line, the route of every campaign
The essence of the Siege of Bianjing is a contest of geography — the Yellow River barrier, the Taiyuan choke-point, Bianjing's indefensible flatlands — all dictated the war's course. Gu Zuyu's Dushi Fangyu Jiyao (Essentials of Historical Geography) is the indispensable classic for understanding Chinese military geography — it analyzes in detail Bianjing's fatal weakness as a "battlefield on four sides" and the strategic position of every prefecture and county in the defensive network. Gu Yanwu's Tianxia Junguo Libing Shu and Zhaoyu Zhi survey national terrain from a macroscopic perspective. Yue Shi's Taiping Huanyu Ji provides early-Song administrative geography. The Taiwan Armed Forces University's History of Chinese Wars Through the Ages replays Song-Jin campaigns through the lens of modern military science. Li Lian's Bianjing Yiji Zhi and Meng Yuanlao's Dongjing Meng Hua Lu are firsthand materials for reconstructing the internal layout of Bianjing — the walls, gates, wards, and palaces.

03
Firsthand Witness
Eyewitness notes: siege diaries, captives' laments, envoys' travelogues — the most precious flesh-and-blood details
The note/memoir category is The Siege of Bianjing's most powerful source of material — they come directly from the pens of those who lived through the siege. The Jingkang Baishi (Unofficial History of the Jingkang Reign) is the core of cores, collecting eight firsthand accounts: Xuanhe Yisi Fengshi Jinguo Xingcheng Lu (an envoy's view of Jin), Wengzhong Renyu (a diary-style siege record), Kaifeng Fu Zhuang (official administrative archives), Nanzheng Lu Hui (from the Jin military perspective), Qinggong Yiyu (oral accounts of captured palace women), Shenyin Yu (the sufferings of captives), and Song Fu Ji (a roster of prisoners). Beyond these, Zhao Shenzhi's Zhongxing Yishi, Cao Xun's Beishou Jianwen Lu (by the man who smuggled out the secret edict), Ma Kuo's Maozhai Zixu (personal experience as envoy to Jin), and dozens of other notes cross-verify one another — together forming the most vivid, flesh-and-blood narrative layer of the Jingkang Incident.

04
Private Records
Song-Yuan miscellaneous notes: from Tieweishan Congtan to Rongzhai Suibi — the vast private sphere beyond official historiography
Song-dynasty miscellaneous notes (biji) are an indispensable supplement to official histories — they record political inside stories, character anecdotes, and social atmospheres that the official record dared not or would not capture. Cai Tao's Tieweishan Congtan (the author was Cai Jing's son) reveals inside details of the Flower-and-Rock Network and Genyue's construction. Hong Mai's Rongzhai Suibi brings a critical scholar's rigor to historical puzzles. Shao Bowen's Shaoshi Wenjian Lu offers a firsthand view of the New-Old Faction struggle. Ting Shi and Qidong Yeyu preserve many political secrets from the early Southern Song. Zhao Yongchun's annotated collection Fengshi Liao Jin Xingcheng Lu gathers the travelogues of Song, Liao, and Jin envoys — Xu Kangzong's description of the Jin Supreme Capital as "earthen hovels" in 1125, set against Zhou Hui's account of Yanjing's resplendent glazed-tile palaces a century later, forms a perfect mirror of Jurchen civilization's fifty-year transformation.

05
Historical Critique
Reviews & later scholarship: from Wang Fuzhi to modern scholars — eight centuries of historical reflection
The Jingkang Incident is not merely a Song-dynasty memory — across eight hundred years, every major historian has re-examined its lessons through their own lens. The peak of Qing-dynasty evidential scholarship — Qian Daxin's Nian'er Shi Kaoyi and Zhao Yi's Nian'er Shi Zhaji — conducted rigorous textual comparison and factual verification of the three histories. Wang Fuzhi's Song Lun (On the Song) dissects the roots of Northern Song institutional decay with a philosopher's depth. Chen Bangzhan's Songshi Jishi Benmo reorganizes Song history around events rather than chronology. Guo Jianlong's own "Empire Code Trilogy" — Fiscal Code, Philosophical Code, Military Code — constitutes his modern analytical framework for interpreting the Jingkang period. Finally, Da Song Xuanhe Yishi and Shuihu Quanzhuan (Water Margin), though literary fiction, are included as corroborating evidence of "period atmosphere," showing how folk memory reconstructed history.
"Every passage on the Jingkang Incident is built upon the cross-verification of these four categories of sources. Official histories provide the skeleton, notes provide the flesh and blood, geographical studies provide the battlefield, and later criticism provides reflection — only by overlaying these four dimensions can the full truth of this era come into view."
🔑 Key Takeaway
The scholarly rigor of The Siege of Bianjing rests on over one hundred reference works — this is not a novel but a documented historical monograph. The four source categories each serve a distinct function: compilation histories provide the macro-framework, geography-and-military works reconstruct the spatial dimension, eyewitness notes supply the flesh-and-blood of firsthand detail, and review works offer eight centuries of critical reflection. Most precious are the records of those who lived through the siege — the daily texture of Wengzhong Renyu, the suffering of Shenyin Yu, the cold bureaucratic archives of Kaifeng Fu Zhuang — investing the tears and decisions of a millennium ago with document-level authenticity. Guo Jianlong's handling of sources distinguishes this book from any "historical romance" — every conclusion rests on multiple source corroboration, and every episode can be traced back to its original documentary source.
