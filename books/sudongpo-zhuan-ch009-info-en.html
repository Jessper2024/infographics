Wang Anshi, the "Stubborn Premier" (拗相公), adopted "The Three Nots" as his creed — refusing to fear Heaven's mandate, heed public opinion, or follow ancestral laws. Backed by Emperor Shenzong, he silenced all dissent, alienated every honest friend, and elevated sycophantic villains — only to be betrayed by his most trusted lieutenant, Lü Huiqing, becoming the ultimate victim of his own obstinacy.

Core QuestionHow did Wang Anshi transform from a widely admired reformer into the "Stubborn Premier"? Why did his obstinacy destroy not only the reform but himself?

Core AnswerThe political storm erupted from the clash between Wang Anshi and the opposition — statesmen nurtured in Emperor Renzong's atmosphere of academic freedom. Wang Anshi could not tolerate dissent from any quarter and sought to silence all criticism, especially from the censors whose constitutional duty was to critique governance. The struggle became a war between "the vulgar herd" and "the enlightened innovators" — Wang Anshi branded all who disagreed with him as "vulgar." With Shenzong's backing, he overstepped his authority, making policy decisions privately at home with Lü Huiqing and Zeng Bu. Without formal party politics in the Song system, factional strife took the form of "one faction in power, the other exiled from the capital" — a pattern that continued until the dynasty's fall.

1
Defining Creed

The Three Nots: A Stubborn Man's Manifesto

"Neither fear Heaven's mandate, nor heed public opinion, nor follow ancestral laws" — these three phrases, pinned on Wang Anshi by Su Dongpo, capture his entire political philosophy: defying Providence, despising consensus, and overthrowing tradition through sheer individual will against the entire world's opposition.

In the earliest surviving vernacular Chinese short story, The Stubborn Premier, Wang Anshi was branded with this nickname immediately after his death. The tragedy of the political struggle originated with a powerful man who "refused to listen to advice or admit mistakes" — the more friends opposed him, the more determined he became. Perseverance is a virtue, but Wang Anshi mistook stubbornness for virtue. He clung to his family maxim that "determination is the path to success," not understanding that determination in the wrong direction only accelerates destruction.

🔴 The Stubborn Man's "Three Nots"

Fear not Heaven's mandate · Heed not public opinion · Follow not ancestral laws. These three were both his source of strength and his path to ruin — disaster looms when a ruler believes himself wiser than Providence, consensus, and tradition combined.

🟢 The Genuine Reformer's Path

A true reformer "transforms" while maintaining reverence: respecting Heaven's mandate (the popular will), welcoming diverse counsel, and adapting ancestral laws gradually. Wang Anshi rejected all three, veering into extremism.

Lin Yutang observes: Wang Anshi could not endure opposition from anyone — friend or foe alike. An eloquent speaker convinced the emperor could build a powerful nation, he was determined to implement socialist policies. He had to silence all opposition, especially the censors. "Opening wide the avenues of speech" defines good governance; "blocking them" defines bad — this is the fundamental principle of Chinese political philosophy.

2
Factional Lines

The Lopsided Battle: Reformers vs. Opposition

The lineup was as tragic as it was absurd: on one side, the Stubborn Premier backed by Emperor Shenzong and a pack of ambitious sycophants; on the other, nearly the entire constellation of Renzong-era luminaries — Fan Zhongyan, Ouyang Xiu, Sima Guang, Su Dongpo — the greatest assembly of scholar-statesmen in Song history. Wang Anshi had alienated virtually every friend he ever had.

Even Wang Anshi's own brothers — Wang Anli and Wang Anguo — stood with the opposition. How had Wang Anshi managed to offend every friend he had? Why would Emperor Shenzong pay such an enormous price — dismissing, demoting, and punishing the opposition — to keep Wang Anshi in power? In the end, Shenzong had no choice but to remove him. "If knowing men is the foundation of sagacity, then the posthumous title 'Shenzong' (Divine Ancestor) was ill-deserved."

3
Freedom of Speech

"The Vulgar" vs. "The Enlightened": The Battle for Free Criticism

Wang Anshi branded everyone who disagreed with him as "the vulgar herd" (流俗) while labeling himself and his allies "the enlightened reformers" (通变) — the most dangerous form of binary thinking: if you are not with me, you are my enemy. Censors whose constitutional duty was to critique the government were accused of "maliciously obstructing the New Policies."

The Song Dynasty lacked formal party politics — there was no vote, no show of hands, no mechanism for determining majority opinion. Officials met, discussed, and reached consensus. In principle and practice, the government encouraged criticism. Opposition figures could overturn the cabinet or resign. When fierce factional strife erupted, the custom was to dispatch the opposition from the capital to provincial posts. Thus "one faction seizes power, the other leaves the capital."

⚠️ Historical Warning: Wang Anshi accused the opposition of "seeking to gag the mouths of others," yet he himself was the one doing the gagging. The prime minister purged all censors who opposed him, marginalized rivals, and packed the court with his own faction — precisely the crimes the opposition charged him with.

The struggle quickly escalated from concrete issues — the Green Sprouts Loan, censorial freedom — to the more fundamental question of the right to criticize and dissent. Wang Anshi won the first round, and from then on the bureaucracy split into two factions whose bitter struggle continued until the Song Dynasty's fall. Years later, the New Laws were revised or abolished — but the factionalism they spawned inflicted far more lasting damage on the state.

4
The Sycophants

Three Villains and One Immortal Quote

Alone and desperate, Wang Anshi elevated obscure, unqualified men — Li Ding concealed his mother's death to avoid resigning for mourning, Deng Wan uttered the immortal shameless line "Let them curse and laugh — I'll keep my fine office," and Shu Dan would later prosecute Su Dongpo — men who excelled at sycophantic compliance and used the Prime Minister for their own ends.

Wang Anshi's greatest supporters were actually Lü Huiqing and Zeng Bu — the former especially important: cunning and eloquent, he ultimately betrayed Wang Anshi and supplanted him. Contemporaries summed up the eight-year reform collapse in one sentence: "Lü Huiqing betrayed Wang Anshi, Wang Anshi betrayed the emperor, the emperor betrayed the people." Huiqing despicably published Wang Anshi's private letters to alienate him from the emperor. In his final years, Wang Anshi would write the characters for "Fujian man" dozens of times a day (Lü Huiqing was from Fujian) to vent his rage.

5
Alienation

Even His Brothers Stood Against Him

Most shocking of all: Wang Anshi's own brothers — Wang Anli and Wang Anguo — both stood with the opposition. Wang Anguo once quoted Confucius to his brother's face: as Wang Anshi and Lü Huiqing were conferring, Anguo played his flute outside. The Prime Minister barked at his brother, "Can't you drive away the Zheng music?" His brother shot back, "Can't you drive away the sycophants?"

Wang Anshi estranged all his good friends. Han Wei, as the crown prince's tutor, had once steered the future Emperor Shenzong's thinking entirely toward Wang Anshi. Lü Gongzhu had been a close friend. Yet the moment these friends disagreed even slightly with his policies, Wang Anshi expelled them from court. Isolated and desperate, he could only elevate sycophants. His son Wang Pang was shrewd, perverse, and cruel — bearing responsibility for many of the New Policies' worst abuses — once bursting in barefoot and disheveled, clutching a woman's sash, and declaring: "We just need to chop off Han Qi's and Fu Bi's heads — then no one will dare oppose us!"

This was not a happy family. Both uncles disapproved of Wang Anshi's conduct and especially warned him to beware the double-dealer Lü Huiqing.

What makes this truly tragic is that Wang Anshi never indulged in vice or corruption — his methods were born of necessity. To realize his radical state-capitalist program, he knew he had to crush all opposition. He had a beautiful dream, and everything pointed toward that distant goal: not a nation happy, peaceful, and prosperous, but one powerful and expansionist like the Han and Tang. Yet in the eyes of future historians, no "great hero" appears anything but pitiable — "trapped in the prison of his own ambition, a sacrifice to his own dream, which grew and expanded until, like a bubble, it burst."

6
Systemic Flaws

The Song Bureaucracy: A Breeding Ground for Power Chaos

The Song Dynasty had no clear office of prime minister — the cabinet resembled a parliament, with the emperor balancing competing powers. The government featured complex, overlapping departments with redundant functions, and final authority always rested with the emperor. This structure, designed for checks and balances, instead created space for Wang Anshi's power grabs.

The so-called "Prime Minister" was formally "Joint Manager of Affairs with the Secretariat-Chancellery," potentially with two deputies. Fiscal agencies operated independently, reporting directly to the emperor. Besides the censors of the Three Departments, there was an independent Censorate. Wang Anshi began merely as Assistant Administrator (deputy premier), but with Shenzong's backing, he exceeded his authority — making decisions privately at home with Lü Huiqing and Zeng Bu — precisely the condition for the bitter confrontations that followed.

Later, Emperor Shenzong abruptly attempted to simplify the bureaucratic structure — the Chancellery would deliberate, the Secretariat promulgate, and State Affairs execute — but chaos persisted with responsibilities unfocused. Lin Yutang attributes the court's incessant quarrels to this institutional design. "Two issues were paramount: the Green Sprouts Loan and the censors' freedom of speech." These two — representing economic centralization and political authoritarianism — found fertile ground precisely in the Song system's structural vulnerabilities.

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

Wang Anshi became the "Stubborn Premier" not because of his reform policies themselves, but because of how he handled opposition: branding all critics as "the vulgar herd," equating himself with "enlightened reform," silencing censors, expelling friends, and elevating sycophants. This binary mindset — if you are not with me, you are my enemy — not only destroyed the reform but planted the seeds of factional warfare that would consume the Northern Song. As Lin Yutang notes, when the New Policies collapsed, "Lü Huiqing betrayed Wang Anshi, Wang Anshi betrayed the emperor, the emperor betrayed the people." An incorruptible idealist who became a sacrifice to his own obstinacy — this is the ultimate tragedy of the Stubborn Premier.
