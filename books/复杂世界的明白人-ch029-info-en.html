Sensible in a Complex World · Ch.29 "Winter of the Pandemic, New Forces Are Thriving"

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer For millennia, China has been a major producer deeply embedded in global trade. Now, with full coverage across 39 industrial categories, it has returned to the top. COVID-19 struck like a black swan — offline shutdowns, factory stoppages, traditional organizations paralyzed — but it was also a Darwinian filter. Which new forces rose against the winter headwind? The answer: distributed remote organizations, digitized large enterprises, and technology-empowered small players — a new wave of production and organizational transformation has arrived.

This is the book's closing chapter, surveying the present from the deep perspective of history. The author first points out what Chinese people have long overlooked: China was the core producer in global trade for thousands of years — from the Silk Road to maritime trade, never disconnected from the world market. After a ~200-year trough in the modern era, China has returned to the peak: all 39 industrial categories fully covered, information technology now in the world's first tier. The pandemic happened to be an accelerator of old-vs-new divergence: offline businesses suffered immensely, while online gaming, collaborative work platforms, and distributed content creators surged. The author predicts: the cubicle punch-clock model will be massively replaced by fully virtual workspaces. Mission-driven OKR organizations will become the new normal. Final verdict: technology empowering the little guy — this is nothing short of a miracle of our time.

01
Historical Depth
The return of a millennial producer: from the Silk Road to Tier-1 IT
China was never separate from the world market — quite the opposite. The men of the mighty Han and prosperous Tang shed blood in the Western Regions precisely to defend trade routes and secure commercial interests. Eurasia was like a carrying pole — Europe on one end, China on the other, Central Asia profiting as middlemen for a thousand years. After the Age of Discovery, trade shifted to the Atlantic, and China declined due to population involution. But history didn't end there: after the century of war from 1840 to 1949, China sprinted back to the summit in 40 years — all 39 industrial categories covered, from clothing and shoes to aerospace. In 2020, Tencent beat American firms to provide IT services to the United Nations, marking China's entry into the global Tier-1 of information technology. Japan and Korea took the same path — first taking outsourcing, then focusing on innovation, then catching up with the West in high-end industries. China is walking the same road.

02
Darwinian Filter
The pandemic is a black swan — and a Darwinian filter
As Dalio put it well: "These things don't happen often in our lifetimes, but from the full sweep of history, these social shocks are quite common — they come around every so often." The pandemic hit different organizational models with radically different force: offline businesses suffered immensely — factory stoppages, restaurant closures, traditional offices emptied. Yet adaptive online businesses and organizations thrived against the wind: online gaming enjoyed a windfall, remote collaboration app stocks surged, virtual KTVs and virtual clubbing emerged. This divergence inevitably forces a reorganization of society's entire structure — the weak eliminated, the strong growing stronger — precisely a periodic Darwinian selection.

03
Org Transformation
The rise of distributed organizations: cubicles being replaced by virtual workspaces
Mission-driven OKR management theory has been around for a while, but the technology tools reached a tipping point in 2021. The author illustrates with three real cases: ① A software project team used WeChat meetings to take on outsourcing projects from home — income went up, not down, and they can no longer imagine going to the office — running a "decentralized company" in pajamas via conference calls. ② A 20-something-person content creator team scattered worldwide, using WeChat groups to source, discuss, and publish content — never had a centralized office, completely unscathed by the pandemic. ③ GitLab employs 1,000+ people across 60+ countries with zero office space, fully remote — the gold standard for a pure online company. If you feel unproductive at home, if you're only efficient when someone is watching you — you might not be doing "mission-driven" work.

04
Enterprise Benchmark
Super-platforms that never go offline: Tencent powers the UN, big tech runs 24/7 remote collaboration
The UN's digital transformation is just the symbolic beginning — if even the world's top traditional institution made the leap, everyone else must follow. The author corroborates with personal experience: during the Spring Festival period, two projects the author led at a Big Tech company didn't stop for a single second — one teammate quarantined in Wuhan, one in Beijing, some overseas — all collaborating entirely via online chat and online meetings. Super-apps (WeChat, Douyin, etc.) sit atop server farms the size of town squares, serving hundreds of millions of users 24/7 — during the pandemic, not only did they not falter, they doubled down on expanding online services. Meanwhile, Google's robot-dog balance algorithms were developed by US and European scientists working distributed; Google's Beijing and Shanghai ML labs collaborate with North America via meeting platforms — remote collaboration has long penetrated the most cutting-edge research frontiers.

05
Individual Rise
Technology empowering the little guy: the miracle of our time
The means of production are no longer monopolized by capitalists — as long as you're willing, online resources are everywhere, extremely cheap, essentially available on demand. Once, organizing a gathering of tens of millions could drain a nation's treasury; now, a few grassroots content creators can pull it off — that's the power of technology. The author cites a major Bilibili channel: several people dividing tasks in their spare time — writing, recording, video editing — collaborating entirely online, a pure "internet commons" mode of production. As remote meeting tools become ubiquitous, they will bring entirely new organizational forms: more efficient, more complex, more flexible. Weak enterprises were eliminated during the pandemic; those that survived toughed out a full cycle and emerged stronger. The author believes: the next decade too will be a decade of the unexpected — some things are declining, more things are rising.
"If you feel unproductive working from home, if you're only efficient when supervised — then you might not be doing 'mission-driven' work. Which means you don't actually love what you do."
— The author's response to remote-work skeptics
🔑 Key Takeaway
The pandemic was a disaster, but also a forced upgrade of society's operating model. Organizations that could go online rose against the wind; old models dependent on physical space accelerated their exit. Distributed remote work is no longer a niche experiment — from GitLab to the UN, from Big Tech super-apps to grassroots content creators, pure virtual collaboration has penetrated every layer of society. Technology empowering the little guy is the most remarkable phenomenon of our era: online resources are available on demand, anyone with a bit of integration becomes an entrepreneur. The next decade, too, will be full of the unexpected — some things declining, more things rising. Whether you're pessimistic or optimistic, in the long run, both are right.
