Great Country, Big City · Ch.4「Beware the 'Twisted Hand'」

Hukou · Land System · Labor Shortage · Industrial Policy · Capital Deepening Under the GDP ranking tournament, local officials pour capital into the interior while the hukou system keeps labor from cities. The author warns: without reforming hukou and land institutions, development that deviates from comparative advantage brings only debt and misallocation.

This is Book Chapter 4. Drawing on field research in a western province in 2012, the author reveals the investment frenzy under the GDP ranking tournament: county-level fixed-asset investment growth exceeded 50%, with five counties exceeding 100%. Zero land prices and tax breaks were standard. The chapter proposes a "Three Principles of Fairness" framework for regional development — efficiency of public investment, free population movement, and fiscal transfers — illustrated through a high-speed rail case study. It then examines the linked reform of land and hukou systems: cross-regional trading of construction land quotas could dramatically improve efficiency — land use efficiency drops 50% for every 500km from a deep-water port. The author rebuts popular anti-urbanization arguments about "labor shortage" and "hollow villages," showing that with urbanization barely above 50%, China's problem is not over-urbanization but obstructed urbanization.

01
Fairness
Three Principles of Regional Development Fairness
Should high-speed rail connect major cities or every small town? A conversation with a lawyer friend frames the core principle: the "Three Fairness Principles": First, efficiency of public investment — HSR connecting big cities is efficient; it should not pass through every county and village. Second, free population movement — people from small cities can migrate to access HSR benefits. Third, fiscal transfers — areas not directly benefiting should receive compensation through fiscal transfers to share national development gains. All three are essential. This is the core framework for government-market relations: "The government should better perform its own functions, not replace the market's." Fiscal transfers should go to public services (education, healthcare), not productive investment — human capital can move with people, but fixed investments that lack competitiveness become worthless.
02
Reform
Linked Reform of Land and Hukou Systems
China's construction land quota system severely distorts regional economic adjustment. Cities with population inflows face tight land quotas; central/west cities have quotas but no enterprises. The solution: let coastal cities purchase construction land quotas from the interior. When migrant farmers return their rural homestead to farmland, the corresponding land quota is transferable to their working city. This way, the interior preserves more farmland, the coast develops industry, and labor migrates east — linked with hukou reform. The efficiency gains are enormous: every 500km from a major port (roughly Shanghai to western Anhui), land use efficiency drops 50%. Within 450km of a port, the scale effect of urban expansion dominates; beyond 450km, the negative effect dominates. Accelerating urban expansion within 450km of ports best improves land use efficiency.
03
Justice
"Veil of Ignorance" and Public Policy Fairness
The author invokes Rawls' "veil of ignorance": in public policy debate, one's position should be independent of one's social identity. A fair policy should satisfy: whether you are a direct beneficiary or loser, if roles were swapped, the policy would still be considered reasonable. Many current policies fail this test: big cities welcome migrant workers but say "children and elderly, stay away." Flip the perspective — if you were a migrant worker, would you want to leave your children and parents behind? "Almost every urban Chinese today, when traced back three generations, came from the countryside." China will soon be the world's largest economy, but "development is material; values are civilizational."
04
Geography
Distance to Port Determines Land Use Efficiency
Rigorous data analysis shows geography's decisive impact on economic efficiency. Figure 4-1: every 500km from a major port reduces land use efficiency by approximately 50%. From 1990–1995, the distance effect was weak; but from 1995–2000 and 2000–2006, geography's impact grew steadily — precisely coinciding with the post-2003 policy of channeling land quotas to the interior. If land allocation policy is not changed, land use efficiency losses will only grow. More importantly, city-level GDP size gaps are expanding rapidly (big cities get bigger), but population size gaps have barely changed — the economy is agglomerating, but population is not, because the hukou system restricts free migration. This prevents cities' per capita GDP gaps from narrowing. "Moving people" is the long-term path; "moving money" is supplementary.

05
Labor
Where Did All the Workers Go? The Truth About "Labor Shortage"
Job shortages in cities alongside hollow villages — does this really mean China faces severe labor shortage? The urbanization rate was only 54% at end-2014 — barely past halfway. Only about 20% of rural-urban migrants move as whole families. Without family migration, young males go to cities for work first. The result: 50 million left-behind elderly, over 10 million left-behind women, 61 million left-behind children. Rural aging and feminization are not signals of labor exhaustion — they are consequences of institutional constraints. With reform, family migration will increase. The service sector is rising, and services require much less physical strength than manufacturing. What we should worry about is not total labor depletion, but whether cities can continuously raise labor productivity.

06
Agriculture
Population Outflow and Agricultural Transformation
Many worry that rural population decline will threaten agriculture. The author counters: agricultural output depends mainly on land area, not population. China's grain output has grown steadily even as urbanization advanced. Machines are replacing labor — this is productivity improvement. China's per capita agricultural land is 1/400 of the US and 1/40 of the EU. Small land plots inevitably mean low agricultural incomes. Continuously reducing the agricultural population is the only path to competitive farm prices. A telling paradox: China's grain inventories and imports are both rising — because government price supports keep domestic prices above import prices. US grain is cheap precisely because of scale farming. Over time, as farm sizes expand, rural population structure will endogenously adjust — younger, more educated workers will return to agriculture.
07
Outflow
Population Outflow May Be Good: A Different View of Hollow Villages
China's "hollow villages" raise concern, but seen differently: population outflow from less-developed areas is a result of optimal resource allocation. Every developed country experienced hollow villages during urbanization. Even after urbanization completion, many cities shrink as population concentrates in major metro areas — Detroit fell from 1.8M (1950) to 710,000 (2010). Norway's 2005-2009 population flow map shows major cities in deep red (net inflow) and small cities mostly blue (outflow). Population outflow is not frightening. 80% of Americans live on 4% of US land, yet per capita GDP is nearly equal across states. For China, hollow villages reflect the spatial optimization of population. Some ancient villages will become tourist destinations; homesteads will return to farmland — the market decides which.
08
Land Rights
Rural Land Reform: Don't "Protect" Farmers by Banning Transactions
Opponents of rural land reform offer a paradoxical logic: protect farmers by banning market transactions. The author counters: land that cannot be transacted is not an asset. The key is not prohibition, but ensuring voluntariness, employment, social security, and market pricing. During field research in a poor Chongqing village, a farmer asked: "City people can mortgage their homes — why can't farmers do the same with their land?" Another objection involves "land annexation" — but China is no longer an agricultural economy; agriculture is only 10% of GDP. "To prevent farmers from transacting out of fear that land values will be underestimated — this is the most absurd logic." Banning homestead transactions has a clear result: vast rural housing stands empty, some newly built — because people think they must eventually return to the village. Much of this investment is wasted.
09
NE China
Northeast China in Crisis? The Truth About Per Capita GDP Growth
Media headlines scream "Northeast China in crisis" — population outflow, rapid aging. But the author points out: population outflow does not mean economic decline. In 2014, per capita GDP in the three northeastern provinces was roughly three times its level a decade earlier. After adjusting for inflation, Liaoning's 2014 per capita GDP was 2.6× its 2005 level, Jilin's was 2.8×, Heilongjiang's was 2.0×, while the national average was 2.5× — Liaoning and Jilin beat the national average. "I don't see any 'crisis' here." The key metric is per capita GDP. When population flows out, even stagnant total GDP means rapidly growing per capita GDP. For regions lacking advantages in industry and services, population outflow is itself a path to raising per capita income. Rather than blindly building infrastructure and housing to "attract" people, regions should leverage comparative advantages in tourism and large-scale agriculture.
10
Direction
The Positive Interaction of "Moving People" and "Moving Money"
"Moving people" (free labor mobility) is the long-term path to economic development and regional balance, but "moving money" (fiscal transfers) is not unnecessary. Quite the opposite: with a larger "pie," the central government has more capacity for interregional fiscal transfers. Future transfers should focus on equalizing public services — raising salaries for teachers and healthcare workers in less-developed areas, reducing their fiscal burden. Human capital from education and healthcare investment can move with people; fixed productive investments that lack competitiveness become worthless. The author concludes: China's problem is not over-urbanization but obstructed urbanization. City exclusion policies cause family separation and human capital waste. Treating reduced educational access for migrant children as an achievement makes these cities "exceptions to all humanity."
"Protecting farmers by banning market transactions is the most absurd logic — it provides cover for administrative expropriation that disrespects land property rights."

🔑 Core Conclusion
The "Three Fairness Principles" — efficiency of public investment, free population movement, and fiscal transfers — are all essential. Linked reform of land and hukou systems is the key to unlocking China's next round of growth. Distance to port determines land use efficiency, proving that geography matters for development and cannot be reversed by administrative force. China's problem is not over-urbanization but obstructed urbanization — only 20% family migration, 61 million left-behind children as a result of institutional distortion. Population outflow is not frightening: Northeast China's per capita GDP growth outpaced the national average. The path forward: remove institutional barriers, prioritize "moving people" over "moving money," equalize public services across regions, and let every Chinese citizen freely choose where to live and work within this unified great country.
