Wenhua Kulv · Ch.25 "Eulogy for Mother"

A lifetime of smiles and shyness that accomplished the most extraordinary strength—the five eternal puzzles Mother left behind

At the memorial for his ninety-year-old mother, the author offers no tears but five unsolvable puzzles she left behind: How did a wealthy city girl spend her entire youth in the poorest village during wartime? How did the only literate person for miles single-handedly shoulder the burden of civilizing an entire community? How did she complete her son's early education in a barren village? How did she solve the impossible arithmetic of feeding eight people on ¥26 a month? She took all the answers with her. This is a eulogy composed of puzzles, a hymn written in silence.

01
Never Angry
In her entire life, Mother never raised her voice, never spoke a harsh word
The nanny who served her for ten years "never heard a single reproach, a single harsh word." The author, her eldest son, never heard one either—not in his entire life. All elder relatives attest: in their long memories, there is not a trace of Mother losing her temper. She was "always just smiling, just listening, just shy, at most, just silent." Half a year before her death, when the author said her calligraphy was better than his, "she blushed shyly all over her face." But the author warns the young: do not underestimate smiles and shyness—this woman left behind a series of profound puzzles.

02
Five Puzzles
Five eternal puzzles Mother left behind, now forever unsolvable
Puzzle One: A wealthy city girl, to support her family in wartime, left her husband and spent her youth in the poorest countryside—"as if dropped into the Stone Age." Puzzle Two: The only literate person for miles, she single-handedly bore the burden of enlightenment—running literacy classes, writing letters, reading letters, keeping accounts for every family—without any pay or anyone asking her to. Puzzle Three: How did she complete her child's early education in a barren village, then at age seven have him replace her writing letters for villagers every night? Puzzle Four: When her son won first prize in Shanghai-wide composition and math competitions, she was completely unsurprised—had she planned this all along? Puzzle Five: ¥26 per month to feed eight people—an unsolvable equation she solved every single day. The only result: no one in the family starved.

03
Hands & Shoulders
Mother’s hands and feet were the family's eternal life-support; her shoulders, the mountain ridge against storms
One day during the Cultural Revolution, Mother walked to the author's college, found him—long without a full meal—and amid blaring loudspeakers pressed her warm palm against his. Between them: a ¥2 note. Later he discovered she had been washing iron sheets barefoot in freezing water at a small factory to earn that money. Her shoulders were the family ridgepole: with everyone imprisoned, driven to death, or exiled, the ancestral altar table moved by itself—Mother was underneath, carrying it on her back. After the author became China's youngest university president, Mother still carried a gray food bag on her shoulder every few days to cook for him. Only he knows: half the weight of his academic achievements was borne by aging shoulders.

04
The Way of Silence
Mother held not to right-vs-wrong, but to a heavenly principle higher than judgment
The "revolutionary masses" from Father's work unit threatened Mother every few days—"If he doesn't confess his anti-Party crimes, he'll die without a burial place!" Mother lowered her head and listened, never refuting a word—her mind only on the next meal. When her brother-in-law was driven to death in Anhui, Mother held the ashes urn, head bowed in silence. Decades later the truth is clear: those people weren't worth debating. To debate was to enter their logic and surely be trapped. Mother held to another set of principles—heavenly reason. Only silence could make room for heavenly reason, could leave space for history's judgment. Her endpoint was simple: "No matter how others make trouble, just keep living well." In this, Mother surpassed Father, the Confucian who believed in "self-cultivation, family harmony, national governance, world peace" and was ultimately angered to death by slander.

05
Mother-Son Mountain Path
At age six, I crossed two mountains to meet Mother—all my life's courage, at root, came from her
One summer evening when he was six, Mother crossed two mountains to visit relatives, planning to return that night. Wanting to surprise her, the boy crossed the mountains alone—despite wild beasts—with no fear at all. At the second summit he met a beggar family in a dilapidated pavilion who urged him to turn back; he didn't listen. When Mother saw him on the mountain path, "she was neither surprised nor reproachful, nor did she interrogate me—she simply said joyfully,「Qiuyu has come」, took my hand, and walked home warmly with me." The scene fittingly echoes Brecht's play title: Mother Courage and Her Children. Years later, when the author risked his life traversing tens of thousands of kilometers through terrorism-riddled ancient civilization sites, hailed as "the world's bravest humanities professor"—every step was still held by Mother's warm hand from that year.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Mother spent her life smiling and shy, yet accomplished the most extraordinary strength. She used silence to make room for heavenly reason, used her shoulders to bear the storms of an entire family. "No matter how others make trouble, just keep living well" — this surpasses all judgments of right and wrong. Many readers wonder why the author never explains or refutes the waves of rumors besieging him — "Once you've met my mother, you understand." That six-year-old's mountain path, Mother's warm palm, is the root of a lifetime of courage.
