Wenhua Kulv · Ch. 17 "Fish Tail Lodge"

The Himalayas, with their extreme geographic height, gave me a height of thought — of all ancient civilizations, only Chinese civilization has survived uninterrupted and unextinguished

In the final year of the 20th century, at the Fish Tail Lodge in Pokhara, Nepal, at the southern foot of the Himalayas, Yu Qiuyu synthesized his impressions from an expedition spanning tens of thousands of kilometers across the ruins of humanity's great ancient civilizations. He had visited the sites of Greek, Egyptian, Babylonian, Persian, Indian, Hebrew, and Arab civilizations — every single cradle of ancient civilization had, without exception, declined. By the fireside, he distilled five reasons why only Chinese civilization survived: geographic barriers, civilizational scale, a unified writing system, practical rationality, and the imperial examination system. Finally, he proposed a profound paradox — after millennia of struggle, what humanity loves most is not its own creations, but unspoiled nature. The urgent task of Chinese civilization in the new century is to shed its burdens and face nature with sincerity.

01
Roof of the World
Fireside at the Foot of the Himalayas: Writing Civilization's Comparisons Through the Night
Two hundred kilometers northwest of Kathmandu lies Pokhara. Crossing a clear snow-melt river, Yu Qiuyu checked into the Fish Tail Lodge. That night, he lit the stove and candles, turning off the electric lights — "The room returned at once to antiquity, quietly surrendering to the flickering fire and candlelight, which somehow felt warmer and safer. But then I slapped my forehead and stood up, thinking: this ancient little room is at the foot of the Himalayas, and I am hiding inside it alone, meditating and writing! Even Qu Yuan, Li Bai, and Su Dongpo would be dumbfounded by this scene — what luxury I have." At dawn, the rising sun dyed the Fish Tail Peak crimson — "The peak had been shrouded in cloud for five days; today it finally showed its face." He understood: this lodge was where he was meant to organize his journey's impressions. The Himalayas "with their extreme geographic height gave me a height of thought."
02
Civilization Tour
Every Cradle of Ancient Civilization Has Declined — Without Exception
Yu Qiuyu first arrived at the Greek sites — the Parthenon, the legacy of Greek tragedy, Aristotle, Olympia — which nearly perfected humanity's healthiest mode of life. A thousand years earlier still, the Minoan civilization on Crete had drainage and bathroom systems so advanced one could "step right into them" — yet they had been destroyed for millennia. "If we can step into them in one stride, then destruction can step into us in one stride." Egypt's pyramids "confront the eyes of later generations with the most accessible, simplest forms" — "everything simple is profound." But Egyptian civilization, living without seeking understanding under totalitarianism, met a majestic interruption — overly centralized dynasties accumulated hereditary bureaucracies; regional powers had the capital to resist the Pharaoh; fragmentation and foreign invasion found their openings.

03
Middle East Trap
Extremism: Where Civilization Is Most Concentrated, Hatred Becomes Most Concentrated
The Middle East cradles several of humanity's most brilliant ancient civilizations — Babylon, Persia, India, Hebrew, Arab... Yet this land of antiquity and glory has been utterly exhausted by extremist conflicts. "Every party to the conflict has painful and resounding justifications — the appeal of extremism lies precisely in this pain and resonance, making it impossible for any side to retreat. When this collective extreme emotion is mixed with each side's religion, history, and culture, the conflict instantly becomes unshakeable faith." People living long-term in such conditions cannot maintain life or social order; "they place very low value on their own and others' lives. Terrorist acts, suicide bombs, kidnapping threats — all can be carried out without a second thought, even with exultation." Tracing the roots, extremism is tied to ancient inter-civilizational warfare — burning Jerusalem and digging three feet deep to erase memory, opening dams to wash away Baghdad's cultural traces, sowing salt and thorn seeds on conquered land to make it barren for a millennium.

04
China's Code
Why Is Chinese Civilization the Only Uninterrupted Ancient Civilization?
By the fireside through the night, Yu Qiuyu distilled five reasons: First, geographic barriers — the Himalayas, Kunlun, Tian Shan, and Altai mountains, plus deserts and ocean, formed a natural closed structure that shielded Chinese civilization from devastating wars with other great civilizations. Second, civilizational scale — the Yellow River and Yangtze River basins combined exceed the total geographic scale of all other ancient civilizations many times over, preventing depletion even in relative isolation. Third, a unified and accessible writing system — from "Do not do to others what you would not want done to yourself" to today, meaning transmits without obstacle across two millennia. Fourth, practical rationality and the Doctrine of the Mean — avoiding religious extremism; Confucian rationality and Legalist statecraft provided orderly governance for over two thousand years. Fifth, the imperial examination system — over a millennium of uninterrupted talent selection nourished cultural transmission through countless scholars' lives.

05
Return to Nature
Excess Civilization Is Catastrophe — The Chinese Philosophy of Subtraction
In Nepal, a place without its own independent civilization, Yu Qiuyu felt the most joy — "Civilized people from all over the world love coming to Nepal, not to seek historical sites, but to immerse themselves in nature." Here a profound paradox emerges: humanity moved toward civilization to escape rough nature, but gradually discovered that "crowded cities may be more barbaric, dense populations more savage." Civilization's denaturalization takes many forms — overpopulation, overconsumption, overcompetition, excessive displays of knowledge, verbal garbage, futile constructions... The summative rebellion against all these disasters is: return to nature. "The urgent task of Chinese civilization in the new century is to shed its many burdens and face nature with sincerity and ease — even if those burdens contain historical honor and civilizational luster." He imagines consulting the Chinese philosophers of two millennia past — Confucius would advocate measured pleasure in harmony with heaven; Mozi would reject all wasteful consumption; Xunzi would insist on stern punishment for selfishness. Smiling wordlessly, Laozi and Zhuangzi finally speak: "Place civilization and nature before us — we choose only nature." "At its highest level, Chinese culture is a culture of subtraction, a culture that yearns for simplicity and nature."
🌿
Nepal's Lesson: After millennia of struggle, what humanity loves most is not its own creations. Fleeing civilizational centers whenever possible — "poetic dwelling" is the ultimate ideal.
🏔️
Crossing Home: The white stone gate on the gorge bridge bears the name of a nation. "Our generation was born too late; we could not speak for you when you needed it most... But there is still time — the new century has just arrived, and today, I have finally made it in time."
🔑 Key Takeaway
Chinese civilization survived as the only uninterrupted ancient civilization thanks to five pillars: geographic barriers, civilizational scale, a unified writing system, practical rationality, and the imperial examination system. Yet this very advantage has brought the heaviest baggage — the urgent task for Chinese civilization in the new century is to shed its burdens and face nature with sincerity. At its highest level, Chinese culture is a culture of subtraction, yearning for simplicity and nature — it is precisely this essence that saved it from so much waste and preserved its life.
