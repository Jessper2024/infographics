The Elements of Style · Chapter 001「Elementary Rules of Usage」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Punctuation is not decoration — it is the skeleton of meaning. A comma shifts causality, a semicolon defines boundaries, an apostrophe marks possession. Yet errors abound: misplaced commas, dangling participles, spliced independent clauses — each one enough to make a careful reader wince. What are the 7 elementary rules of English usage? The answer lies not in intuition, but in precise principles distilled from a century of teaching practice.

This chapter is the foundational core of The Elements of Style, presenting 7 elementary rules of usage formulated by Cornell professor William Strunk Jr. in 1918. These cover possessive nouns, comma usage (in series, for parenthetic expressions, before conjunctions, between independent clauses), sentence integrity, and logical agreement of participial phrases with their subjects. Still the cornerstone of English writing instruction worldwide — master them, and you will have mastered the minimum necessary discipline of clear writing.

01
Rules 1-2
Possessives & Serial Commas
The most basic punctuation conventions in English. Form the possessive singular of nouns by adding 's. In a series of three or more terms with a single conjunction, use a comma after each term except the last — a rule followed by the U.S. Government Printing Office and Oxford University Press alike.
⚠️

Common Pitfalls

Ancient proper names ending in -es or -is take only an apostrophe: Jesus'. But prefer "the heel of Achilles" over "Achilles' heel". Pronominal possessives (its, hers, theirs) never take an apostrophe.

✅

Correct Usage

Charles's friend · Burns's poems · red, white, and blue · gold, silver, or copper · He opened the letter, read it, and made a note of its contents.

02
Rule 3
Enclose Parenthetic Expressions in Commas
Parenthetic expressions are "asides" within a sentence. If the interruption is slight, commas may be safely omitted — but never omit one comma and leave the other. Dates, abbreviations, non-restrictive relative clauses, and introductory phrases all qualify as parenthetic.
①
Date format: February to July, 1916 · Monday, November 11, 1918 — wrap the year and the day-month after a weekday in commas.
②
Non-restrictive clauses: The audience, which had at first been indifferent, became more interested. — additional, not defining information.
③
Introductory elements: Partly by hard fighting, partly by diplomatic skill, they enlarged their dominions... — set off phrases and clauses preceding the main clause.
03
Rules 4-5
Comma Before Conjunction · Semicolon for Independent Clauses
Rule 4: Place a comma before a conjunction (and, but, for, or, nor, while) introducing an independent clause. Rule 5: Do not join independent clauses with a comma — use a semicolon or period instead.
Loose sentence (comma + and)
→ Use for →
The early records have disappeared, and the story can no longer be reconstructed. Occasional loose sentences keep style natural.
Semicolon (no conjunction)
→ Better →
Stevenson's romances are entertaining; they are full of exciting adventures. More concise and powerful.
Adverbial exception
→ Note →
When the second clause is introduced by an adverb (accordingly, besides, therefore, thus), still use a semicolon, not a comma.
04
Rules 6-7
Sentence Integrity & Agreement of Participles
Rule 6: Do not break sentences in two. Use a comma when a period would sever the natural connection — unless an emphatic word or phrase is intentionally isolated. Rule 7: A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the grammatical subject.
❌
Rule 6 error: I met them on a Cunard liner. Coming home from Liverpool. → replace the period with a comma.
✅
Rule 6 correct: Again and again he called out. No reply. → intentional emphasis, justified isolation.
❌
Rule 7 error: Being in a dilapidated condition, I was able to buy the house very cheap. → subject "I" is dilapidated? Absurd!
✅
Rule 7 correct: Walking slowly down the road, he saw a woman accompanied by two children. → "he" is walking.
05
Mastery
From Rules to Instinct
Strunk insists: Rules 3, 4, 5, and 6 cover the most important principles of punctuation. They should be so mastered that their application becomes second nature. Punctuation is not decoration — it is the structural expression of meaning. Commas layer thought; semicolons define boundaries; periods complete ideas.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Strunk's 7 elementary rules construct the skeleton of English punctuation and grammar: from possessive nouns to serial commas, from enclosing parenthetic expressions to commas before conjunctions, from semicolons between independent clauses to participial agreement — each is a precise tool for eliminating ambiguity and establishing clarity. Punctuation is not decoration; it is the structure of meaning. Master Rules 3 through 6, and you will have mastered the minimum necessary discipline of clear English writing.
