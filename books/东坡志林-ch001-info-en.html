Dongpo's Miscellany · Chapter 1「The Wanderer Among Mountains and Waters」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Su Shi spent his life buffeted by political storms, exiled from Huangzhou to Huizhou to Danzhou (Hainan), his footprints covering half of China. Yet career setbacks never crushed his spirit — quite the opposite: he found in nature a way to anchor his soul, transforming every exile into a poetic pilgrimage. How did Dongpo maintain such equanimity in adversity? The answer lies in his crystalline travel sketches.

This chapter collects over ten of Su Shi's travel sketches — from surviving a storm at sea ("Crossing to Hepu"), to a moonlit stroll with a friend ("A Night Visit to Chengtian Temple"), to his epiphany at Pine Wind Pavilion and his nights in Danzhou. Each piece is a spiritual soliloquy written at life's lowest points. He did not flee suffering; he performed a series of self-rescues amid landscapes, dissolving individual misfortune into the greater beauty of heaven and earth — calling himself an "idler," entertaining himself with poetry and wine, and turning places of exile into spiritual homelands.

01
Crossing the Sea
"Heaven will not let these works perish — we shall cross!" — Faith at Sea
On the last night of the sixth month, 1100 CE, Su Shi crossed the sea toward Hepu. Continuous rain had destroyed all bridges. Without moonlight, his boat anchored in the vast ocean. Water merged with sky, stars filled the galaxy. He clasped his manuscripts of the Book of Documents, I Ching, and Analects — the only surviving copies — and sighed: "Heaven would not let these perish. We shall make it across!" The next day, they landed safely.
"Water and sky merged into one, the Milky Way filled the heavens. I sat up and looked around, sighing: 'Why do I encounter such peril so often? I had already crossed Xuwen — am I trapped here again?' My young son Guo snored beside me, unresponsive. The manuscripts of the Documents, Changes, and Analects — for which no other copies existed — were all with me. I touched them and sighed: 'Heaven does not wish these to perish. We shall certainly cross!'"
— "Crossing to Hepu," fourth day of the seventh month, 1100
02
Moonlit Visit
"We just lack idlers like the two of us" — Moonlight Immortalized in 85 Characters
On the night of October 12, 1083, exiled in Huangzhou, Su Shi was about to sleep when moonlight entered his room. He rose joyfully and walked to Chengtian Temple to find Zhang Huaimin. The two strolled in the courtyard. "The courtyard was like a pool of limpid water, with algae and waterweeds intertwining — but they were merely the shadows of bamboo and cypress." The final line is the masterpiece: "What night has no moon? Where are there no bamboo and cypress? We just lack idlers like the two of us."
🌙

Courtyard as Limpid Water

Moonlight flooded the courtyard like transparent water. The "algae" were bamboo shadows. An optical illusion revealing the beauty of heaven and earth.

🍃

Idlers Like the Two of Us

"Idler" is a declaration of identity: exiled and dismissed, yet also free under the moon — a self-deprecation infused with transcendence.

03
Shahu Encounter
"Who says one cannot be young again? The stream before my door still flows west"
While buying farmland at Shahu near Huangzhou, Su Shi fell ill and sought treatment from the deaf physician Pang Anchang. Though deaf, Anchang was brilliant — "I would write a few characters on paper, and he would grasp my meaning fully." Su Shi joked: "I use my hand as mouth, you use your eyes as ears — both of us are extraordinary!" After recovering, they visited Clear Spring Temple, where Su Shi saw the Orchid Stream flowing westward — a rarity since streams normally flow east. This triggered his famous lines: "Who says one cannot be young again? Look — the stream before my door still flows west. Do not sing 'Yellow Rooster' with your white hair!"

04
Pine Wind Epiphany
"Where can one not rest?" — The Ultimate Art of Acceptance
Exiled in Huizhou, Su Shi climbed toward Pine Wind Pavilion. His legs grew weary while the pavilion still loomed in the distance. Suddenly he had an epiphany: "Where can one not rest?" — why must one reach the pavilion to stop? Right here, right now — where cannot one rest? "Like a fish unhooked, suddenly liberated." This realization transcended hiking: it was a complete reversal of life's orientation. Stop fixating on destinations; wherever you are is home.

05
Night in Danzhou
"Advance and retreat have their fate, delay and speed have their time" — Final Settlement at the Edge of the World
Exiled to Danzhou on Hainan Island — literally the end of the known world — Su Shi was already in his sixties. One night he toured temples, drank in alleyways, and walked home tipsy, seeing "Han Chinese and ethnic Li mingling together, butchers and wine-sellers bustling." He laughed. At home past midnight, his son snored. He put down his walking stick and laughed again: "I laugh at Han Yu, who couldn't fish anything out of the water — but I'm fishing for myself." Su Shi spent three years in Danzhou, setting up a school. From that point on, Hainan's literary tradition flourished.
🏝️

Mingling at World's End

"Han and Li mingling, butchers bustling" — Su Shi saw vitality, not vulgarity. He drank in small lanes, blending with locals, finding joy where others found only hardship.

📚

Civilizing the Southern Frontier

In three Danzhou years, Su Shi taught students. Hainan's first provincial graduate came from his tutelage. True civilization is not condescending instruction but gentle transformation through immersion.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Dongpo's travel sketches are not geographical notes but spiritual autobiography. Every landscape was a site of self-rescue: surviving a storm at sea with his manuscripts as faith; calling himself an "idler" under the moon; seeing in a westward-flowing stream the possibility of reversing life's decline; realizing at Pine Wind Pavilion the truth of acceptance. Su Shi's greatness lies not in never falling, but in rising again — and rising higher — from within mountains and waters after every fall. "Where can one not rest?" — this one sentence encapsulates the entire Dongpo philosophy: anywhere can be home; one need not wait for the far shore.
