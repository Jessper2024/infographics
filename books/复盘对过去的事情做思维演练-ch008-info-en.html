Fupan: Mental Rehearsal of Past Events
Ch8 "How to Judge Whether Review Conclusions Are Thorough"

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer Are your review conclusions truly reliable? This chapter proposes four evaluation criteria — rejecting accidental attribution, focusing on systems not people, ≥3 consecutive Why questions, and cross-validation through evidence chains — to build a quality defense line right in the review session.

Whether review conclusions are reliable must be judged during the review itself. While practice is the ultimate test of truth, by the time we enter the practice phase, the review work has already ended. This chapter's four intrinsic evaluation criteria build a defense line for validating conclusion quality in real time: from rejecting accidental attribution, to distinguishing person-oriented vs. system-oriented conclusions, to at least three rounds of consecutive questioning, and finally cross-validation — all ultimately returning to practice for continuous refinement and genuine insight.

01
Red Line Rule
Accidental Factors: Zero-Credibility Attribution
Grounding review conclusions in accidental factors is always wrong. The goal of fupan is to discover repeatable patterns, not to blame failure on non-reproducible happenstance. Napoleon's "hemorrhoid theory" and the folk ballad's "nail theory" — both may be facts, but neither can bear the burden of an empire's collapse. Reviews must ascend to the level of process and institutional design: let the nail point to horseshoe quality control, let the hemorrhoid prompt a chain-of-command contingency plan. Otherwise, you've merely selected a scapegoat.

"For want of a nail, the shoe was lost; for want of a shoe, the horse was lost… for want of a battle, the kingdom was lost." — If the review stops at the nail, you never discover the process defect.
02
Attribution Check
Person-Oriented vs. System-Oriented: The Fork in the Review Road
Person-oriented conclusions rarely guide future practice; system-oriented ones uncover real patterns. Blaming lateness on "the slow driver" or "the noisy neighbor" is external attribution — simplifying things, even drifting toward conspiracy thinking. In team reviews, person-oriented conclusions undermine the review itself, diverting participants from discovering patterns to defending justifications.
👤

Person-Oriented · External Attribution

"Slow driver" "Noisy neighbor" — uncontrollable, unpredictable. ✕ Leads to excuses and conspiracy, eroding team review trust.

⚙️

System-Oriented · Internal Attribution

"Leave 30 min earlier" "Set multiple alarms" — controllable, replicable. ✓ Discovers essential patterns to guide future practice.

⚠ Team Review Warning
When conclusions point only to individuals, reviews are easily perceived as "office politics" or "excuse-seeking" rather than problem-solving — diverting attention from discovering patterns onto a detour.
03
Interrogation Rule
≥3 Consecutive Why / Why Not Questions: Piercing the Information Fog
Fewer than three rounds of questioning likely means the real cause remains untouched. Human cognition has limits; reaching a conclusion in one shot — unless you're a genius — is best left to God. Seek the Question Behind the Question (QBQ), find the answer behind the answer — only consecutive questioning can strip away surface phenomena to grip the essence.

04
Confirmation Method
Cross-Validation: Reinforcing Conclusions with Multi-Source Evidence Chains
A single piece of evidence cannot decide a case; only an evidence chain qualifies. Cross-validating review conclusions with other events effectively prevents conclusions that only apply in special cases. A wife suspecting infidelity won't conclude from one ambiguous text alone — she checks call logs, recalls late returns, notes attitude shifts… The more dots connected into an evidence chain, the more credible the conclusion.

Four Evaluation Criteria + Practice Refinement = True Knowledge
Reaching a conclusion is not the goal of fupan — fupan exists to guide future practice. The four criteria above make conclusions more reliable, but do not guarantee reliability. Ultimately, we must apply them in practice and continuously refine based on results to obtain genuine insight — for practice is the sole criterion of truth.

🔑 Key Takeaway (BLUF)
The reliability of review conclusions must be judged during the review itself — all four defense lines are essential: First, reject accidental attribution; conclusions must ascend to process and institutional levels. Second, orient toward systems, not people, avoiding the external attribution trap. Third, at least three rounds of consecutive questioning to pierce surface appearances and reach root causes. Fourth, multi-source cross-validation forming evidence chains to reduce systemic risk. But these four criteria only make conclusions "more reliable," not "guaranteed reliable" — ultimately, they must be applied and refined in practice to yield genuine understanding of patterns.
