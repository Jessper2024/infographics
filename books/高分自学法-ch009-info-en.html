Repeat them like spells to steady your wavering heart.

01
Root of Frustration
The Two Sources of Frustration: Ambition — Engine of Success or Source of Collapse?
Frustration is the feeling that crushes willpower and strips away the belief that "I can do this too." It stems from two kinds of ambition: the ambition to achieve a goal, and the speculative ambition to achieve it "easily" — faster than others, without mistakes, more efficiently. Wanting equal results with less effort is the true origin of frustration. As Buddha taught: face the pain and it disappears. Once you recognize that frustration comes from ambition, and firmly resolve that "cause always leads to effect — if you're behind, put in more effort," the frustration washes away instantly.

Mantra One
"Others a Hundred, I a Thousand": From Hironaka Heisuke to the Victory Formula of Ordinary Effort
Japanese mathematician Hironaka Heisuke, at a Harvard filled with geniuses, experienced his own inadequacy firsthand, gave up the ambition of being "faster than others," and made "spending twice as much time" his creed — eventually winning the Fields Medal. A college friend, immune to frustration, had a note on his bookshelf reading "ren-bai-ji-qian" (others a hundred, I a thousand). The author wrote 200+ essays and recorded 300+ podcast episodes over many years, precisely because he held the mindset: "Put in a bit more effort, accept a bit less reward. Spend a bit more time learning. It's okay to reach achievements a bit more slowly."

Brain Science
The Science of Slumps: Why Starting to Study Is Always the Most Painful Part
When you start studying, your brain must gradually raise its focus level before it can release the pleasure chemical dopamine. But the process of elevating focus is like climbing a steep mountain path — intensely painful. This is the core reason people procrastinate, avoid studying, and cram before exams. Once you understand this principle, you only need to endure briefly, continuing to input study-related stimuli into your brain until the reluctance fades and focus kicks in.

Mantra Two
"Take It Easy to Go Far": Flatten the Slope of the Focus Curve
When focus is hard to raise, the answer is not to try harder, but to deliberately "take it easy." The pain of climbing the focus curve is proportional to its steepness — by slowing the ascent, pain is reduced, and you can sustain learning until dopamine kicks in. Practical techniques include: reading aloud (dual visual + auditory stimulation), listening to lecture audio, and engaging in conversations about the study material itself. The author uses this method when writing too: "Just write one more paragraph. No, just one more sentence," lowering the bar bit by bit.

Mantra Three
"Right Now, Right Here": Inamori Kazuo's Secret — From Desperate Employee to God of Management
When there is too much to do and strength runs short, people fabricate painful stories about the past and future in their minds, sinking into despair. But the only time we can actually experience is "right now, right here." Inamori Kazuo — failed high school entrance exam, entered a mediocre university, struggled to find employment, joined a near-bankrupt company — one day decided to abandon past and future, focusing only on what was before him. He ate and slept in the lab, taught himself by translating American papers, spent his own money on research... His life reversed: founded Kyocera (a Global 100 company), founded KDDI, rescued bankrupt JAL, and became one of Japan's most revered "Gods of Management."

Distinguish two kinds of pain: real pain that actually exists (difficulty solving problems, physical fatigue) vs. fabricated pain from stories your brain creates (about past and future). Treat the stories in your head as reality and pain inflates infinitely.

🔑 Key Takeaway
A mentally fragile learner needs three keys: when facing frustration, let go of speculative ambition and invest effort with the "Others a hundred, I a thousand" resolve; when facing a study slump, understand the neuroscience of dopamine and use the "take it easy to go far" strategy to reduce the pain of climbing the focus curve; when facing the despair of endless fatigue, use the Zen-like power of "right now, right here" to cut through fabricated stories of past and future. Three mantras, three collapse moments, one set of mental armor — remember them and your wavering heart will grow firm.
