The Great Compound Interest · Ch.3 "The Trillion-Yuan Business Hidden in County Towns"

SCQA · Situation → Complication → Question → Answer China has ~300 cities, 2,856 counties, 41,658 towns, and 662,238 villages—this is the real China. Yet mainstream business narratives focus almost entirely on tier-1 and tier-2 cities, overlooking the trillion-yuan opportunity hidden in the vast lower-tier market. How do we truly understand and capture the business logic of this market? Answer: get into the mud, understand the distinct consumption needs, channel structures, and brand perceptions of tiered markets.

Using Huai'an City and its subordinate counties, towns, and villages in northern Jiangsu as a sample, this chapter dissects the true face of China's lower-tier market. Beginning with the daily lives of a foot-massage worker, taxi driver, and hotpot restaurant waitress, the author reveals a world completely ignored by mainstream narratives: the collective fate of 10 million+ foot-massage workers, the ecosystem of a county town you can circle in 20 minutes by car, the massive rural population outflow and agricultural transformation, the category wars visible from highway billboards (baijiu/electric vehicles/home goods), and vivo/OPPO's channel capability reaching capillaries like China Post. The core philosophy: to achieve dominance, you must first befriend the mud. All castles in the air are illusions.

The Real Map
Beyond 300 Cities: The 660,000 Villages Are the Real China
China has approximately 300 cities, 2,856 counties, 41,658 towns, and 662,238 villages—this is the complete Chinese business landscape. Those born into middle-class families in Beijing, Shanghai, Guangzhou, and Shenzhen may be forever confined to a tiny fraction of the country's land area and mistake it for the whole world. Only by seeing through the market beyond tier-one and tier-two cities can one truly contend for the realm. The author uses Huai'an in Jiangsu—sitting on China's north-south dividing line—as a median benchmark with national reference value. Northern Huai'an started late with a weak foundation; compared with Suzhou, Wuxi, and Nanjing, each boasting hundreds of listed companies, it represents the more widespread Chinese reality.

02
Portraits
County Town Faces: The Consumer Majority Mainstream Narratives Ignore
The foot-massage worker in Jinhu County, born in 1984, earns ¥5,000 monthly, sends ¥3,000 home, loves "rags-to-riches" novels—he doesn't read the news, feeling most of society's affairs have nothing to do with him. This is the epitome of tens of millions of county and township residents: they have never believed they could become protagonists of this world; they are spectators and content to remain so. Hotpot waitresses use ¥5 lipstick from Pinduoduo (selling 100K+ units monthly) and buy phones bundled with free data. County shops do acquaintance-based business; an old man ran a sundry shop for 25 years before expanding by 10 square meters to add two mahjong tables and a lottery terminal. Understanding this group's consumption decision path is the key to deciphering the lower-tier market.

03
Rural Transformation
The Countryside Empties: Population Exodus and the Vanishing Professional Farmer
Rural population loss is already extremely severe—virtually no young people are seen anywhere. Village women sun-dry soybeans by the roadside at an extremely slow pace—to them, time is the cheapest commodity. Many villages, aside from paved roads, have hardly changed in decades. Meanwhile, places like Tonglu in Zhejiang, known as the "express delivery hometown," have undergone earth-shaking transformation. Farmland is contracted out en masse to large operators; Jiangsu's plains are highly suitable for mechanized planting and harvesting, drastically reducing labor demand. Professional farmers have dropped to historically lowest proportions and continue declining. A baseline judgment: China's urbanization has not yet saturated and will continue to grow—but post-urbanization supporting issues are deeper challenges.

04
Ground War
Highway Billboard Witness: Trillion-Yuan Battles of Baijiu, EVs, and Home Goods
Driving on the Beijing-Shanghai expressway, nearly 60% of highway billboards are taken by Yanghe and Gujing Gongjiu—this is saturation bombardment in the ground market. Baijiu consumption supports a multi-trillion-yuan market: Moutai's 2017 revenue was ¥76.4B; Yanghe's H1 2018 revenue was ¥14.5B. Electric vehicles form another major category: Yadea broke through 4M annual sales in 2017; Huai'an, a city of under 2M people, has an estimated 300-400K EVs. Home furnishing, born from 40 years of urbanization, created enormous fortunes but has also reached a turning point. Internet advertising still holds a tiny share—the ground market remains immensely promising. Those who can't understand why Yadea can sponsor the World Cup reveal their ignorance of real China.

05
Channel Code
vivo/OPPO and China Post: The Organizational Code of Ground-Level Channels
The success of vivo and OPPO is extremely difficult to replicate—their penetration capability rivals that of China Post. In a small town in northern Jiangsu, on a "commercial street" barely 100 meters long (one vertical, one horizontal), vivo and OPPO stores occupy the core positions at every must-pass point. In India, they even branded an entire metro station. This ground-level channel organizational capability cannot be replicated in the short term. The deeper lesson: in tier-4 and tier-5 markets, chain brands have barely penetrated—KFC and McDonald's reach only up to county towns with 300K population. Vast markets remain in a primordial state. Hailan Home and Seven Wolves have become the "must-buy-a-suit-for-New-Year" standard for county elites; Sunkyo rose from the lower-tier market to become China's fourth-largest hotel group. This is the code to the trillion-yuan market.

🔑 Key Takeaway
China's true business landscape lies not in its ~300 cities but in its 2,856 counties, 41,658 towns, and 662,238 villages. This trillion-yuan market has long been ignored by mainstream business narratives yet harbors enormous opportunities in electric vehicles (tens of millions of annual units), baijiu (multi-trillion-yuan consumption), and chain retail (still in primordial brand state). The core to understanding lower-tier markets is not "downgrading" but comprehending a completely different consumption logic: acquaintance-based commerce, random consumption, and channel supremacy. To achieve dominance, you must first befriend the mud—all castles in the air are illusions that will vanish at any moment.
