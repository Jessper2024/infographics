Siege of Bianjing · Interlude "Do Commoners Really Care About Emperors?"

Loyalty to the Throne or Schadenfreude? Commoners Doubled the Emperors' Humiliation in Fiction The two emperors were marched north, and the literati wept — but ordinary people turned their ordeal into comedy skits. In folk novels like Records of the Southern Ashes and Records of Righteous Indignation, commoners gleefully imagined the emperors' humiliations: tied to pillars, soldiers urinating and defecating on them, and after death, their corpses burned into lamp oil — malice so extreme it chills the blood. Between imperial authority and popular will, there never truly was any shared national sentiment.

While the Zhao imperial clan grieved over the two emperors' fate, and patriotic generals vowed to avenge the shame, what were ordinary commoners doing? They were writing fictional tales that multiplied the emperors' humiliations several times over. In novels circulating during the mid-Southern Song — Records of the Southern Ashes, Records of Righteous Indignation, and its sequel (falsely attributed to Xin Qiji) — the two emperors are tormented day and night without end: denied food, tied to pillars overnight, soldiers urinating and defecating on them, and after death, their bodies burned into lamp oil. These malicious tales bear little resemblance to historical fact (the Jurchen did not deliberately mistreat the Zhao family), yet they circulated openly among the people, and even Southern Song emperors could do nothing about them. Events of the Xuanhe Era took this further, turning the fate of two fallen emperors into a public carnival. This reveals a brutal truth: throughout Chinese imperial history, the pendulum has always swung between "loyalty to the throne" and "boundless contempt for power," never settling into any genuine "national sentiment."

01
Folk Narratives
While Literati Wept, Commoners Wrote Skits
In the mid-Southern Song, several novels falsely attributed to Xin Qiji circulated among the people. Records of the Southern Ashes, Records of Righteous Indignation, and its sequel focused on the two emperors' northern captivity — ostensibly meant to stir patriotic fervor, but in reality they were a form of revenge against the Northern Song imperial house, making the two emperors taste the humiliation they had imposed on others. The depictions are vicious: the emperors and empresses eat only one meal and drink only once a day; Empress Dowager Zheng is tormented with stomach pain to the brink of death; Zeli (Prince Gaitian) constantly molests the two empresses; soldiers urinate and defecate on the emperors and empresses tied to pillars; at night they hear sounds of violation, too terrified to open their eyes. Nearly all of this is pure fabrication — the Jurchen did not deliberately mistreat the Zhao family — but commoners delighted in reading these amplified versions.

02
Fictional Details
From Humiliation to Lamp Oil: The People's Most Malicious Imagination of Emperors
The depiction of the Retired Emperor's death in Records of Righteous Indignation reaches the extreme of malice. The Retired Emperor falls ill and dies in a earthen pit in Junzhou. As the young emperor weeps, the escort officer Ajiti tells him: Junzhou has no burial ground; the dead are all burned, and when half-burned, the corpse is skewered with a wooden pole and dragged to a large stone pit in the northern part of the city — the pit contains a liquid thicker than southern oil, suitable for lighting lamps, which is produced from corpses. Burn the Retired Emperor's body into lamp oil — this is the ultimate insult to the dead. The young emperor's death is even more theatrical: the Jurchen Hailing Prince orders the Liao emperor Tianzuo and the young emperor to each lead a mock cavalry exercise, then suddenly shoots Tianzuo through the heart with an arrow, then shoots the young emperor — thousands of horses trample the bodies into the mud, nowhere to be found. Two fallen emperors exit the stage together in the most humiliating fashion.

03
Cultural Significance
Events of the Xuanhe Era: The Curious Fusion of the Song Jiang Story and the Fall of a Dynasty
Modern readers know Events of the Xuanhe Era because it is one of the most important sources for Water Margin. The book first records in full the Song Jiang rebellion and the names of the 36 Heavenly Spirits, laying the foundation for the later masterpiece. But Song Jiang is only a small part — the book also covers Wang Anshi, Emperor Huizong's corrupt ministers, the emperor's faith in Daoist priests, the Lantern Festival in Bianjing, and even the legendary courtesan Li Shishi. Toward the end, the author pivots sharply to the Jurchen invasion and the two emperors' northern captivity, multiplying their humiliations severalfold and tormenting them without mercy — utterly indifferent to the fact that they had once been the leaders of the Great Song. Such a book could only have appeared in the Song dynasty — in any other feudal dynasty, mocking the sitting dynasty's emperors would have been a capital offense.

04
Truth of Power
Two Ends of the Pendulum: Between Loyalty to the Throne and Vicious Curses
Chinese imperial history has always oscillated within a giant pendulum. At one extreme stands the doctrine of "loyalty to the throne and love of country" — patriotism twisted into unconditional submission to power, the common people living numbly in petty pursuits, the emperor's authority absolute and beyond question. But the moment imperial power collapses, the pendulum swings violently to the other extreme — commoners become the most venomous cursers, eager to subject the former imperial house to endless insult and abuse, even tearing them apart with their bare hands. Between these two poles, a middle ground — genuine "national sentiment" — has always been absent. Emperors knew full well that the unlimited obedience they enjoyed came not from affection but from force; they therefore maintained rigid stability through violence, postponing the day of brittle fracture for as long as possible.

Historical Echo
The Tolerance of the Song and Its Curses: Why the Most Tolerant Dynasty Drew the Most Poisonous Imagination
The Song dynasty was the most tolerant in China's imperial history — yet its emperors received the most venomous curses. Freedom of speech in the Song far exceeded that of any other dynasty — in the Southern Song, novels mocking the sitting dynasty's emperors circulated openly, and the emperors, knowing the fabrications about the imperial house, could not ban them. This itself testifies to Song tolerance. But tolerance did not buy affection — commoners did not recount the two emperors' ordeal in sympathetic tones; instead they wrote the most vicious skits, stripping the Song imperial dignity to the bone. Even today, one can still feel the palpable resentment and curses radiating from those texts. This reveals an unsettling truth: in two thousand years of imperial history, the chasm between ruler and ruled was never truly bridged.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Do commoners really care about emperors? The answer is no. In two thousand years of imperial history, ruler and ruled never formed any genuine "national sentiment." When imperial power was strong, commoners were taught unconditional submission; when it collapsed, they instantly became the most venomous cursers. The two emperors' northern ordeal was turned into comedy skits in popular fiction, their humiliations amplified boundlessly — in Records of the Southern Ashes, emperors were tied to pillars as soldiers urinated and defecated on them; in Records of Righteous Indignation, the Retired Emperor's corpse was burned into lamp oil. These vicious imaginings differ dramatically from the historical record but reflect the common people's true attitude toward imperial authority. The Song was China's most tolerant dynasty — but tolerance did not buy affection. History proves that the unlimited obedience emperors enjoyed never came from love, only from force.
