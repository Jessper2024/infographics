Clear Thinker in a Complex World · Ch.23 "Two Centuries of Technological Catch-Up"

Profit and War Drive Technology · Latecomer Advantage Accelerates Catch-Up The West surpassed the East around 1750. By the Opium War China lagged completely. What widened the gap over two centuries? New World gold → arms races driving weapon advances → the scientific spirit and university R&D → the Industrial Revolution rewrote the game. Meanwhile, the Qing Dynasty enjoyed peace on all borders, had no war demand, lost motivation to improve weapons, and "involuted" on agriculture. Today China leads in 23 of 30 frontier technologies, but R&D spending is only half of America's—yet R&D cost gaps exceed 4×. Latecomer sweat equity is kicking in.

This chapter uses Tencent's WE Summit as entry point for a panoramic review of two centuries of technological catch-up. The core causal chain: the West rose via New World gold + arms race demand + scientific spirit → Industrial Revolution; China fell behind via no war demand → no weapon innovation → technology lock-in → involution; the true engine of human technological progress = profit desire + war pressure → bottom-up corporate R&D + state-funded basic research. China's latecomer advantage: Western direction-signposts + full industrial portfolio + R&D cost advantage + government-led gap-filling in basic science. China leads 23 of 30 frontier technologies. The path from isolated to minority to majority to cutting-edge leadership is clear.

01
Historical Roots
The Western Rise: New World Gold → Trade → Arms Race → Industrial Revolution
The West kicked off a positive-feedback loop with New World gold. Western Europeans struck precious metals in the Americas, shopped in the East (Chinese porcelain and tea, Indian cotton, Philippine spices), grew richer with each purchase, and poured gains into expanded production. Meanwhile, hundreds of fragmented European states fought endlessly. To win battles they continuously upgraded weapons and reformed military systems. This bred the scientific spirit—technicians rose in status and income, universities began researching technology. The Qing Dynasty, by contrast, faced no threats on any border and could beat all enemies, so it completely lost any incentive to improve weapons. Firearms stagnated at Ming Dynasty levels. China's pattern of adding labor, not technology—\"involution\"—contrasted fundamentally with Western Europe's tendency to add machinery, not workers.

02
Core Engine
The True Engine of Technological Progress: Desire for Profit and Victory in War
R&D itself is painful—enormous uncertainty, real risk of sunk investment. Nobody does it without sufficient incentive. Technology, trade, and warfare form a positive-feedback loop: technology improves war and production efficiency → war seizes colonies and expands influence → colonies and production increase wealth → wealth feeds back into technology. The Industrial Revolution's three key technologies (spinning jenny, improved steam engine, locomotive) all came from grassroots technicians—Watt and Edison rose from humble origins to become super-rich. America started as a thin strip on the Atlantic coast, pushed westward for a century seizing land, then used two world wars to soak up half of Europe's wealth and scientists. The third \"cake\" was the tech-revolution dividend.

03
Conversion Pattern
Military to Civilian: Ammonia Saved Agriculture—Atomic Energy and Computing Changed Humanity
World-changing technologies often originated in military projects, converting to civilian use decades later. German chemist Haber synthesized ammonia—originally for ammonium nitrate explosives in WWI, afterward applied to agriculture. Grain output surged. Humanity went from \"half the population farming\" to \"one-third farming.\" He was later called \"father of war chemistry\"—his wife took her own life under the pressure. The atomic energy program originally aimed at atomic bombs. Computers originally calculated missile trajectories. Only years later did they convert to civilian use. State R&D and private R&D, military and civilian—they are always intertwined. Tencent's \"Science Exploration Award\" rewards precisely this kind of forward-looking research.

04
Catch-Up Logic
The Tech Diffusion Effect: The Hardest Part Isn't Research—It's Confirming the Direction
When facing a tech problem, the most painful part is not the research but not knowing whether your path is a dead end. Even your PhD advisor cannot tell if your topic is impossible. Spending three to four years only to discover it leads nowhere is all too common. The West's lead provides signposts—\"simply knowing which direction they broke through from already solves the hardest problem.\" Add Moore's Law hitting its ceiling, with other nations stalling too, and it is like squatting near the finish line waiting for China. But this also means humanity's shared barrier stands right ahead—good news and bad news together.
🧭

Latecomer Advantage: Signpost Effect

Knowing the direction solves 30% of the problem. Multiple teams break through separately → see others' roadmap → catch-up accelerates.

05
Real Path
China's Catch-Up Strategy: R&D Cost Advantage + Full Industrial Portfolio + 80:20 Private–State Investment
China's R&D spending now ranks second only to America, but investment is about half of US levels. R&D costs are the massive lever—a team with the same function costs 4× more in the US than in China. Include overtime work and the gap widens further. Latecomers have no choice: where technology falls short, human effort makes up the difference—one generation's sweat buys the next generation's ease. China's government-to-enterprise R&D investment ratio runs about 2:8, similar to America's (bottom-up corporate + state basic R&D). China leads 23 of 30 frontier technologies (perovskite, monolayer materials, sodium-ion batteries, etc.); the US leads 7. The path is clear: lead in isolated areas first → then minority areas → then majority areas → sprint toward the frontier. Sustained effort, bright future.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Two centuries of tech catch-up reveal core patterns: ① Tech engine = profit desire + war demand—\"borderless technology\" was always an empty phrase; only by mastering your own substitutes can you stand tall. ② Latecomer advantage lies in the \"tech diffusion effect\"—confirming the direction solves over 30% of the problem. ③ China's leverage comes from a 4× R&D cost advantage + full industrial portfolio + 80:20 private–state investment split. Cardiac stents dropped from over ¥10,000 to a few hundred yuan—tech breakthroughs are not just about technology but life and death. From isolated to minority to majority to frontier-sprint leadership, the path is clear. Sustained effort, bright future.
