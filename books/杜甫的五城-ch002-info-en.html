杜甫的五城 · 第2章「Planting Willows by the Liu River」

Guilin · Liuzhou · Wuzhou · Xijiang River

Armed with the National Railway Timetable—his 'travel bible'—Lai took the No. 161 night train south from Yueyang to Guilin. In the soft-sleeper compartment, he met herbal-medicine 'profiteers' earning 1,000 yuan/month—triple a professor's salary. After the Li River cruise to Yangshuo, he boarded Train No. 115 (originating in Xi'an, terminating in Liuzhou) to trace Liu Zongyuan's exile route, then took a long-distance bus through northern Guangxi to Wuzhou, and a night boat down the Xijiang River back to Guangzhou—where the ship's bonus-driven collective contracting made its sales hustle more energetic than any capitalist market.

01The 'Travel Bible' & Train Sociology
The National Railway Timetable, printed in 1-2 million copies annually, was likely China's bestselling book. With it, Lai could plan his northwestern journey months ahead from Hong Kong, down to each transfer station and arrival time. At Yueyang, the ticket office refused soft-sleeper for a passing train—but onboard, the conductor warmly upgraded him: reserved berths were held for party officials. The soft-sleeper car became a micro-theater of social strata: managers, herbal-medicine 'profiteers,' and overseas scholars—each with their own upgrade strategy.
02Guilin's Landscape & Tourism Classes
Li River cruise boats came in two classes: cheap for domestic tourists, expensive (with lunch) for foreigners. Curiously, two Guangzhou cadres boarded the foreign boat with 'female companions'—all reimbursable by their work unit. At Yangshuo pier, a fisherman charged 2 yuan per photo with his cormorant; several Westerners sat writing letters in a dim shop, deliberately 'a tourist attraction to be viewed.' On Zhongshan Road, Guilin women in high heels rode bicycles—a unique tableau of urban everyday life.
03Liuzhou: Tracing Liu Zongyuan's Millennial Trail
Liu Zongyuan took three months from Chang'an to Liuzhou; Express No. 115 needed barely two days and nights. 1,350 years later, Lai entered Liuhou Park, filled with weeping willows—echoing Liu's self-mocking poem 'Liuzhou's Liu Prefect, planting willows by the Liu River,' the five Liu/liu characters now materialized as gardens of willow trees. Outside the shrine stood Liu's cenotaph and a monumental stone statue. Having researched the 'Yongzhen Reform,' Lai felt deep sympathy for Liu's fate: a promising official in his thirties, forever exiled by political storms.
04Northern Guangxi Mountain Roads
The long-distance bus from Liuzhou to Wuzhou wound through mountains: 'a mountain on one side, water on the other.' Cattle grazed on emerald sandbanks in the river. At noon, a nameless roadside restaurant listed over 50 banquet dishes but served only a few vegetable dishes at 3-4 yuan each—'a ruthless fleecing of passing travelers.' Thirty-plus passengers wisely brought their own dry provisions. This was Lai's first mainland bus ride and first encounter with the 'tourist scalping' culture.
05Xijiang Night Boat: Hustle Beats Capitalism
Wuzhou to Guangzhou, only steerage tickets remained. Stepping in: mold, urine, chicken dung—cages of chicks lined the corridor. Yet the night's unforgettable feature was the sales activity. Cantonese broadcasts rolled endlessly: boxed meals, sweet potato soup, pork congee, fried noodles… crew members with baskets hawked everything. The reason? Collective contracting—surplus revenue meant staff bonuses. Lai marveled: 'Even livelier than a capitalist market!' The Xijiang is calm inland water; he slept soundly through the night.
💡 KEY INSIGHT
From the Railway Ministry's timetable to the millennial willows on the Liu River, from train profiteers to the Xijiang hawkers—the best guide for traveling China is not a travel agency, but a keen eye sensitive to both history and the present.
