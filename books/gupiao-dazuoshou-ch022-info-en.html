Imperial Steel was a widely acknowledged valuable but dormant stock: insiders held 70% they couldn't sell, investors found it "boring," and speculators found it "asleep." Livermore activated this stock through a four-step system: ① independent due diligence confirming real value; ② trust agreement locking 70% of float to eliminate backstab risk; ③ gradual demand creation — from sluggish to active to price advance; ④ staged distribution. Throughout, only 7,000 shares were needed to push the price up over 30 points. In stark contrast, Petroleum Products failed because the client secretly dumped shares after seeing the price rise — same system, same Livermore, the only difference was "people."
01
Preparatory Work
Due Diligence: Not by Feeling, But by Facts
The first thing Livermore did after taking the project was not trade — it was to send professionals to conduct on-site research into Imperial Steel's production, commercial, and financial departments. "My purpose was not to discover strengths or weaknesses, but to grasp the facts — as they were." The reports confirmed it was a valuable asset. Livermore's conclusion was emphatic: "I could see no reason why I could not, in good conscience and with confidence, undertake the project." Note "in good conscience" — he was not speculative pumping, but creating a legitimate market for a genuinely valuable asset.
🔬

What Proper DD Accomplishes

On-site research of production, commercial, and financial departments. Independent reports. No preconceptions — "as they were."

⚠️

The Cost of Skipping DD

Judging value by gut feel. Endorsing inferior assets. No conscience — this is the line between speculation and fraud.

02
Risk Control
Trust Lock-Up: I Am Not a Dumping Ground
Livermore's first line of defense before launch: "I had them place 70% of their holdings under a trust agreement. I had no intention of becoming a dumping ground for what the large shareholders wished to sell." This firmly strapped down the vast majority of potential selling pressure — the remaining 30% of dispersed holdings were all he needed to manage. He uses a brilliant analogy: just as life insurance policyholders won't all die on the same day, dispersed shareholders won't all dump simultaneously. Reducing risk to a calculable scope is essential professional discipline.

03
Operation Cadence
Four-Step Activation: From Hibernation to Boiling Point
Livermore decomposed stock activation into four interlocking phases. Phase 1: create a sluggish market to shake out weak hands through natural fluctuation. Phase 2: increase two-way trading volume — not to push up price but to make the market "see" this stock. Phase 3: 7,000 shares push price up over 30 points — true power lies not in capital size but in precise grasp of market psychology's rhythm. Phase 4: staged distribution leveraging market absorption. Without a sufficient liquidity foundation, any price run-up is a castle in the air.

04
Cautionary Tale
Petroleum Products: The Cost of Broken Trust
In the same chapter, Livermore contrasts another operation — Petroleum Products. Same strategy, same methods, same Livermore — completely different outcome. The cause lay not in the strategy, but in "people." After seeing Livermore successfully push up the price, Petroleum Products insiders let greed override their contract — they secretly dumped massive quantities of their own shares, thoroughly destroying the market structure Livermore had painstakingly built. The operator's system, no matter how perfect, is no match for the client's betrayal.

05
Ultimate Lesson
Trust Is the Most Fundamental Asset
With 7,000 shares, Livermore levered a 30-point advance — his secret lay not in capital size but in the four-step rhythm: "lock up first, create demand, then push up, finally distribute." But the most important investment wisdom in this chapter may not be the operating strategy itself, but the paramount importance of trust. The Imperial Steel vs. Petroleum Products comparison is the best textbook: same operating system, same operator — the difference between success and failure lay not in strategy, but in "people." Choosing the right partner is as important as choosing the right stock. A trust agreement is not legal decoration — it is a survival necessity.
