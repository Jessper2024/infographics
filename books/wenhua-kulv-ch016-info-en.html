Wenhua Kulv · Ch. 16 "Tianyi Pavilion in Wind and Rain"

Mature civilization is transmitted through the written word — Tianyi Pavilion is more than a building; it is the physical embodiment of a great will to sustain Chinese civilization across time

Amidst a typhoon and pouring rain, Yu Qiuyu waded barefoot toward Tianyi Pavilion — "Shedding refinement, shedding leisure, removing my shoes, bowing and scraping, shivering, paying reverent homage." Tianyi Pavilion is China's oldest surviving private library, and the third oldest in the world (after two Renaissance libraries in Italy). Its founder, Fan Qin, built this bibliographic fortress through a tumultuous official career, driven by an unreasoning obsession and engineer-like precision. After Fan Qin's death, his "the books must never be divided" rule became a centuries-long ordeal for the Fan clan — strict prohibitions drove the book-loving Qian Xiuyun to her death, but also brought the great scholar Huang Zongxi through its doors and attracted the attention of the Qianlong Emperor's Siku Quanshu project. Modern thieves and wartime bombs struck repeatedly, but Tianyi Pavilion endures like a worldly elder, "gazing upon all who come after with a face full of philosophy."

01
Storm Pilgrimage
Shedding All Dignity — A Pure Act of Homage
He had decided to visit Tianyi Pavilion tomorrow. That night, a typhoon struck and torrential rain fell — all of Ningbo trembled weakly. The next morning at the Pavilion, the courtyards were a vast flood. The elderly gatekeeper offered rain boots and umbrellas, but the water was too deep — the only way was to remove shoes, roll up trousers, and wade through. "I knew the weight of Tianyi Pavilion, so I was willing to accept heaven's arrangement — shedding refinement, shedding leisure, removing my shoes, bowing and scraping, shivering, paying reverent homage. Today there were no other visitors; this ritual of homage was both quiet and pure."
02
Enemies of Books
How Hard Is It for a Single Book to Survive?
Mature civilization is transmitted through the written word. Yet books have encountered too many mortal enemies: imperial book burnings — from Qin Shi Huang and Li Si through Emperor Yang of Sui, Cai Jing, Qin Hui, and Ming Chengzu, not to mention the Qing literary inquisitions; wartime destruction — Dong Zhuo's rebellion destroyed over 6,000 cartloads, Western Wei forces burned 140,000 volumes in a single day at Jiangling, peasant uprisings in the late Sui burned 370,000 volumes; water and fire — ancient books traveled by boat; countless shipments capsized in the Yellow River during the Han and Tang, while fires at the Song's Chongwen Institute and Ming's Wenyuan Pavilion reduced imperial collections to ashes. Insects and mold, too, were irresistible foes. "Two centuries of accumulation can scatter in a single morning; three thousand li of gathering can burn in a single evening." This is the very silhouette of civilization's fate.

03
Founder
Fan Qin: Officialdom Was a Side Job; Book Collecting Was His Life's Work
Fan Qin was born in the early 16th century — two years before his birth, Michelangelo completed his sculpture of David; the same year he was born, Leonardo da Vinci finished the Mona Lisa. Fan Qin's official career was tumultuous: he was once falsely accused and subjected to "court beating" — stripped of his trousers, bound in hemp, and struck thirty-six times before an audience of officials at the Meridian Gate. After such punishment, a person's "psychological sinews" take on a different form — the astonishing will and perseverance he later displayed as a book collector were all connected to this. He traversed most of China. At every posting, beyond official duties, what most interested him was the blue cloth bundle discreetly delivered by a runner, the book-purchasing list tucked in his sleeve. "Looking back from a distance of centuries, there is no doubt: for Fan Qin, book collecting was his life's primary work; officialdom was merely a side occupation."

04
Cost of Legacy
"The Books Must Not Be Divided" — A Centuries-Long Forced Labor
Fan Qin's greatest challenge was transforming his will into an unshakeable family inheritance. He had two sons: Fan Dachong and Fan Daqian. He decreed that Fan Dachong would inherit all the books alone, while Fan Daqian would receive ten thousand taels of silver as compensation — and "the books must not be divided" became the Fan family's iron law. The clan established draconian penalties: descendants who opened the pavilion without cause were barred from three ancestral rites; those who brought relatives or friends inside were barred for one year; those who lent books outside were barred for three years... The punishment — exclusion from ancestral ceremonies — was considered the ultimate humiliation, a "yellow card" against one's bloodline. Qian Xiuyun, a woman who loved books passionately, married into the Fan family hoping to access the library, only to discover that women were strictly forbidden from ascending. She fell into despair and died heartbroken. "If you care for me, bury me near Tianyi Pavilion — then I can close my eyes in peace!"

05
Calamity & Rebirth
Thieves, Bombs, and Huang Zongxi: 450 Years of Tianyi Pavilion
In 1673, the great scholar Huang Zongxi became the first outsider permitted to ascend — the Fan clan unanimously agreed. He read the entire collection, compiled a catalog, and wrote Record of Tianyi Pavilion. From then on, a new rule allowed genuine scholars access, though only about a dozen were admitted over the next two centuries — Wan Sitong, Quan Zuwang, Qian Daxin, Yuan Mei, Ruan Yuan, Xue Fucheng — all names worthy of Chinese cultural history. When the Qianlong Emperor compiled the Siku Quanshu, Tianyi Pavilion contributed over 600 rare texts, 96 of which were included; the Emperor ordered new imperial libraries to be modeled after its design. But modern calamities arrived: during the Taiping Rebellion, thieves dismantled walls and sold books as scrap paper to mills. In 1914, a thief named Xue Jiwei stealthily entered, hiding by day and stealing by night, making off with nearly half the precious collection. Zhang Yuanji of the Commercial Press spent a fortune to rescue what remained, storing it in the Hanfen Lou — which was ultimately incinerated by Japanese bombs.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Tianyi Pavilion is more than a library — it is the physical embodiment of "the great will to sustain Chinese civilization across time." Knowing that private book collecting was a tragic enterprise yet pressing forward anyway — this is the power of character shown by Fan Qin and his descendants. It has withstood typhoons, thieves, warfare, and bombs, yet stands like a worldly elder, gazing with philosophical countenance upon all who follow — "Have you arrived? Which generation of Chinese scholar are you?"
