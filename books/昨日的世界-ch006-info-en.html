The World of Yesterday · Ch.6 "My Arduous Path"

The passion for collecting manuscripts, the curse on theatrical productions, thirty years with Insel Verlag — a writer's growth on a path shaped by fate

This chapter is Zweig's profound retrospective of the first half of his literary career. Through the thread of "temporary feeling," he recounts his deliberately provisional lifestyle, his near-obsessive collection of famous manuscripts, and the eerie fate that struck down three great actors who were to perform his plays. Ultimately, his thirty-year bond with Insel Verlag became his literary home. The chapter reveals a core truth: life's path often deviates from our wishes, yet it inevitably leads us toward goals we cannot yet see.

1 "Temporary Feeling" — A Wise Philosophy of Living

Zweig deliberately kept his Vienna apartment as "merely an address," never settling permanently — this "temporary feeling" unexpectedly became his spiritual shelter during exile.

From graduating secondary school until WWI, Zweig lived with a "wise sense of provisionality." Every undertaking came with the caveat "this doesn't really count." Writing was practice for "real" creation; the apartment was just an address; furniture was deliberately modest. While peers married and built careers, Zweig saw himself as "a beginner with plenty of time ahead."

Temporary Living

  • Small suburban flat, not restricting freedom
  • No fine furniture, no "maintenance"
  • Writing as rehearsal for "real work"
  • Delaying all final decisions

Unexpected Benefit

  • Pain of leaving homeland was eased
  • Loss of possessions felt less devastating
  • "Detachment" became emotional protection
  • Provisional living became exile rehearsal
2 Collecting Genius: From Autographs to Living Legends

Zweig's passion evolved from chasing poets' autographs as a student to obsessively collecting genius manuscripts — he believed masters' original drafts reveal the mysterious moment art is born.

He lost interest in mere signatures and sought poetry and music manuscripts instead. Beethoven's smudged pencil corrections thrilled him; Balzac's proof sheets — "almost every sentence revised" — drove him to ecstasy. A missing fragment of a Mozart manuscript was found at a Stockholm auction, allowing him to reassemble the aria complete after 150 years.

Romain Rolland gave the manuscript of Jean-Christophe; Rilke gave The Song of Love and Death; Claudel, Gorky, and Freud donated too. Upstairs lived Frau Delmelius, Goethe's doctor's daughter — a living link to the Weimar era, connecting the age of Goethe to Zweig's suburban flat via a fragile red thread.

3 The Theatrical Curse: Three Actors' Deaths

Zweig's dramatic career was haunted by an eerie pattern — every great actor about to premiere his play died during rehearsals, a threefold tragedy that made him abandon theater forever.

🔴 Mitterwurzer (1906)
Berlin's Royal Theatre was to premiere Thersites with Mitterwurzer as Achilles. Rehearsals began, sleeping-car tickets booked — then a telegram: "Performance postponed due to Mitterwurzer's illness." Eight days later, his obituary appeared. Zweig's verses were the last words his reciting mouth ever spoke.
🟠 Kainz (1910)
Joseph Kainz requested to play Thersites and commissioned Zweig to write a one-act play. The script was finished, rehearsals imminent — Kainz returned from tour and was sent to a sanatorium. Cancer. "Will dear God still let me perform this play?" Weeks later, they stood at his coffin.
🟡 Moissi (1935)
Alexander Moissi asked to star in Pirandello's premiere and requested Zweig's translation. Out of superstition, Zweig refused Moissi for his own plays — but could not refuse the translation. Before rehearsals began — Moissi caught a cold in Vienna, and two days later he was dead. Twenty-five years later, the same horror repeated.

"Life's path is determined by inner forces; our roads often deviate from our wishes, chaotic and inexplicable, yet they ultimately lead us toward goals invisible to ourselves." — Zweig

4 Insel Verlag: Thirty Years of Spiritual Home

Becoming Insel Verlag's permanent author at 26 was Zweig's luckiest turning point — this publisher that "only printed worthwhile books" became his spiritual home for thirty years.

Alfred Walter Heymel founded Insel Verlag with the principle of never prioritizing profit, only publishing valuable works. Every detail — title design, layout, typeface, paper — was meticulously considered. Hofmannsthal and Rilke had already set the highest standard. Zweig's friendship with director Kippenberg deepened through their shared passion for manuscript collecting.

"I can admit today: leaving home and homeland did not trouble me greatly, but never again seeing the familiar 'Island' emblem on my books caused me unbearable pain." — Zweig

At Zweig's suggestion, the "Island Library" series was born, with millions of copies elevating Insel to the most prestigious German-language publisher. This organic bond between author and publisher became a model of literary partnership.

5 The Core of Creation: The Tragedy of Failures

Zweig never glorified heroes — he focused always on the tragedy of the defeated, a creative philosophy that ran through all his novellas and biographical writings.

His first play, Thersites, embodied this: not Achilles but his obscure opponent — a man of suffering who lacked the power to inflict pain. In biography, he chose Erasmus over Luther, Mary Stuart over Elizabeth I, Castellio over Calvin.

Zweig's Choices

  • Erasmus, not Martin Luther
  • Mary Stuart, not Elizabeth I
  • Castellio, not Calvin
  • Thersites, not Achilles

Creative Philosophy

  • Protagonists are fate's victims
  • Noble moral spirit over worldly success
  • Tragedy more worthy than triumph
  • Failures' stories reveal deeper humanity

💡 Key Takeaway

Zweig's "arduous path" reveals a profound paradox: those seemingly off-course detours — the three theatrical curses, the indecisive "temporary feeling," the preference for the defeated — ultimately guided him to the path best suited for him. Provisional living became exile rehearsal; theatrical setbacks forced him toward biography and novella; and thirty years with Insel Verlag proved that the best partnerships arise from shared values, not commercial interest.
