01
Root Question
A Question Pursued for 20 Years
From a rural elementary school in Gyeonggi Province to a career in Seoul, the author was haunted by the same question. With only 50 students per grade and cows visible during the bus ride to school — why were some classmates outstanding scholars? At university, seeing "study gods" who ignored cram schools deepened the question. In professional life, observing people transform themselves through learning in spare moments kept it alive. The question spanned high school, university, career, and personal crisis — until three treasure chests finally revealed the answer.

03
Debunking
Two Debunked Answers: Talent & Study Volume
The author systematically examined and discarded the two most common explanations. The talent hypothesis claimed "aptitude" is innate — but like muscles, aptitude develops with use and atrophies with neglect. Good aptitude is merely the result of earnest learning. The study-volume hypothesis argued more hours guarantee success — yet "I studied all day and still failed" proves that quality matters far more than quantity. Study volume answers only half the question; the other half lies in a different dimension.
🧬

Talent Hypothesis (Debunked)

Aptitude is not fixed or innate. Like muscles: use → grow, neglect → atrophy. Good aptitude = result of genuine learning.

📏

Volume Hypothesis (Debunked)

Volume matters but only answers half. "I studied all day and still failed" — quantity alone is insufficient for success.

04
Breakthrough
Anders Ericsson: Solitary Practice Is the Key
Swedish psychologist Anders Ericsson's research on music conservatory students became the decisive breakthrough. When asked which activities improve performance, all students gave the same answer — "solitary practice." Like riding a bicycle, the know-how is elusive in words but must be personally experienced. Even attending famous lecturers' courses, trying complex methods, or spending fortunes on tutoring — without solitary practice, ability never improves. 05
Action Guide
Change Direction: From Seeking Outside to Growing Within
The pain of learning is not caused by insufficient aptitude — the direction is simply wrong. People frantically attend full-day classes, enroll in expensive cram schools, and chase others' study secrets — then lament "I can't take it anymore, maybe my aptitude is just poor." The correct direction: reduce external activities, sit down immediately, and increase solo study time. This book covers learning principles, life management, mental management, and brain science to help every self-learner master the "fixed pattern of studying well."
