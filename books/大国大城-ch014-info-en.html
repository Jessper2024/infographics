Great Country, Big City · Ch.6「Great Cities in Global Perspective」

Mega-Cities · National Scale · Primate City · Giant Metropolis The laws governing China's urban development are no different from those elsewhere. This chapter examines from a global perspective the determinants of primate city population, the trajectory of giant metropolises, and Shanghai's future population trends.

This is Book Chapter 6. Core thesis: China's urban development is not exceptional but another confirmation of global trends. The author presents three stories — "density and wages," "people flock to opportunities," and "ports and wages" — to demonstrate geography's decisive impact on urban development. Using Zipf's Law and data from 142 countries, the analysis reveals that national population is the primary determinant of primate city population (R²=85%), suggesting Shanghai remains too small rather than too large. Tokyo serves as a reference for giant metropolis trajectories — its population continues to grow slowly even as Japan's total population declines. The model predicts Shanghai's population will reach approximately 41 million when China's urbanization process concludes. The key message: city administrators should not impose subjective population targets over objective economic laws.

01
Story 1
Density and Wages: Higher Density, Higher Earnings
China's urban development is another example of global trends. First story: higher population density cities have higher wages. Consider: if living costs were the only reason for higher wages, firms wouldn't pay. Firms pay more because employees create more value — productivity determines wages. Worldwide, there is a stable positive correlation between urban density and labor productivity. When comparing population density at equal distances from city centers, Shanghai's level matches Tokyo, New York, and Hong Kong — only Paris and London are lower. China's urban development laws are no different from the rest of the world. Geography has a decisive impact on urban growth.
02
Story 2
People Flock to Opportunity: The Agglomeration of Talent
Second story: big cities retain college graduates better. Data show that cities with higher shares of college graduates in the 1982 census saw even greater increases in graduate share by 2010 — mirroring the US phenomenon of graduates clustering in graduate-rich cities. Cities with higher average education levels have higher wages. Meanwhile, low-skilled workers also migrate to big cities for service jobs, so cities with initially high graduate shares also experienced faster overall population growth. Human capital externalities create a self-reinforcing cycle of "talent attracting talent." Policy efforts to artificially reverse this trend defy economic laws and will be costly.
03
Story 3
Ports and Wages: Geography's Decisive Role
Third story: firm clustering near ports generates higher labor productivity. The shortest distance to one of China's three major ports — Shanghai, Hong Kong, or Tianjin — significantly impacts wages. Within 500 km, the further from a port, the lower the wage — about 50% lower at 500 km compared to port-adjacent areas. Beyond 500 km, the distance-wage relationship disappears. The 500 km radius roughly corresponds to the distance from Shanghai to western Anhui — basically the Pan-Yangtze River Delta range. Geography's influence is decisive. Economic policy must respect these laws; working against market forces is counterproductive and self-defeating.
04
Zipf's Law
Zipf's Law and the Primate City
According to Zipf's Law: a country's largest city has twice the population of the second-largest, three times the third-largest, and so on. This law holds across many countries. China's pre-reform planned-economy migration policies caused dispersed population distribution; post-reform market forces have pushed distribution increasingly toward Zipf's Law. Research with 142 countries by the author and Chen Zhao for the Asian Development Bank reveals: national population explains 85% of primate city population variation (R²=0.85). Each 1% increase in national population corresponds to only 0.76% increase in primate city population — people factor agglomeration costs into their decisions. According to this fitted line, Shanghai is below the curve — too small, not too large.

05
Asian Sample
Asia's Distinctiveness: Tight Man-Land Relationships
Asian countries generally have higher population densities, making primate city "urban ills" more severe. Analyzing the Asian sample alone, the R² remains high at 75%, but the coefficient drops to 0.63 — higher density makes land prices and housing costs rise faster, slowing population inflow. Yet Shanghai still lies below the fitted line. Countries with higher agricultural shares have more dispersed populations; as industry and services grow, populations concentrate. This means as China's urbanization rises, many rural residents will enter cities — including mega-cities. When China's urbanization process concludes, the author's model predicts Shanghai's population will be approximately 41 million — squarely between prior estimates of 30–54 million.
06
Tokyo Model
Giant Metropolis Trajectory: Lessons from Tokyo
The author uses Tokyo as Shanghai's primary reference for three reasons: East Asia's tight man-land relationship, similar cultural preferences for service consumption, and China's need for a world-class urban agglomeration. Tokyo's postwar population growth reveals consistent patterns — 1945-1965: annual growth of 370,000; 1920-1975 (55 years): still 145,000 annually, with Tokyo's area being only 1/3 of Shanghai's. Scaling to Shanghai's area → ~450,000 per year. In the mid-1990s, Tokyo's population briefly declined due to a capital function dispersal plan — but Japan quickly abandoned it, as administrative dispersal damaged Tokyo's international competitiveness and Japan's overall growth. This history merits China's reflection. Tokyo still grows slowly today, while Japan's total population declines. Every global metropolis without exception continues to grow.

07
Re-urbanization
The Reversal of "Counter-Urbanization": Return to City Centers
"Counter-urbanization" is popular discourse in China. Though Beijing and Shanghai's central areas have seen some population decline, this is not a lasting trend. Tokyo's experience is instructive: from 1965-1995, Tokyo's nighttime-to-daytime population ratio fell steadily — but since 1995, it has rebounded significantly. People are moving back downtown. The US shows a similar trend — in 2010-2011, 27 of the 51 largest US metropolitan areas saw central city growth outpace suburban growth. Reasons include: the era of cheap oil has passed, modern service industries increase demand for face-to-face interaction, central city crime rates have fallen, environments have improved, and cultural amenities have multiplied. As incomes rise, the opportunity cost of time increases — people no longer want to live "either at work or on the way to work." High central city housing prices are simply the cost of paying for convenience in work and life.

08
Shanghai Forecast
Shanghai's Population: 30-54 Million by 2040
From 1978 to 2013, Shanghai's average annual population growth (including migrants) was about 480,000. Given Shanghai's area is 3× Tokyo's, this is not an "explosion." Shanghai's non-hukou population growth has recently slowed with economic growth. For 2040, the Fudan Planning Institute projects three scenarios: Scenario 1: Keep current population, no new labor → 19 million total, elderly share rises from 15% to 41% (unsustainable). Scenario 2: Maintain working-age stock → 30 million (still unsustainable). Scenario 3: Maintain working-age share → 54 million (unadjusted for cost-of-living selection). The author's model predicts: when China's urbanization concludes, Shanghai's population will be approximately 41 million. Key attitude: population is the result of economic development, not a government control target — scientifically forecast and increase public service supply.

09
Density
Population Density Not Above International Levels
Some argue against city size based on density. But comparing density at equal distances from city centers, Shanghai is on par with Tokyo, New York, and Hong Kong (see Fig. 6-10) — Paris and London are lower. Beijing is much lower still. Claims that Shanghai is "too dense" have no empirical basis. Two common comparison errors must be avoided: dividing Shanghai's total population by total area (area is large, making density seem low) and using only the inner ring road (which is like using only the core area of other cities, making density seem high). The correct method is comparing population density at equal distances from the urban center — this is the international standard.
10
Policy
Respecting Laws: Scientific Forecasting, Not Subjective Control
Globally, few countries treat city population as a government control target — most treat it as an economic outcome used for forecast-based policymaking. New York expects 1 million more residents; Singapore plans growth from 5.5 to 7 million. Beijing and Shanghai consistently shatter government population targets years ahead of schedule — yet instead of questioning faulty targets, authorities claim "carrying capacity has been exceeded." A simple question: if carrying capacity were truly breached, how are the people currently exceeding it being carried? According to the Shanghai Master Plan (1999-2020), the 2020 population target was ~18 million; actual population in 2014 already exceeded 25 million. It is precisely this lagging forecast that caused public service tensions and infrastructure shortages. The scientific approach: forecast population trends, adjust urban planning, increase infrastructure and public service supply — not control demand by controlling population.
"I sincerely hope city administrators will not impose a subjective population target over universal economic laws, delaying the opportunity to increase urban public services and infrastructure supply."
— Lu Ming
🔑 Core Conclusion
China's urban development is not exceptional but confirms global patterns. Data from 142 countries reveals national population as the primary determinant of primate city size (R²=85%), and by this law Shanghai remains too small. Tokyo shows that once administrative intervention stops, giant metropolises resume growth — even against a backdrop of national population decline. Shanghai is forecast to reach ~41 million by urbanization's end. City administrators should not impose subjective population targets — they should scientifically forecast trends and expand infrastructure and public services. The global trend is reversing "counter-urbanization," as people return to city centers driven by the needs of the modern service economy for face-to-face interaction.
