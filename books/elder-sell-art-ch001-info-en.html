Trading for a Living II: The Art of Selling · Chapter 1
"Buying"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Financial markets are vast and complex — no one can master everything. Like doctors who must specialize, traders must make deliberate choices about their method, style, and process. Most traders blindly copy others without ever asking whether these choices fit their own personality. How should a trader build a solid foundation across multiple dimensions? The answer lies in four deliberate decisions: analysis method, trading style, decision process, and a focused, deep toolkit.

Trading requires both confidence and humility. The market is too vast for one person to master. Modern doctors cannot simultaneously be surgeons, psychiatrists, and pediatricians — the "renaissance physician" of centuries past no longer exists. Traders must become experts in specific domains while staying humble about areas outside their expertise. Successful trading rests on three pillars: Method, Mindset, and Money Management — all three are essential.

01
Mindset
Specialization Is a Requirement for Traders
Trading demands a balance of confidence and humility. Markets are too vast for one person — specialization isn't a limitation but a survival strategy. Focusing on a few mastered markets and tools yields far better results than shallow breadth.
🏥

Analogy: Modern Doctor

No one expects the same doctor to be a surgeon and pediatrician. Specialists go deep; generalists skim the surface. Trading is the same.

📊

Application: Expert Trader

Know your temperament, preferences, and style. Be an expert in a few areas. Stay humble about the rest — knowing what you aren't good at is as vital as knowing what you are.

02
Analysis
Technical Analysis vs. Fundamental Analysis: The First Choice
A trader's first critical choice: does price already reflect everything, or must you investigate intrinsic value? Technical analysis reads charts to determine the winner between bulls and bears. Fundamental analysis studies company value and supply-demand balance. Rigorous traders combine both — fundamental traders check charts for timing, technical traders monitor fundamentals to avoid swimming against the tide.

03
Style
Trend Trading vs. Counter-Trend: Three Types of Traders
Every chart consists of trending moves and consolidations. Richard Weissman identified three trader types, each with its own game. Trend trading is like riding a wild horse; counter-trend exploits oscillations; day trading is an entirely different battlefield. None is inherently better — only one is better for you.
Trend-Following
→ traits →
Ride the major trend, demands great patience, largest potential reward — but trends look clear in hindsight while feeling like a wild horse in real time
Mean-Reversion
→ traits →
Exploit oscillations, shorter holding periods, smaller but steadier gains — patience to wait for the right setup is key
Day Trading
→ traits →
Intraday entries and exits, speed is everything, high-frequency decisions under constant pressure — different challenges, different skills
04
Process
Discretionary Trading vs. Systematic Trading: Follow Your Temperament
Discretionary traders read charts analytically and decide step by step — an engaging, hands-on process. Systematic traders study historical data and design robust systems to generate signals. Don't analyze cost/benefit here — follow your temperament, like choosing where to live or whom to marry. At elite levels, the boundary blurs: top systematic traders allocate like discretionary ones; savvy discretionary traders have mechanical rules.

Toolkit
The Five-Bullet Rule: Sharpen Your Tools
A single candlestick gives only five data points — open, high, low, close, volume. Elder's "Five Bullets" caps indicators at five per chart. He uses four: Moving Averages, Envelopes/Channels, MACD, and Force Index. He distrusts classical patterns (head-and-shoulders, triangles) as too subjective, preferring calculated indicators whose signals are unambiguous. 🔑 Key Takeaway
None of a trader's choices — analysis method, trading style, decision process, or toolkit — are inherently right or wrong. The only criterion that matters: does it fit your personality perfectly? Only tools researched and built by your own hand can sustain confidence through adverse markets. Specialize, know yourself, and keep your toolkit lean. Combine these three, and you have a good trade.
