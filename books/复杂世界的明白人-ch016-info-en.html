Understanding the Complex World · Chapter 16 "What Skills You Need to Earn a Million-Yuan Annual Salary"

300K Is the Ceiling of What Money Can Buy · Tech Alone Won't Get You to a Million — Management Is the Ladder Ten years in a major tech company. Hit the million mark in year six. But after 300,000 yuan a year, the quality-of-life improvement curve goes flat. Very few people break the million barrier on technical skill alone — the key is becoming a leader, managing relationships, and becoming the person your boss trusts. Once promoted, you claim a share of the output from those below you. Mindset, your body, and long-term thinking are the real foundation.

This chapter distills the essence of the author's ten years in a major tech company. A million-yuan annual salary is not a direct payoff for technical ability — it is the composite result of management, relationships, reporting, mindset, and physical stamina. The author breaks down the three stages of the boss-subordinate relationship (task execution → private trust → inner circle), defines the standard of a "killer" employee, and candidly discusses the hardest part of the technical track: not the technology itself, but unfair compensation and the corrosive effect of comparison. The chapter closes with honest advice on the programmer's exit strategy: your body is the real floor beneath everything.

01
Income Truth
Breaking a Million on Tech Alone Is Brutally Hard — Effort and Reward Are Wildly Out of Proportion
High income comes primarily from becoming a leader, not from technical ability. Being a leader doesn't mean your output tripled or that you're now worth three people — it means you can claim a share of the output of those beneath you as your own. About one in four or five people is actually suited for a purely technical track — laser-focused, reads code like it's a game, doesn't even want to go home on weekends. Everyone else just gets by, lacking real fire. The worst part of a technical career isn't the technology itself — it's \"unfair compensation.\" Someone less skilled than you is doing better. Can you swallow that? Your buddy who joined at the same time gets a 30,000-yuan bonus while you get 7,000 — can you not spiral into depression?
💻

Tech Track: Effort and Reward Out of Sync

Grind to exhaustion → get overtaken by new hires → mental breakdown → quit and hop. Job-hop every two years, salary creeps up, long-term resources vanish.

👑

Management Track: Leverage Others' Output

Win your boss's trust → get promoted → claim your team's output as yours → ride your boss's coattails upward.

02
Relationship Management
Three Steps to Becoming Your Boss's Trusted Person: Reliable Execution → Private Trust → Inner Circle
Your relationship with your boss typically goes through three stages. Stage One: your boss assigns you more and more work. You deliver every time. That alone is immense support for their work and life — when promotion time comes, you'll be their top candidate. Stage Two: your boss occasionally asks you out for dinner, shares things they don't tell others. Guard your mouth — never leak a single thing about your boss. Stage Three: your boss comes to you with hard problems to brainstorm solutions. If you consistently offer good ideas, they'll almost certainly take you along when they get promoted. Stick with the right person — wherever they go, they bring you, and you rise with them.

03
Execution Code
Build a "Killer" Reputation: Execute Fast + Report Proactively + Max Two Errors — Make Your Boss Completely at Ease
Everything your boss hands you — do everything you can to nail it fast. Don't drag your feet. Report progress on your own initiative; never make them chase you for updates. If something can't be resolved quickly, send daily status reports without fail. Your boss generally won't give you a third chance — at most two mistakes. Make every task a source of confidence for your boss, and they'll come to you for everything. Pay special attention: some tasks look low-level but were actually assigned by your boss's boss. Screw one of those up, and it takes forever to repair the impression.

04
Communication Leverage
Reporting Is the Amplifier of Income: Frame Your Work from Others' Perspective — Being Tongue-Tied Is Fine, Scorning Those Who Aren't Is Not
Most people have no idea what you actually do — it all depends on what comes out of your mouth. For the same piece of work, your report says \"improved feature X,\" while your colleague says \"cracked problem Y, solved client need Z, served N users, saved M yuan.\" You have to see your work through other people's eyes. Once you become a team lead, don't get arrogant — take your people out one-on-one and actually talk to them. You will regress technically; don't force yourself to stay deep in the code. Spend more time talking with leadership and securing benefits for your team. Arrive earlier than everyone; don't slip out before quitting time. When there's a crisis requiring overtime, be there alongside the team. But inspect deliverables ruthlessly — the moment your team thinks you're easy to fool, things will go wrong.

05
The Hard Floor
Mindset and Body Are the Hard Floor Under Your Career — Unfair Pay Cuts Deepest, Your Body Is the Real Dividing Line
Income is always compared to the people around you — and that comparison hurts the most. You might already have a very high income, but if a classmate or peer makes even more, it can plunge you into depression. Long-term upward mobility in an organization requires one of the following: overwhelming professional dominance, attaching yourself to someone who takes you wherever they go, riding a business that explodes in growth, or having your team's key people all bail so you're the last one standing. Physical stamina is the hard differentiator — the bosses you worked under were basically on all year with no breaks, and they genuinely didn't feel tired. After thirty, effort increasingly depreciates — the years you can charge ahead at full speed are just a handful. When programmers can't keep up anymore, they can shift to state-owned enterprises — salary collapses but you preserve your body. A single serious illness resets your entire outlook on life. Better to plan ahead.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The essence of a million-yuan salary is not a return on technical ability — it's monetized trust leverage. You win your boss's trust, get promoted, and channel your subordinates' output into your own account. The three-stage relationship progression (tasks → private trust → inner circle) is the only upward passage. The biggest enemy for technical workers isn't skill obsolescence — it's the mental corrosion of \"unfair compensation.\" Reporting is the income amplifier; your body is the hard floor beneath everything — those bosses who work year-round and those tech fanatics who don't want to go home on weekends aren't running on willpower, but on a body that simply doesn't know fatigue. In this life, the years you can charge at full speed are just a handful. Plan ahead.
