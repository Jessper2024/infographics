Sensible in a Complex World · Ch.33 "Why Japan Couldn't Choke South Korea's Throat"

Japan cuts off three semiconductor materials → South Korea counter-attacks with permanent replacements → A lesson for China's chip blockade

In July 2019, Japan suddenly restricted exports of fluorinated polyimide, photoresist, and high-purity hydrogen fluoride to South Korea — three lifeblood semiconductor materials, and the Korean electronics industry nearly went into shock overnight. Everyone predicted South Korea would beg for mercy, but half a year later: Korea not only didn't kneel, it developed homegrown alternatives and permanently reduced imports from Japan. Japanese corporate profits plunged (one etching gas company saw capacity drop 30%, profits down 18%). By late 2020, Japan wanted to restore supply — Korea's response: "Even if you resume, we don't want it anymore." This game reveals the universal law of embargo strategy: in a buyer's market, the blockader ultimately wounds itself.

01
Trigger
Japan-Korea Standoff: A left-wing president hit Japan's sore spot
Moon Jae-in was South Korea's most "left" president in history — anti-US, anti-Japan, anti-chaebol. In 1965, Japan paid Korea $800 million in reparations, but all of it went to Japanese-invested Korean industrial conglomerates — the actual victims never saw a dime. After Moon took office, he relentlessly attacked Japan, even confiscating some Japanese corporate assets in Korea. Japan, infuriated, restricted semiconductor material exports to Korea — turning Korea from a "tofu seller" into a nation that couldn't buy "soybeans."

02
Embargo
Japan's precision strike — the fragility of the Korea-Japan semiconductor division of labor
Post-war, Korea and Japan formed a semiconductor division of labor: Korea specialized in manufacturing ("selling tofu"), Japan in materials ("wholesaling soybeans"). When Japan cut off the three materials, the Korean electronics industry nearly went into shock instantly. The three materials: fluorinated polyimide (display substrates), photoresist (chip lithography), high-purity hydrogen fluoride (etching gas). No one had prepared — it was all too sudden. At the time, everyone thought Korea had no choice but to beg Japan or appeal to the US.

03
Counterattack
Desperate counterattack: domestic substitution + permanent reversal
Korea not only didn't kneel — it pulled through and secured stable supply of all three materials on its own. The domestic substitutes weren't as good: purity dropped from 12N to 10N, costs were higher — but "the old supermarket stopped selling, so everyone had to go to the new one." Samsung's people joined the R&D effort, and domestic material quality improved. Japanese companies, while supporting the government, also invested in plants in China and Europe to keep supplying Korea — protecting their market share. By late 2020, Japan wanted to restore supply — Korea's response: permanent substitution, we don't want it anymore.

04
Lessons
Goldman Sachs Analysts' Warning — why embargo strategies are doomed to fail
The capitalist world never has a shortage of production capacity — it always has a shortage of purchasing power. Goldman Sachs analysts made three points: ① Buyer's market — the US didn't beat Japan's chips with tech blockades, it used non-purchasing (imposing 100% anti-dumping tariffs + forcing yen appreciation). ② Restricting imports = subsidizing domestic alternatives. ③ Never underestimate capitalists' determination to "guard their food" — when law conflicts with profit, capitalists will pay any price to break the law.

05
Challenges
The challenge China faces: chip blockade and building consensus
Trump not only exposed the truth — he slapped China's economists in the face. The blockade ironically forged a national consensus: "We must develop our own chips." Korea turned crisis into opportunity, and so can China: a few years ago, semiconductor majors were less sought-after than graphic design; now they're red-hot. The challenge is enormous but the opportunity is even bigger — if chips break through upward, the technology tree climbs further, the equilibrium level rises, and countless opportunities emerge upstream and downstream.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Japan couldn't choke South Korea's throat because the blockader underestimated the power of a buyer's market. Korea not only pulled through but permanently replaced Japanese materials — Japanese corporate profits plunged. The lesson for China: China is the largest chip buyer ($312B annual imports) — blockades only accelerate domestic substitution. But the challenge is: if housing prices keep skyrocketing, research funding risks being eroded by arbitrage mentality. Opportunity and challenge coexist — Korea proved that determined people always find a way.
