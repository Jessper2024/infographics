Chapter 8 "The Terrifying Chen Youliang"

Regicide, Usurpation, and Naval Dominance — Chen Youliang Becomes Zhu Yuanzhang's Greatest Rival

Situation: After Zhu Yuanzhang captured Ying Tian, three powers dominated the Yangtze: Zhu at Ying Tian, Zhang Shicheng at Pingjiang, and Xu Shouhui/Chen Youliang upstream. Conflict: Chen Youliang murdered Xu Shouhui and seized the Tianwan state, leading 100,000 naval forces downriver. Question: How could Zhu defeat an enemy with superior forces and navy? Answer: Strategic patience to avoid direct clash, Liu Ji's counsel for decisive battle at Longwan, using cunning ambush to overcome military inferiority.

01
Rise to Power
Heart Black Hands Cruel — Chen Youliang's Path
Chen Youliang climbed through betrayal: First he killed Chancellor Ni Wenjun (who had mentored him), then general Zhao Pusheng (who respected him deeply), and finally murdered his lord Xu Shouhui with an iron hammer at Wutong Temple. Each betrayal raised his power but cost him the one thing no army can win without — the hearts of men. His success philosophy had only eight characters: heart black, hands cruel, bold and reckless.

02
Usurpation
Rainy Night at Wutong Temple — Regicide
On June 16, 1360, in a thunderstorm: Chen Youliang invited Xu Shouhui to worship at Wutong Temple in Caishi. As Xu praised Chen's coming victory, Chen coldly replied: "Unfortunately you will not see that day." When Xu begged to live as a commoner, Chen asked: "How did you survive in this chaotic world?" Seconds later, guards shattered Xu Shouhui's skull with an iron hammer. Chen then declared himself emperor, naming his state Han with the paradoxical era name "Great Righteousness."

03
General's Arrival
Madman Chang Yuchun — Born Vanguard
Chang Yuchun opened with a bold demand: Arriving at Zhu's camp as a starving refugee, he declared "I came to be vanguard — give me the seal." He proved himself at Caishi, leaping from boat to shore with a long spear, cutting down enemies to create a beachhead. He led every charge thereafter. But he had a fatal flaw: he loved killing prisoners — burying 3,000 captives alive just to provoke Chen Youliang. This cruelty would nearly bring disaster.

04
Three-Way Struggle
Zhang Shicheng vs Zhu Yuanzhang — Hundred Battles
Before Chen Youliang seized power, Zhang Shicheng was the most active enemy: From the moment Zhu captured Ying Tian in 1356, they fought over a hundred engagements. Zhu tried diplomacy — Zhang replied: "Go back where you came from, I have sworn to destroy you." At Longtan, Xu Da and Chang Yuchun crushed Zhang's forces. Chang later took Changzhou, fighting with three arrows in his chest. Zhang had more troops but suffered devastating defeats — proving "a thousand horses are easy to find, but one great general is not."

05
Countdown to War
Undercurrents Before the Longwan Battle
After Chang Yuchun buried 3,000 prisoners alive, he left survivors with a message: "I am Chang Yuchun, I defeated you!" This infuriated Chen Youliang. In 1360, he led 100,000 naval forces downriver with legendary ships — "Mixing River Dragon," "Blocking River," "Toppling Mountain." Meanwhile, Zhu gained the crucial strategist Liu Ji (Liu Bowen), an astronomy master who would turn the tide at Longwan.

🔑 Core Conclusion
Chen Youliang was Zhu Yuanzhang's most terrifying rival — stronger forces, invincible navy, greater ambition. But violence and betrayal cannot win hearts: killing Ni Wenjun, Zhao Pusheng, and Xu Shouhui shattered his army's morale; burying 3,000 prisoners alive enraged the entire Yangtze region. Zhu used strategic patience to avoid direct clash, and cunning ambush at Longwan to overcome military inferiority. History proves: those who win battles are many, but those who win hearts are the ones who take the world.
