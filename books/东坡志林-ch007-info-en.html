Dongpo's Forest of Jottings · Chapter 7「The Human Spectrum — Medicine, Divination, and Social Portraits」

SCQA · 情境 → 冲突 → 问题 → 回答 Su Shi was not merely a man of letters, a bureaucrat, and a gourmand — he was also a keen observer of society. From the moral character of physicians to the absurdity of divination, from a poor scholar who didn't recognize unwrapped coins to a southern merchant returning home in silks, from Lady Jia's five fatal flaws that doomed the Jin dynasty to the bandit who spared a wine-seller — how did a literatus who'd seen it all glimpse the subtleties of human nature in everyday affairs? With a single brush, Su Shi painted a panorama of Song society: doctors, fortune-tellers, merchants, craftsmen, women, bandits, foreign emissaries — every stratum gained presence and dignity under his pen.

This chapter collects Su Shi's essays on medicine (The Physician, On the Words of Doctor He, A Conversation with Ouyang Xiu, Canliao Seeks Treatment, Wang Yuanlong's Leprosy Cure, The Art of Longevity, Shan Xiang and Sun Zhao), divination (A Monk Reads Ouyang's Face, The Zhenjun Oracle, On Faith and Wisdom, I Ching Divination, Fei Xiaoxian's Cracked Shadows, Tianxin Exorcism Spell, Five Planets at Eastern Well), the four social classes (On Poor Scholars, The Merchant of Liang, The Artisan of Liang), women and concubines (The Five Flaws of Lady Jia, Granny Jia Recommends Changchao, Shi Chong's Maid), bandits (The Bandit Who Spared Scholar Xing's Wine, The Gentleman on the Beam), and foreign peoples (Cao Wei on Wang Yuanhao, Goryeo, The Goryeo Case). Twenty-five essays spanning five themes — a vivid cross-section of Song dynasty society.

01
Portraits of Healers
From Qiu Ding to Shan Xiang——Su Shi's Gallery of Physicians
Su Shi had a distinctive eye for medical practitioners. The contemporary physician Qiu Ding was "the best of his time at treating abscesses," but his "character was not entirely pure, and the world feared him." Zhang Junyi, by contrast, was "calm and gentle, devoted solely to saving lives, far surpassing Ding" — beyond technical skill, medical ethics mattered more. Pang Anchang of Qizhou was a skilled physician who was deaf — "to converse, one had to write to be understood." Su Shi formed an exquisite complement with him: "I use my hand as mouth, you use your eye as ear." Disability between them was not obstacle but wondrous symmetry. Ouyang Xiu recounted a case of psychological therapy: a patient fell ill from fright during a storm at sea; the physician scraped powder from the sweat-stained tiller of an aged boat, mixed it with herbs, and the patient recovered — a case of suggestive therapy, which Su Shi deemed rational: "using intent to apply medicine, many cases are like this." And on the art of longevity, Su Shi maintained astonishing clarity — Zhao Baoyi, Xu Deng, Zhang Yuanmeng, all claimed to be near a hundred, "yet they all died, no different from ordinary people" — he satirized worldly folk who rushed to worship longevity masters, "yet in the end they too died." He did not deny the value of health cultivation, but refused the myth of immortality.
I use my hand as mouth, you use your eye as ear — are we not extraordinary men?
— Su Shi, laughing with the deaf physician Pang Anchang
02
Divination & Fate
Oracles, Hexagrams, and Celestial Signs——A Rational Bystander in the World of Divination
When Ouyang Xiu was young, a monk read his face: "Ears whiter than face — fame fills all under heaven; lips not touching teeth — slandered without cause." The words proved true. In his troubled times, Su Shi visited the Tianqing Temple to consult a spirit lot for his remaining life's fortune — the lot read: "The Dao takes sincerity as its bond, the law takes wisdom as its foundation. If the two do not separate, one's lifespan cannot be extended." His attitude toward this was "I bow my head, cleanse my heart, return my fate to true stillness" — he did not superstitiously believe the lot, but used it as a whetstone. He consulted the I Ching divining his brother Ziyou's whereabouts — receiving the Huan hexagram, third line, the judgment reading "using a rescued horse, strong and auspicious" — in the anxiety of long without his brother's letters, divination was psychological comfort. And in "Discerning Five Planets at Eastern Well," Su Shi displayed his astronomical knowledge: "Five planets gathering at Eastern Well" was recorded in history as an auspicious omen, but Su Shi, analyzing solar and lunar positions ("in the tenth month, the sun is in Ji and Wei"), pointed out that Venus and Mercury "always stay near the sun and do not travel far" — thus they could not simultaneously appear at Eastern Well. This was rational criticism based on astronomical fact.

03
Three Livelihoods
Poor Scholar, Liang Merchant, Liang Artisan——Psychological Sketches of Three Social Roles
Su Shi's observations across social strata were remarkably incisive. "On Poor Scholars" tells of a student who entered a government treasury and didn't recognize the coins — "I knew they were coins, but I was puzzled that they weren't wrapped in paper." Su Shi connected this to Tao Yuanming's "Returning Home" — "children fill the rooms, but jars hold no stored grain" — confirming the legend's truth. The poor scholar's pedantry and Tao's contented poverty form a subtle contrast. "The Merchant of Liang" is a brilliant tale of transformation: a man from Liang traded in the south for seven years, then returned home, "eating apricot seeds and seaweed, breathing the elegance of mountains and streams... having abraded away his tumors, looking as smooth as a grub" — from repulsive to handsome. But when he "returned weary of travel, gazing at his reflection with daily self-satisfaction," and looked down on his hometown wife, she mocked him: "When you went south, I thought nothing strange of it; now that you've returned, I think nothing glorious of it." — The spiritual alienation of the nouveau riche merchant is captured by Su Shi with penetrating depth. "The Artisan of Liang" records a craftsman's obsession with alchemical furnaces — working the furnace daily, "now yellow, now white, unceasingly generating" — Su Shi implies this obsession is no different from the scholar's pursuit of fame.

04
Women & Bandits
Lady Jia's Five Flaws & Scholar Xing's Wine——Observations on Women and Moral Undercurrents
Emperor Wu of Jin wanted to choose a wife for the crown prince. Wei Guan spoke bluntly: "The Jia girl has five fatal flaws: dark, short, jealous, and childless." But the ministers praised the Jia family, and Emperor Wu married her — "and thereby destroyed the Jin dynasty." Su Shi sighed: a woman's beauty or ugliness, everyone can see — and "loving one's son, wanting to find him a wife who will bear many children, is human nature" — so why was the obvious flaw ignored? Because political interests blinded common-sense judgment. The tale of Granny Jia recommending Changchao exposes the patronage chain between the harem and court. And the story of Shi Chong's maid reveals human nature through a single detail — Wang Dun, using the lavatory at Shi Chong's mansion, changed from old to new clothes "without a trace of embarrassment"; the lavatory maid judged: "This guest will certainly become a rebel." Later, Wang Dun indeed rebelled. "This maid could read people, yet Shi Chong assigned her to lavatory duty — he knew nothing of recognizing talent." Someone with the gift of insight was placed in the humblest position. Among the bandit tales, "The Bandit Who Spared Scholar Xing's Wine" is the warmest: the old Jinling scholar Xing Sishun sold wine in Jiangzhou — everyone, wise and foolish alike, liked him. A river bandit, caught, claimed that the wine stolen from Scholar Xing had been taken; officials found Xing Sishun, who not only didn't press charges but pleaded for the bandit. The power of morality lies not in punishment but in transformation.

05
Foreign Affairs
Cao Wei Predicts the Western Xia Threat——Geopolitical Wisdom in Three Essays on Foreign Peoples
During the Tiansheng era, Cao Wei governed Dingzhou as military commissioner. He told Vice Commissioner Wang Zong: "Your physiognomy is most noble — you shall become Commissioner of Military Affairs." He then analyzed the Western Xia threat: "I hear that Deming annually sends men to trade sheep and horses at the border, rewarding or punishing based on quantities gained — at times even killing for this. His son Yuanhao, at age thirteen, remonstrated: 'Our nation was founded on sheep and horses; now we instead enrich the Central Plains — what we obtain are all frivolous goods like tea and colored silk, precisely what makes our people arrogant and lazy. Better to distribute our gains to our subordinates — even if they do not submit, they will not harbor disloyalty.' Deming followed this advice. I heard this and thought it extraordinary — had someone sketch his portrait — truly magnificent. If Deming dies, this son will surely become a threat to China. You shall be Commissioner of Military Affairs — why not prepare early?" Cao Wei's prediction was fully vindicated when Yuanhao declared himself emperor and the Song-Xia war erupted — Su Shi recorded this in admiration of a brilliant military strategist's strategic foresight. "Goryeo" uses the metaphor of "toying with a monkey" — "people speak of toying with a monkey, not knowing they are being toyed with by the monkey" — satirizing the mutual manipulation in diplomacy and the gains and losses of Song policy toward Goryeo. "The Goryeo Case" records the inside story of how Song-Goryeo relations began — originating when Zhang Chengyi, in a Khitan tent, happened to meet a Goryeo envoy who privately expressed admiration for China; upon return, he memorialized this, thus opening diplomatic intercourse. These three records display Su Shi's deep concern for national security.
You shall be Commissioner of Military Affairs — why not prepare early? — Cao Wei foresaw Yuanhao would surely become a threat to China
— Su Shi, "Cao Wei on Wang Yuanhao as a Threat to China"
KEY INSIGHT
What is most moving about Su Shi's observations of the human spectrum is that his moral judgments never depend on social status — a physician can surpass his technical skill through character, a poor scholar can become a laughingstock through pedantry, a maid can possess the insight to read a man's ambition, a bandit can preserve a thread of humanity for a jar of wine. He saw charm in the scholar's foolishness, sadness in the merchant's arrogance, admiration in Cao Wei's foresight — this is a complete portrait of Song society, and Su Shi was its faithful recorder, without prejudice, without filters.
