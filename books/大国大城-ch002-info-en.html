Great State Needs Big CityCh.1 · The Europeanization of China's Economy

Unified Market · Unified Currency · Regional Gap · Public Interest The Eurozone crisis exposed a deep contradiction: market segmentation + unified currency = disaster. This same logic applies to China's provinces — when regional labor productivity gaps persist and population mobility is blocked, a unified great nation risks "Europeanization."

This chapter uses the Eurozone debt crisis as a cautionary tale for China. The author argues that China's greatest risk is not "Latin Americanization" but "Europeanization" — specifically, "Eurozone-ization." The Eurozone's predicament stems from the conflict between market segmentation and a single currency: without sufficient population mobility, regional labor productivity cannot converge; when weaker countries lose independent monetary and exchange rate tools, debt crises become inevitable. Mapping this logic to China's provincial level — if labor mobility remains blocked and regional income gaps persist — a unified fiscal and monetary China may face structural challenges similar to the Eurozone. The chapter then analyzes the benefits of large-country economies of scale and the endogenous challenges of maintaining a unified market.

01
Eurozone Crisis
The Eurozone Trap: Market Segmentation + Unified Currency = Disaster
Rich Eurozone countries face a moral dilemma: bail out Greece or let it leave? If Greece exits, a sharp devaluation would boost exports and tourism. But if Greece leaves, who's next — Spain? Ultimately, the unified European market faces disintegration. Two root causes: market segmentation — even within the EU, multiple languages and cultures prevent full labor mobility, so regional labor productivity cannot converge; and unified currency — member states lose independent monetary policy and exchange rate tools, unable to devalue in response to recession. Wage rigidity compounds the problem: labor productivity adjustments cannot be achieved through flexible wages, so debt accumulation fills the gap.

02
China Analogy
From the Eurozone to China's Provinces: Same Logic, Different Scale
What is an inter-country problem for Europe is an inter-province problem for China. The Eurozone lesson for China: in a unified currency area, free population mobility is essential — or regional labor productivity convergence becomes impossible. Less-developed regions with low productivity, constrained outmigration, rigid welfare spending, and inflexible wages can only borrow to fill the gap. Though China's provinces share a single currency (not a problem), they face the same structural contradiction: limited population mobility → persistent regional gaps → administrative resource allocation distorting markets → efficiency loss.

03
Large Country Advantage
Why Is the Dream of a Great Nation So Compelling? — Four Returns to Scale
From Qin Shihuang to today, the appeal of a large nation has never faded. Historically: tax maximization, risk diversification, defense cost-sharing. In the modern era, the benefits are even greater. Strategic industries — developing large passenger aircraft requires enormous markets; only the US, Europe, and China qualify. Innovation — China's market scale sustains countless niche innovations, from Taobao to WeChat. Public goods — satellite navigation systems, Gulf of Aden escort missions, international medical aid. Modern services — finance, healthcare, education, and cultural industries all need scale to attract top global talent.

04
Governance Challenge
The Great Nation's Governance Challenge: 2,000 Years of Centralization vs. Decentralization
A large nation isn't always better — scale benefits must be weighed against governance costs. After unifying China, Qin Shihuang did two critical things: standardized writing (preventing Beijing dialect and Minnan from becoming separate languages) and built an imperial highway-and-postal relay system. For two millennia, successive dynasties oscillated between centralization and decentralization: feudal enfeoffment threatened unity, while the commandery system centralized appointment power. Today, provincial officials are still appointed by the central government. But governing a nation of 1.4 billion people, 56 ethnic groups, and enormous regional diversity carries high costs — inter-ethnic transaction costs can rival international trade costs.
👑

Centralization (Commandery System)

Local officials appointed by the center, power concentrated, unity preserved. But information asymmetry between center and periphery leads to one-size-fits-all policy errors.

🔱

Decentralization (Feudal System)

Local autonomy, strong adaptability. But powerful regional lords threaten unity — historical experience shows excessive decentralization leads to dynastic collapse.

05
The Way Forward
Free Migration: The Only Path to Balance in a Unified Large Country
The Eurozone lesson points to a clear conclusion: in a large country with unified currency/fiscal policy, free population mobility is a necessity, not a luxury. Only free labor mobility can equalize real incomes across regions (after accounting for cost-of-living differences). International evidence shows that every country that achieved regional balance did so through free labor mobility; no country that pursued balance through administrative resource allocation succeeded. For China, the warning is clear: without removing institutional barriers to population movement, structural inter-provincial contradictions will deepen, potentially leading to a "unity vs. efficiency cannot both be achieved" trap similar to the Eurozone's.

🔑 Key Conclusion
China's greatest risk is not "Latin Americanization" but "Europeanization" (Eurozone-ization). The Eurozone's predicament stems from the inherent conflict between market segmentation and a unified currency — when people cannot move freely, a unified currency becomes a disaster amplifier. China, a unified economy larger than the entire Eurozone, faces the same structural challenge. The solution is not to restrict population mobility and allocate resources administratively, but to remove mobility barriers and let market forces guide people to high-productivity regions, achieving per capita income equalization through movement. A large country's economies of scale are enormous, but they require free factor mobility as a prerequisite.
