The Elements of Style · Ch.12 "Put Statements in Positive Form"

Readers are dissatisfied with being told what is not; they wish to be told what is — not is for denial, not evasion Rule 11 embodies one of Strunk's most penetrating insights into the psychology of language. He reveals a law of writing: readers are naturally dissatisfied with being told what something is not — they hunger to know what it is. Not should express denial or antithesis, never serve as a means of evasion. Three contrast examples — from "he was not very often on time" to "he usually came late" — cleanly demonstrate that positive statements possess far greater force than negative ones. Strunk goes so far as to observe that even negative meaning is best expressed in positive form.

Rule 11 is a deep observation about language psychology, not a simple grammar rule. Strunk builds five progressive layers: ① The correct use of not — only for denial and antithesis; never as a refuge for evasive, ambiguous expression. ② Three classic contrasts — from "He was not very often on time" → "He usually came late" to the rewriting of The Taming of the Shrew critique from vague negation to crisp affirmation. ③ The core psychological insight — readers, consciously or unconsciously, are dissatisfied with being told what is not; they wish to be told what is. ④ A concrete conversion table — five pairs of negative→positive vocabulary transformations (not honest→dishonest, did not remember→forgot). ⑤ The special power of negative-positive antithesis — "Not charity, but simple justice" is more forceful than pure affirmation because contrast creates tension.

01
Core Principle
Not is a tool for denial, not an excuse for evasion — readers want to know "what is"
Make definite assertions. Avoid tame, colorless, hesitating, non-committal language. Strunk draws a clear boundary for not: use it to express denial or antithesis, never as a means of evasion. When a writer writes "not important" instead of "trifling," or "did not remember" instead of "forgot," he is presenting the reader with a fill-in-the-blank exercise rather than a complete statement. The reader must mentally supply what lies behind "what it is not" — this increases cognitive load and reduces expressive efficiency. Consciously or unconsciously, the reader is dissatisfied with being told only what is not; he wishes to be told what is.

02
Three Contrasts
Three classic rewrites from vagueness to affirmation: the expressive power released by positive form
Strunk reveals the massive advantage of positive form through three contrasts. ① "He was not very often on time" → "He usually came late": a negative, vague statement requiring reverse inference from the reader versus a positive, definite statement that lands in one step. ② "He did not think that studying Latin was much use" → "He thought the study of Latin useless": the former circles through a double-negative detour, the latter gives the conclusion directly. ③ The most brilliant case — the critique of The Taming of the Shrew is rewritten from a paragraph of vague negation ("Shakespeare does not portray Katharine as a very admirable character...") into three affirmative sentences: "Katharine is disagreeable, Bianca insignificant." This case proves: positive form is not just a language technique but an attitude of mind — the writer must know definitively what he wants to say.

✅

He usually came late. / He thought the study of Latin useless. Lands in one step, no cognitive burden on reader.

03
Conversion Table
The negative-to-positive vocabulary replacement table: express even negative ideas in positive words
Strunk provides five direct negative→positive conversions: not honest→dishonest, not important→trifling, did not remember→forgot, did not pay any attention to→ignored, did not have much confidence in→distrusted. This table reveals a startling fact: virtually every "not+X" expression in English has a corresponding positive word — and that positive word is always briefer and more forceful. Strunk's advice: unless you are deliberately setting up a "negation versus affirmation" contrast effect, always use the positive. Because every time you write "not honest," you make the reader read one extra word and perform one extra "reverse decoding" step.

04
Special Technique
The power of negative-positive contrast: "Not charity, but simple justice" — contrast creates tension
Negation is not bad in all cases. When negation forms a clear contrast with affirmation, the expressive effect is actually stronger. Strunk notes: "Not charity, but simple justice" is more forceful than pure "simple justice." Likewise, "Not that I loved Caesar less, but Rome the more" — this negation→affirmation contrast structure creates dramatic linguistic tension. This is the correct use of not: not evasion, not vagueness, but establishing a clear antithesis. When you say "not A," the reader needs to know exactly what you are excluding, and "but B" immediately provides the positive answer. Additionally, negative words other than not (never, etc.) are usually stronger — "The sun never sets upon the British flag" carries more force than "The sun does not set."
❌

not for evasion (wrong)

"He was not very often on time." — not conceals the writer's true meaning. The reader doesn't know when he actually arrived.

✅

not for contrast (right)

"Not charity, but simple justice." — not explicitly excludes one thing, immediately gives the positive answer. Contrast creates tension.

05
Self-Checklist
A writing self-check: five questions to root out your dependence on negation
Transform Rule 11 into an actionable writing habit. ① Does my sentence contain "not"? → If yes, check: is it denial/antithesis, or evasion/vagueness? ② If evasion, what is the positive version? → Write it directly. ③ Can I replace "not+X" with one positive word? → Consult the conversion table. ④ Does my negation serve a deliberate contrast? → If yes, keep it and ensure the "but" portion is clear and forceful. ⑤ Would a stronger negative word (never, etc.) create more force than not? → Consider upgrading for dramatic tension. These five questions form a rapid filtering system to help you break free from the habit of "not daring to say what is."

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
Rule 11 is not a ban on "not" — it is a lesson in expressive courage. Strunk's core insight is psychological: when a reader encounters "he was not very often on time," their response is not to accept this information but to ask "so when did he arrive?" — your not becomes the reader's fill-in-the-blank exercise. The solution is stunningly simple: say directly "he usually came late." When you replace "did not pay any attention to" with "ignored," or "did not remember" with "forgot," every word substitution is a "vague→definite" mental leap. The sole exception — and the most elegant part of this rule — is when you deliberately use "not A, but B" to create contrastive tension. In that case, negation serves affirmation, rather than being an excuse for avoiding it.
