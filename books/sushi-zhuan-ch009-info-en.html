Su Shi Biography · Ch9 "Under Brilliant Lamps and Flying Canopies, Dwelling in the Capital's Splendor"

Mizhou elders lined the roads welcoming him, Dengzhou's mirage vanished in an instant, and then came the capital's summons under brilliant lights.

Core QuestionReturning to official life, how did Su Shi view fame and reclusion? How had his state of mind changed from before his exile?

Core AnswerIn 1085, Emperor Shenzong died and Empress Dowager Gao took the regency, restoring the old faction. Su Shi was first transferred to Ruzhou, then appointed Prefect of Dengzhou — only to be recalled to the capital as Vice Director of the Ministry of Rites after merely five days in office. En route, revisiting Mizhou after ten years, elders and children lined the roads to welcome him; infants he had saved from famine were now sturdy youths. Atop Penglai Pavilion at Dengzhou, he gazed upon a sea like a mirror and a mirage that came and went in an instant. Before departing for the capital, he submitted two memorials pleading the people's cause — proof that four years at Huangzhou had not eroded his spirit of service.

1
Turning Point

Career Revival: Old Faction Rises, Exile Ends

"Mountains doubled, waters winding — no path in sight; willows dark, flowers bright — another village appears." Six years of idle exile had ended, the official road stretched smooth and open once more — yet Su Shi felt no great excitement or joy, only boundless reluctance and melancholy.

In "Butterflies Lingering over Flowers" he wrote: "Cloud and water wind along the streamside path; layered green hills encircle the stream flowing east. Moonlight whitens the sandbar, rousing roosting egrets — not a speck of dust finds its way here." After nearly thirty years of official storms, the Crow Terrace Poetry Trial's brush with death, and Huangzhou's Zen meditation and return to simplicity, youthful competitiveness and desire for fame had all faded to nothing.

In late July 1085, he set out for Dengzhou. En route, he crossed the Huai River for the seventh time. As early as 1079 he had sighed: "Thank goodness for the long Huai waters — three crossings in ten years. Fame and achievement are truly done; plans for retirement are distant too." Now he felt even more tired and indifferent — this trip was merely heading to a seaside town to pass his declining years.

2
Revisiting Old Haunts

Mizhou Elders: A Ten-Year Welcome on Both Sides

Entering Mizhou after a decade's absence, Su Shi was stirred: "I am the former governor of Jiaoxi." But looking up at Mount Chang, towering and aloof like a proud old man showing no sign of recognition, the poet felt a stab of melancholy. Yet if the hills were heartless, the people were not.

The elders of Mizhou had not forgotten the governor who had blessed their land. Everywhere he passed, people helped the elderly and carried the young, lining both sides of the road, vying to offer wine and birthday wishes. They were delighted to see that although Su Shi was no longer in his prime, his spirit remained vigorous. The thousands of abandoned infants who had survived the great famine had now grown into sturdy youths — they stared with wide, curious eyes at the benefactor they had heard about daily since childhood.

The current prefect Huo Xiang hosted a banquet for Su Shi on the Transcendence Terrace. Revisiting the terrace, Su Shi found the inscriptions of his "Record of the Transcendence Terrace" and other writings still perfectly preserved. Moved by old traces, he earnestly advised Huo Xiang to develop irrigation works. Later, at Jinshan's Miaogao Terrace under the mid-autumn moon, he asked the famous singer Yuan Tao to perform "Prelude to Water Melody" — "How often does the bright moon appear?" soared to the clouds. Su Shi danced beneath the moon: "This is the realm of immortals!"

3
Pleading for the People

Five Days in Dengzhou: Two Memorials for the People

On October 15, when Su Shi arrived at Dengzhou, the shore was already crowded with welcomers. Several elderly men stepped forward: "Sir Su, you govern with love for the people — can you be like Prefect Ma?" Ma Mo had been a celebrated Dengzhou governor. Su Shi solemnly accepted their wine: "I shall strive my utmost and not betray your hopes."

Immediately upon taking office, Su Shi launched into extensive social investigation, sharply identifying two major abuses in military affairs and taxation. But just as he prepared to implement reforms, the court summoned him back to the capital as Vice Director of the Ministry of Rites. His tenure as Dengzhou prefect lasted merely five days! Yet on his journey back to the capital, he still submitted two memorials: one on strengthening Dengzhou's naval defenses, and another petitioning to abolish the salt monopoly and reduce the people's burden.

These two memorials perfectly embody Su Shi's undiminished spirit of public service after four years of tempering at Huangzhou — with only five days in office, he still made time to accomplish something real for the people. This governing attitude of "five-day prefect, two memorials" testifies more powerfully to his character than any lengthy bureaucratic pronouncement ever could.

4
Illusion and Reality

Penglai Mirage: The Fleeting Vision of Immortal Isles

After completing the administrative handover, Su Shi used his few remaining leisure days before returning to the capital to explore Dengzhou's scenic beauty. Ascending Penglai Pavilion on Cinnabar Cliff, he gazed upon a limitless expanse — five islands emerging and vanishing among the waves, ethereal and indistinct, truly resembling the dwellings of immortals.

"Atop Penglai Pavilion at Dengzhou, gazing at the sea like a mirror's surface, meeting the sky at the horizon." But the most celebrated sight was the Dengzhou mirage: "In the east, cloud-sea empties into emptiness; immortals appear and vanish in translucent space. The swaying floating world generates myriad forms — could there really be shell towers hiding pearl palaces?" On clear spring and summer days, multicolored clouds transformed into palaces and towers, carriages and canopies, figures in flowing robes — gone in an instant. Su Shi believed these were all illusions, not the dwellings of sea gods — yet the mirage's unreal beauty kindled profound reflection.

The mirage that came and went in a flash reminded Su Shi of his Red Cliff meditation — "like mayflies between heaven and earth, a single grain in the vast sea" — and of the ultimate awakening that "life is but a dream." The illusory beauty of Penglai's immortal isles and mirage perfectly mirrored his current attitude toward fame and fortune: all ostentation is like a mirage; only "the cool breeze on the river" and "the bright moon among the mountains" are truly attainable.

5
Inner Detachment

Homeward Heart: Clarity Beneath the Capital's Brilliant Lamps

"Wine cup never empty, a hundred acres of fields — returning home to share leisure's delight." Beneath the capital's brilliant lamps and flying canopies, what Su Shi yearned for most was still buying farmland in Changzhou and retiring to the rivers and lakes. The competitive drive and hunger for fame of his youth had all faded to nothing.

En route to Dengzhou, passing through Runzhou during the Mid-Autumn Festival, he climbed Jinshan's Miaogao Terrace with friends to admire the moon and asked Yuan Tao to sing "Prelude to Water Melody." The melody of "How often does the bright moon appear? Wine cup in hand, I ask the blue sky" rose clear to the clouds. When the song ended, Su Shi danced beneath the moon — "This is the realm of immortals!" In this moment, he had found a truer paradise than "dwelling in the capital's splendor": it was the present instant where art and nature fused.

From the Crow Terrace Poetry Trial in 1079 to returning to the capital in 1085, six full years of exile had changed Su Shi profoundly. He was no longer the sharp-edged young prodigy but an enlightened one who could see "Hell and Heaven as equal." The capital's brilliant lamps still shone brightly, but Su Shi's heart had already flown toward the distant horizon where "a little boat from now on departs, to rivers and seas entrusting the rest of life." He knew: his true home was not in the court but on the rivers and lakes; his true achievement was not in official rank but in poetry and prose.

💡
Key Takeaway

The end of six years' exile brought not ecstasy but detachment — this was the most precious gift Huangzhou's four years had given Su Shi. The Mizhou elders lining the roads confirmed his unchanging commitment to governing with love for the people. The two memorials after merely five days in Dengzhou proved that "in old age, the rivers and lakes are never lost" — his essential character of public service remained intact. The Penglai mirage's fleeting beauty mirrored his thorough disillusionment with fame's ostentation. Returning beneath the capital's brilliant lamps, Su Shi was a transformed man: no longer swayed by officialdom's honor and disgrace, he found the true immortal realm in the song of "How often does the bright moon appear?" and in dancing beneath moonlight. As he had written ten years earlier on Mizhou's Transcendence Terrace: "All things have something worth observing. If there is something worth observing, there is something worth enjoying" — this was the ultimate wisdom forged through four years at Huangzhou.
