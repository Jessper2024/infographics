Fupan+: Turning Experience into Capability · Ch4 "Team Review"

SCQA · Core Insight Fupan's mechanism is simple, but effective team review is anything but easy — the U.S. Army made it Standard Operating Procedure; Lenovo, Huawei, and BP have practiced it for decades. Why? Because professional facilitation is the key to team fupan success. This chapter reveals the Army's AAR four-phase method, the two schools of review, the facilitator's 10 responsibilities, and the proprietary "Three-Stage, Nine-Step Method™."

Team fupan's core isn't about "knowing" — it's about "facilitating." The quality of facilitation determines the quality of the review. Starting from U.S. Army AAR practice, this chapter breaks down formal vs. informal review processes (Plan → Prepare → Execute → Follow-up). Integrating Lenovo and BP practices, it presents the four-phase micro-process (Review & Assess → Analyze & Reflect → Extract & Distill → Transform & Apply), and contrasts the Process Reconstruction school versus the Results-Oriented school. It focuses on how facilitators distinguish "process" from "content," fulfill 10 core duties, and master the proprietary "Three-Stage, Nine-Step Method" (Thorough Preparation → Effective Facilitation → Follow Through).

U.S. Army Practice
The U.S. Army AAR: The World's Most Mature Team Review System
An AAR is a professional discussion of an event focused on performance, where soldiers discover for themselves what happened, why it happened, and how to improve: The Army makes it Standard Operating Procedure for all commanders, divided into formal AAR (planned 6–8 weeks ahead, with OC observers/controllers) and informal AAR (conducted immediately on-site), spanning four phases: Plan → Prepare → Execute → Follow-up.

Operating Method
The Four Phases of Team Fupan: From Review to Transformation
Based on the "Way of Fupan," team review divides into four phases: ① Review & Assess — compare objectives vs. results to identify strengths and gaps, the key is being factual and reaching team consensus; ② Analyze & Reflect — use 5 Whys, fishbone diagrams to find root causes; ③ Extract & Distill — like forging a sword requires mold, materials, craftsman, and heat, knowledge extraction requires five elements; ④ Transform & Apply — Start doing, Continue doing, Stop doing, assigned to owners with deadlines.

03
Two Schools
Process Reconstruction vs. Results-Oriented: Different Paths, Same Destination
Two approaches for the assessment phase: Process Reconstruction (Army/BP) replays key events chronologically — builds shared understanding but can be lengthy; Results-Oriented (Lenovo/Google) directly lists strengths and gaps against goals — efficient and focused but newcomers may lack context. Both converge on the same outcome — the choice depends on team familiarity and review purpose.
📋

Process Reconstruction (Army · BP)

Replay key phases chronologically → identify deep-dive topics. Pros: simple, clear trail, builds understanding. Cons: may become a rambling narrative.

🎯

Results-Oriented (Lenovo · Google)

List strengths/gaps directly against goals → replay process only when needed. Pros: simple, efficient, stays focused. Cons: newcomers may lack shared context.

04
Facilitator
The Fupan Facilitator: Distinguishing "Process" from "Content" Is Key
Facilitation literally means "to make easier": The facilitator's ultimate goal is to encourage participation, improve interaction, and boost team effectiveness. The core skill is separating "process" (how to converse, methods, atmosphere) from "content" (what to discuss, the topic itself). The facilitator manages process and stays neutral, leaving content to all participants. However, a facilitator totally ignorant of the content may miss opportunities for deep inquiry.

05
Three-Stage Nine-Step
The Three-Stage, Nine-Step Method™: A Complete Framework for Team Fupan Facilitation
Qiu Zhaoliang's proprietary team fupan facilitation system: Stage 1 "Thorough Preparation" — plan the approach, organize the meeting, prepare in advance; Stage 2 "Effective Facilitation" — open with rules, sequential discussion with deep dives (don't skip the four steps), close with summary and thanks; Stage 3 "Follow Through" — organize and share results, track implementation, evaluate and improve. Ensures fupan doesn't end with "just a meeting."
📐

Stage 1: Thorough Preparation

Plan → Organize (face-to-face preferred, key people must attend) → Prepare (compile materials, logistics)

🎤

Stage 2: Effective Facilitation

Open (rules + warm-up) → Sequential discussion (Review→Reflect→Extract→Transform, with deep dives on key points) → Close

Stage 3: Follow Through · Share review results (micro-courses / KM) → Track implementation → Evaluate & improve (review effectiveness, next directions)
🔑 Key Takeaway
The key to team fupan success isn't the fame of the methodology — it's whether facilitation is done right. The U.S. Army AAR standardizes operations through four phases, but what matters most is the facilitator: distinguishing process from content, staying neutral, letting the team find answers themselves. The Three-Stage, Nine-Step Method provides an actionable framework for organizations: preparation is the foundation, facilitation is the core, and follow-through closes the loop. The quality of fupan hinges on the quality and fluency of facilitation.
