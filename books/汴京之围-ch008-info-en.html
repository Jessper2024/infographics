The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.6 "From Peace to War"

Xuanhe Years 5–7 · From the Zhang Jue Incident to the Outbreak of Song-Jin War A secret imperial edict to recruit a defector — seemingly advantageous — triggered a chain reaction: Zhang Jue was killed, the Ever-Victorious Army lost faith, and the handover of the prefectures north of the mountains fell through. By the time Xu Kangzong's mission returned with intelligence that the Jin were about to invade south, Emperor Huizong had already decreed: "Those who recklessly speak of border affairs shall be exiled three thousand li."

In the fourth month of Xuanhe Year 5, the Song had just acquired Yanjing; by the end of the fifth month, they faced the dilemma of Zhang Jue — this former Liao general occupied the three strategic prefectures of Ying-Ping-Luan and had rebelled against the Jin; territory the Song had long dreamed of but could never obtain through negotiation was suddenly within reach. Emperor Huizong issued a secret edict to recruit him, unleashing a catastrophic chain reaction: the Jin captured the imperial edict during a raid — irrefutable proof of the Song's treaty violation; Zhang Jue was killed and his head, preserved in silver solution, was delivered to the Jin; Guo Yaoshi's Ever-Victorious Army lost morale — "If they wanted my head, what would happen then!" The handover of the prefectures north of the mountains evaporated. Thereafter, Emperor Tianzuo of Liao was captured, Western Xia dared not offer him refuge, Xu Kangzong's mission witnessed massive Jin troop movements southward, and Ma Kuo's final mission to Nianhan was met with a face-to-face declaration of war — "The army has already been mobilized; what is there to tell?" In the eleventh month of Xuanhe Year 7, full-scale Song-Jin war erupted.

01
Fatal Temptation
The Zhang Jue incident: how one secret recruitment edict accelerated the outbreak of Song-Jin war
The three Ying-Ping-Luan prefectures held by Zhang Jue were the strategic territory the Song had long dreamed of but could never obtain through negotiation; when they suddenly fell within reach, Emperor Huizong could not resist the temptation. He secretly ordered Yanjing's deputy commander Zhan Du to "covertly recruit" Zhang Jue while maintaining a public posture of non-involvement. Yet during a raid, the Jin captured the emperor's handwritten edict — irrefutable evidence that the emperor had violated the treaty. Worse still, after months of stalling, the Song was forced to kill Zhang Jue and deliver his head, preserved in silver solution, to the Jin. The entire process was witnessed by the Ever-Victorious Army's officers — Guo Yaoshi said in anguish: "If they wanted my head, what would happen then!" Thereafter the Ever-Victorious Army grew indolent; soldiers began tattooing their faces, and morale collapsed completely.

02
Chain Reaction
Prefectures north of the mountains gained then lost; the only combat-effective army begins to disintegrate
The destruction unleashed by the Zhang Jue incident was systemic. In the Yanjing direction: the large number of Jin refugees he had released could not be repatriated under the treaty, and the Jin used this to apply relentless pressure. In the Shanxi direction: the three prefectures of Shuozhou, Yingzhou, and Weizhou had already submitted to the Song, but the Zhang Jue incident gave the Jin a pretext — Nianhan launched a major offensive and retook all three, along with the two counties of Feihu and Lingqiu (the strategic corridor linking Shanxi and Hebei). In terms of personnel: Tong Guan was replaced by Tan Zhen, then reinstated — the back-and-forth reflected the chaos in Song high command. Most fatally: the Song raised the Righteous Victory Army to counterbalance the Ever-Victorious Army, but this new force proved utterly incapable against the Jin, and over three thousand of them defected — the turncoats told Nianhan everything about the Song's strengths and weaknesses.

03
Mission Observations
Xu Kangzong's mission: eight-thousand-li diplomatic observation, bringing back a war warning that was blocked
In the first month of Xuanhe Year 7, Xu Kangzong led an eighty-person mission departing from Bianjing, traveling over seven months and 4,270 li one way, reaching the Maoli Encampment near modern-day Acheng, Heilongjiang. His travel record is a firsthand source on society during the Song-Jin transition: inside Yanjing, prosperity had returned, but from the fifth stage onward he saw mass famine — corpses with price tags stuck in them being sold as meat, frontier guards dead of starvation at eight or nine out of ten. At the Jin court, he witnessed the early stages of Jin's adoption of Han culture — crude earthen fortifications, an unfinished Qianyuan Hall, a "mountain shed" garden imitating Genyue. On his return journey he discovered the Jin were moving supplies and troops to their southern border on a massive scale; Han residents openly told him: the Jin were about to invade. Yet when he returned to Bianjing, the emperor had already decreed: those who recklessly speak of border affairs shall be exiled three thousand li, not exempted even by general amnesty.

04
End of Remnant Liao
Emperor Tianzuo captured: the last buffer vanishes, and Jin is free to face the Song
In the second month of Xuanhe Year 7, the last Liao emperor Tianzuo was captured by Jin forces while fleeing toward Western Xia. Before this, Tianzuo had been hiding in the Jiashan mountains to the northwest, beyond the Jin's easy reach. Emperor Huizong, accepting Tong Guan's advice, had attempted to win over Tianzuo — offering treatment befitting an imperial brother, a mansion of a thousand rooms, and three hundred female musicians. Tianzuo decided to defect to the Song and set out from Jiashan while Nianhan was away in the northeast reporting to court. But this was a trap set by the Jin — Wushi cut off his retreat route while Nianhan suddenly returned to block his advance. Tianzuo fled to the Xiaohulu tribe and was finally captured by Lousu while attempting to reach Western Xia. Tianzuo's capture meant the complete collapse of remnant Liao power; Jin territory now covered all of northern China. More importantly, the Liao Han officials who had surrendered to the Jin — Liu Yanzong, Shi Liai, and others — became the hardliners pushing for the invasion of the Song: their former lands and estates had been incorporated into Song territory, and only by attacking the Song could they get them back.

05
War Erupts
"The army has already been mobilized; what is there to tell?" — War breaks out in the eleventh month of Xuanhe Year 7
From the ninth to the eleventh month of Xuanhe Year 7, border alerts poured in like snowflakes: Month 9, Day 24, Nianhan's army moved south; Month 10, Day 5, Jin troops held military reviews at Weizhou; Month 10, Day 18, fifteen thousand Jin troops encamped at Pingzhou and Yunzhong; Month 10, Day 21, Jin forces requisitioned grain at Weizhou and Feihu. Over 170 memorials on the Jin incursion piled up, yet no one paid attention — Emperor Huizong was busy preparing for the Southern Suburban Sacrifice on the nineteenth of the eleventh month. On the eleventh of the eleventh month, Tong Guan dispatched Ma Kuo on his final mission to see Nianhan at Yunzhong. Nianhan laughed: "The lands south and north of the mountains are all my family's territory; you will need to cede a few more cities as atonement." The day after Ma Kuo returned to Taiyuan, the Jin envoy Sa Luma arrived with Nianhan's declaration of war. Tong Guan, trembling, asked: "Why didn't you tell me sooner?" The envoy replied: "The army has already been mobilized; what is there to tell?" (Jun yi xing, he gao wei?)

🔑 Key Takeaway
The road from peace to war was not a straight line but a downhill slope paved with "opportunistic decisions." When Emperor Huizong accepted Zhang Jue, he saw the temptation of the three Ying-Ping-Luan prefectures but failed to see how treaty violations would collapse trust; when he sought to win over Emperor Tianzuo, he imagined flanking the Jin from the side but failed to realize that the elimination of remnant Liao would free Jin's hands for a full-scale assault on the Song. Over 170 border alerts were ignored not because intelligence was lacking, but because a system fed on lies had lost the capacity to process negative information. When Nianhan's envoy coldly declared "Jun yi xing, he gao wei" — The army has already been mobilized; what is there to tell? — the Song discovered it hadn't even received a warning of war. Not because the enemy was too well hidden, but because the emperor had decreed that "those who recklessly speak of border affairs shall be exiled three thousand li."
