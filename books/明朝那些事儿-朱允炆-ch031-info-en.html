"One Step From Victory"

1401–1402 CE: After Dongchang's catastrophic defeat, Zhu Di nearly collapsed psychologically — but rallied with Da Yan's brutal honesty. Third sandstorm sealed victory at Jiahe; the final push toward the capital began.

Situation After Dongchang, Zhu Di's elite army was shattered and his best general Zhang Yu was dead. His subordinates, facing years of terror, openly demanded retreat — mutiny was brewing.
Conflict Zhu Di confronted his darkest night: internal army dissent, elite forces destroyed, and a psychological terror that made victory feel impossible.
Question How does a commander rebuild an army's will to fight after total military and psychological defeat?
Answer Public grief ritual for Zhang Yu + personal reconnaissance under "bulletproof vest" + third sandstorm miracle + Zhu Neng's rallying speech — will forged in crisis.

01
Dongchang Catastrophe: Zhu Di's Closest Call With Death
At Dongchang, Zhu Di personally led the cavalry charge, broke through left flank, attacked center — and fell into Sheng Yong's encirclement trap. Three horses shot beneath him, arrows exhausted, sword shattered. He was saved by Zhu Neng, who broke through enemy lines with personal bodyguards; Zhang Yu charged after him but arrived too late — hacked to pieces by vengeful Southern soldiers. The Northern elite army was almost completely destroyed. Yet Zhu Di himself was unharmed: Southern firepower and bows "dare not target Zhu Di directly" — the no-harm edict's bulletproof effect at its peak. Zhu Yunwen's mercy physically pulled his uncle back from death's door.

02
No-Harm Paradox: The Southern Army's Greatest Institutional Error
Dongchang revealed a surreal truth: the safest person on the battlefield was not Sheng Yong (the Southern commander), but Zhu Di (the enemy general). Sheng Yong's firepower killed most of the Northern Army — but weapons "dare not target Zhu Di himself." The logic: Southern soldiers calculated that if Zhu Di and Zhu Yunwen eventually reconciled, anyone who killed Zhu Di would face purging. The no-harm edict's deepest effect was not its text, but its signal — "reconciliation is possible" — which made soldiers agonizingly hesitant to kill Zhu Di. He survived two near-death battles because of this single order.

03
Fear: Zhu Di's Darkest Night and Psychological Collapse
After Dongchang, Zhu Di sat alone in darkness — a broken man confronting the abyss of three years of terror. Every day was potentially his last; every battle could be his final one. He confided to Da Yan: "If this campaign continues, it would be better to become a monk with you — that would be a kind of happiness." Da Yan grabbed his sleeve and roared: "My lord, there is no turning back! We have committed treason — failure means only death!" Zhu Di lowered his head, then lifted it — cold resolve replacing despair. "Yes, you are right. We have no retreat." The terror never left him — but from that night, he learned to use it as fuel rather than let it destroy him.

04
Battle of Jiahe: Third Sandstorm and Tan Yuan's Fatal Mistake
March 1401 — Battle of Jiahe: Zhu Di personally led a daredevil scout team across enemy lines. Protected by his no-harm "bulletproof vest," he calmly surveyed Sheng Yong's formation and returned unscathed. During the assault, general Tan Yuan mistook a probing attack for the main offensive, charged prematurely, and was killed. His death inadvertently broke the tactical stalemate — Zhu Di seized the moment for a counterattack. Next day: a third sandstorm arose from clear skies, northeast winds blowing sand into Southern soldiers' eyes — Zhu Di's third "jackpot." Without these three sandstorms, Zhu Di's grave could already be harvesting crops.

05
Creative Breakthrough: From "Must Take Shandong" to Capital Dash
Despite continuous victories, Zhu Di felt unprecedented despair — his territory was just three prefectures (Beiping, Baoding, Yongping) against Zhu Yunwen's national control. He believed his campaign would end with him captured by a foot soldier. Then palace eunuch intelligence arrived: the capital's defenses were thin. But Shandong lay across the path — how to bypass? Da Yan shattered the mental trap: reaching the capital doesn't require taking Shandong. December 1401: Zhu Di's last gamble. "This expedition is our final battle — no retreat, live or die!" January 1402: crossing at Guantao, sweeping through Dong'a and Dongping, striking Xuzhou directly — bypassing Shandong's defenses, racing for the capital.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Dongchang was the campaign's definitive turning point. Sheng Yong became the first commander to defeat Zhu Di in open battle — but three sandstorms and the no-harm edict allowed Zhu Di to survive two near-annihilation defeats. Zhu Di's darkest night, confronted by Da Yan's brutal truth, forged unbreakable resolve. From Jiahe's third sandstorm to the creative breakthrough bypassing Shandong, Zhu Di proved that willpower — "just one more push" — often decides battles when all else seems lost.
