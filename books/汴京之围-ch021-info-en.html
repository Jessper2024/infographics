The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.18 "Returning Power to the Zhao"

Part IV · The Jingkang Calamity After the Jurchen withdrew from Bianjing, a puppet emperor, a twice-deposed empress dowager, and a prince who dared not enter the capital — three-way maneuvering decided whether the Zhao dynasty would survive.

After the Jurchen army withdrew from Bianjing, puppet emperor Zhang Bangchang was trapped in a dilemma — continuing to reign would bring certain death, but abdicating might not earn Prince Kang's forgiveness. On Lü Haowen's advice, Zhang Bangchang welcomed the twice-deposed Empress Dowager Meng (Yuanyou) to the palace to rule as regent behind a lowered curtain, effectively returning power to the Zhao clan. Meanwhile, Prince Kang Zhao Gou ascended the throne in Nanjing Yingtianfu as Emperor Gaozong, founding the Southern Song. This chapter traces the critical power transition following the Jurchen withdrawal and explores the fierce debate between hawks and doves that would define the early Southern Song — culminating in the Song-Jin military stalemate along the Huai River, a balance of power that neither side could easily break.

01
Power Transfer
Twice deposed, Empress Meng became Bianjing's sole legitimate Zhao authority
Empress Meng was the only empress who remained in Bianjing after the Jingkang calamity — her "misfortune" turned out to be her salvation. Lady Meng was installed as Empress to Zhezong in 1092 by Grand Empress Dowager Gao. A year later Gao died, and the now-personally-ruling Zhezong — resentful of Gao's influence — deposed Meng with the help of his favored Consort Liu, sending her to Yaohua Palace as a Daoist nun. Four years later Zhezong died childless; Empress Dowager Xiang reinstated Meng as empress. But when Xiang died too, Meng was deposed again. Precisely because she had lost her formal title, she escaped the Jurchen dragnet that swept up every other titled consort. When Zhang Bangchang was trapped between ruling and abdicating, Lü Haowen proposed welcoming Meng into the palace. As "Empress Yuanyou" she ruled as regent, formally returning power to the Zhao family. Zhang Bangchang retreated to Zishan Hall, withdrew his amnesty — his 33-day "emperorship" was over.

02
Imperial Succession
Prince Kang took the throne in Nanjing — Cao Xun's secret imperial note broke the final legitimacy deadlock
Prince Kang Zhao Gou's enthronement in Nanjing Yingtianfu accomplished a rare feat in Chinese history — selecting a new emperor while two sitting emperors were still alive. After the Jurchen withdrew, Prince Kang did not rush to reclaim Bianjing. Instead, he prudently stationed himself at Yingtianfu, a city from which he could advance north or flee south. His "pardon edict" was a stroke of political wisdom — blaming the capital's fall on "Jurchen cunning" and "treacherous ministers," thereby absolving Zhang Bangchang, Fan Qiong, and the entire populace. But one obstacle remained: never before had a new emperor been chosen while two previous ones lived. Enter Cao Xun, who brought a secret edict that Huizong had hidden inside his collar lining, plus intimate family anecdotes known only to Prince Kang and the imperial couple — this "legitimizing narrative" made Prince Kang's succession appear heaven-ordained. On May 1, he ascended the altar, gazed north, wept aloud, and became Emperor Gaozong, inaugurating the Jianyan era.
⚔️

❌ Why Prince Kang Couldn't Attack

Though commanding a relief army, the Jurchen repeatedly stalled with "negotiations." Emperor Qinzong forbade military action — Kang could not disobey. The relief force was never deployed.

🛡️

✅ Pardon + Legitimization

The city-wide pardon edict plus Cao Xun's secret note transformed Prince Kang from a cautious observer into a legitimate successor.

03
Political Divide
Zong Ze and Li Gang urged war; Huang Qianshan and Wang Boyan urged peace — the foundational rift of Southern Song
Gaozong's enthronement reignited the centuries-long Song debate between hawks and doves — but this time, the outcome would determine whether half the empire survived. Hawk Zong Ze advocated defending Bianjing as the base for a northern counteroffensive; Li Gang proposed relocating the capital to Shaanxi to leverage its natural defenses. Doves Huang Qianshan and Wang Boyan argued for retreating south. The core question: if the emperor stayed in the north, recovering lost territory was possible; if he fled south, a Huai-River partition might become permanent — and historically, no southern regime had ever reconquered the north from below the Huai. But Gaozong was no strategic visionary; his first priority was avoiding the fate of his father and brother.

04
Military Stalemate
Three Jurchen southern invasions failed to capture Gaozong — exposing Jin's "sharp edge but shallow depth"
The Jurchen built their state around a single ethnic group — the Jurchen population was small. Their military had lethal precision but lacked the depth to hold territory. In Jianyan 1 (1127), Jin launched a three-pronged invasion: the western army took Chang'an, the central army captured Luoyang, the eastern army plundered Shandong — but logistics forced all three to withdraw. In Jianyan 2, after Zong Ze died, Jin attacked again; Wanyan Zongbi (Wu Zhu) made a dash for Yangzhou, but Gaozong fled across the Yangtze just in time. In the winter of Jianyan 3, Zongbi crossed the Yangtze again — Gaozong escaped to the East China Sea and boarded a ship, the first Chinese emperor ever to flee by sea. Han Shizhong trapped 100,000 Jurchen troops with just 8,000 men at Huangtiandang for 48 days — though the Jurchen ultimately escaped via a new canal, the myth of Jin invincibility was shattered.
📉

Jin Weakness

Population too small; couldn't sustain occupation. Thousand-li sprints from Hebei to the Yangtze left troops exhausted. No stable administration between the Yellow and Huai Rivers.

📈

Song Turning Point

Huangtiandang shattered the "Jurchen undefeatable" myth. Song and Jin entered equilibrium. The Huai River became a stable frontier.

05
Historical Pivot
The Huai River became the new border — the Song-Jin balance of power was locked in
As Gaozong retreated from Yangzhou to Hangzhou, then fled by sea to Dinghai, a historical moment arrived: the permanent loss of the northern heartland was now inevitable. Southern Song military geography was rewritten by the emperor's survival anxiety. Kaifeng fell after Zong Ze's death → the Guanzhong Plain in Shaanxi was lost → all of Hebei and Shandong fell → both sides stabilized along the Huai River. In Shaoxing 12 (1142), Gaozong traded the life of general Yue Fei for a peace treaty with Jin, perpetuating the tradition of "paying tribute for peace." In Shaoxing 31 (1161), Jin's Prince of Hailing, Wanyan Liang, invaded south but suffered a decisive defeat at Caishiji and was killed in a mutiny — by then, Jin, like the Liao before it, could no longer pose a mortal threat to the Southern Song.
"In all Chinese history up to the Song dynasty, whenever a north-south standoff formed along the Huai River, no southern regime had ever managed to counterattack, reconquer the north, and unify the country. In this sense, once the court retreated to the south, the north might never return."
🔑 Key Takeaway
After the Jurchen withdrawal, Bianjing underwent a triple power conversion: from Zhang Bangchang's puppet regime → Empress Dowager Yuanyou's regency → Prince Kang's legitimate enthronement. Gaozong's strategy of "survival first, recovery later" — relocating to Hangzhou, fleeing by sea — led to the gradual loss of northern territories. But Jin's fatal weakness also became clear: the Jurchen had "sharpness but no depth" and could not sustain occupation after thousand-li sprints. Once the Huai River became the equilibrium line, the Song-Jin war shifted from blitzkrieg to prolonged attrition, frozen for over a century by "tribute for peace." This history reveals a brutal logic: once a de facto border is accepted as reality, breaking it requires far more strength and courage than setting it.
