Five Cities of Du Fu · Ch21 "We Gaze, Never Tiring"

Xuancheng

After completing the book manuscript, his ninth journey across China. Boarding a train in Zhenjiang, he spontaneously detoured to Xuancheng — Li Bai's home in his later years. Stepping out of the station, he was stunned by a sea of vermilion rickshaw canopies — "a strange, deep, dark vermilion, like a color from the Middle Ages" — which brought to mind Du Fu's poem "Dreaming of Li Bai": "Official canopies fill the capital, but this man alone withers." On the eve of Mid-Autumn, he walked dark streets alone, bought two mooncakes for dinner. The next morning, he climbed Jingting Mountain — the only visitor — finding the same solitude Li Bai felt when he "sat alone gazing at the mountain": "We gaze at each other, never tiring — only me and Jingting Mountain." The book ends with Li Bai's loneliness and the author's quiet, understated farewell.

01A Spontaneous Detour: Following Li Bai's Railroad
Late September 1993, after finishing the first draft, he took three weeks off. On a train from Zhenjiang to Huangshan, he discovered the railroad traced Li Bai's own route: Nanjing, Ma'anshan, Dangtu, and Xuancheng. A teenage boy across the aisle said his hometown was Xuancheng, Jingting Mountain just outside the city. He decided on the spot — "Huangshan can wait."
02Vermilion Canopies: Time Flowing Backward
Exiting Xuancheng Station, he saw rows of rickshaws — their sunshade canopies all painted the same vermilion. "A strange, deep, dark vermilion, like a color from the Middle Ages, glowing with a low, muted light under the sun." Looking down from the station stairs, "a great field of vermilion canopies, divided into two columns, surged up like waves." Suddenly he recalled Du Fu's line — "Official canopies fill the capital, but this man alone withers" — from "Dreaming of Li Bai, Two Poems." "It was as if time had truly flowed backward, as if I had returned to Li Bai's High Tang."
03Xuancheng Night: Mooncakes and Salted Duck in the Dark
"I was merely a postmodern traveler, a busybody. I just wanted, as a passerby, to spend one night in Li Bai's Xuancheng, to breathe its air." A few days before Mid-Autumn, he bought two mooncakes for dinner. At a roadside stall, he found a literary magazine. When full darkness fell, the streets had no lamps. Walking back to the hotel, "I passed a vendor selling salted duck, chopping intently in the darkness. There was no candle. I worried he might chop his own fingers." That night, police came to check papers — he handed over his passport with a smile. They left without a word.
04Climbing Jingting Alone: We Gaze, Never Tiring
A rickshaw took him to the foot of Jingting Mountain. "On both sides, pear trees had shed all their leaves, leaving bare branches." In half an hour he reached a nunnery halfway up. A fierce dog barked at him. He was "the only climber that morning." "Over the years, I had climbed nearly every famous mountain in China… all of them bustling with tourists. Only Jingting Mountain was so quiet, so still — on the morning I arrived, not a single other visitor." Li Bai, too, must have come alone — which is why he "sat alone" gazing, forming that bond of "we gaze at each other, never tiring."
05A Quiet Ending: Li Bai's Solitude, the Author's Farewell
The book's closing brushstroke. Li Bai in his later years "had no official post, no fixed residence. He drifted, as Du Fu wrote, 'like a solitary cloud drifting all day long' — no one knows how he made a living." "Coming down the mountain, thinking of Li Bai's 'solitary cloud' state of mind, my own feeling was equally light and faint." He wrote to a friend afterward, saying he had climbed Li Bai's Jingting Mountain. The friend replied gently: "You too, I fear, were lonely." The book ends not with a grand conclusion or soaring epiphany, but with a historian of the Tang Dynasty climbing a nearly forgotten mountain on an autumn morning, sharing a moment of wordless communion with a poet from twelve hundred years ago.
💡 Key Insight
The final chapter is so quiet it barely has a plot — one man, one mountain, one poem. Lai Ruihe follows Li Bai's footsteps to Xuancheng not to examine any Tang Dynasty ruins (Jingting Mountain has none), but simply to "breathe the air" and feel Li Bai's solitary-cloud state of mind. Vermilion canopies evoke Du Fu's poem about Li Bai. Two humble mooncakes on a dark Mid-Autumn eve. The only visitor on Jingting Mountain. Everything points to loneliness — but not anguished loneliness. It is the "light and faint" loneliness that Li Bai's poem transforms into "we gaze at each other, never tiring." Nine journeys, over ten thousand kilometers across China, and the book comes to rest on a mountain with no tourists — because he wasn't seeking landmarks. He was seeking a moment of stillness to commune with a poet twelve centuries gone.
