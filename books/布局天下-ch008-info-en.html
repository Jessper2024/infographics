Layout of the World · Chapter 5
「Afterword: Carving Ice, Shearing Snow, Discussing Blades with a Smile」

Rao Shengwen on his historiographical mission, methodology, and the contemporary significance of military geography

In the afterword, Rao Shengwen reflects on his decade-plus dedication to historical study, guided by Sima Qian's maxim: 'Investigate the boundary between Heaven and Man, comprehend the transformations of past and present, and form one's own school of thought.' He chose military geography as his entry point, believing that strategic calculation rests on two pillars: 'assessing the times' (understanding the historical stage) and 'measuring the momentum' (grasping the overarching trend of the realm). He affirms the value of this subject — quoting British geographer Holdich: 'The price paid for geographic ignorance is absolutely incalculable.' How many heroes in Chinese history, he asks, dominated for a time only to fail because they misunderstood the greater momentum of the realm? The book is not just an academic exercise but carries a spirit of engagement: through insight into China's geographic momentum, strengthen territorial integration, dissolve latent centrifugal factors, eliminate threats before they sprout — and thereby consolidate unity.

1
Original Mission
Investigating Heaven and Man, Comprehending Past and Present

Rao Shengwen takes Sima Qian's historiographical mission as his own: 'Investigate the boundary between Heaven and Man, comprehend the transformations of past and present, and form one's own school of thought.' He believes that reading history requires mastery of two fundamentals: the overarching momentum of the realm (天下大势) and the rise and fall of dynasties. 'At moments of dynastic transition, heroes emerge in droves. Their rises and falls, successes and failures — seemingly chaotic, yet patterned.' Tracing the 'patterns' that determine political survival and military victory forms a persistent pull for the author. This is the enduring fascination of history — to discern, beneath the apparent chaos of events, the consistent, underlying logic that runs through everything.

2
Military Geography
Military Geography as Entry Point: Where Strategy, Geography, and History Converge

The author chose military geography as his entry point because military strategy, military geography, and the two fundamentals of historical study are deeply intertwined. 'Strategic calculation rests on two aspects: assessing the times and measuring the momentum. Assessing the times means understanding the historical stage of development; measuring the momentum means grasping the overarching trend of the realm.' Insight into the greater momentum is the prerequisite for sound political and military decision-making. Over more than a decade of historical study, Rao paid close attention to the grand strategies of dynastic transitions, analyzing how forces rising from different regions at different times adopted strategies in relation to their moment in history. He quotes British geographer Holdich to underscore the stakes: 'The price paid for geographic ignorance is absolutely incalculable.'

3
Historical Sense
Cultivating 'Historical Sense': Intuitive and Penetrating like a Go Player's Instinct

Rao introduces a profoundly insightful concept — 'historical sense' (历史感). 'The greatest benefit history bestows is the cultivation of historical sense. Historical sense is like a Go player's positional instinct — less a body of knowledge, more a cultivated sensibility. It is a perspective sharpened through striving for a dynamic grasp of history's solemn, majestic, and immense process.' Historical sense is 'intuitive, deep, and penetrating — able to see through surfaces to grasp the greater momentum, to seize fleeting opportunities amid complex historical movements.' The core of strategic calculation lies precisely here. This historical sense requires not just accumulated learning but a unique form of cultivation — akin to a Go master's intuitive reading of the board.

4
Engaged Spirit
'Carving Ice, Shearing Snow, Discussing Blades with a Smile': An Engaged Spirit and Strategic Wisdom

Rao emphasizes that to discuss strategy, one must first possess a profound spirit of engagement with the world. 'Treat historical events as current events, treat others' affairs as one's own — sitting in a quiet study, heart immersed in the storms of history; enduring hardships together with the ancients, deliberating together — only then can one assess their successes and failures, discuss the patterns of order and chaos. Only through such repeated reflection, absorbed contemplation, can one attain historical wisdom — and only then discuss strategy. Without this, discussions of strategy inevitably fall to a lower plane.' He describes his own disposition with the poetic line 'carving ice and shearing snow, discussing blades with a smile' — attentive to the grand contours of rise and fall, devoted to the deep currents of order and chaos. This engaged spirit elevates the book beyond pure academic writing, imbuing it with a profound concern for the nation's fate.

5
Contemporary Relevance
Consolidating Unity: The Contemporary Political Significance of Geographic Knowledge

In his conclusion, Rao underscores this subject's positive contemporary significance: 'Through insight into China's geographic momentum, by strengthening territorial integration, overcoming natural geographic barriers, reinforcing regional economic and cultural connections, accelerating the nation's integration process, dissolving latent centrifugal factors, eliminating threats before they sprout — thus consolidating unity.' He believes that the historiographical approach can offer strategic studies a distinctive perspective — just as Mackinder's Heartland Theory and Mahan's Sea Power Theory were both built on historical investigation, and in turn profoundly shaped twentieth-century world history. In July 2001, Rao Shengwen completed this three-year labor at the foot of Jundu Mountain in Beijing.

📌 Key Takeaways

Guided by Sima Qian's mission to 'comprehend the transformations of past and present,' Rao Shengwen chose military geography as his lens, distilling strategic calculation into two core capacities: 'assessing the times' and 'measuring the momentum.' His concept of 'historical sense' is especially penetrating — like a Go master's positional instinct, intuitive, deep, and sharp, able to see through surfaces to grasp the greater momentum and seize fleeting opportunities amid complexity. The foundation of this historical sense is an engaged spirit — treating historical events as one's own, enduring hardships with the ancients, deliberating together — to attain historical wisdom. Completed over three years at Jundu Mountain, this book is not merely an academic endeavor but carries a deep concern for consolidating national unity through geographic knowledge. China's chessboard-like geographic structure is both the stage on which history unfolds and the blueprint for future integration.
