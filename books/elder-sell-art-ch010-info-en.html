The Art of Selling
Chapter 10 "Exploring the Bottom"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer In autumn 2008, the S&P 500 weekly NH-NL collapsed to -18,000 — a level never before seen in U.S. stock market history. Bear Stearns bankrupt, Lehman liquidated, institutions refusing to trade. When markets plunge into complete despair, how do you systematically identify true bottoms and enter with precision using coordinated multi-tool signals?

01
Extreme Indicator
NH-NL Extreme Oversold: The Contrarian Signal in Despair
When NH-NL drops below -4,000, it generates a powerful buy signal — in S&P 500 history, the indicator had never fallen below -6,000. In October 2008, that rule was shattered: weekly NH-NL plunged to -18,000, a spike reflecting markets at the point of total despair. When the indicator returned above the zero line from severely oversold levels, it meant the market would not "shut down." The question shifted from "Will it collapse?" to "When will the bottom appear?"

Confirmation System
The Double Helix: Multi-Timeframe Signal Confirmation
Signals appearing simultaneously across different timeframes are rare. The Triple Screen system provides a clear methodology: start with weekly charts for strategic decisions, then shift to daily charts for tactical timing. Because the daily NH-NL is highly sensitive, you must wait for a "second crossover" to confirm — what Spike group's Stephen Morris calls the "Double Helix" buy signal.

03
Core Pattern
False Breakout + Divergence: The Most Reliable Bottom Pattern
A false breakout accompanied by bullish divergence is one of the most important bottom patterns, especially reliable on weekly charts. The RIMM (BlackBerry) case perfectly illustrates the "missing right shoulder": the stock broke below its prior low by just 4 cents ($35.09→$35.05) then quickly recovered. The MACD histogram formed a divergence structure: Bottom 2<0→Top 3>0→Bottom 4≥0. The composite system turning from red to blue confirmed the buy.
✅

Correct Approach

Wait for break below prior low → confirm false breakout with quick recovery → verify MACD bullish divergence → system color change → enter in value zone, stop below new low.

❌

Common Mistakes

Blindly buying the dip without false breakout confirmation; ignoring MACD or Force Index divergence; entering impulsively without a stop or system signal.

04
Entry & Exit
Value Zone Channel: Precise Entry, Exit & Staged Profit-Taking
Buy in the value zone (near the lower channel band), sell near the upper band in overbought territory — the core rule of Elder's channel trading system. CEPH case: bought at $54.62 in the value zone, sold at $57.06 near the upper band, capturing 53% of channel width — Grade A. A more refined strategy: sell half at the upper band to lock in profits, then sell the rest when the composite system changes color. Accepting you "sold too early" is forgivable — far wiser than the pain of holding floating profits.

05
Psychology & Discipline
Trading Psychology: Walk With Your System
Bull markets do not rise in a straight line — Charles Dow and Robert Rhea described three classic phases: Phase 1 — oversold recovery rally (where 2009 was), Phase 2 — real economic growth driving prices (most stable), Phase 3 — speculation runs rampant, about to jump into the next bear. Professional traders repeatedly trade the same stock, enduring multiple stop-outs without giving up — like building a relationship, not bar-hopping. Calm, relaxed execution beats chasing perfect signals.

🔑 Key Takeaway
A bottom is not a single signal — it is the systematic coordination of multiple tools: extreme indicator alert → double-helix multi-timeframe confirmation → false-breakout divergence pattern recognition → value-zone channel execution → disciplined staged profit-taking. Trust your system and execute calmly and relaxedly. It is worth having a simple trading system, with the plan in your head and risk relatively small.
