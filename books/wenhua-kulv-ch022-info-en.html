Wenhua Kulv · Ch.22 "Huang Zuolin's Last Words" — Infographic

Three lines from Bernard Shaw's fireplace crossed oceans and decades—They have said. What said they? Let them say!

Huang Zuolin (1906–1994), master of Chinese theatre and Bernard Shaw's direct disciple. Three days after the Marco Polo Bridge Incident in 1937, he bid farewell to Shaw to 'meet the national calamity,' returning to 'lonely island' Shanghai where he ushered in the golden age of Chinese theatre. Twenty years later, under extreme political conditions, he published 'On Dramatic Concepts,' marking Stanislavski, Brecht, and Mei Lanfang as humanity's three great dramatic systems—a work that shook international academia. Yu Qiuyu: 'In all these decades, which achievement in all of Chinese cultural theory surpasses this? Truly, none can be found.' He lived by 'only make art, never be a tool.' In his final years, he sent Shaw's three lines as his last words to a besieged Yu Qiuyu: Let them say!

01
Farewell to Bernard Shaw
July 10, 1937, three days after the Marco Polo Bridge Incident: 'I'm going home!' 'It's a battlefield!'
July 10, 1937, Bernard Shaw's residence. Huang Zuolin, just over thirty, came to say farewell. Shaw assumed his student would request help finding long-term work in Britain, with China now a battlefield. Instead Huang said: 'That is why I've come to say goodbye.' 'Goodbye? Where to?' 'Back home.' 'Back home? It's a battlefield, the war will only grow. You're no general, no soldier—why go back?' Huang could not explain in English the weighty Chinese concept of 'fu guonan'—going to meet the national calamity. Shaw inscribed his guest book: 'Arise, China! The future of the Eastern world is yours. If you have perseverance and courage, it will be Chinese drama that makes the coming celebrations more magnificent.'
02
Island Theatre
Amidst national catastrophe, China welcomed its golden age of theatre
After returning to China, Huang settled in Shanghai. The foreign concessions became 'lonely islands', where artists who had fled through blood and gunfire unleashed unprecedented creative passion that persisted even after the Pacific War engulfed everything. One relatively minor play, The Inspector, ran 77 performances in 40 days. Huang's lead actor Shi Hui was hailed as 'the emperor of spoken drama'—'to this day, no one has truly surpassed him.' Veteran artists recalled: 'Those performances could not have been better'; 'What I saw in my lifetime at the theatre reached its peak there.' This proves Yu Qiuyu's point: 'The noblest art does not necessarily emerge from massive funding, official attention, or media campaigns. On the contrary, it often answers directly to harsh circumstances. Art's best backdrop is not gold, but black.'
03
Three Dramatic Concepts
April 25, 1962: a milestone in world theatre studies quietly erected
Huang Zuolin's essay 'On Dramatic Concepts' achieved five theoretical breakthroughs: ① summarizing all of theatre history through 'creating illusion' and 'breaking illusion'—first-rate distillation of complexity into simplicity; ② borrowing the French Jouvet's 'fourth wall' concept with precision and power; ③ using illusion-breaking to elevate Brecht, transforming his innovation into a 'historical period-demarcation function'; ④ marking the three great dramatic concepts of the twentieth century with Stanislavski, Brecht, and Mei Lanfang—an insight no one had articulated before, unchallenged internationally to this day; ⑤ transitioning to the 'xieyi (essence-capturing) dramatic concept', now common working vocabulary. Yu Qiuyu: 'In all these decades, which achievement in all of Chinese cultural theory surpasses this? Truly, none can be found.' Two weeks later, on May 9, Ba Jin delivered his speech in the same city—Shanghai shone so brilliantly in the late spring of 1962.

04
Northern Jiao, Southern Huang
'Only ask about resistance, not party; only make art, not tools'—the price of a lifetime's insistence
After 1949, former underground Communist cultural figures became cultural leaders, deeply impressed by Huang Zuolin's 'stubbornness' in 'only asking about resistance, not party; only making art, not tools.' He was treated as a mere 'fellow traveler.' In directing circles, 'Northern Jiao, Southern Huang' was the saying—Jiao Juyin in Beijing collaborated with Lao She and Cao Yu; 'Southern Huang' faced the ultra-left syndrome of Ke Qingshi, Zhang Chunqiao, and Yao Wenyuan—Ke walked out mid-performance, Zhang instructed newspapers to 'only promote the author, not the director.' In the 1980s professional title review, Huang and Yu Qiuyu served as co-chairs: those who persecuted others and rebel faction leaders were rejected: 'Make works, or make trouble? That is the question.' When Yu was appointed president of Shanghai Theatre Academy, Huang's four-character comment: 'Delightful, deplorable!'—delighting in public approval, deploring the waste of academic genius.
05
Let Them Say
Three lines from Shaw's fireplace, crossing oceans and decades, written to me from a hospital bed
At 87, from his hospital bed at Huadong Hospital, Huang Zuolin wrote to Yu Qiuyu, quoting the three lines inscribed on Shaw's fireplace: 'They have said. What said they? Let them say!' He wrote: 'A great tree must attract wind, but after the wind, the tree still stands.' 'I hope you won't be distracted by various "storms"—concentrate on cultural exploration and writing, that is the true culture.' The letter was dated May 21, 1993; Huang died the following year. Yu Qiuyu looked back at this giant's terminus: farewell to Shaw three days after July 7, 1937 → creating theatre's golden age in the 'lonely island' → publishing the three dramatic concepts twenty years later → returning north after the catastrophe to interrogate intellectual souls → a noble old age full of childlike wonder—'Before him or since, which Chinese artistic giant has had such a complete and beautiful life?' Huang Zuolin was a towering peak, giving the stumbling ship of modern Chinese culture an extra mast.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Huang Zuolin was a towering peak, giving the stumbling ship of modern Chinese culture an extra mast. His last words—three lines from Shaw's fireplace, crossing fifty-six years—comforted a besieged younger colleague, and comfort all those who hold fast to art amidst the noise.
