Su Dongpo Biography · Ch. 15 "The Crow Terrace Poetry Trial"

1079 — one phrase, four indictments, a literary inquisition that changed the course of Chinese literary history

This was the greatest crisis of Su Dongpo's life — imprisoned for his poetry. Censors extracted four phrases from his memorial of thanks and several poems, accusing him of "mocking current affairs with hidden malice." During six to seven weeks of interrogation, Su Dongpo believed he faced certain death, composing farewell poems for his brother. Emperor Shenzong, valuing his talent and moved by the poems and many intercessions, spared his life — exiling him to Huangzhou. This case became the most famous literary inquisition of the Northern Song.

01
The Trigger
Four Words That Made a Governor a Prisoner
In his official memorial thanking the emperor for his transfer to Huzhou, Su Dongpo wrote that he was "too dim-witted to keep up with the new blood" — the term "new blood" being Wang Anshi's specific label for "suddenly promoted incompetents." This publicly distributed memorial instantly made court politicians the laughingstock of the literati. Four censors, including Shu Dan and Li Ding, jointly submitted indictments, combing through four volumes of Su's poems.
📜

The Formality

A standard memorial thanking the emperor for transfer. Yet Dongpo smuggled in sharp satire — "unable to keep up with the new blood" became the fatal handle.

⚡

The Reality

"New blood" mocked Wang Anshi's promoted incompetents. Four censors searched four poetry volumes, twisting literary creation into political crime.

02
The Arrest
Official Robes, a Tablet, and a Joke That Turned Tears to Laughter
When the imperial envoy Huangfu Zun arrived in Huzhou with troops, Su Dongpo, believing he faced certain death, opened with: "I know I have offended the court and believe I cannot escape death. I do not fear dying, but please allow me to go home and bid my family farewell." The envoy coldly replied: "It is not so serious." The whole family wept. Dongpo told a story — of the hermit Yang Pu, whose wife composed a poem when he was arrested: "Today they've seized my old man — this time they'll do in his old scalp." His wife dissolved into tearful laughter.

03
Life-or-Death Twist
Smoked Fish and Two Farewell Poems That Moved an Emperor
Dongpo and his eldest son Su Mai had a secret code: regular meals meant all was well; fish meant bad news. When Su Mai went to borrow money and asked a friend to deliver food, the friend — unaware of the code — sent smoked fish. Dongpo, believing death was imminent, wrote two farewell poems to Ziyou, expressing gratitude to the emperor and wishing to be brothers in every future life. Guards, per regulation, submitted the poems. Emperor Shenzong read them and was deeply moved.

04
The Trial's Core
Honest Criticism or Malicious Slander?
The trial's single core dispute: were Su Dongpo's poems honest criticism or malicious slander of government and emperor? Zhang Fangping argued forcefully for Dongpo, pointing out that the Book of Songs, edited by Confucius himself, was filled with political satire — good government should tolerate frank criticism. Censor Shu Dan countered that Dongpo "harbored evil intent, resented his superiors, mocked and reviled without any subject's loyalty — none like Su Shi," adding that "ten thousand deaths would not suffice."

05
The People's Response
Officials Hid; The People Wept Like Rain
When Su Dongpo was arrested, officials hid in fear, but ordinary people poured out to see their governor off, "weeping like rain." According to local gazetteers, the people turned out en masse, every face wet with tears. The guards treated him like a common bandit. Dongpo later wrote: "To arrest a governor as if capturing a bandit." Only the Wang brothers and Chen Shixi dared give him a farewell banquet. This stark contrast revealed the vast chasm between his standing among the people and the hatred of his court enemies.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The Crow Terrace Poetry Trial was not fundamentally a legal trial — it was a contest over whether intellectuals could criticize power. Su Dongpo proved, through the genuine emotion in his farewell poems and his humor at the moment of arrest, that true literary integrity lies not in how sharp one's pen is, but in being able to comfort one's family with a joke at the boundary between life and death. The phrase "they'll do in his old scalp" was worth more than a thousand defenses. He was ultimately spared death and exiled to Huangzhou — where his greatest works would be born. A literary inquisition can imprison a man's body, but it can never imprison a poet's soul.
