The World of Yesterday · Ch.11 "In the Heart of Europe"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer After two and a half years of war, the fever was subsiding. Jeremiah met with unexpected support — because people were beginning to doubt the war. Zweig obtained permission to visit Switzerland and breathed once more in the free air of a neutral country. He reunited with Romain Rolland in Geneva and encountered Joyce, Busoni, and other exiles in Zurich. When the war finally ended, the whole world believed "a new world had begun" — though this would soon prove a naive illusion.

This chapter chronicles Zweig's time in Switzerland: the physical and psychological shock of crossing the border, his reunion with Rolland in Geneva; the fierce debates among exiled intellectuals in Zurich and encounters with "men without a country" like Joyce and Busoni; the leakage of Austria's secret peace plan, and Henri Guilbeaux's audacious anti-war journal Demain. The war ended with Germany's defeat, and Wilson's idealism ignited hope across Europe — a "better, more humane world" seemed within reach.

TURNING POINT
Jeremiah's Unexpected Success: Time Made People Sober Up
Easter 1917: Jeremiah sold 20,000 copies almost immediately. Not only did Rolland openly support it, but pro-war figures like Rathenau and Dehmel also endorsed it. The reason wasn't artistic merit but that war had lasted over two and a half years — "the fever was beginning to subside." Soldiers bled at the front while speculators profited at home — "a deep crack ran through the entire nation." Zweig had merely said what others dared not.
📖

Unexpected Support

20,000 copies sold out. Theater directors requested postwar premiere rights. Even pro-war factions "showed courtesy and considerable respect."

💔

A Nation Divided

Soldiers endured the cruelest suffering at the front while people crowded theaters and profiteered. Citizens no longer trusted any bulletins or newspapers.

02
SHOCK
Crossing the Border: Five Minutes from Suffocation to Freedom
From a blockaded belligerent country to neutral Switzerland took only a few minutes by train — "as though emerging from a stifling atmosphere into clear, snowy space." Swiss stations brimmed with goods — no ration stamps needed. The post office could cable anywhere. Zweig marveled: "The entire absurdity of the European war could be revealed by comparing these two closely neighboring border stations." Even his body could no longer tolerate real coffee and cigars.

03
POLITICAL SECRET
Lamasch's Secret: Austria Nearly Broke Away from Germany
In Salzburg, privy councilor Lamasch revealed a stunning secret: Emperor Karl's government was planning to break away from Germany's military dictatorship. Lamasch said: "We have already lost over a million dead! We can no longer sacrifice a single life for Germany's world hegemony!" This was Austria's last chance for salvation — but Karl lacked the courage to publicly assert his conviction. The secret mission failed. History's turning point was missed.

04
SPIRITUAL ALLIANCE
Geneva: The Spiritual Alliance of Exiles and "Men Without a Country"
In Geneva, Zweig reunited with Rolland — the first Frenchman he had spoken with in three years. He saw in Rolland "the heroism of thought, the heroism of morality." Around the anti-war journals La Feuille and Demain gathered Jouve, Masereel, and Arcos. Masereel's anti-war woodcuts "stood comparison even with Goya's Disasters of War." In Zurich, Zweig met Joyce — the Irishman who had "nothing to do with England" — and Busoni, living amphibiously between two nations.

05
ENDGAME
The War Ends: Wilson's Ideals and an Illusory Hope
America's entry sealed Germany's defeat. The Kaiser fled, Ludendorff slunk away to Sweden. The world hailed Wilson as savior; enemy soldiers embraced and kissed. "At last there was space on earth to build a kingdom of justice and brotherhood. We dreamed of immediately creating a common Europe." But Zweig later admitted — "We were all very foolish then. And not just us." This beautiful illusion would soon be shattered by the betrayal at Versailles.
🌅

Idealist Dawn

Wilson's grand program was accepted worldwide. Soldiers embraced; Europeans showed unprecedented trust. "A new world had begun."

💭

Naive Illusion

"We were all very foolish then." Generals and politicians tore up Wilson's plan. Secret negotiations and treaties never stopped.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
Switzerland's existence as a neutral state was Europe's last refuge of conscience. There, Rolland defended humanity with his frail body, and exiles debated the world's future in cafés. But the deepest lesson is this: idealism's radiance, however dazzling, cannot withstand the darkness of realpolitik. The moment Wilson's peace plan was torn to pieces was precisely when the seeds of World War II were sown. The greater the hope, the deeper the disappointment.
