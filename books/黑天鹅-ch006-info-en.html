The Black Swan · Ch6「The Narrative Fallacy」

We Cannot Observe Facts Without Inventing Explanations Saddam captured → Treasury bonds rise. One hour later: Saddam captured → Treasury bonds fall. The same event explains opposite outcomes? Humans are wired to find patterns in randomness, to weave stories from chaos. This "narrative fallacy" makes the world appear more orderly than it is — blinding us to real Black Swans.

This chapter is the cognitive psychology core of the book. Taleb reveals the narrative fallacy: we cannot observe a series of facts without weaving an explanation or imposing a logical connection. From split-brain experiments to dopamine research, the chapter builds the biological case that our causal-impulse is physical and beyond conscious control. Kolmogorov complexity theory explains why we need to simplify information — the more compressed, the more orderly the world appears. The narrative fallacy creates a double illusion about Black Swans: those discussed are overestimated; those unmentioned are severely underestimated. The solution: use System 2 (slow, logical thinking) against System 1 (fast, intuitive narrative).

01
Core Concept
The Narrative Fallacy: We Can't Observe Facts Without Explanations
An Italian professor praised Taleb's opposition to excessive causal explanation — then immediately invented a cause for Taleb's work: "You grew up in an Eastern Orthodox Mediterranean environment."This is the perfect demonstration. Human explanatory behavior is automatic and uncontrollable. "The king died, then the queen died" — two pieces of information. "The king died, then the queen died of grief" — one piece of information. The second is easier to remember because it's compressed into a story. Narrative makes us feel we understand better when we're merely simplifying complexity, making the world appear more orderly than it is.

02
Biological Basis
Explanation Is Physical: Split-Brain Reveals the Brain's "Interpreter"
The left brain specializes in inventing explanations, even when it has no idea of the true cause.Split-brain experiments: researchers make the right brain perform an action (lift a finger), then ask the left brain (language-dominant) why. The patient immediately provides a perfectly reasonable but entirely fictional explanation. The left brain is the pattern-recognition region — it sees the forest; the right brain sees individual trees. When the left brain is suppressed, people become more realistic — better at drawing, seeing objects as they are, free from narrative and bias. Dopamine reduces skepticism and enhances pattern recognition; L-Dopa can turn patients into compulsive gamblers — "seeing" patterns in random numbers.

03
Information Theory
Kolmogorov Complexity: The More We Compress, the More "Orderly" the World Seems
The brain's bottleneck is not storage space but indexing.Consciousness is like a desk in the Library of Congress — no matter how many books exist, the desk limits retrieval capacity. 100,000 random words cannot be compressed; a book with a repeating pattern can be reduced to a single sentence. The more we generalize, the more orderly things seem, and the lower randomness appears. This is the deep mechanism of the narrative fallacy: our simplifying behavior makes the world appear less random than it is. And Black Swans are precisely what we do not simplify.

04
Black Swan Blindness
Discussed Black Swans Are Overestimated; Silent Ones Underestimated
There are two types of rare events: narrated Black Swans (discussed, heard on TV) and unmentioned Black Swans (they fit no pattern).The former have overestimated probability — people are more likely to buy terrorism insurance than general insurance (which already covers terrorism). The latter are severely underestimated — lottery buyers overestimate winning odds while ignoring truly impossible disasters. The probability of death from "a California earthquake causing a flood" is estimated higher than "a flood" alone — because the earthquake provides a "reason" that makes the event more imaginable. Emotion makes abstract statistics no match for vivid anecdotes.

05
Dual Thinking Systems
System 1 and System 2: Most Errors Come from Thinking We're Using System 2
Most reasoning errors happen when we think we're using System 2 (logical thought) but are really using System 1 (intuition).System 1 is effortless, automatic, fast, fuzzy, error-prone — "fast and frugal" shortcuts. System 2 is effortful, methodical, slow, logical, self-aware. The key problem: we are unconscious of using System 1! The round-trip fallacy (confusing "no proof of Black Swans" with "proof of no Black Swans") is System 1 at work. The solution: emphasize experiments over stories, experience over history, objective knowledge over theory. Predict and record outcomes. Use narrative against narrative — tell better stories that convey correct information.
⚡

System 1: Experiential Mode

Fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional. Creates shortcuts (heuristics). Operates unconsciously — we don't know we're using it.

🧐

System 2: Cognitive Mode

Effortful, methodical, slow, logical. Self-aware — you can retrace reasoning steps. Fewer errors, but requires active engagement.

🔑 Key Takeaway
A paradox of Black Swans: why do our brains over-worry about some Black Swans yet ignore the real ones? The answer is the narrative fallacy — we habitually invent stories for random events. Events with a "cause" (like terrorism) are overestimated; abstract events with no story to tell (like genuine systemic risk) are severely underestimated. To avoid the narrative fallacy: ① recognize that explanatory behavior is automatic and physical; ② consciously engage System 2 for logical verification; ③ replace stories with experiments and data; ④ predict and record outcomes, letting reality, not narrative, judge your judgments. Only diamonds cut diamonds — use good narratives to fight bad ones.
