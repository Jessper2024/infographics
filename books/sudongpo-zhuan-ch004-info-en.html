Core Insight：Three forces shaped young Su Dongpo's soul in the library and school of Meishan: his mother's moral education, his father's guidance toward a simple prose style, and his brother Ziyou's lifelong companionship

Core QuestionWhat kind of education did Su Dongpo receive in his youth that made him a literary giant?

Core AnswerBetween ages eight and ten, his mother taught him the "History of the Later Han" — the story of Fan Pang planted in him the resolve to be an upright person. At eleven, he entered secondary school and copied ancient texts character by character, building a foundation so solid that he never misused a single historical reference in drafting imperial edicts. His father, returning home after failing the exams, lay on a couch listening to his sons read aloud, pinning his hopes on the next generation. His younger brother Su Zhe (Ziyou) became the closest person in his life — "He nurtured me as an elder brother, taught me as a teacher."

1
Moral Awakening

The Resolve of Fan Pang: An Immortal Dialogue Between Mother and Son

His mother taught Su Dongpo the "Biography of Fan Pang" from the History of the Later Han — Fan Pang calmly faced death at 33 for opposing the eunuchs. The young Dongpo looked up and asked: "Mother, if I grow up to be like Fan Pang, would you allow it?" His mother replied: "If you can be Fan Pang, can I not be Fan Pang's mother?"

The Later Han dynasty, through misrule, had fallen into the hands of eunuchs. Loyal scholars repeatedly risked their lives to remonstrate, suffering torture. Fan Pang was among these upright scholars. When arrested, the magistrate wanted to flee with him, but he refused, saying: "If Pang dies, the trouble ends. How dare I implicate you and cause my aged mother to wander homeless!" His mother said at their farewell: "Now you will share fame with Li Ying and Du Mi — what regret is there in death? Having won a noble name, can you also seek long life — can both be attained?"

This mother-son dialogue was the cornerstone of Su Dongpo's lifelong character. Lin Yutang commented: Su Dongpo's courage in adhering to principles and defying power throughout his political career was seeded when he was ten. He was always caught in political whirlpools yet always transcended politics — this personality trait originated from the sense of justice planted when his mother taught him the biography of Fan Pang.

2
Study Method

Copying Character by Character: Effort Rewarded

The most diligent students copied all ancient texts and dynastic histories character by character — Su Dongpo did this in his school days. This seemingly clumsy method ensured he never misused a historical reference when drafting imperial edicts.

To prepare for official examinations, students had to read widely in classics, histories, poems, and selected essays. The traditional method was to have students read unpunctuated historical texts and try to punctuate them. But Su Dongpo went further, copying entire books character by character. This served him enormously later — when memorializing the emperor or drafting imperial edicts, he never once used a historical example incorrectly.

Commercial printing had existed for over a hundred years by then. Movable type was invented by the merchant Bi Sheng — special clay characters were hardened, arranged on a metal tray coated with resin. But woodblock printing was also still prevalent. Su Dongpo and his brother Ziyou accumulated vast knowledge of literature and ancient texts in this environment.

3
Father's Return

A Hunter Whose Last Arrow Missed: Su Xun's Hopes

His father returned home after failing the exams and lay on a couch listening to his sons read aloud — his mood like a hunter whose last arrow had missed, as if forging new arrows to send his sons after that elusive deer.

Su Xun's weakness was poetic rhyme; he was only interested in concepts — being too clever and original actually hindered exam success. Customarily, children read aloud while the father lay on a couch listening — said to be one of the most pleasing sounds in the world. The children's fluent recitation of ancient texts, their eyes and voices, convinced him they would succeed, and thus his hope was restored and his wounded self-esteem healed.

Su Xun always supported a simple prose style, opposing the ornate fashion of the day. Later, when Dongpo went to the capital for exams, Ouyang Xiu as chief examiner decided to reform the literary style, rejecting all candidates with empty, flowery writing. Su Dongpo described that pitiable style as "piled up phrase by phrase with no regard for the overall effect — like an old lady at the opera opening, her arms and neck covered in jewels."

4
Brotherly Bond

Brother and Teacher: Su Dongpo and Ziyou

The love between the two brothers and their loyalty through hardship was the theme Su Dongpo most often celebrated throughout his life — they comforted each other in sorrow, helped each other in disaster, often dreamed of each other, and constantly exchanged poems.

Ziyou was calm, cool, and practical by temperament, and somehow his official rank was always higher than his brother's, though their political views were identical and their entire careers rose and fell together. Ziyou was less emotional and often gave his elder brother advice. Perhaps he was less stubborn; perhaps his literary talent and fame were not equal to his brother's, making him seem less dangerous to political enemies.

Su Dongpo wrote in a poem: "I knew Ziyou from youth — his natural gifts were gentle and pure. Not only is he my younger brother, he is also my worthy friend and companion." Ziyou wrote in Su Dongpo's epitaph: "In the beginning I followed you, and through you gained knowledge. You nurtured me as an elder brother, taught me as a teacher." Throughout this period, Su Dongpo was not only his brother's schoolmate but also his teacher. The two supported each other throughout their official careers, forming the most moving brotherly bond in Chinese literary history.

5
Naming Tradition

Given Name, Courtesy Name, Literary Name: The Multiple Identities of a Chinese Scholar

According to ancient custom, Chinese scholars always had several names — given name, courtesy name, literary name, alias, posthumous title — each layer representing different social relationships and life stages.

Old Su was named Xun, courtesy name Mingyun, literary name Laoquan (from his ancestral burial ground). The eldest son was Su Shi, courtesy name Zizhan, literary name Dongpo — "Dongpo the Recluse" was adopted during his exile in Huangzhou and later became his best-known name across China. The second son was Su Zhe, courtesy name Ziyou. Additionally, many scholars used studio names engraved on seals.

Taboo against using parents' or grandparents' given names was an ancient rule that sometimes caused great embarrassment. Su Dongpo, as a famous scholar, had to write many prefaces (xu), but he avoided the character "序" because it was his grandfather's name Su Xu — so in his works, all "prefaces" became "introductions" (yin). The great historian Sima Qian's monumental work contains not a single "谈" (tan) character, simply because it was his father's given name.

💡
Key Takeaway

Three layers of education shaped young Su Dongpo's lifelong character: his mother's moral lessons taught him integrity (the resolve of Fan Pang), his father's stylistic views taught him simplicity (opposing ornate emptiness), and his brother's companionship gave him the most precious friendship of his life. These three forces intertwined to shape the great soul who could later say: "Above, I can accompany the Jade Emperor; below, I can keep company with beggars."
