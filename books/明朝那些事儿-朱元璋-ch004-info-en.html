1351: the final straw falls—"a Stone Man with One Eye" is unearthed, the Red Turban Rebellion ignites. Zhu Chongba faces three paths: stay, flee, or rebel. One divination, one curse, one "Rebel, damn it!"—the most famous forced-to-rebellion in Chinese history begins.

Question With all escape routes sealed, how will a man who values life above all choose?

01
The Final Straw: How the Yellow River Lit the Fuse
Tuituo's Yellow River repair became the Yuan's funeral pyre—170,000 laborers conscripted under brutal, corrupt management. When workers unearthed a one-eyed Stone Man at Shandong, its back bore the exact prophecy laborers had chanted for years: "A Stone Man with One Eye, stirs the Yellow River and all beneath Heaven rebels." Days later, at Yingzhou—where Zhu Chongba once begged—Han Shantong and Liu Futong raised the White Lotus banner. History records forever: 1351, the first flag of anti-Yuan resistance rose.

02
Four-Class Order: When Killing a Peasant Cost One Donkey
The Yuan divided subjects into four racial classes: Mongols, Semu, Han, Southerners. A Mongol killing a Southerner paid only one donkey fine—often nothing. Mongol nobles treated China as a guesthouse, seizing whatever they wanted. They assumed Southerners would endure forever. They were wrong. When anger crosses every limit, when "living like a dog" becomes too much—resistance is the only road. The fire spread: tens of uprisings in one year, millions in rebel armies, Mongol cavalry no longer invincible.

03
Not a Born Hero: Zhu Chongba's Real Fear and Rational Calculation
"In many books, Zhu Chongba is cast as a born hero who immediately joined the rebellion upon hearing the news. I believe this is not the real Zhu Chongba." The real Zhu Chongba was afraid—he knew the value of life, knew rebellion meant no return. "True courage is not the absence of fear, but choosing to advance despite it." A man who has lost everything—what more can he fear? His hatred for Yuan was carved into bone; his choice was inevitable, but not without terror.

04
All Paths Sealed: A Genuine "Forced to Liangshan"
Three paths lay before Zhu Chongba: stay at the temple, flee, or rebel—none offered safety. Worse, Yuan officials fabricated rebel connections, seizing random villagers to execute as rebels and claim credit. Childhood friend Tang He's letter arrived, inviting him to join—Zhu burned it without expression. That night, his monk brother warned: someone had seen him reading the letter, planned to denounce him. Three paths, all blocked. He consulted Zhou Dexing. The oracle spoke: "Fleeing or staying—neither auspicious; rebelling amid violence—harmless."

05
"Rebel, Damn It!": The Inevitable Fate of the Oppressed
"Who does not cherish their own life? Who wishes to fight? When survival itself becomes impossible, peasants are forced to use their own blood and lives to push feudal society toward its end—this is their fate." Zhu Chongba's choice was not born of idealism but of unbearable provocation. Tang He became his first comrade in arms—and, unbeknownst to either, the only person who would walk the entire hard road beside him. History is not written by heroes choosing paths, but by circumstances and fate writing together.

🔑 Key Takeaway
"True heroism is not fearlessness, but choosing to advance despite fear." This chapter completely dismantles the romantic myth of a "born rebel." The real Zhu Chongba was terrified, calculating, fully aware of the cost—yet pushed forward anyway. When all three paths (stay, flee, rebel) are sealed, he roars "Rebel, damn it!"—not from idealism, but from unbearable indignation at a world with no justice. History thus vindicates peasant uprisings: they had no choice, yet their blood pushed feudalism toward its end.
