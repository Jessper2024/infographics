Surfaces and Essences · Ch8「Earth-Shaking Analogies」

When analogy enters the deepest realms of mathematics and physics—from the solution of cubic equations to Einstein's light-quantum hypothesis, every great scientific breakthrough has been guided by analogy. This chapter shatters the myth that "mathematics is a fortress of pure logic," proving that even in the most rigorous discipline, analogy remains the central guide of thought.

Chapter 7 showed how mathematical novices rely on naive analogies to avoid stumbling in the abstract world, and how naive analogies can trap non-mathematicians for life. This chapter flips the lens: what role does analogy play in the minds of professional mathematicians and physicists? Some consider it fanciful to imagine that analogical thinking plays any role in the professional activities of mathematicians—after all, mathematics is usually regarded as the pinnacle of rigor and logic. But as Poincaré said: "Who would think that they always march straight ahead, step by step, without any clear idea of the goal to be attained? To reach the goal, one must guess the right path. For this, they need a guide. That guide is chiefly analogy." Through a set of breathtaking case studies from the history of science—from cubic equations to Einstein—this chapter proves: in humanity's most rigorous intellectual domain, analogy has always been the source of thought and the fire of thinking.

01
Busting the Myth
The Beauty of Mathematics—Fortress of Pure Logic or Stage for Analogy?
To some, the idea that analogical thinking plays any role in the professional activities of mathematicians seems wildly fanciful. After all, among all intellectual domains, mathematics is usually regarded as the pinnacle of rigor and logic. A mathematical paper looks like an impenetrable fortress built with pure logic. But this impression is not groundless—because most mathematicians like to present themselves that way. The standard view is that intuition, hunches, ambiguity, and vagueness occupy far less space in mathematics than in other disciplines. Yet this is merely a prejudice that applies equally to mathematics as to any other human activity. The great French mathematician Poincaré, who thought deeply about the nature of scientific creativity, offered this comment—the keynote quotation for the entire chapter.
Poincaré's Famous Words

"Who would think that they always march straight ahead, step by step, without any clear idea of the goal to be attained? To reach the goal, one must guess the right path. For this, they need a guide. That guide is chiefly analogy." This comment on mathematical thinking hits the mark. When Poincaré says mathematicians need a "guide," he is referring precisely to analogy—the cognitive ability to build bridges between the unfamiliar and the known.

02
Historical Case
From Quadratic to Cubic—An Irresistible Analogy That Slid for 1,500 Years
In the early 16th century, European mathematicians sought to solve equations of the form ax³+bx²+cx+d=0. For the preceding 1,500 years, people had been familiar with the solution to quadratic equations of the form ax²+bx+c=0—whose core was a square root, namely the square root of b²−4ac. The few people interested in such arcane matters would naturally think: might something broadly similar apply to cubic equations? Any serious mathematician would guess that since the two equation forms are so similar, there must be some implicit connection between them. Specifically, the general formula for solving cubic equations should contain, at its core, a cube root of some quantity analogous to b²−4ac, and this quantity should involve the four coefficients a, b, c, and d of the cubic. This slippage from "two" to "three" was irresistible—just as irresistible as the "me too" analogies described in Chapter 3.

Micro-Analogical Slippage

Sliding twice from two to three, then once from three to four—this slippage is itself a micro-analogy: "4 is to 3 as 3 is to 2." Without such conceptual slippages, ubiquitous in mathematics and seemingly trivial, no progress would be possible.

💡

Mathematicians Are Human Too

Mathematicians, even the greatest ones, are ordinary people. Without any conscious thought process, they naturally expect an analogy to exist—this is the mathematical embodiment of "me too."

03
Physics Case
Einstein and the Light Quantum—Analogy Reshapes Physics
In 1905, Einstein proposed an earth-shattering hypothesis: light is composed of particles—light quanta (photons). This idea was so radical that many physicists took decades to accept it. But how did Einstein arrive at this idea? The crucial step was an analogy. Einstein noticed that the mathematical formula describing the distribution of radiant energy across frequencies bore a striking formal similarity to the formula describing the energy distribution of gas molecules. The analogy between these two formulas was so compelling that he inferred: if gases are composed of particles (molecules), then radiation should also be composed of particles (light quanta). This is the quintessential "earth-shaking analogy": deriving a revolutionary conclusion about the nature of the physical world from formal similarities between the mathematical structures of two different domains.

04
Category Refinement
From Naive Analogy to Sophisticated Analogy—The Expert's Journey of Category Refinement
Chapters 7 and 8 form a profound contrast. Chapter 7 depicts how naive analogies can trap non-mathematicians for life—those simplistic analogies based on surface similarities (like "division is just sharing equally") lodge permanently in lay minds and lead them into dead ends. Chapter 8 shows how experts transcend naive analogies: professional mathematicians and physicists rely on analogy just as much, but their crucial difference is that they continually "refine" their category systems, replacing initial naive concepts with more precise, richer ones. This process is not about abandoning analogy but about continually upgrading its quality. From the naive analogy "division = equal sharing," to understanding division as a broader class of inverse operations, to redefining "division" in abstract algebra—each step of refinement is accomplished through new analogies. Experts rely on analogy just as novices do, but experts possess more refined categorical equipment.

Through the Book
From Chapter 1 to Chapter 8—Analogy as the Unifying Thread
Looking back across all eight chapters, a clear thread emerges: Chapters 1–2 show how words and phrases emerge through analogy, demonstrating analogy's foundational role; Chapters 3–4 reveal that analogy is everywhere and underpins abstract thought; Chapters 5–6 form a two-way picture of analogical manipulation—analogies manipulate us, and we manipulate analogies; Chapters 7–8 form a progression from naive to sophisticated—from spontaneous, unconscious naive analogies to category-refined sophisticated analogies, which sit at the core even in humanity's highest intellectual halls. As Einstein proved by analogizing the motion of gas molecules with radiant energy—discovering deep connections between two seemingly disparate domains is the noblest achievement of human thought. And this achievement was accomplished by analogy. This is why the book's subtitle declares: Analogy is the Source of Thought and the Fire of Thinking.

🔑 Core Takeaway
In humanity's most rigorous intellectual domains—mathematics and physics—analogy is not an optional auxiliary tool but the central guide of thought. Poincaré said analogy is the mathematician's "guide"; Einstein discovered light quanta through analogy—these "earth-shaking analogies" share the identical cognitive mechanism as a three-year-old saying "I took off the banana's clothes." The difference lies not in whether analogy is used, but in the degree of refinement of one's category system: experts and children alike rely on analogy, but experts' category systems, refined over decades, allow analogies to be built upon deeper, more precise conceptual structures. This is the book's ultimate message: from the most mundane everyday cognition to the greatest scientific breakthroughs, analogy is the unifying force that runs through it all—the source of thought, the fire of thinking.
