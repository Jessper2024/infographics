Cultural Perplexity · Ch.24 "A Dialogue Between Heaven and Man"

By the pine grove beside a funeral parlor, one man completes a philosophical colloquy across the boundary of life and death with four departed souls

Core insight: Through meditative dialogues with four deceased figures at a columbarium, Yu Qiuyu conducts a comprehensive examination of the roots of slander, the nature of good and evil, and the ultimate meaning of life — from the castle of institutions to poetic exchanges with heaven, he finds his spiritual home in the wisdom that "great intelligence needs no crowd, great goodness needs no alliance."

QuestionAfter his father was literally angered to death by slander, how does a man enduring decades of nationwide attack reconcile with both the good and the evil among the departed?

AnswerThrough four dialogues — with the matchmaker (good), the persecuted scholar (good), the informer (evil), and the mysterious tomb raider (beyond good and evil) — he peels back layer after layer: why the good are always hurt, why slanderers never apologize, where the independent soul finds an exit — culminating in a set of poetic exchanges that dissolve into the transcendent Way of heaven and earth.

1
System Question

Why do those who leave the system inevitably become targets?

The dialogue with matchmaker Yu Hongwen reveals a paradox: when Yu Qiuyu held office, no one attacked him; after he resigned, slander poured in — because "a system is a mechanical structure, like a castle," where insiders restrain each other for safety, while the freedom of one who leaves becomes a mockery of the ecology within.

Yu Qiuyu refuses to return to any castle: the official system, where "administrative thinking and cultural thinking are different by nature, and the former may eventually swallow the latter"; and the other castle claiming "democracy," which is "tainted with too much opportunism, falsehood, and performance." He can only recite Lu Xun: "Between two camps, a single soldier remains, halberd in hand, pacing alone" — and he is more thorough than Lu Xun, "without even the halberd."

Yu Hongwen's closing words: "Great intelligence needs no crowd, great goodness needs no alliance. Why fear walking alone? Why fear slander?"
2
Good Man's Plight

Why are good people always the ones who get hit?

The dialogue with persecuted scholar Xu Fuming delivers a brutal answer: the attacker attacks so as not to be attacked, while the good man gets hit because he does not attack — "Your suffering was not because of him, but because of yourself. You do not know how to attack others."

In China, attacking others is a "phantasmagorical enterprise": the public, upon hearing from an attacker that someone "might have a problem," excitedly follows, instantly clothing the attacker in "golden robes, with throngs of followers." Official media brands every mob assault as "public opinion," while the target becomes a "controversial figure" — the attacker is forever invincible.

Yu says: "I don't need victory. I only hope that one day, when a new 'Zeng Yuanfeng' chases a new 'Xu Fuming' down the street, China's public and media will no longer cheer along."

3
Informer's Truth

What is the lifelong skill of an informer?

The "dialogue" with informer Zeng Yuanfeng reveals: those who spend their lives denouncing and slandering — graduates of Chinese literature departments whose creative talent ran dry — applied the "fabrication, imagination, exaggeration, and emotional manipulation" of literary creation to real-life character assassination.

They later collectively performed as "dissidents," shouting "democracy," "human rights," and "freedom" — while it was precisely these people who, through rumors, violated Yu Qiuyu's right to reputation, his wife's right to work, and his father's right to life. Yu's retort: "In all of history, across the world, who has been more 'free' than you?"

The columbarium's layout is suggestive: Xu Fuming and Zeng Yuanfeng are placed face to face — "The judge of the Last Judgment is the lifelong victim. The language of judgment is an unwavering stare."

4
Heaven-Man Verses

Where does the most beautiful moonlight come from?

The dialogue with the mysterious tomb raider Yu Yixian transforms into a series of poetic exchanges — this "wanderer of the dark," with a reputation for grave-robbing yet also known for secretly delivering textbooks to literacy classes and digging channels to save villagers from floods, becomes a medium connecting heaven and man.

"The most beautiful moonlight always comes from desolate valleys."
"The most profound artifacts always come from wordless wilderness."
"The most laughable lies are always uttered with righteous conviction."
"The most shameful slander always wears an air of courtesy."
"The purest solitude always belongs to the gates of a master."
"The most vulgar fellowship is soaked day and night in wine, swelling ever larger."
"The speechless heavens still preserve one word: goodness."
"The cold-faced mountains still hide one talisman: love."

The dialogue concludes: "A drop in the vast ocean — why ask its source? From the infinite it comes, to the infinite it returns." A complete ascent from specific human affairs to the Great Way of heaven and earth.

💡
Key Takeaway

Four dialogues crossing the boundary of death form a complete chain of inquiry from "the trap of the system" to "the Way of heaven and earth": leaving the castle is the root of slander; not attacking is the reason for being wounded; the informer's lifelong blindness is the tragedy of human nature; and the final remedy lies not in worldly victory — but in the moonlit valleys, the wordless wilderness, and the "goodness" and "love" between heaven and earth.

Source: Yu Qiuyu, Cultural Perplexity (文化苦旅), Changjiang Literature & Art Press, 2014
