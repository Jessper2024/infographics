Wenhua Kulv · Ch.24 "Heaven-Earth Dialogue"

Beside the funeral parlor's pine grove, the author engages four departed souls in a conversation across life and death—examining systems, slander, castles, and solitude

While paying respects to his late father at a funeral parlor, the author stumbles upon the ash urns of his parents' matchmaker Yu Hongwen, his mentor Xu Fuming, the mysterious tomb-raider Yu Yixian, and his slanderer Zeng Yuanfeng. Sitting on a stone bench outside, he closes his eyes and enters a spiritual dialogue with the four dead—Yu Hongwen interrogates "why you became the most-slandered independent intellectual in China," Xu Fuming illuminates the social psychology of "hitting and being hit," Zeng Yuanfeng exposes the literary fraudulence behind slander, and Yu Yixian exchanges aphoristic wisdom reaching to heaven and earth.

01
Elder's Inquiry
Yu Hongwen's interrogation: Why are you the most-slandered independent intellectual in China?
Yu Hongwen questions the author on behalf of all ancestors: Was there a power struggle?—"I resigned twenty years ago; I hold no office, no titles, have left all official associations." A rivalry for profit?—"Millions of words of research, tens of thousands of kilometers of field study—never applied for a single cent of government funding." Academic rivalry?—"My topics never collide with others', my routes never cross theirs, my expression never resembles theirs." Personal resentment?—"They have attacked me for over twenty years; I have never retaliated once, never named a single one." Conclusion: the person least deserving of slander received the most.

02
Castle Allegory
Leaving the system is like walking out of a castle—all arrows fly from within
The author uses the castle as metaphor for the system: "Inside, even mutual jealousy holds everyone in check, providing balance and safety. One day, someone quietly opens the gate and leaves. The gate closes behind him, yet his horseback deeds remain visible to those inside. His freedom, independence, and visibility unwittingly become mockery of life within. He inevitably becomes the target of arrows—with the gate shut, the archers face no consequences." There is more than one castle—some are stately with national support, some bustling with foreign backing. Lu Xun wrote: "A lone soldier between two fronts, halberd in hand, I pace alone." The author is more radical—not even a halberd.
🏰

Official Castle

Administrative thinking arrives at orderly pleasure through grand ceremony. Money flows freely while culture withers—eventually it will swallow culture whole.

🎭

The Other Castle

Too much opportunism, falsity, and performance. Self-styled saviors spread rumors, reducing everything to political critique until it all becomes tedious.

03
Hitting & Being Hit
Xu Fuming's philosophy: He hits to avoid being hit; you are hit because you never hit
The author tells the life-long victim Xu Fuming: "He hits people to avoid being hit; you are hit because you never hit." This connects to Chinese collective psychology—people excitedly believe any allegation about another and instantly elevate the accuser to moral champion. Once the attacker strikes, golden robes descend, followers swarm. Xu Fuming could have escaped by attacking others, but he couldn't. The author, a chief judge of the World University Debates, could have verbally destroyed them—but chose not to. "We don't need victory. We only hope that one day, when a new Zeng Yuanfeng chases a new Xu Fuming down the street, the people and media will no longer cheer."

04
Fraud as Trade
Zeng Yuanfeng's trick: using literary fiction, imagination, and sensationalism on real people
The author examines his lifelong attacker Zeng Yuanfeng. All slanderers share a pattern: "graduated from Chinese literature departments, dabbled in literary creation, then—when their literary talent dried up—switched to writing denunciation articles and informant letters." Three of the "Gang of Four" came from literary backgrounds—they applied fiction, imagination, exaggeration, and melodrama to real society, passing off fabrication as plot, fantasy as romance, abuse as recitation, rumor as script, mob attacks as rehearsal. Now they rebrand as "dissidents," chanting "democracy, human rights, freedom"—while their "democracy" actually means mob persecution, and their "human rights" violate others' rights.

05
Night Immortal's Wisdom
Yu Yixian's aphorisms: The most beautiful moonlight always comes from desolate valleys
The mysterious tomb-raider Yu Yixian (nicknamed "Night Immortal")—a man of ill repute who secretly delivered textbooks at night, guided lost souls, and dug flood channels to save villages. The author engages him in a Taoist aphoristic duet: "The most beautiful moonlight always comes from desolate valleys." "The most profound artifacts always come from wordless wilderness." "The most laughable falsehoods always sound righteous." "The most shameful slanders always come wrapped in courtesy."... reaching cosmic dimensions: "The speechless heaven and earth still reserve one word: goodness." "The cold-faced mountains and rivers still hide one symbol: love." "All is emptiness, no joy no sorrow, only autumn mountain mist drifting faintly." "A thousand years like a shuttle, no birth no death, only moonlit bird songs hazy and forlorn." Final answer: "A drop in the vast ocean—why ask its source? From the infinite, returning to the infinite."

🔑 Key Takeaway
A system is a castle—leaving it means becoming a target for every arrow. But true cultural creation does not reside within castles. The magic of slander is rooted in China's collective psychology. Yu Hongwen says: "Great wisdom needs no crowd, great goodness needs no support—why fear walking alone, why fear slander?" Yu Yixian says: "A drop in the vast ocean—why ask its source? From the infinite, returning to the infinite." This is the ultimate answer of the independent soul.
