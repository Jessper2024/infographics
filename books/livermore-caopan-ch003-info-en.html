Livermore's Trading Method · Ch.3 "Follow the Leaders"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → AnswerMarket prices are always moving up and down, and behind every major movement lies an irresistible driving force. The wise speculator rides the tide. After a string of successes, the market releases a seductive haze — the most common mistake is spreading too thin across many stocks. How to avoid blindly expanding positions and concentrate on true profit opportunities? Answer: focus on the most prominent leaders of the day, analyze only a few stock groups, and wait patiently for follow-through signals.

This chapter distills a lesson Livermore bought at great cost. During the wild bull market of the late 1920s, he accumulated massive paper profits shorting copper and auto stocks — but because he prematurely shorted utilities, the losses from the latter exceeded the profits from the former. Ultimately, when utilities and other groups did top out, Anaconda had already declined 50 points from its peak, and auto stocks had fallen by a similar percentage. His directional call was perfectly correct — but his timing was fatally early. This experience crystallized his core principle: follow the leaders, concentrate on a few key stock groups, and wait patiently for clear follow-through signals before acting.

01
Fatal Error
Spreading Too Thin: The Biggest Trap After a Winning Streak
Whenever a speculator has a run of success, the market releases a seductive haze that breeds complacency and inflated ambition — spreading too thin across too many stocks is the classic symptom. Handling a few stocks at once is manageable, but handling many simultaneously is overwhelming.
✅

The Right Way

After spotting a reversal in one stock group, wait patiently until other groups also signal that their decline or advance has ended. Act only when the time is right.

❌

The Wrong Way

After seeing one or two groups turn, immediately adopt a uniformly bearish or bullish stance and rush to play everything. Impatience replaces common sense and judgment.

03
Core Principle
Follow the Leaders: Concentrate Firepower on the Most Prominent Stocks
"Concentrate your attention on the stocks that are the most prominent in that day's trading. If you cannot make money out of the leading active issues, you are not going to make money out of the stock market as a whole." Just as women's fashions change over time, the market continually discards past leaders — railroads, sugar, and tobacco were once leaders; then steel took over; later automobiles moved to the fore. Today four groups dominate: steels, automobiles, aircraft, and mail-order.

04
New Era
The Modern Speculator's Survival Code: Patience, Judgment & Right-Sized Positions
Today's market conditions no longer permit the large-scale manipulation of old — this brings safer opportunities for the wise, diligent speculator, but demands more prudent position sizing. In the old days, a speculator could take 5,000 or 10,000 shares without affecting the price; today's narrower markets mean a wrong initial position could cause severe losses upon liquidation.

05
Stay Flexible
Keep Your Mind Flexible — Today's Leaders Are Not Tomorrow's Leaders
Livermore emphasized: while following the leaders, you must keep your mind flexible. Attempting to follow too many stocks at once is definitely unsafe — you will become overworked and confused. Analyze only a relatively small number of groups. You will find it far easier to obtain the true picture of the market this way than by slicing it into many small pieces.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Follow the leaders — don't spread yourself too thin. Concentrate on the most prominent stocks of the day within a few key groups. Wait patiently for clear follow-through signals before acting. Keep your mind flexible — today's leaders are not tomorrow's leaders. This is the only sound path to safe profits in the new era.
