Great Country, Big City · Ch.8「The Europeanization of China's Economy」

Euro Crisis · Unified Currency · Market Fragmentation · Local Government Debt · Institutional Reform People worry about China's "Latin Americanization." But the author argues China should truly fear "Europeanization"—specifically, "Eurozone-ization." Unified currency + market fragmentation + immobile labor = widening regional gaps + local government debt risk. If China's inter-provincial relations adopt an "inter-state" mindset, the advantages of a great nation will vanish.

This is Chapter 1 of the full book. Using the Euro crisis as a mirror, it analyzes the risk of "Europeanization" facing China's economy. The author first dissects the deep causes of the Greek debt crisis: market segmentation + unified currency. Then revisits the ancient emperors' "three values of a large nation," extending to four dimensions of modern economies of scale and seigniorage benefits. Turning to governance challenges: the tension between political centralization and economic decentralization leads to market segmentation. Using rich data, the author reveals the severity of local government debt—central-western provinces' bond issuance exceeded 50% of national incremental share in 2012 while contributing only 41% of GDP. Ultimately emphasizing: free migration is not just about fairness—it is a "national development strategy." China has reached a point where every region must abandon local thinking and consider the public interest.

01
Euro Crisis
Greece's Predicament: Market Segmentation + Unified Currency
The deep roots of the European debt crisis are two factors: First, market segmentation—despite the EU having no institutional barriers to mobility, linguistic, cultural, and religious differences make intra-European migration insufficient and unfree. Germany and France have roughly double Greece's per capita GDP. Second, unified currency—without an independent monetary policy, Greece cannot devalue its currency to stimulate exports during recession. When wages are rigid and the exchange rate is determined by Europe's average labor productivity, Greece's exchange rate becomes "overvalued," exports stall, and borrowing becomes the only way to fund social welfare. This closely mirrors China's situation: though China has a unified currency, the institutional barriers to labor mobility are even greater than between EU countries. Could China face "Europeanization"?
02
Economic Logic
The Eurozone's Fundamental Flaw: Failure of Productivity Convergence
The deep contradiction of the Eurozone: when a monetary union is formed among countries with vast development gaps, the negative effects of a unified exchange rate become fully apparent during crises. The euro's exchange rate, determined by average European labor productivity, is too low for Germany and France but too high for Greece and other "peripheral countries." The overvalued exchange rate restricts exports and constrains growth. Meanwhile, population does not flow freely, yet countries must provide rigid social welfare and public services. The only recourse is borrowing. Before the crisis, easy monetary policy encouraged weak countries to borrow heavily. When the crisis hit, the artificially booming economies proved extremely fragile. If Greece had its own currency, it would devalue. But in the Eurozone, countries have surrendered independent monetary policy. For China, under unified monetary policy, the less developed central-western provinces face a remarkably similar dilemma.
03
China's Mirror
China's Local Government Debt: A Potential Crisis
China has a unified currency but no unified labor market—this is precisely the Chinese version of the Eurozone problem. In response to the global financial crisis, China implemented loose monetary policy, and local governments borrowed heavily. By June 2013, local governments had 10.9 trillion yuan in repayment debt, while their annual local fiscal revenue was only 6.9 trillion yuan. More troubling, the debt burden falls most heavily on poorer central-western provinces. Figure 1-3 shows that provinces with lower per capita GDP carry higher debt ratios—Guizhou's reached 70%, exceeding the international warning line of 60%. This debt has largely been poured into oversized new towns and industrial parks. Even if central government bailouts avert immediate crisis, the efficiency losses persist. Ultimately, the entire nation pays—through taxes or inflation.

04
Moral Hazard
The "Advantage" and Trap of Unified Fiscal System
Many believe China's unified fiscal system can prevent a Greek-style sovereign debt crisis. But the author points out: it is precisely this expectation—that the center will bail out indebted localities—that creates a severe "moral hazard" problem. Local governments continue borrowing despite repayment difficulties because they expect central government to ultimately bear the cost. If local government debt financed productive investment, it should generate output. The problem: much of the spending on oversized new towns and industrial parks will never yield returns. More seriously, if economic growth (the denominator in debt/GDP) cannot be raised fast enough, the only alternative is inflation—which is essentially a tax on all citizens. Don't assume that the absence of visible defaults means safety. Local government debt that cannot be repaid will ultimately be borne by the entire population, either through taxes or inflation.

05
Hard Choice
Rescue Greece or Let It Go? Great Power Responsibility
After the 2008 global financial crisis, Greece became the first developed country to default on its sovereign debt. Europe faced a painful choice: rescue Greece, or let it leave the Eurozone? Rescuing means using Western European funds to subsidize "peripheral countries"; not rescuing means reversing integration progress. The author argues that the Eurozone's dilemma carries a crucial lesson for China. In any currency union, free population mobility is essential—otherwise, regional productivity convergence is impossible. If less developed regions have lower productivity but immobile populations, while social welfare and public service expenditures are rigid, borrowing to close the gap becomes inevitable. The core issue for China's provinces: if they treat each other as "nations rather than regions of the same country," the public interest of China as a great nation will be severely damaged.
06
Prisoner's Dilemma
"Don't Understand" and "Playing Dumb": Psychological Roots of Policy Gridlock
Why has breaking the barriers to inter-regional factor mobility taken so long? The author offers two answers: First, at the knowledge level, society has not fully recognized the necessity of cross-regional factor mobility, and the downsides of urban development and economic agglomeration have been overestimated. This is "not understanding"—a knowledge deficit. Second, even if the knowledge problem were solved, every locality is trapped in a "prisoner's dilemma" where local and short-term interests trump overall and long-term interests—"Even if everyone recognizes that market integration is beneficial, each region still has an incentive to segment the market." This is "playing dumb." The key manifestations: every region wants more construction land quotas, more transfer payments, more highly-skilled labor—but none is willing to grant permanent citizenship to the low-skilled migrants already living and working locally.
07
International Comparison
China's Hukou Barriers > EU Inter-State Barriers
Compared to Europe, China should be better positioned to leverage its great nation advantage—with a unified currency and common market. But in reality, China's institutional barriers to labor mobility are greater than those between EU countries. In many countries (not just developed ones), long-term foreign residents can access basic public services (healthcare, education, low-income subsidies). In China, long-term residents without local Hukou cannot equally access these services. Major cities set Hukou eligibility conditions comparable to Western residency requirements for immigrants. This is paradoxical: mobility barriers between Chinese provinces are larger than those between European nations! While the EU strives for integration, China—a unified country—institutionally obstructs labor mobility. This contradiction is unsustainable. Without market integration, China's economy faces the danger of "Europeanization."
08
Growth Engine
Transitioning and Developing Great Nation: Urbanization Unlocks Institutional Dividends
China superficially resembles 1980s Japan (trade surplus, currency appreciation, rising housing prices), but differs fundamentally: China is a "transitioning and developing great nation"—its greatest advantage. Thirty years ago, Japan had already completed urbanization and industrialization, with services >63.2% of GDP. China's urbanization is barely half complete. If unconstrained, it can release new vitality for economic growth. In the "development" sense, urbanization facilitates population agglomeration, knowledge spillovers, and human capital improvement—sustainably raising labor productivity. In the "great nation" sense, different regions have different endowments, and free factor mobility enables a differentiated division of labor. Integrating low-skilled migrants as citizens is not a burden but a foundation for cities' long-term competitiveness. Yet institutional barriers precisely obstruct this process.

09
Policy Path
Free Migration: Not Just Fairness, But a National Strategy
The author emphasizes: Free migration is not just a matter of fairness—it is a "national development strategy." If China wants to leverage its great-nation advantages long-term and avoid Europe's predicament, it must achieve convergence of per capita GDP across regions. The optimal path is internal free migration. This conclusion rests on core economic logic: given a unified currency and exchange rate, while wages are sticky, they ultimately reflect productivity differences. If labor moves freely, regional productivity and income converge. Otherwise, the only alternative is maintaining massive inter-regional income gaps—which cannot be a policy objective in any country. Therefore, free labor mobility, ultimately achieving regional productivity and income equalization, is the only viable strategic choice for a great nation.
10
Public Interest
Abandon Local Thinking, Serve the Public Interest
The author concludes by quoting the first line of his earlier book "The Power of Space": "Any great nation exists because its people are willing to coordinate inter-regional conflicts to pursue the public interest of all its people—a public interest that cannot be realized in a smaller country." Remember, the relationships between China's provinces and cities are, after all, relationships between regions within one country. China must guard against a situation where, in dealing with cross-regional factor mobility (especially labor), every region adopts an "inter-state" mindset. If this happens, China's public interest as a great nation will be severely damaged. China has reached the point where every province, every city, every county, every person must abandon local thinking and consider the public interest. Beyond appeals, this requires institutional design to constrain government behavior—let the central government pursue national welfare, and let local governments consider the overall interest.

🔑 Core Conclusion
China's greatest potential risk is not "Latin Americanization" but "Europeanization"—specifically, "Eurozone-ization." China has a unified currency and common market, but market segmentation and labor mobility barriers prevent the great nation from realizing its economies of scale, while the negative effects of a unified currency manifest through vast regional gaps. Local government debt—especially in central-western regions—is an urgent warning sign. The solution is not to continue allocating resources administratively and restricting population movement, but the opposite: eliminate institutional barriers to labor mobility, let the market guide resources to where they are most efficient. Free migration is not just about fairness—it is a national development strategy. Achieving per capita balance through agglomeration is the only viable strategic path for a great nation. China has reached the point where every province and every city must abandon local thinking and serve the public interest.
