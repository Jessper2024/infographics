The Gay Genius · Ch.19 "Years of Exile"

From Huangzhou to Ruzhou — a long wandering, a meeting with Wang Anshi, a burned deed, and the search for home

📖 Overview

The next year and eight months showed that holding office meant no personal freedom. The emperor decreed Su's transfer from Huangzhou to Ruzhou, but Su delayed "like a child dawdling to avoid school." He wandered — visiting Ziyou at Gao'an, touring Mount Lu, meeting Wang Anshi in Nanjing, and finally buying farmland at Yixing to retire. Along the way, he did something "foolish": after spending nearly all his savings on an old house, discovering it was a widow's ancestral home sold by an unfilial son, he burned the deed on the spot and walked away penniless.

Core Question: When one loses control of one's fate, how does one hold onto kindness while wandering?

Key Insight: Su's burning of the house deed proves that character is revealed most clearly when one can least afford to be generous.

01
TURN

The Transfer Order: "Like a Child Dodging School"

In March 1084, the emperor decreed Su's transfer from Huangzhou to Ruzhou — but Su delayed "like a child dawdling to avoid school."

Lin Yutang analyzed Su's attitude toward officialdom: he sought neither fame nor wealth, nor the pleasure of ruling others. He knew even the prime ministership could not "add luster to his immortal literary fame." Two days before the order arrived, he was still roaming with friends, planting orange trees. His enemies even tried to find fault in his thank-you memorial. The emperor calmly replied: "I understand Su Shih. There is no malice in his heart."

Nineteen neighbor-friends accompanied him by boat. The roadside was filled with acquaintances and strangers — farmers, parents whose children he had saved. As he left Huangzhou, he wrote: "Going home, going home — but where is my home? ... The business of life — coming and going like a shuttle."

02
REUNION

Nanjing Meeting: Two Failures in Conversation

In Nanjing, Su visited his political nemesis Wang Anshi — two great poets, once fierce opponents, now both political failures, finding common ground in Buddhism and poetry.

Wang was now a weary, sick old man, often riding alone on a donkey into the countryside "muttering to himself like a madman." Su reproached him for bringing on wars and persecuting scholars. Wang replied: "Both were caused by Lü Huiqing. What could I say from outside the capital?" Su countered: "The emperor treated you with extraordinary courtesy — how can you respond with ordinary courtesy?" Wang grew angry, then said: "What comes out of Anshi's mouth enters Zizhan's ears alone" — meaning the conversation must not leave the room, for he had been betrayed by Huiqing before.

Legend says they once competed in composing poetry on the same theme and rhyme — Su won, and Wang gave up midway. In his final years, Wang saw visions of his deceased only son suffering in hell and sold his property to donate to temples.

03
KINDNESS

Burning the Deed: Generosity When He Could Least Afford It

Su spent nearly all his savings — 500 strings of cash — on an old house, but upon learning it was a widow's ancestral home sold by an unfilial son, he burned the deed before her eyes and walked away with nothing.

Walking with his friend Shao Minzhan one moonlit night, he heard a woman weeping inside a house. The widow explained: "I have a house passed down for over a hundred years. My unfilial son has sold it to someone else. Today I must leave the old home I've kept all my life." Su asked the address — it was the very house he had just bought. He immediately took out the deed and burned it in front of her. The next day, he summoned her son and told him to let his mother move back — without demanding repayment.

Lin Yutang's verdict: "A good deed — that was all." Su returned to the city empty-handed, 500 strings poorer, but "a sudden impulse of kindness seized him, irresistible, and he forgot his own family's needs."

04
WANDER

The Hunger Journey: A Poet Compared Himself to a Hungry Rat

During the transfer, Su's family was so poor they could barely eat — in his poems, he compared himself to a hungry rat gnawing things all night, while his children's cries shook the heavens.

The court delayed approving his settlement in Changzhou. Forced to travel toward the capital, he arrived at Sizhou on the Huai River, where at least three poems mention hunger. He petitioned again and stayed at Zhang Fangping's home in the southern capital. Then Emperor Shenzong died — the Empress Dowager restored Sima Guang to power, and all of Wang Anshi's measures were suspended or abolished. Su's fate reversed with the political winds.

05
TRAGEDY

Loss of a Child: Chaoyun's Last Childbirth

During the wandering, Chaoyun's ten-month-old son died of illness — "My tears can still be wiped away, but the mother's weeping cannot be heard."

Su took his family down the Yangtze, arriving in Nanjing in September. Chaoyun's son was only ten months old when he died. This was a devastating blow to the young mother — Su's elegy says she "lay rigid all day." Chaoyun never bore another child. This tragedy, layered upon their homeless wandering, made these years of exile all the more desolate.

"My tears can still be wiped away, but the mother's weeping cannot be heard." — Su Dongpo, mourning Chaoyun's son

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

During his years of exile, Su Dongpo did two things that defined his character: facing his political enemy Wang Anshi, he spoke frankly yet with grace — demonstrating the Confucian ideal of opposing the deed, not the person; and burning a house deed to help an old widow when he could barely feed his own family — proving that kindness is not a luxury of the well-off but a choice made in scarcity. Homeless, bereaved, hungry — through it all, he held onto himself.
