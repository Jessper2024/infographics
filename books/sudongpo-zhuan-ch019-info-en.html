The Gay Genius · Ch.19 "Years of Exile"

From Huangzhou to Ruzhou — a long wandering, facing Wang Anshi, burning a deed, searching for home

Su Dongpo's next year and eight months revealed the truth: being an official meant no personal freedom. The emperor ordered him transferred from Huangzhou to Ruzhou, but he procrastinated "like a child avoiding school." He wandered — visiting Ziyou at Gao'an, touring Mount Lu, meeting Wang Anshi at Nanjing, and finally buying farmland at Yixing to retire. On the way, he spent his entire savings buying an old house, only to discover it was a family home sold by an unfilial son — he burned the deed on the spot and took nothing. Core question: When one loses control of destiny, how does one preserve goodness while adrift?

01
TURNING POINT
The Transfer Order: Procrastinating Like a Child Avoiding School
In the third month of 1084, the emperor ordered Su transferred from Huangzhou to Ruzhou — but he procrastinated "like a child avoiding school." Two days before the order arrived, he was still touring and planting tangerine trees. His enemies nitpicked his thank-you memorial, claiming phrases like "my startled soul not yet settled" were veiled complaints. The emperor calmly said: "I know Su Shih. There is no ill will in his heart."

02
HISTORIC MEETING
Nanjing: A Dialogue Between Two Defeated Men
In Nanjing, Su visited his political nemesis Wang Anshi — two great poets who had once clashed fiercely, both now political failures. Su reproached Wang for bringing war and persecution: "Great armies and great purges destroyed Han and Tang." Wang deflected blame to his lieutenant. Su retorted: "The emperor treated you with extraordinary courtesy. Can you respond with ordinary courtesy?" Yet amidst the criticism, they also discussed poetry and Buddhism together.

03
COMPASSION
Burning the Deed: Kindness When He Could Least Afford It
Su spent nearly his entire savings — 500 strings of cash — buying an old house at Yixing. While strolling on a moonlit night, he heard a woman weeping: "My ancestral home of over a hundred years has been sold by my unfilial son." Su asked the address — it was the very house he had just bought. He took out the deed and burned it before her eyes. The next day he summoned her son and let the old mother move back, not demanding the money back. Su returned to town with empty hands.

04
HARDSHIP
The Hungry Journey: A Poet Compared Himself to a Starving Rat
During the transfer journey, Su's family was so impoverished they couldn't get enough to eat — he compared himself to a starving rat gnawing at things all night, as his children screamed with hunger. The court delayed approving his request to settle in Changzhou. Forced to drag his large family toward the capital, he wrote at least three poems mentioning hunger near Sizhou. Then Emperor Shenzong died — the political landscape shifted dramatically: the Empress Dowager restored Sima Guang, and all of Wang's reforms were suspended or abolished.

05
TRAGEDY
Loss of a Child: Chaoyun's Last Childbirth
Amidst the wandering, Chaoyun's ten-month-old son fell ill and died — "My tears can still be wiped away, but the mother's weeping cannot be heard." This was a devastating blow to the young mother Chaoyun; Su wrote in his mourning poem that she "lay rigid all day." It was among the most heartbreaking lines he ever wrote. Chaoyun never bore another child. This tragedy, layered atop homelessness, hunger, and wandering, deepened the desolation of these exile years.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
In his years of exile, Su Dongpo did two things that define his character: when facing his nemesis Wang Anshi, he spoke truthfully yet maintained dignity — showing the gentleman's art of separating the person from the issue. And when he could least afford it, he burned the deed to help an old woman — proving that kindness is not the charity of abundance but the choice made in scarcity. Wandering, loss of a child, hunger — through all of it, he preserved himself.
