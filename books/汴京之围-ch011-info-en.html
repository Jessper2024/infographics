The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.11 "The Absurdity of War"

War Hawks Ascendant · The Camp Raid Farce · Mass Protest · Jurchen Withdrawal 200,000 relief troops converged on Bianjing. Generals Zhong Shidao and Li Gang advocated encircling and annihilating the Jurchen. But Yao Pingzhong, greedy for glory, launched a night raid on the Jurchen camp — secretly backed by the emperor, yet leaked citywide beforehand. Student Chen Dong led tens of thousands in a siege of the palace, killing eunuchs and forcing the emperor to reinstate Li Gang — the first popular coup in Northern Song history.

This chapter records the most absurd month of the Jingkang era (January–February 1126). On one hand, over 200,000 northwestern relief troops arrived — including veteran generals Zhong Shidao and Yao Pingzhong — giving the Song a 10:3 numerical advantage over the 60,000 Jurchen. On the other hand, Yao Pingzhong, with the emperor's secret backing, launched a night raid on the Jurchen camp on February 1 — yet the date was chosen by a diviner and leaked to the entire city, with victory banners raised in advance. After the raid failed, the emperor blamed the uninvolved Li Gang and Zhong Shidao, dismissing the war hawks. Student Chen Dong led hundreds of scholars and tens of thousands of citizens to kneel at Xuande Gate demanding reinstatement — the furious crowd killed eunuchs and dismembered their bodies. Under popular threat, Qinzong was forced to restore Li Gang. For the first time in 160 years of Northern Song rule, imperial authority yielded to the street. The Jurchen, spooked by the relief army's might, accelerated withdrawal — but took Prince Su as hostage and the cession edict for the Three Towns.

01
War Hawks Rise
200,000 Relief Troops Converge: Zhong Shidao's One Speech Shook the Court
On January 21, Zhong Shidao and Yao Pingzhong arrived with elite northwestern troops — the relief army numbered 200,000 against 60,000 Jurchen, a 10:3 advantage. Zhong was stunned upon seeing the capital: "The walls stretch over eighty li — how could the Jurchen encircle it? The walls are tens of zhang high, grain can last years!" He demolished peace-party minister Li Bangyan's excuse that "we made peace because we had no troops": "Battle requires real soldiers, but defense is different — the capital has millions of civilians who can defend. How can you say there are no troops?" Li Gang proposed the strategy that Zhou Yafu used to suppress the Seven Kingdoms Rebellion: hold the Yellow River fords, cut enemy supply lines, fortify and refuse battle, then strike when the enemy retreats exhausted. The plan: launch the general offensive on February 6. Yet a surreal parallel universe operated: while generals sharpened their blades, the emperor continued requisitioning gold and even sent dancing girls to entertain the Jurchen.

02
Camp Raid Farce
Yao Pingzhong's Glory Raid: Emperor-Backed, Diviner-Dated, Citywide-Leaked
On the night of February 1, Deputy Commander Yao Pingzhong led 7,000 troops against the Jurchen camp — a "secret operation" backed by the emperor, dated by a diviner, and known to every household in the capital. Yao, fearing he'd be lost in the crowd during the planned February 6 offensive, persuaded Qinzong to let him attack alone and capture Wolibu. Even more absurd: the date was chosen by diviner Chu Tianjue — and the information immediately leaked. Every household in Bianjing knew there would be battle on February 1. The emperor erected three giant "Imperial Victory" banners at Kaibao Temple and built an imperial reviewing pavilion atop Fengqiu Gate to inspect prisoners. Result: the Jurchen camp was mostly empty — they'd gotten wind of the plan and encircled the Song troops from outside. Yao fled, his deputy Yang Kesheng was captured. Prince Kang Zhao Gou, held hostage in the camp, thought he was dead — saved only because Yang carried a letter claiming he'd acted without the emperor's knowledge, matching Zhang Bangchang's explanation.

03
Popular Coup
Xuande Gate Mass Protest: Imperial Authority Yielded to the Street for the First Time
On February 5, student Chen Dong led hundreds of scholars kneeling at Xuande Gate, demanding Li Gang and Zhong Shidao's reinstatement and the dismissal of the peace party — tens of thousands soon gathered, their shouts shaking the earth. Peace-party leader Li Bangyan, leaving court, was surrounded, cursed, and had his boots torn off before fleeing. Kaifeng's prefect accused the students of "coercing the Son of Heaven" — the retort: "Is coercing with loyalty not better than coercing with treachery?" The palace guard commander warned the emperor: if the crowd's demands weren't met, there would be a popular uprising today. When eunuch Zhu Gongzhi was sent to announce the decision, he was torn apart before speaking a word — "Killing eunuchs is no crime!" echoed at the palace gates. Over a dozen people were killed, beheaded, and their heads mounted on poles. Only when Li Gang was urgently reinstated and rushed to the scene was the uprising quelled. Li Gang immediately restored the freedom to fire cannons — "heavy reward for every hit" — and the next day's Jurchen probe attack was repulsed. He then executed gang leaders and imposed military discipline, restoring order.

04
Diplomatic Absurdity
Zheng Wangzhi's Night of Life and Death: Interrogated Alone, Prince Kang Wept
After the failed raid, the enraged Jurchen summoned the hostages — Prince Kang and Zhang Bangchang barely survived, but envoy Zheng Wangzhi was taken alone for interrogation. Zheng was led through the Jurchen camp's winding paths — a straight-line distance of a dozen li that wound for sixty or seventy li. Through a corridor of blades, the Jurchen commander declared: "You were the first to negotiate. Today, soldiers have clashed — you cannot escape death." He demanded Zheng confess the entire raid conspiracy. Zheng argued desperately: if he'd known in advance, why would he have stayed in the camp on raid day? He was detained overnight and released the next day. Prince Kang, believing him dead, had shed many tears. Meanwhile, Qinzong blamed the uninvolved Li Gang and Zhong Shidao for the failure, dismissing Li Gang. Absurdly — the raid had only lost about a thousand men, Li Gang's relief force barely a hundred. Yao Pingzhong wasn't dead either — he'd simply fled, fearing Zhong Shidao would punish him for acting without orders.

05
Withdrawal & Regret
Jurchen Withdrawal Accelerated: Prince Su for Prince Kang, Yuwen Xuzhong Wept but Couldn't Save the Three Towns
The "failed" raid had an unintended effect — the Jurchen, intimidated by the relief army's might, accelerated their withdrawal. Wolibu stopped haggling over ransom payments and focused entirely on getting home safely. He demanded a hostage swap: the emperor's uncle Prince Yue for Prince Kang, plus a top-ranking minister. Yuwen Xuzhong braved three round-trips to the hostile Jurchen camp, weeping but unable to save the Three Towns — the cession edict and maps were formally delivered on his final visit. Li Gang's effort to withhold these orders failed completely. Qinzong swapped his full brother Prince Su for Prince Kang — Prince Su thus became the first imperial prince taken north by the Jurchen. A year later, he joined the captive Emperors Huizong and Qinzong at Minzhong Temple in Yanjing, and died at Wuguocheng in 1130. Prince Kang Zhao Gou escaped — he would later become Emperor Gaozong of the Southern Song. The Jurchen broke camp on February 10, crossing the Yellow River in a single day. Zhong Shidao, Li Gang, and Shen Guan all begged to pursue — but the peace party's fear overrode all military logic.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Everything that happened between January and February 1126 perfectly encapsulates "the absurdity of war." Northern Song had 200,000 relief troops — triple the enemy's strength — plus clear-headed war hawks like Zhong Shidao and Li Gang. Yet victory was squandered by the emperor's terror, Yao Pingzhong's glory-seeking, political scapegoating, and a "secret raid" leaked to the entire city. The most surreal scene unfolded on February 5: tens of thousands of citizens killed eunuchs at Xuande Gate and forced the emperor to reinstate Li Gang — a rare popular coup in imperial Chinese history. But even with Li Gang restored, the Three Towns' cession edict had already been issued, and Prince Su had taken Prince Kang's place as hostage. The scales had tipped irreversibly toward annihilation.
