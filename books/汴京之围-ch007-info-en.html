The Siege of Bianjing · Interlude "The Other Version of Recovering Yanjing"

Xuanhe Year 5 · Lies, Celebration & the Death of Aguda In Tong Guan's memorials to the throne, the Yanjing campaign was a string of "great victories"; the real history was a rout, a buyout, and an empty city. As the sovereign and his ministers reveled in the illusion of surpassing the Zhou and Han dynasties, Aguda's death brought Jin's hawks onto the stage of history.

There exist two starkly different versions of how the Song recovered Yanjing. In the memorials Tong Guan submitted to the emperor, the northern expedition was a dazzling string of "great victories" — from the fifth month to the twelfth, roughly one battle won every ten days, with the people of Yanjing joyfully welcoming the imperial army. In the real version, the Song army was routed by a Liao force a fraction of its size and had to "buy" an empty city back from the Jin at an astronomical price. While the Song sovereign and his ministers drowned in celebration — Tong Guan was enfeoffed as a duke, Cai You as a Preceptor, Wang Fu as Grand Mentor — only a few clear-headed individuals sounded the alarm: Zhao Liangsi predicted peace would last three years at most, Zheng Juzhong refused his ennoblement, and Ma Kuo urgently called for defensive fortifications. Yet the most fatal turning point came in the fifth month of that year: the Jin founding emperor Wanyan Aguda died. His parting words — "Once I am dead, do as you please" — formally announced the arrival of the hawk era.

01
Historical Narrative
One war, two starkly different versions: Tong Guan's "nine consecutive victories" vs. real history
Had the Jingkang Incident never occurred, Song official history would have followed Tong Guan's narrative, recording Emperor Huizong's "great achievements" amid a chorus of victory cheers. Tong Guan recorded every battle in his memorials as a "great victory" — great victory on May 22, great victory on May 25, great victory on May 29... His fabricated narrative even included: Guo Yaoshi surrendered because he was moved by Tong Guan's charisma; Yizhou was occupied because the Song army had killed nearly all the Khitans in the city; Yanjing was recovered through "joint attack" by Jin and Song forces. Even more stunningly, he compared Huizong's accomplishments to those of the Zhou and Han dynasties — "The Zhou campaign against the Xianyun only reached Taiyuan; the Han strike against the Xiongnu went no further than the Wei River; Emperor Huizong, marching all the way to Yanjing, has surpassed all previous dynasties."
📜

Tong Guan's Memorial Version

A string of great victories, Guo Yaoshi charismatically won over, Song and Jin jointly recovering Yanjing. Huizong's achievements surpass Zhou and Han — this was the "official narrative" being prepared for the official histories.

📖

The Real Yanjing Campaign

Rout at Baigou, Liu Yanqing burning camp and fleeing, buying an empty city at an astronomical price. The one bright spot — Guo Yaoshi's surprise assault — was destroyed by Liu Guangshi's failure to reinforce.

02
Power Festivities
Post-recovery revelry: everyone celebrating, except a few ill-timed clear-headed souls
Tong Guan was promoted to Duke of Xu-Yu, Cai You was enfeoffed as Junior Preceptor, Wang Fu as Grand Mentor — even the engineer Zhu Mian, who had transported the "Pan Gu Marquis" rock, was made a military commissioner. Grand Chancellor Wang Fu submitted celebratory memorials; the hundred officials vied to offer congratulations. Amid this nationwide revelry, three people kept their heads: Zheng Juzhong refused the position of Grand Guardian on the grounds of "having rendered no service"; Zhao Liangsi privately predicted peace would last only three years and petitioned to retire and farm, only to be rejected by the emperor; Ma Kuo proposed that with just 400,000 strings of cash and one month, a solid defensive system could be built for Yanjing — his proposal was drowned out by the congratulatory memorials. Tong Guan stationed himself in Yanjing for less than half a month before hastily withdrawing, leaving defense to Zhan Du and Guo Yaoshi.

03
Personal Fate
The man who first proposed allying with Jin to destroy Liao ended up as everyone's scapegoat
Zhao Liangsi was named Academician of Yankang Hall, yet he saw through the danger behind the revelry. He privately predicted that after recovering Yanjing, peace could be maintained for three years at most, and requested retirement — "Buying a little farmland and living off it would be enough." He even fantasized about someone seeing him farming and remarking behind his back: "That is the chief architect of the Yanjing recovery, and now, seeking no wealth or rank, he farms at home — what a beautiful story for the ages!" But the emperor firmly refused to let him retire. Everything that followed proved his pessimism right: he was demoted for opposing the acceptance of Zhang Jue, reviled by the entire court, and bears infamy to this day. And Ma Kuo — the warrior whom the Jin respected more — was appointed Grand Master of Military Merits and continued calling for stronger military preparedness, to which no one paid any attention.

Zhao Liangsi's Wish

Ma Kuo's Warning

Repair walls, resettle refugees, train soldiers, redistribute abandoned farmland — 400,000 strings could build a solid defense. But "everyone was celebrating," and no one listened.

04
Power Transition
The death of Aguda: the era of peace ends, and the hawk Nianhan takes the stage
In the third month of Xuanhe Year 5, while demonstrating that elaborate set of Han-style rituals, Ma Kuo keenly observed that Aguda was already gravely ill. In the fifth month, the Jin founding emperor passed away. Aguda was the last guardian of the Song-Jin treaty — he had rejected Nianhan's proposal to recognize only Zhuozhou and Yizhou, insisting on returning Yanjing according to the Maritime Alliance. Yet he also left behind that fatal remark: "Once I am dead, do as you please." The throne passed to his younger brother Wuqimai. The handover of the prefectures north of the mountains abruptly halted in the sixth month. The hawks who had been suppressed under Aguda — led by Nianhan — rapidly rose to power. They had been tested in war and had tasted the prosperity of the south; more importantly, the Song's weakness had converted even Nianhan, originally favoring peace, to a hardline stance.

05
Deeper Lesson
Fabricated victories create real defeats: how lies destroyed an empire
Tong Guan's lies were not merely personal vanity — they systematically paralyzed the Song's early-warning mechanism. If Emperor Huizong had heard not of "nine consecutive victories" but of "the rout at Baigou, burning camp and fleeing," he might have taken Ma Kuo's defense proposals seriously. The false victory narrative created disaster on at least three levels: it made the emperor overestimate the Song army's combat capability and underestimate the Jin threat; it allowed officers who should have been punished (Liu Yanqing) to continue commanding troops, while defenses that should have been strengthened were neglected; and it trapped the entire bureaucracy in a vicious cycle of "reporting only good news." Xu Kangzong returned with intelligence that the Jin were about to invade south, but the emperor's edict — "those who recklessly speak of border affairs shall be exiled three thousand li, not exempted even by general amnesty" — shut his mouth. A celebration built on lies ultimately ended with the real catastrophe of the Jingkang Humiliation.
🔕

The Consequences of Lies

Liu Yanqing faced no court-martial, the defensive system was never built, 170+ border alerts were ignored. The early-warning mechanism was systematically paralyzed.

💀

The Price of Truth

Xu Kangzong returned home intending to report the Jin's imminent southern invasion — the emperor had already decreed: "Those who recklessly speak of border affairs shall be exiled three thousand li, not exempted even by general amnesty."

🔑 Key Takeaway
"The Other Version of Recovering Yanjing" is a parable about how lies kill an empire. Tong Guan's "nine consecutive victories" narrative was not harmless self-deception — it built the nation's entire defense system on fiction. When real defeats were packaged as great triumphs, early-warning signals were systematically filtered out, and decision-makers saw a distorted mirror. Zhao Liangsi saw the disaster three years ahead, Ma Kuo calculated the 400,000 strings needed for defense, Xu Kangzong brought back intelligence of the Jin's imminent southern invasion — but every voice was drowned out by the celebration. And Aguda's death closed the window of peace from the outside. When all the information channels of an empire serve only to make the emperor happy, destruction is only a matter of time.
