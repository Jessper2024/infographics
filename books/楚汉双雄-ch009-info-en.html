The Chu-Han Contention · Chapter Nine 「Han Xin's Choice: Divide the Empire or Repay Gratitude」

Wu She's three-kingdom proposal, Kuai Tong's lethal persuasion, Hou Gong's mysterious Honggou peace — the historic pivot point

In 203 BC, after Han Xin pacified Qi, Xiang Yu humbled himself for the first time and sent Wu She to persuade Han Xin to divide the empire into three. An even deadlier persuader, Kuai Tong, followed with his maxim “What Heaven offers, refusing invites calamity,” analyzing the situation from both strategic and human-nature dimensions. Han Xin, driven by the core value that “a scholar dies for one who understands him,” chose to bet on Liu Bang's character — giving up the chance to become a third hegemon. At the critical moment, the mysterious Hou Gong volunteered and single-handedly persuaded Xiang Yu to split the realm at Honggou and return Liu Bang's father and wife before vanishing into obscurity. Han Xin's choice and Hou Gong's miracle pushed the Chu-Han contest toward its finale.

01
命运抉择
Wu She's Appeal: Xiang Yu Lowers His Proud Head for the First Time
After Long Ju's death, Xiang Yu felt unprecedented danger. Liu Bang to the west, Zhang Er to the north, Han Xin to the east, Peng Yue everywhere — he was surrounded. For the first time since rising to power, Xiang Yu lowered his proud head and sent envoys to persuade Han Xin to stay neutral. Wu She's analysis was penetrating: “Liu Bang is insatiable; he won't stop until he devours the entire realm. You haven't been destroyed only because the King of Xiang is pinning him down. The deciding chip for this realm rests in your hands. If King Xiang perishes, you're next.” But Han Xin replied resolutely — the King of Han gave him the seal of supreme command, shared his clothes and food, heeded every counsel. Liu Bang's investment in Han Xin paid off at this critical moment.
02
终极游说
Kuai Tong's Lethal Persuasion: The Three Kingdoms Era Almost Arrived 400 Years Early
Kuai Tong was the deadliest persuader of the late Qin era. He began with “I have studied physiognomy,” precisely capturing the powerful man's curiosity about the occult. After Han Xin dismissed his attendants, Kuai Tong delivered his lethal blow: “Reading your front face, at most a marquisate, precarious and unstable. But reading your back — ineffably noble!” He argued from three dimensions — strategy, human nature, and historical precedent. Zhang Er and Chen Yu were sworn brothers who ended up killing each other. Wen Zhong helped Goujian dominate, then died by Goujian's hand. “When the birds are gone, good bows are stored; when hares are dead, hounds are boiled.” Han Xin's achievements had grown so vast that neither Chu nor Han could trust him — China nearly entered a Three Kingdoms era 400 years early.
03
价值抉择
A Scholar Dies for His Confidant: Why Han Xin Abandoned Dividing the Empire
Kuai Tong's persuasion was extraordinarily brilliant, advancing layer by layer through strategy, human nature, and the iron laws of history. But a single idea ran through Han Xin's entire life: a scholar dies for one who truly understands him. Values fixed at age three determine destiny at eighty — your core value system is always the most critical factor deciding your life's direction and choices. Han Xin's dream was “unmatched achievements, eternal renown” — and part of that renown was the unwritten story of “ruler and minister in perfect harmony.” He was like an unchained tiger who only wanted to lie still, but no one could permit that. Like Xiang Yu, he chose to be personal rather than principled. Kuai Tong, seeing through everything, immediately feigned madness, became a shaman, and withdrew from the stage of history — surviving to a peaceful end.
04
神秘和议
Hou Gong: The Mysterious Persuader Who Settled Chu and Han in One Breath
Liu Bang wanted to seize the favorable situation and negotiate peace. Lu Jia was sent as envoy but rejected — Xiang Yu, though in a disadvantaged position, couldn't care less. At the critical juncture, a never-before-mentioned wandering scholar named Hou Gong volunteered. First he convinced Liu Bang (who had initially cursed him out), then entered the Chu camp and successfully persuaded Xiang Yu to split the realm along Honggou and return Liu Taigong and Lü Zhi. This miraculous figure who settled both Chu and Han leaders in one breath left only fragmentary records — he refused Liu Bang's title of Marquis Pacifying the State and vanished into seclusion. He departed as quietly as he came, sweeping the dark clouds southward with a wave of his sleeve.
05
人性洞察
Why Xiang Yu Never Admitted Defeat — The War God's Ultimate Confidence
Behind Liu Bang's peace initiative lay deep terror: as long as Xiang Yu lived, Liu Bang could not rest. But Xiang Yu never took him seriously. Julu: outnumbered, crushed the Qin. Pengcheng: outnumbered, crushed the coalition. Three years of Chu-Han stalemate: never lost a single battle. Wherever the King of Xiang was, the Chu army was invincible. Xiang Yu's only frustration was that Liu Bang kept slipping away. These years, Xiang Yu's looming shadow kept Liu Bang perpetually on edge. But from the War God's perspective, what did tactical disadvantage matter? Victory was certain — the only variable was how many enemies to kill. Xiang Yu had never, from beginning to end, regarded Liu Bang as a worthy opponent.
📌 KEY TAKEAWAY
Han Xin's pivotal choice reveals life's most important lesson: values determine destiny. Every word Kuai Tong spoke has been vindicated by history — when the birds are gone, good bows are stored; Han Xin's final fate was tragically miserable. Yet his choice to “die for one who understands him” was not foolishness but an uncompromisable conviction embedded in his soul. Every major life decision is constrained by your deepest values — you can change techniques but not the foundational code written in your youth. Moreover, Xiang Yu's confidence illustrates a counterintuitive truth: winning every battle is not a genuine advantage; it merely postpones the inevitable detonation of accumulated vulnerabilities. Vulnerabilities can only be postponed, never self-eliminated.
