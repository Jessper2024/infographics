Fupan · Chapter 11 — Not Bumped in Vain

SCQA · Core Insight After the 2008 Mid-Autumn Festival, Ah Jiu fainted at the office and hit a wall corner — 5cm gash, 5 stitches, hundreds in medical costs, and nearly a month of recovery. But he didn't let the suffering go to waste: through fupan, he traced the causal chain from chest tightness → dizziness → vomiting, found the root cause in festival overeating that disrupted his stomach's equilibrium, and derived a universal philosophy — health is balance, illness is disruption of balance, not poor health.

This is a story of how blood became wisdom. Ah Jiu was a healthy thirty-something — regular routines, frequent exercise, never sick. On the first workday after Mid-Autumn Festival, he suddenly felt dizzy and vomited at his desk. As he felt better and got up to clean, he fainted — his head hit the sharp protruding corner where two walls met. Five centimeters of gash, five stitches. Afterward, Ah Jiu didn't let the suffering go to waste. He replayed the entire process like a case analysis: chest tightness occurred because his stomach drew blood from the nearby chest area; dizziness followed as blood was drawn from further — the head; once enough blood energy gathered, the stomach expelled its contents. Blood returned, the episode subsided. The mistake: standing up too quickly before head blood flow normalized — causing fainting and the wall collision. Digging deeper, he found the root cause: Mid-Autumn overeating had disrupted his stomach's long-established equilibrium. From this he derived a profound insight: illness isn't poor health — it's broken balance.

03
Deep Root Cause
Fupan Layer 2: Breaking Balance Is the Real Cause
Ah Jiu didn't stop at symptoms — he asked "why was the stomach uncomfortable?": the Mid-Autumn dinner was too rich — preserved fish and meat, chicken legs and eggs, mooncakes and rice, red wine and baijiu, cold dishes and hot drinks — stuffed to bursting. His normal evening meal was simple tomato-egg noodles or egg fried rice. The festival binge broke the stomach's habitual long-term balance, exceeding muscle memory. Only his youth prevented an immediate episode that night.

04
Philosophy Distilled
From One Fainting Spell to a Philosophy: Balance Is Health
From a single head-bump incident, Ah Jiu distilled a universal life philosophy: "Health isn't absolute — the key is maintaining balance. Balance is the result of organs mutually adapting over long periods; it's their optimal working environment. When balance is broken beyond tolerance, an organ rebels and forcibly repairs itself — this manifests as 'illness.' So-called 'illness' is broken balance, not poor health."
