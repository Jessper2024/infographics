01
Battle of Lingbi: Three Cannon Shots, Fatal Coincidence
Ping An and He He combined forces at Lingbi, but their supply train was destroyed by Zhu Di — soldiers were starving and exhausted. They decided to break out at dawn, agreeing on "three cannon shots" as the signal. At first light, Southern soldiers were packing to flee when three cannon shots rang out — they assumed it was their own breakout signal and ran. But those three shots were the Northern Army's进攻 signal! Both sides had chosen the exact same code. The Northern Army pursued and completely annihilated the Southern main force, capturing 37 generals. Only He He escaped alone on horseback. The Jingnan Campaign's final obstacle was removed.

02
Crossing the Yangtze: Traitors Open Jinchuan Gate
After Lingbi, Zhu Di swept south: Yangzhou surrendered without a fight, he crossed the Yangtze and reached the capital's outskirts. At Puzikou, Sheng Yong briefly held the Northern Army back — but Zhu Gaoxu arrived with reinforcements. Zhu Di slapped his son's back: "The heir is unwell — work hard!" Zhu Gaoxu understood this as a succession promise and fought ferociously, enabling the Northern Army to cross the Yangtze. The capital's walls were formidable (built with granite and sticky rice mortar), but the Jinchuan Gate was held by Gu Wang Zhu Xi and Li Jinglong — both opened the gates simultaneously. Li Jinglong evolved: from "privileged brat" to "military idiot" to "double agent" — the Jingnan Campaign's greatest internal saboteur.

03
Martyrs: From Fang Xiaoru to the Woodcutter of Taizhou
Zhu Di's bloody purge began immediately after taking the capital. Fang Xiaoru — a scholar-official of national renown — refused to write the accession edict, writing only four characters: "Yan thief usurps the throne." Zhu Di executed him by lingchi and, uniquely in Chinese history, exterminated ten clans — including his students and friends. Tie Xuan was executed by lingchi; his son killed; wife and daughters forced into the brothel. Huang Guan, another triple-zhuangyuan, jumped into the Yangtze with his wife and two daughters. At Taizhou, an unnamed woodcutter — who had no connection to politics at all — heard the capital had fallen and drowned himself in East Lake. Integrity is not an exclusive property of scholar-officials. Ordinary people can be heroes too — if they have courage.

04
The Mystery: Zhu Yunwen's 600-Year Disappearance
Before entering the capital, Zhu Di ordered his troops to stop outside the palace walls — he did not want to be blamed for killing his nephew. He gave Zhu Yunwen time to abdicate or commit suicide. But Zhu Yunwen burned the palace and vanished — neither abdicated nor died, only escaped. His whereabouts became one of history's greatest mysteries, spawning endless speculation. This mystery directly motivated Zheng He's voyages — part of their mission was to locate the missing Jianwen Emperor across the seas. Zhu Yunwen understood that the emperor's position was a public target, requiring constant vigilance against all threats. High altitude is cold — everyone knows this, but no one stops climbing.

05
Cruelty and Weakness: Decoding Zhu Di's Duality
Zhu Di's cruelty was directed exclusively at opponents — and this precisely revealed his insecurity. Julius Caesar did not punish his wife's lover, confident in his own position; Zhu Di, by contrast, killed and rewrote history to secure his throne. Tie Xuan, after having his ears and nose cut off and cooked, was asked: "Does it taste good?" He replied: "The flesh of a loyal subject and filial son — why would it not taste good?" Then he was executed by lingchi. True strength never needs terror to sustain it — tolerance is the highest form of confidence. Zhu Di was, in truth, a pale-faced middle-aged man on a gleaming throne, watching those around him with vigilance, constantly repeating: "This is my throne. Do not come near."

🔑 Key Takeaway
Lingbi was the final act of the Jingnan Campaign — 37 Southern generals captured, main force completely destroyed, Zhu Di at the capital gates. Traitors Li Jinglong and Gu Wang Zhu Xi opened Jinchuan Gate, delivering the city without a fight. Zhu Di's bloody purge followed: Fang Xiaoru's unprecedented ten-clan execution, Tie Xuan's brutal lingchi, hundreds of loyalists perished. Zhu Yunwen's 600-year-old disappearance mystery partly drove Zheng He's voyages in search of the missing emperor. Zhu Di's cruelty — far from proving strength — laid bare his insecurity: an illegitimate throne can only be held through terror. True power never requires fear to sustain it — tolerance is the ultimate proof of confidence. The victor writes history. The rule is: "The winner becomes king; the loser becomes a bandit."
