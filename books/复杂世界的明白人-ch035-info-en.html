Brazil's middle-income trap × Japan's high-income trap — technology breakthrough is the only way out

Why does it feel like opportunities are disappearing? Because the tech dividend is running dry. Brazil and Japan represent two kinds of traps: Brazil is the middle-income trap — little industry, extreme polarization, housing prices that are actually falling, everyone living day to day. Japan is the high-income trap — a society as precise as a program, nobody dares switch jobs, everyone stays at one company for life. The root cause: neither caught the mobile internet explosion, their societies weren't reshuffled, the big companies from twenty years ago are still the big companies today. China stands at a crossroads: if chips can break through upward, the technology tree keeps climbing, new opportunities will emerge. Otherwise, we'll quickly fall into a \"low-opportunity trap,\" frozen in stagnation at a per-capita GDP far below Japan's.

01
Comparison
Brazil and Japan — two models of societal sclerosis
Brazil is the middle-income trap; Japan is the high-income trap — both are what happens when the dividend runs out and society freezes. Brazilians live in the moment — the word \"overtime\" barely exists in Brazil, the land is covered with fruit trees, nobody starves. But the country is split between extreme rich and poor, wealthy neighborhoods adjacent to favelas, decades without social change. Japan is the polar opposite — a precision society where everyone is a pre-set program: graduate, join a company, stay forever, promotion by seniority, switching jobs = restarting as the \"new guy\" at 40. Everyone wears a permanent fake smile; beneath the polite surface lies a suffocating social hierarchy.

02
Mechanism
Tech dividend exhaustion — the root cause of societal sclerosis
Neither Brazil nor Japan caught the mobile internet explosion — their social structures were never disrupted, never reshuffled. The big companies from twenty years ago are still the big companies today — no new giants emerge, ordinary people can't benefit from reshuffling. When an industry's dividend is exhausted, you can't even jump ship — stuck at one company climbing seniority. China is already seeing the early signs: Taobao, JD, and Pinduoduo have carved up the online retail pie; only a few phone manufacturers remain; one dominant player each in long-video, short-video, and social — this is the hallmark of a maturing, stabilizing market. Once giants sink roots in mature fields, they can stay there for decades or centuries.

03
National Destiny
Tunnel boring machines × Chips — how tech breakthroughs change a nation's fate
Before China successfully developed TBMs, Germany just lay back and collected — whatever price they set, we had to pay. Once R&D succeeded, a single machine can support tens of thousands of families, hundreds of thousands of people, generate foreign currency from overseas sales, and fund more advanced equipment R&D — the \"production → market → R&D\" industrial flywheel starts spinning. The same story is unfolding in chips: SMIC's USTC + US PhDs are returning to China, with two project teams already underway. \"Chips aren't easy, but they're not impossibly hard either — the hardest parts are 'cost-effectiveness' and 'cost.' When a mature supply chain already exists, even if you produce it, nobody buys. Now that the US has stopped selling, we have no choice but to build our own.\"

04
The Trap
The essence of the middle-income trap — technology locked, condemned to wage labor
The middle-income trap is what happens when technology is blocked and you can't climb further — forever eating only what others toss you. Too many people, too little meat, inevitably descending into brutal involution. High-tech fields are the cash cows of Western nations — they absolutely refuse to let outsiders grab a share. But China can't afford not to climb — our per-capita GDP is nowhere near Japan's level yet. If we freeze now, the consequences will be far worse than Japan's. \"The dignity of the little guy\" is completely linked to national rise — if the country can't develop, everyone is emaciated, eating meat only once a year at New Year's.

05
The Only Way
Beyond climbing upward, there is no other path
We can only frantically add tech points at every level — engineering, technology, management — otherwise we'll quickly fall into a \"low-opportunity trap.\" Globalization won't meaningfully reverse, but tech blockades are a certainty. \"Without indigenous R&D, we definitely can't go on — we'd quickly descend into involution, then rapidly freeze. Before 2018, almost no one considered China producing chips from scratch. But now a consensus has essentially formed: having no choice is itself a choice.\" The challenge: if housing prices keep surging, it breeds an arbitrage mentality — research funds diverted to real estate, ordinary people saving every penny for housing instead of consuming, entrepreneurs not creating wealth but flipping properties.
