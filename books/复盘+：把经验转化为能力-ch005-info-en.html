01
WHAT & WHY
Definition & Value of Project Retrospective
Projects are a natural fit for retrospectives — whether during or after completion, you can always learn, master patterns, and build capability. A project retrospective is a structured process of systematic review and reflection, aimed at understanding project patterns and key elements, enhancing project management and organizational collaboration. As former BP CEO John Browne noted: "Most activities are not one-off events. Our goal is simple: every time we repeat something, we do it a little better than before."
⚡

BP AAR FRAMEWORK
BP's After Action Review: 7 Guiding Principles
The After Action Review is a structured team discussion process, emphasizing facilitation, role clarity, and atmosphere building to promote full participation. BP calls AAR the "After Action Review," a core mechanism for "learning by doing." A candid, pragmatic AAR session typically takes no more than 20 minutes, conducted immediately after the event concludes.

12 SECRETS
12 Secrets of Project Retrospective
BP's 12 battle-tested operational secrets cover the entire process from convening meetings to distributing records, ensuring retrospectives deliver real value. Project retrospectives go deeper than AARs: they require systematic planning and preparation, take longer, and produce more comprehensive analysis; they are forward-looking, specifically distilling lessons for future projects; and they invite client participation to gain deeper understanding of causes and effects.

04
MULTI-ITERATIVE MODEL
Multi-Iterative Retrospective Operating Model
Large projects must adopt a multi-iterative retrospective — layered and phased, embedding retrospectives into project operations rather than doing one at the end. Retrospecting only at project end has three major problems: facts become blurred over time, personnel changes cause information gaps, and accumulated issues make discussions difficult. The solution is a "layered × phased" systematic approach.
Layered
Sub-team / functional line reviews
↓
Project team level reviews
Leads + key members participate
Phased
Critical events → Periodic reviews
Stage reviews → Overall review
Embedded across full lifecycle

05
SUCCESS FACTORS
Key Success Factors: Making Retrospectives Stick
For project retrospectives to be effective and sustained, four key factors must be mastered: leadership commitment, clear accountability, accumulation over time, and integration into work. With busy workloads, retrospectives cannot become mere formalities. Lenovo's ERP project had two successive project directors personally attend and drive results; at Legend Capital, from the president to Liu Chuanzhi himself, project retrospectives received top-level attention. As Laozi said: "Begin difficult tasks while they are easy; accomplish great things while they are small."
👑

Leadership + Accountability

Leaders personally attend and drive follow-through; designate dedicated owners, each team lead organizes their own team's review; investing dedicated time and resources is worthwhile

🌱

Accumulation + Integration

Start with simple event reviews, progress gradually; retrospectives are not extra tasks but a fundamental work method; results must be acted upon — adjust goals and plans promptly

💡 KEY INSIGHT
Project retrospectives are not an "add-on" after project completion — they are a fundamental work method embedded into project operations. By adopting the "multi-iterative" model — layered (sub-team → project team), phased (events → periodic → stage → overall) — combined with BP's 12 secrets and four success factors, scattered experiences can be systematically transformed into organizational capability, achieving the goal of "doing it a little better each time."
