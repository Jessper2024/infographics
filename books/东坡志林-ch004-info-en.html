Dongpo's Miscellany · Chapter 4「Past and Present: Historical Insights」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Su Shi was not merely a literary man but a thinker of profound historical insight. He surveyed past and present — from King Wu's conquest to Qin's abolition of feudalism, from Sima Qian's "two great crimes" to Fan Zeng's tragedy, from the ancient wax festival rites to the mystery of the Eight-Formation Diagram, from the sagely rule of Yao and Shun to children's Three Kingdoms nursery rhymes. How did a man steeped in history distill wisdom for governance from a thousand years of rise and fall? His answer: measure everything by "human feeling" — judge no ancient by moral absolutes, but understand every historical choice through ordinary human experience.

This chapter assembles Su Shi's wide-ranging historical commentaries: meditations on the past, military strategy, ancient rites, character sketches of dozens of historical figures, and essays on governance. Su Shi's historical method defied orthodoxy — his doubts about King Wu's sainthood, his analysis of Qin's abolition of feudalism, his sharp criticism of Sima Qian's motives, all show an independent thinker at work. He understood deeply: reading history is not about judging the ancients but about understanding the eternal predicament of human nature in the face of power.

Meditations
"No heroes in the age, letting brats make their names" — Su Shi Solves Ruan Ji's Riddle
Ruan Ji ascended Guangwu Castle and sighed: "No heroes in the age, letting brats make their names!" Whom was he cursing? His friend Shi Jingchen thought it was Liu Bang; Li Bai agreed. Su Shi alone offered a penetrating alternative: Ruan Ji mourned the absence of heroes like Liu Bang and Xiang Yu; "brats" referred to the people of the Wei-Jin era. Ruan Ji, though unrestrained, had worldly ambitions — he would never curse Liu Bang as a "brat." Su Shi also drew profound conclusions from children listening to Three Kingdoms tales: hearing of Liu Bei's defeat, they frowned and wept; hearing of Cao Cao's defeat, they clapped and cheered — "thus we know the legacy of noble and petty, undying across a hundred generations."

02
Strategy & Rites
Zhuge Liang's Stone Formation and Ancient Rites — Su Shi's Military and Anthropological Eye
Su Shi personally surveyed the remains of Zhuge Liang's Eight-Formation Diagram — stones arranged in eight rows on the sandy flats of Fish Belly. "Looking down from the mountain, over a hundred zhang across, eight rows forming sixty-four circles, perfectly round, with no protrusions or indentations, like the shadow of the sun at noon." Up close, they were just pebbles, "indistinguishable" — most strange. Such fieldwork was rare among Song-dynasty literati. In "The Eight Wax Sacrifices as Three Dynasties' Ritual Theater," Su Shi revealed the essence of ancient rites as theatrical performance — year-end festivities are simply human nature, and "who plays the cat and tiger corpses if not actors?" He restored sacred temple rites to their earthly origins.

03
Character Sketches
"The Matter of Yao and Shun" — A Gallery of Historical Figures Through Su Shi's Eyes
Su Shi judged historical figures without bowing to received opinion. "The Matter of Yao and Shun" questioned the myth of sage-king abdication — how could any subject calmly accept the Son of Heaven's throne? Such tales were later fabrications. "Confucius Executed Shao Zhengmao" argued this incident did not fit the sage's character. In his colophon to Li Yu's lyrics, he praised the fallen ruler's post-capitulation poetry. And his analysis of Fan Zeng was uniquely incisive: Fan Zeng should have left Xiang Yu when the latter executed Song Yi on his own authority, not wait until the Hongmen Banquet — "Zeng's departure should have occurred when Xiang Yu killed the Champion Commander."
👑

Yao & Shun: Questioning the Myth

Su Shi found the sage-king abdication narrative implausible — no subject would calmly accept the throne. He dared challenge Confucianism's most sacred narrative.

🗡️

Fan Zeng: Should Have Left Earlier

"Zeng's departure should have been when Xiang Yu killed Song Yi" — Su Shi argued that the moment Xiang Yu arbitrarily executed a commander, Fan Zeng should have seen the end.

04
Historical Essays
"Qin Abolished Feudalism" and "Sima Qian's Two Great Crimes" — Su Shi's Historical Edge
In his historical essays, Su Shi displayed his sharpest thinking. "Qin Abolished Feudalism" analyzed the historical inevitability of replacing the feudal system with centralized commanderies — pointing out this was not one man's creation but evolution's necessity. "Sima Qian's Two Great Crimes" boldly criticized the Grand Historian's motives, arguing he projected personal misfortune onto his historical writing, unfairly judging Shang Yang and Meng Tian. "King Wu Was Not a Sage" directly challenged orthodox Confucian narrative, noting that carrying King Wen's ancestral tablet to war against Zhou was ritually improper. In all his historical writings, Su Shi's compass was "human feeling" — judging no success or failure absolutely, shackling no ancient with moral absolutes.
"The sage cannot create the times, but also does not miss the times. The times are not something the sage can create — he can only avoid missing them."
— "Qin Abolished Feudalism"
05
Popular Justice
The Final Arbiter of History: The People's Heart and Common Sense
Su Shi's most distinctive historical insight was his use of the "folk perspective" as the ultimate yardstick. "Alley Children Listening to Three Kingdoms Tales" recorded the simplest historical judgment — illiterate children wept at Liu Bei's defeats and cheered at Cao Cao's. From this Su Shi concluded: "The legacy of the noble and the petty remains undying across a hundred generations." Official histories may whitewash and distort, but the moral judgment in folk memory — like an underground current flowing deep — grows clearer with time. This was the foundation of Su Shi's entire historical outlook: real historical justice dwells not in the temple but in the human heart.
👂

Folk Memory: Justice Undying

Children's instinctive responses to Three Kingdoms tales — weeping for Liu Bei, cheering for Cao Cao's defeat — reveal a simple justice no official history can overwrite. The people are history's final judges.

⚖️

"Human Feeling": Su Shi's Core Method

Judge neither by success nor by moral absolutes — understand every historical choice through ordinary human experience. This is why Su Shi surpassed nearly all historians of his time.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Su Shi's historical insights transcend his era because he always measured by "human feeling." He believed in no sage-king myths (questioning Yao and Shun), followed no official narrative (criticizing Sima Qian), worshiped no military legend (personally climbing the Eight-Formation site), but sought historical truth in the simplest human feelings and common sense. "The legacy of noble and petty, undying across a hundred generations" — this is the core of his historical vision: real historical justice is not written in official chronicles but flows through marketplace rhymes and folk memory. Eight centuries later, the instinct to "weep at Liu Bei's defeat and cheer at Cao Cao's" remains unchanged — this is precisely what Su Shi meant by "undying across a hundred generations."
