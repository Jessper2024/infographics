Great Country, Big City · Ch.3「Breaking the 'Impossible Trinity'」

Urbanization · Agglomeration · Administrative Allocation · Efficiency Loss If a large country pursues unity, efficiency, and "balanced" development (understood as uniform distribution) through administrative fiat, these three goals cannot be achieved simultaneously — this is the "impossible trinity" of great-power development.

This is Book Chapter 3. The author proposes a core theoretical framework — the "impossible trinity" of great-power development: the three goals of unity, efficiency, and balance (understood as uniform distribution) cannot be achieved simultaneously. The chapter examines the boundary between government and market in urbanization and regional development, using global spatial economic maps to prove that the world is not flat — all countries exhibit extreme economic concentration. China's Hu Huanyong Line reveals 93% of the population on 43% of the land. But the fundamental problem is that administrative allocation of resources runs counter to the direction of population flows: people move upward (to eastern big cities), while resources flow downward (to central/western regions). Since 2003, TFP growth has declined, resource allocation efficiency has deteriorated, and "land urbanization" has far outpaced "population urbanization" — all efficiency costs of pursuing uniform distribution.

01
Impossible Trinity
The "Impossible Trinity": Unity, Efficiency, and Uniform Distribution
If a country simultaneously pursues three goals — national unity, economic efficiency, and balanced regional development — and "balance" is understood as the uniform distribution of economic resources and population through administrative intervention, these three goals cannot be achieved at the same time. This is the "impossible trinity" of great-power development. The solution: redefine "balance" — shift from uniform distribution of resources to balance in per capita GDP, real income, and quality of life. This per capita balance can be achieved through free population movement without large-scale administrative controls. Conversely, insisting on uniform distribution sacrifices both efficiency and market unity.
02
Gov vs Market
Government and Market Boundaries: "Legislating" Urbanization
The debate about urbanization centers not on speed or level, but on where to urbanize. Market role: let firms and workers weigh benefits and costs. Big cities offer higher incomes and greater diversity — but also higher housing prices, longer commutes, congestion, and pollution. These costs are themselves the market's mechanism for achieving quality-of-life balance across regions. Government role: correct market failures. Amplify positive externalities (knowledge spillovers) through infrastructure and human capital investment; reduce negative externalities (congestion, pollution, crime) through technology and management. Key conclusion: no theory supports direct administrative population control, and certainly none supports classifying workers as "needed" or "unneeded."
03
Economic Geography
The World Is Not Flat: Global Spatial Economic Maps
Global evidence of economic concentration: 50% of US GDP is concentrated in a tiny red zone. Japan's three major metro areas hold over 50% of the population — the Tokyo area occupies only 3.5% of national land. The UK, France (like the Eiffel Tower, concentrated near Paris), India, Brazil, South Africa, Russia — all exhibit extreme concentration. Germany appears more dispersed, but this is a legacy of its long history of division; since reunification, it too shows "balance through agglomeration." Economic agglomeration is an inherent law. China's Hu Huanyong Line (Heihe-Tengchong) has barely shifted since 1935: 43% of land in the southeast holds 93% of the population and 95% of GDP.

04
Opposite Flows
Administrative Allocation vs. Market-Driven Migration
Over the past decade, market forces have guided people upward (eastern big cities); government intervention has pushed resources downward (central/western towns). These forces operate in opposite directions! Central resource transfers to the west focused on two channels: more construction land quotas for central/west, and more fiscal transfers. From Figure 3-3, the central/west land supply share kept rising. In 2011, central/west GDP was ~40% of national total, but new land supply share was 50%. Their local government bond share was also ~50%. Central/west land oversupply meets limited demand — many industrial parks offer "zero land price" with no takers. Meanwhile, eastern land prices keep rising with supply insufficient. "Total surplus, local shortage" describes China's labor market — and the root cause is mobility barriers.
05
Efficiency Decline
The Price of Uniformity: TFP Decline and Misallocation
Figures 3-4 to 3-6 reveal troubling efficiency trends: Before 2003, TFP growth was accelerating; after 2003, it declined continuously. This inflection coincides exactly with the policy shift toward heavy resource allocation to the west. Between-firm TFP standard deviation fell before 2001 (improving resource allocation) then rose after 2003 (worsening). By region, the east saw the smallest deterioration; the central region showed a clear inflection; the west had the most severe decline. Land urbanization outpaced population urbanization (Table 3-1) — the two rates were close in the east but diverged sharply in the central/west. In the far west, urban expansion was ~3× the rate of non-agricultural population growth. Meanwhile, eastern land supply restrictions drove housing prices rising faster than wages, pushing up labor costs and harming export competitiveness.

06
Land Mismatch
Regional Land Misallocation: A Chain from Coast to Interior
A chain of perverse effects from land policy: Step 1, construction land quotas go to the west; eastern supply is restricted. Step 2, western industrial parks lie idle at zero land price; eastern land supply falls short, prices soar. Step 3, housing prices rise faster than wages, raising living costs in the east; some workers stay local. Step 4, eastern regions face labor shortages; firms raise wages. Step 5, labor-intensive firms move not to China's interior (high transport costs) but directly to Southeast Asia. Final result: reduced export competitiveness, prematurely abandoning the comparative advantage in labor-intensive industry.

07
Industry Transfer
"Empty the Cage for a New Bird": But the Bird Flew to Southeast Asia
Administratively forcing industry relocation often backfires. Entrepreneurs seek profit, not poverty alleviation. Compared with China's interior, Southeast Asian countries offer lower wages, comparable deep-water ports, and are closer to Europe. So labor-intensive firms leapfrog China's west and move directly to Vietnam and Cambodia. Since 2015, this trend has accelerated — just check where the clothes and toys sold abroad are made. What does relocate to China's west? State-owned enterprises, resource-extraction firms, and polluting industries. More troubling: central/west and east are converging in industrial structure. Research shows that China's geographic concentration and industry specialization peaked around 2005 and have since declined — provincial industrial isomorphism is rising, directly contradicting the logic of interregional specialization and cooperation.
08
Western Trap
Overbuilt Western Cities: Industrialization in the Wrong Places
The author's field research in western China reveals disturbing patterns. One national-level poverty county, population 50,000, planned to become a city of 300,000. Its built-up area tripled in two years. The province had over 100 industrial parks — "if 20 parks have no investment, that's coincidental; if 100 do, that's systemic." A project under construction for two years was just a steel frame with no factory building. Land vacancy rates in western provincial-level development zones were high, but officials wouldn't speak openly. Local governments have a severe "industrial upgrading" bias — one western province's leaders wanted to develop an auto industry with zero prior base. Capital deepening is faster in the west than the east, and the sharpest decline in investment returns is also in the west. Overbuilding is directly tied to the GDP ranking tournament among local officials.
09
Debt Burden
Local Government Debt: Unsustainable "Growth"
Western urban construction relies heavily on government debt, and investment efficiency in industrial parks and new towns is deeply questionable. In 2011, central/west local government bond increments were ~50% of the national total, while their GDP share was only ~41%. This debt funded massive overbuilding — new towns and industrial parks with insufficient enterprises and population. In the short term, investment-driven growth boosted western GDP figures, but this growth lacks efficiency and is unsustainable. More seriously: investment that deviates from local comparative advantage doesn't help development — it creates an unbearable debt burden. The author states bluntly: some of these investments will "never yield returns." If local governments cannot repay their debts and residents cannot freely move out, that region becomes an "isolated island" rather than part of a unified country.
10
Solution
Breaking the Trinity: Redefining Balance
The "impossible trinity" of great-power development does have a solution. The key is to redefine "balance" — abandon the pursuit of uniform resource and population distribution, and instead pursue convergence in per capita GDP, real income, and quality of life. This per capita balance can be achieved through free population movement without large-scale administrative controls. World Bank research confirms: every country that achieved balanced regional development also achieved free labor mobility. Conversely, pursuing uniform distribution inevitably sacrifices both efficiency and market unity. This is fundamentally a question of the relationship between government and market — placed into the concrete context of urbanization and regional development.
"If you simultaneously endorse unity, efficiency, and balance defined as uniform distribution achieved through administrative intervention, these three goals are impossible to achieve simultaneously. I call this the 'impossible trinity' of great-power development."
— Lu Ming
🔑 Core Conclusion
Great-power development confronts an "impossible trinity": unity, efficiency, and balance-as-uniform-distribution cannot be achieved simultaneously. The solution is to abandon the "uniform distribution" mindset and pursue per capita balance instead. China's real problem: administrative resource allocation runs counter to the market-driven direction of population flows. Since 2003, this anti-market policy has caused declining TFP growth, worsening resource allocation efficiency, land urbanization outpacing population urbanization, and rising central/west debt burdens. Global spatial economic maps show clearly: the world is not flat. Economic agglomeration is an irresistible law. The correct policy direction is to respect market forces, prioritize "moving people" over "moving money," and achieve per capita balance through agglomeration.
