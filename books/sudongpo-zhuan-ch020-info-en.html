The Gay Genius · Ch.20 "The Empress Dowager's Favor"

Rising three ranks in eight months — how a reluctant official was pursued by power

After Emperor Shenzong's death, the Empress Dowager took the reins, and Su Dongpo's fortunes reversed dramatically. In just eight months, he rose from rank seven to rank three — Hanlin Academician and Imperial Edict Drafter — near the pinnacle of power. The Dowager revealed the reason: it was the late emperor's dying wish. "Shenzong often praised you as a rare talent and intended to promote you, but he passed away before he could." Su drafted over 800 edicts. Yet at his peak he wrote: "We long for joy and dread suffering — but these are just feelings before they arrive." Happiness does not depend on status.

01
RISE TO POWER
The Emperor's Dying Wish: The Dowager Reveals the Secret
When Su asked the Dowager why she had suddenly promoted him, he received an unexpected answer: it was the late Emperor Shenzong's dying wish. Shenzong, if he put down his chopsticks mid-meal, was surely reading Su's writings. The Dowager summoned Su, asked his official history, then said: "Do you know why you were suddenly promoted?" Su guessed imperial grace, the young emperor, ministers' recommendation — all denied. Finally she revealed the truth. At the mention of the late emperor, all three wept.

02
NIGHT WORK
Hanlin Nights: Red Candles, Water Clocks & 800 Edicts
As Hanlin Academician, Su drafted over 800 imperial edicts — elegant, fitting, precise — and he never needed to consult reference books. All night work was done "locked within the palace." Edicts were dictated by the Dowager; he rendered them in the most classical, solemn prose. When he died, his successor Academician Hong asked an old servant how he compared to Su. The answer: "Su Dongpo's literary talent may not exceed yours, sir, but he never opened a reference book."

03
FACTIONAL CONFLICT
Sima Guang's Funeral: One Joke Planted a Decade of Hatred
At Sima Guang's funeral, Su Dongpo mocked the Neo-Confucian scholar Cheng Yi with a single joke — sowing an irreconcilable feud with the Henan School. Cheng insisted on strict ancient rites, quoting Confucius' Analects: "On a day the Master had wept, he did not sing" to bar officials from paying respects after the morning's temple ceremony. Su laughed: "The Analects did not say 'On a day the Master had sung, he did not weep.'" This marked the formal beginning of factional struggle between Su's Sichuan faction and Cheng's Henan Neo-Confucianists.

04
CAPITAL LIFE
East Floral Gate: Splendor with Simplicity
Su built an official residence near East Floral Gate, living in splendor incomparable to his Huangzhou farm. Nearby were silver shops, silk stores, and multi-story buildings. Restaurants offered forty to fifty dishes, waiters darting between tables. Yet Su maintained his simple habits — rising at dawn, combing his hair a hundred times, putting on his official robes then lying down again for a nap, "sweet beyond compare." Splendid days and simple days, he found, differed little in happiness.

05
PHILOSOPHY
Joy and Suffering: Happiness Does Not Depend on Status
At the peak of his power, Su Dongpo wrote a startling revelation: "We long for joy and dread suffering — but these are just feelings before they arrive. When joy or suffering actually comes and you experience it firsthand, the longing and dread you once felt are nowhere to be found." In other words, our anticipation of happiness and fear of hardship are mere imagination. He also wrote: "The joy of being a high official is not necessarily greater than that of a successful blacksmith."

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
Su Dongpo's clarity at the peak of power is astonishing: he knew that "the joy of being a high official is not necessarily greater than that of a successful blacksmith." This insight made him both the Dowager's favorite and a man unwilling to play political games. Yet it was precisely this unwillingness — to conform, to stay silent, to surrender independent thought — that would ultimately make him the target of factional warfare.
