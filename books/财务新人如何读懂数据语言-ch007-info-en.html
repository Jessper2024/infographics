Buy a chicken for $8, sell for $9, buy again for $10, sell for $11—earned $2 or lost $4?
A Citibank interview question reveals the fundamental divide between accounting profit and economic profit: earning less = losing.

Neil Shen faced a classic Citibank interview question: buy a chicken for $8, sell for $9, buy again for $10, sell for $11—how much did you earn? The ordinary answer is $2. Shen's answer: "lost $4"—exactly what the investment bank wanted. This chapter reveals two profit views behind two answers: accounting profit records past historical transactions, while economic profit introduces opportunity cost to compare relative outcomes. From EVA assessments and NPV investment decisions to budget allocation comparisons, the financier's eye looks backward, the investor's eye looks forward.

01
Profit · Two Views
Accounting Profit vs. Economic Profit: Two Answers for One Trade
Round 1: buy at 8, sell at 9 → earn 1. Round 2: buy at 10, sell at 11 → earn 1. Total: +$2—this is accounting profit, faithfully recording actual transactions by bookkeeping rules. But from the single-trade maximum opportunity view: 11−8 = $3, twice = $6. Against the possible $6, only $2 was earned—the $4 shortfall is a loss. This is economic profit, incorporating opportunity cost for comparison.
📋

Economic Profit

02
Core · Opportunity Cost
Why Earning Less Equals Losing: How Opportunity Cost Disrupts Financial Thinking
Accounting profit only accounts for the past—it introduces no more relevant comparison benchmark: opportunity cost. Like a child scoring 90% seems fine, until you learn the class average is 95%. Similarly, evaluating economic performance requires comparing absolute profit against relative cost. In daily decisions, we naturally use opportunity cost thinking—weighing Apartment A vs. Apartment B. But in the financial world, accounting profit frameworks often make us forget this.
Absolute profit: $2 (seems decent)
Opportunity loss: 6 − 2 = $4 (max possible − actual)
Conclusion: Earning $4 less = Losing $4
03
Evaluation · From Absolute to Relative
The Performance Evaluation Revolution: How EVA & ROCE Replace Accounting Profit
At the highest management level, more companies now use EVA (Economic Value Added) or ROCE (Return on Capital Employed) instead of accounting profit. Simply, EVA = accounting profit minus cost of capital. If earnings don't even beat bank fixed-income returns, management is underperforming. NPV is also embedded with cost of capital in every project investment decision—this is economic profit thinking in practice.

04
Budget · Relative Choice
Economic Profit Thinking in Budget Allocation: Which One?
In a resource-constrained world, every company operates within limits. Engineering proposes a new process doubling output; production proposes automation halving labor—budget only covers one. Both have positive NPVs, so choose the one with higher NPV. If numbers are close, pick automation: with labor costs rising double digits annually, labor-replacing investment yields accelerating returns. This is forward-looking relative comparison.

05
Mindset · Forward-Looking
The Investor's Eye Looks Forward: The Storytelling CFO
The fundamental difference between financiers and investors: the former looks back, the latter looks forward. The essence of finance is pre-packaging and withdrawing future cash earnings. From this angle, a CFO who can't tell stories is mediocre. Don't despise the "art of spin"—the highest realm for a financial professional is: selective spin. Having mastered the skill of spin, choose not to spin. This is the cognitive freedom gained from upgrading accounting profit thinking to economic profit thinking.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The same trade: accounting profit earns $2, economic profit loses $4—the gap lies in whether opportunity cost is introduced. Accounting profit records the past; economic profit focuses on relative outcomes. From EVA assessments to NPV investment decisions, from budget allocation to forward-looking judgment, finance professionals must learn to see the future with an investor's eye. The essence of finance is pre-packaging future cash earnings—the ability to tell stories, to selectively spin, is the highest realm of a financial professional.
