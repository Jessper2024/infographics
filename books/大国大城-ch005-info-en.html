Great Country, Big City · Ch.5「Global Economic Spatial Agglomeration」

Spatial Agglomeration · Economic Geography · Migration · Urbanization Laws In every country—big or small, rich or poor—economic activity is overwhelmingly concentrated in a few regions. This is not an accident, but an economic law. 50% of global economic activity occurs on just 1.5% of the world's land. From the US GDP cone map to Japan's Tokyo metro, from France's "Eiffel Tower" to China's Hu Huanyong Line—all nations experience spatial agglomeration. Understanding this law is the starting point for understanding all problems of urbanization in a large country.

This chapter uses satellite images and economic geography maps to visually demonstrate the spatial agglomeration of the global economy. The Earth at night reveals that economically active regions are concentrated in a small fraction of the world's land area. The US GDP distribution map shows that the orange-highlighted area, though small, accounts for half of the US economy. Japan, the UK, and France all show extreme concentration—France's economy resembles the Eiffel Tower. Developing countries like India show similar patterns. Australia, despite its vast empty interior, concentrates its economy in coastal cities. Germany's relative dispersion is a historical anomaly. China's Hu Huanyong Line divides the country, and east of this line, the degree of concentration is actually not high by international standards. Population flows in Norway confirm that worldwide, people are moving to big cities without exception.

01
View from Space
Earth at Night: The Truth Told by Lights
On August 11, 2002, NASA's satellite captured the Earth at night (Fig.1), revealing a simple fact: global economic activity is highly concentrated in a few regions—a phenomenon called "spatial agglomeration." Between countries, light intensity reflects economic development—the contrast between South Korea and North Korea is the starkest. Within a country, light intensity reflects population and economic concentration—the US East Coast megalopolis and the narrow West Coast corridor are the brightest. Europe's brightest areas cluster around its major cities. This map is the starting point for understanding economic geography.
02
US Example
US GDP Distribution: Half the Economy in the Orange Zone
Figure 6 shows the US GDP distribution map—the orange-shaded area, though small in size, accounts for half of the US economy. More importantly, on a per capita GDP basis, the US has achieved "balanced development." This demonstrates that "balanced development" is not about resource uniformity, but about per capita income convergence. Figure 7 uses cone height to represent GDP output per unit area—the cones rise sharply within a small area, revealing extraordinary concentration. Despite massive spatial agglomeration, the US maintains regional balance in per capita terms.
03
Developed Nations
Japan · UK · France: The "Cone Effect" of Developed Economies
Figure 8 shows the economic geography of Japan, the UK, and France: Developed countries' economies are highly concentrated in a few metropolitan areas. Japan's three major metropolitan areas hold over 50% of the national population. Tokyo's 36 million people represent one-third of Japan's population in an area the size of Shanghai plus Suzhou. The UK's economy concentrates in London and the Southeast. France's economy is an "Eiffel Tower"—Paris absolutely dominates, with a single towering peak while the rest of the country remains flat. This is not the result of policy design but of spontaneous market forces.

04
Developing Countries
India and Others: Spatial Agglomeration is Not Just for the Rich
Figure 9 shows the economic geography of India and other developing countries: Developing countries also exhibit extreme spatial agglomeration. India's economy is concentrated in a handful of cities—Mumbai, Delhi, Kolkata, Bangalore—and their surroundings. This proves that spatial agglomeration is not a luxury of developed countries, but a universal law of economic development itself. Even in the early stages of economic takeoff, activity concentrates in a few advantaged areas. Latecomer nations cannot escape this fundamental economic law.
05
Unique Case
Australia: Vast Land ≠ Dispersed Economy
Figure 10 shows Australia's economic geography: Despite being a vast and sparsely populated continent (3 people/km²), Australia's economy is still concentrated in a few coastal cities. Sydney, Melbourne, Brisbane, and Perth together generate the vast majority of the nation's economic output. This case powerfully refutes the "sparse population therefore should disperse development" argument—even on a vast landmass, economic and population agglomeration remains the unchanged rule. Geographic conditions (especially coastal advantage) have a persistent, decisive impact on development.
06
Historical Anomaly
Germany: Relative Dispersion as a Historical Scar
Figure 11 shows Germany's economic geography: Germany's relative dispersion is not evidence of successful "dispersal policy." Setting aside the decades of East/West German division, Germany's unified history spans barely a hundred years. Long-term political fragmentation prevented the emergence of a super-dominant capital like London or Paris. Instead, Germany developed multiple centers—Berlin, Munich, Hamburg, Frankfurt. Germany's dispersion is not natural economic integration, but the result of political-historical path dependency. Citing Germany as evidence for dispersal policies requires great caution in China's context.
07
China's View
The Hu Huanyong Line: China's Spatial Reality
Figure 12 shows China's economic geography: West of the Hu Huanyong Line (Aihui-Tengchong Line), natural conditions are poor and unsuitable for large-scale development. But even looking only at the eastern half, China's economic concentration is not high—compared to the US, Japan, and France, China's eastern region is far less concentrated. This means China's urbanization is far from over, and there is still enormous room for population to flow to advantaged regions (especially the eastern coastal urban belt). Any administrative attempt to reverse this trend risks enormous efficiency costs.

08
Population Flow
Global Trend: People Moving to Big Cities is Unstoppable
Figure 13 shows Norway's population flow map (2005–2009): Worldwide, people are moving to large cities—even in a small country like Norway. Meanwhile, China's rural areas face severe hollowing and aging (Fig.14-18, Chengkan, southern Anhui). However, villages with tourism and cultural value are being redeveloped and reborn—people flow out, money flows in. The market economy guides resources to where they yield the highest returns, even actively preserving cultural heritage. This is a natural process that should not be reversed by administrative force.
09
Social Cost
Migration vs Left-Behind: Two Sides of Urbanization
Figure 19 shows a grassroots project "Sunflower"—providing after-school support for migrant workers' children. 2010 census data reveals: China has about 61 million left-behind children in rural areas, plus over 30 million "migrant children" living with parents in cities. Migrant children cannot access equal public services in destination cities, while left-behind children face the combined hardship of parental absence and educational deprivation. This reveals a massive institutional barrier—the Hukou (household registration) system splits families apart, turning urbanization into "semi-urbanization": labor moves to cities, but families do not.
10
Shanghai Paradox
The Megacity Paradox: Population Growing, City Center Shrinking
Figures 20 and 21 show Shanghai's population density and subway commuting maps: Between 2000–2010, while Shanghai's total population grew rapidly, the city center population actually declined sharply. Population concentration in big cities is not a uniform inflow—it involves suburbanization and the separation of jobs and homes. Shanghai residents commuting long distances between center and suburbs by subway is the inevitable result of population dispersal and work-housing separation. This phenomenon is universal across global megacities: people first concentrate in cities, then within cities they spread from center to suburbs, forming metropolitan areas—but this is the second stage of urbanization, not an argument against inflow.

🔑 Core Conclusion
Global economic activity is overwhelmingly concentrated in a few regions—this is not a policy failure or market malfunction, but a fundamental law of economic development. From the Earth at night to the GDP cone maps of every country, the World Bank confirms that over half of economic activity occurs on just 1.5% of land. The US economy is "a platform with peaks," France is an "Eiffel Tower," Japan concentrates in three metro areas. China's problem is not over-agglomeration but under-agglomeration—the degree of economic concentration east of the Hu Huanyong Line is far below that of the US, Japan, and other developed countries. Population concentration in big cities is universal across the world, without exception. Understanding this spatial agglomeration law is the prerequisite for understanding China's urbanization path.
