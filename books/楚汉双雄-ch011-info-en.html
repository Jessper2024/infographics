The Chu-Han Contention · Chapter Eleven 「The Hegemon-King's End: Besieged by Chu Songs and Suicide at Wu River」

Trapped at Gaixia by Chu songs from all sides, Consort Yu's death for love, the final charge of twenty-eight riders, the hero's farewell at Wu River — the most magnificent exit in Chinese history

After the Gaixia defeat, Xiang Yu held the city with no medicine, no food, and his army spent. The allied forces sang Chu songs — “Chu songs from all sides” shattered the Chu army's morale. Xiang Yu composed his “Gaixia Song”; Consort Yu danced and took her own life for love. Xiang Yu broke out with 800 riders, was misled by a farmer into a marsh at Yinling, and was reduced to 28 riders. On a nameless hill, he delivered his final speech — “Heaven seeks my death, not any fault in my warfare” — and led 28 riders to three victories: breaking the encirclement, beheading a general, and seizing a banner. At Wu River, a ferryman waited with a boat. Xiang Yu refused to cross, citing shame before the elders of Jiangdong. After gifting his horse Wuzhui, he drew his sword and took his own life. The most magnificent hero's exit in Chinese history — aged thirty-one.

01
英雄悲歌
Chu Songs from All Sides: The Ultimate Psychological Weapon
After the Gaixia defeat, Xiang Yu held the city and refused to come out. No medicine, food exhausted, army spent. The allied forces deployed the ultimate psychological weapon — singing Chu songs. Chu songs from all sides! The trapped Chu soldiers instantly understood: Chu must have been completely occupied, or where would so many Chu people come from? Xiang Yu, undefeated in his entire life, sank into a spiritual abyss. Victory keeps you sailing smoothly, automatically smoothing over countless vulnerabilities. But the day you can no longer win, those vulnerabilities bare their fangs and “counter-strike” with fury. Amid the encircling Chu songs, Xiang Yu sighed and sang: “My strength could uproot mountains, my spirit dominated the age — but times are against me, and Zhui runs no more. Zhui runs no more — what can I do? Oh Yu, my Yu, what fate awaits you?” Consort Yu danced mournfully and answered: “The Han army has taken the land, Chu songs rise from all sides. My king's spirit is spent — what reason has this lowly concubine to live on?” She finished, then took her own life — giving her man the most magnificent dignity through death.
02
绝境突围
From 800 to 28: The Escape Artist vs. The Pursuit Artist — A Fatal Contrast
That midnight, Xiang Yu led 800 close-guard cavalry breaking out of Gaixia and heading south. Seeing that Xiang Yu could flee as well as himself, Liu Bang was infuriated and sternly ordered Guan Ying to pursue with 5,000 elite cavalry. By the time Xiang Yu crossed the Huai River, his 800 were down to fewer than 100. Entering Yinling County, Xiang Yu asked a farmer for directions — unlike Liu Bang, whose every escape seemed divinely assisted, Xiang Yu's luck was abysmal. Liu Bang was a “professional escape athlete” — Heaven had equipped him with escape skills from birth. But Xiang Yu, the “professional pursuer,” had been granted peerless offensive power without any escape skills. The farmer pointed the wrong way, leading Xiang Yu into a dead-end marsh. By the time he emerged, Guan Ying had caught up. Fleeing into Dongcheng County, only 28 riders remained at his side.
03
战神之魂
The Final Speech and Charge: “Heaven Seeks My Death, Not My Fault in Warfare”
On a nameless hill, watching the rolling dust of pursuit, Xiang Yu assembled his 28 generals for a final address: “Eight years since I rose in arms, over seventy battles great and small — whoever stood before me was shattered, whoever fought me submitted. Never a single defeat. Today's extremity is Heaven seeking my death, not any fault in my warfare! I resolve to fight this final battle to the death — break the encirclement in the first charge, behead a general in the second, seize a banner in the third!” He divided the 28 riders into four units of seven. Xiang Yu led the first charge personally, splitting the Han formation and beheading a general as promised. The four units broke through in four directions, reaching three elevated positions. Guan Ying divided his 5,000 cavalry into three columns and surrounded the three hills. Xiang Yu led two units charging down the first hill, once again splitting the encirclement and killing dozens; then picking up the third unit at the second hill and charging down again, killing a cavalry colonel. Reunited, the four units counted only two riders lost. Xiang Yu laughed: “How was today's battle?” The 26 riders knelt in unison: “As Your Majesty declared!” The man who single-handedly destroyed the mighty Qin, now leading only 28, still fought like a force of thousands.
04
壮烈谢幕
Suicide at Wu River: The Most Magnificent Hero's Farewell in Chinese History
Xiang Yu arrived at Wu River. The ferryman had his boat ready: “Cross the river and you're in Jiangdong — a thousand li of territory, hundreds of thousands of people, enough to rule as a king in one corner.” The proudest man of his generation gazed at the rolling river — his will to die had crystallized. When he first achieved greatness, he insisted on returning home in glory; now utterly defeated, he naturally could not face crossing back. “Heaven seeks my death — why cross the river! Eight thousand sons of Jiangdong crossed the river with me; not a single one survives today. Even if the Jiangdong elders, in their loving mercy, did not cast me out — what face would I have?” He gifted his five-year-old steed Wuzhui to the ferryman (it later threw itself into the river for its master). Twenty-five men formed ranks with their backs to the Wu River. The Han army arrived; blood-soaked battle at Wu River's bank. Xiang Yu killed hundreds; none among the heroes could find a way. Amid the carnage he spotted his old subordinate Lü Matong: “This head of mine — the King of Han offers a thousand gold and a marquisate of ten thousand households as reward. I grant it to you!” With these words, he drew his sword and took his own life. The ten-thousand-man slayer whom none could kill — ended by his own hand. The hero's curtain call — aged thirty-one.
05
人性剖析
Why Xiang Yu Chose Death Over Crossing the River — The Spirit of a “Shi” and the Dignity of a “King”
Xiang Yu's refusal to cross the river has sparked millennia of debate. Du Mu said “who knows if he could have rallied?”; Wang Anshi said “a hundred battles left the hero exhausted.” But truly understanding Xiang Yu requires returning to his spiritual core: Xiang Yu was the last representative of the aristocratic spirit — “a scholar may be killed but not humiliated.” His pride would not permit him to return to Jiangdong as a failure. The death of all 8,000 Jiangdong sons itself was the ultimate judgment upon a king. He chose to end his life his own way rather than begin a humiliating new life as a fugitive. Liu Bang could flee repeatedly, endure humiliation, and fight another day — but Xiang Yu could not. Because the sole meaning of Xiang Yu's existence was “victory.” When he could no longer win, living itself became the greatest insult. This extreme pride forged his legend as the “War God” — and sealed his tragic fate.
📌 KEY TAKEAWAY
Xiang Yu's suicide at Wu River is the most magnificent hero's farewell in Chinese history. The Chu songs from all sides demonstrate the ultimate power of psychological warfare — breaking morale is deadlier than breaching walls. The 28-rider charge proves: true combat power depends not on numbers but on the unconquerable conviction within the soul. Xiang Yu's choice reveals the ultimate divergence between two survival philosophies — Liu Bang's pragmatism allowed for humiliating survival and comeback; Xiang Yu's aristocratic spirit preferred shattered jade over intact tile. Who was right? History offers no standard answer. Liu Bang won the realm; Xiang Yu won eternal commemoration. One became emperor; the other became legend. Most noteworthy: Xiang Yu won every battle and never knew defeat — but when defeat finally came, he possessed no psychological mechanism to handle it. For one who never lost in life, a single defeat was the end.
