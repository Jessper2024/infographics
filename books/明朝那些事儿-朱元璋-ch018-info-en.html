Ming Dynasty Those Stories · Chapter 18 "The Hu Weiyong Case"

Hu Weiyong held absolute power for seven years while Zhu Yuanzhang watched silently. When the final straw fell, a political purge implicating over 10,000 people unfolded — and the chancellorship itself was headed for extinction.

📖 Chapter Overview · SCQA

Situation (S): At the Ming founding, Zhu Yuanzhang established the Central Secretariat and Six Ministries, with Li Shanchang and Hu Weiyong as chancellors. After defeating the Zhedong faction and eliminating strategist Liu Ji, Hu Weiyong held sole chancellorial power for seven years.

Complication (C): Hu Weiyong increasingly overstepped — intercepting memorials, appointing officials, executing without imperial approval. Zhu did nothing for seven years while the court watched in silent understanding of the deadly game being played.

Question (Q): Why did Zhu Yuanzhang tolerate Hu Weiyong's abuse for seven long years? What was his true target?

Answer (A): Zhu's target was never Hu Weiyong the man, but the chancellorship itself. He would use Hu's crimes as the pretext to abolish the thousand-year-old position, concentrating all power in his own hands.

01
The Rise of a Tyrant — Hu Weiyong's Seven-Year Reign

Hu Weiyong, with Zhu Yuanzhang's tacit approval, defeated the Zhedong faction, eliminated the empire's top strategist Liu Ji, and held sole chancellorial power for seven years, co-opting or压制 nearly every court official.

Hu Weiyong was never truly stronger than Liu Ji — his victory existed only because Zhu Yuanzhang stood behind him. Whether the Hu landlord or Liu landlord won, the ZHU landlord's supremacy was guaranteed. Zhu chose Hu precisely because Hu posed the smallest threat.

But Hu Weiyong failed to understand this. After crushing Liu Ji, he grew ever more arrogant: taking bribes, excluding dissenters, intercepting subordinates' memorials, appointing and executing officials without imperial approval. In 1373 (Hongwu Year 6), he ousted fellow chancellor Wang Guangyang and held sole chancellorial power.

02
Seven Years of Patience — Zhu Yuanzhang's Strategy of Letting the Tiger Grow Fat

Zhu Yuanzhang ignored Hu Weiyong's abuses for seven years — a meticulously planned conspiracy. To catch a tiger, one must first let it grow fat. The ancient strategy of "欲擒故纵" (feigning leniency before striking) was in full play.

When Hu Weiyong's son fell from a horse and died, Hu in a rage executed the coachman without judicial authorization. Zhu ordered an explanation. Hu offered every excuse, but Zhu only stared in cold silence, then calmly said: "A life for a life," and walked out. Hu trembled — he finally understood the gulf between himself and the man before him.

Zhu had never intended to punish Hu for the coachman's death. His real target was the chancellorship institution itself — a thousand-year power-sharing mechanism he despised. Hu's crimes would provide the perfect pretext for its abolition.

Strategy: Let the Tiger Grow Fat

Zhu allowed Hu seven years of unchecked abuse to accumulate evidence, waiting for the perfect moment to strike — not negligence, but a long-prepared political trap.

The Real Target

Hu Weiyong was merely a pawn. Zhu's true objective was the abolition of the chancellorship — ending a thousand-year tradition of power-sharing with the emperor.

03
Pulling Everyone In — Hu's Desperate Alliance Strategy

Facing Zhu's inevitable strike, Hu Weiyong's counter-strategy was to drag as many people as possible into the conspiracy — through official promises, emotional bonding, and blood oaths of mutual protection.

Those he pulled in included Marquis Ji'an Lu Zhongheng, Censor-in-Chief Chen Ning, and Commander Mao Rang, among other top officials. But Hu was not satisfied — he needed to win over the most important man of all: Li Shanchang, who possessed the ultimate political insurance — the "Death-Exemption Iron Tablet."

The Death-Exemption Iron Tablet was the highest imperial reward, supposedly granting immunity from the death penalty. Many ministers schemed for decades to obtain one. Yet before Zhu Yuanzhang, even the Iron Tablet proved worthless.

04
Tu Jie's Betrayal — A Political Purge Sweeping Away 10,000

The Champa envoy incident became the trigger. Hu Weiyong failed to report the foreign mission and Zhu exploded in fury. Hu's ally Tu Jie switched sides and exposed the plot. Zhu immediately executed Hu Weiyong,灭 his three clans, and the purge continued for years — over 10,000 killed.

Within just one month of Hu's death, Zhu abolished the chancellorship, dissolved the Central Secretariat, and reassigned all personnel with surgical efficiency. The six ministries now reported directly to the emperor. The thousand-year chancellorship ended in a single stroke.

Tu Jie, the turncoat who delivered Hu to the executioner, was himself executed shortly after — sharing the same fate as the man he betrayed. Political irony at its most exquisite. Li Shanchang survived initially, but Zhu's nature meant he was merely delaying the inevitable.

05
The End of the Chancellorship — A Thousand-Year Balance Shattered

Zhu Yuanzhang's abolition of the chancellorship concentrated all power in himself, but created an inescapable burden for his descendants — who turned to the Grand Secretariat (Neige) as a replacement, creating a power even more formidable than the old chancellorship.

The personal cost was staggering: from September 14 to 21, 1384, just eight days, Zhu reviewed 1,666 documents and handled 3,391 matters — averaging 200 files and 400 matters daily. He needed no chancellor because he WAS the CEO, CFO, and CTO. But his descendants, lacking his extraordinary stamina and ability, had no choice but to rely on Grand Secretaries — whose power dwarfed any previous chancellor's.

History exacted its revenge. Zhu tried to break a thousand-year balance of power with sheer personal will, but history forced upon him a new actor: the Grand Secretariat. He wanted to buy soap; historical dialectics forced a roll of toilet paper upon him as well.

💡 Core Conclusion

The Hu Weiyong case was not merely a political purge triggered by one man's abuse of power — it was a carefully premeditated institutional revolution. Zhu Yuanzhang spent seven years deliberately nurturing Hu's arrogance, accumulating charges to provide a pretext for abolishing the chancellorship. The personal cost was staggering: Zhu himself had to process 200 documents daily. His descendants, lacking his iron will and stamina, had no choice but to rely on Grand Secretaries — who, though nominally not chancellors, wielded even greater power than any before them. Zhu eliminated the title of chancellor, only to birth a more formidable shadow government.
