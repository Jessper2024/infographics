The Siege of Bianjing · Postscript "Only Three Years from Prosperity to Collapse"

By Guo Jianlong · Written at Wind Howl Residence, Dali A writer who wandered half a lifetime found his life's anchor in a small village by Erhai Lake. From then on, every manuscript page was written under the same roof. This is not a Biography of Yue Fei — it stirs no fervor, only seeks to find reasonable explanations for clashes of civilization.

The Postscript is Guo Jianlong's retrospective on his own writing career. In the winter of 2013, he spent three months in a small village by Erhai Lake in Dali, washing dishes at a nearby eatery between writing sessions — there he met the proprietress, Meng Wujun. Half a year later, returning bloodied from the battlefields of the Middle East, he "pulled her aboard my pirate ship." Together they bought a small house — the wind howled outside, so he named it "Wind Howl Residence." For the first time in his life, he had a stable space for his book collection, and for the first time, a book was conceived, written, and revised entirely in one place. The Siege of Bianjing became a standalone monograph because, while writing The Military Code of the Central Empire, he realized the Jingkang-era wars had "transcended military affairs, becoming a kaleidoscope of politics, economics, and diplomacy" — an era so rich it deserved its own anatomy. Guo Jianlong closes by stating: this book is not meant to stir passion, but to provoke reflection. At any moment, prosperity and catastrophe are only one step apart.

01
Personal Journey
From Tibet's no-man's-land to a small Dali eatery — a writer's history of wandering
Every one of Guo Jianlong's books had traveled through upheaval to reach completion — until he met Meng Wujun. His earliest novel, Farewell to Shambhala, was conceived in Tibet's uninhabited wilderness, drafted in Wenshan (Yunnan), moved through two Beijing apartments, revised in Hangzhou and Sapa (Vietnam), and finally sealed back in Wenshan. A Hundred Years Across the Middle East had its earliest chapters written in Egypt and Ethiopia, completed in Guangzhou. In the winter of 2013 by Erhai Lake, he alternated between drafting The Fiscal Code of the Central Empire and washing dishes at Meng Wujun's small restaurant. In spring 2014 he left for the Middle East; six months later, bloodied from an Afghan war zone, "the moment I knew I was safe, my first thought was to tell her I was all right. In that instant, I understood what it felt like to have roots."

02
Writing Environment
Wind Howl Residence — over 100 reference books arrayed on the desk, consulted as he wrote
For a writer, the greatest use of a home is to house a library. Guo Jianlong had bought countless books across his earlier life, but they had scattered across the country as he wandered. Previously, his research materials were bought on the fly, photographed in libraries, or downloaded as e-books; once a book was finished, the materials dispersed. With Wind Howl Residence, he could gather nearly all his references — of the 100+ works listed in this book's bibliography, all but two (the Sanchao Beimeng Huibian and Jianyan Yilai Xinian Yaolu, unavailable for purchase, used as e-books) were bought and kept on his desk for real-time consultation. The Siege of Bianjing thus became his most unhurried book — and the first conceived, written, and finished entirely in one place. Meng Wujun now had a studio to pursue her painting; Guo Jianlong had his study and his anchor.

03
Motivation
An era stuck in the throat: from prosperity to collapse in just three years — this speed is shocking
The Jingkang period transcended military affairs, becoming a kaleidoscope of politics, economics, and diplomacy — one book could not contain it; it demanded its own monograph. While writing The Military Code of the Central Empire, Guo Jianlong realized the Jingkang-era wars could not be explained solely through military geography — they represented a total eruption of multi-layered conflict. What struck him even more was the timescale: just three years earlier, Northern Song had been saturating society with proclamations of a golden age; people generally believed this was a great era of national strength and economic flourishing. Yet three years later, the state was destroyed. From prosperity to collapse in only three years — "it reminds us to be vigilant in times of peace. At any moment, crisis and prosperity are only one step apart. Peace is not a given; it demands that we view the world with humility."

04
Contemporary Lessons
Northern Song's chaotic diplomacy was rooted in historical humiliation — reckless strategy did not recover Yanyun but lost even more
The Northern Song government carried a profound sense of historical humiliation, born from Liao's occupation of the Sixteen Prefectures of Yanyun — consequently, all late-Song diplomacy was fixated on this northern territory. Lacking fixed principles, Song rulers and ministers spiraled deeper into diplomatic entanglements. The resulting reckless strategies not only failed to reclaim Yanyun but cost even more territory. In military strategy, this history offers equally many lessons: once war erupts, the fiscal imbalance it triggers can cause society itself to disintegrate. Guo Jianlong emphasizes — "Today, Chinese national self-esteem is in a phase of surging intensity. In this phase, we need constant vigilance more than ever."

05
Writer's Creed
A book for deep reflection — not to stir fervor, only to find reasonable explanations for civilizational clashes
In this postscript, Guo Jianlong articulates his writing credo: a person can live without depending on any organization. He thanks Meng Wujun for her companionship — both of them holding to this belief, "only then do we feel we have lived a life worthy." He thanks his grandparents for teaching him not to follow the crowd; thanks Wen Xuefeng, Zhou Hangjun, Gu Chongqing, and Qin Xudong for crucial help at different stages of his life; and thanks what he considers the best editorial team in China. But the most important message comes at the end — "This is not a book like The Biography of Yue Fei, which stirs the blood and fires the soul. It is a book that provokes deep reflection on the lessons of history. It inflames no emotion, yet always seeks to find reasonable explanations for the clash of civilizations."
"Peace is not a given; it demands that we view the world with humility, learning from what others do well while avoiding arrogance and hubris. More importantly, we must consciously avoid war. Humility is not a mistake — misjudging the situation is what is truly terrifying, for every situation is interlinked; once the first step is taken, there is not only no turning back, but no way to grasp where the path will lead."
🔑 Key Takeaway
The value of this Postscript lies not in its acknowledgments but in how it reveals the soul of The Siege of Bianjing. Guo Jianlong spent over one hundred reference works, writing unhurriedly at Wind Howl Residence, because the Jingkang period was an era "stuck in his throat" — the speed from three-year prosperity to sudden collapse is exceptionally rare in world history. This is not a nationalist tract but a mirror: it reminds everyone living in a "golden age" mentality that prosperity and catastrophe are only one step apart. Every situation is interlinked; once the first step is taken, there may be no turning back. Peace requires humility, lucidity, and tireless guardianship — not a gift to be taken for granted.
