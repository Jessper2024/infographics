Understanding a Complex World · Ch.14 "The Key to Life Is Voluntarily Embracing Hardship"

The Only Weapon Against Suffering Is Daily Routine, Voluntary Persistence · Those Impressive People Are Just Like You, Grinding Slowly Whatever you do, once it becomes your livelihood as a job, pain, disappointment, and anxiety follow — with almost no exception. The thing you love most becomes grueling the moment it turns into a profession. The only weapon against this suffering is daily routine, voluntary persistence. Life is boring and tedious. Every once in a while, a small burst of progress erupts. Knowing that exceptionally impressive people also struggle, also hit creative blocks, also have to grind it out — somehow, this makes you feel less alone.

This chapter is the book's most relatable entry for everyday people. The author opens with the painful writing journey of a top-tier web novelist — at their peak they published 5,000-6,000 characters daily, novels stretching into millions of words. Yet the more they wrote, the more they felt like a clown juggling too many balls, increasingly unable to handle the storylines they had set up. They eventually killed off large numbers of characters just to close the plot threads barely. The argument unfolds layer by layer: First, passion turns grueling the moment it becomes a profession — no exceptions. Second, the only weapon against suffering is daily routine, voluntary persistence — sometimes inspiration flows fast, most times you grind it out through gritted teeth. Third, life is fragments and tedium — the author's public account ran two posts weekly for two and a half years without fail, gaining only 40,000 followers in year one before hitting a viral post. Fourth, real work is boring — programmers spend most of their time writing docs, replying to emails, and fixing bugs; actual coding time is under a quarter. The final point: knowing that exceptionally impressive people also struggle and grind makes you feel far less alone.

01
Reality Paradox
The Thing You Love Most Turns Grueling the Moment It Becomes a Profession — No Exceptions
Whatever you do, once you turn it into a job and make a living from it, pain, disappointment, and anxiety follow — almost without exception. The author knows a top-tier web novelist — at their peak, 5,000-6,000 characters daily, 20,000-30,000 on a good day, novels running millions of characters, flowing effortlessly. But as they pushed further, it got worse. Their study walls were plastered with sticky notes tracking plot threads and foreshadowing. They spent whole days reading stacks of books searching for inspiration. The more they wrote, the more they felt like a clown — started with three balls to juggle, then more and more until they couldn't catch them anymore. Even their finished novels barely wrapped up by killing off large numbers of characters to close the threads. In the later writing stages, getting up each morning felt "like going to a funeral." Several times their hair fell out so severely they had to go to the hospital.

02
The Weapon
The Only Weapon Against This Suffering Is Daily Routine, Voluntary Persistence
Zhang Yimou once said something similar — one-third of the way into a film, you already know whether it's garbage. But with investors watching, even when you know it's garbage, you have to keep shooting. Web novelists don't have this problem — when they can't continue, they decisively abandon it. A work that becomes the Venus de Milo with severed arms is still better than Venus sprouting two octopus tentacles. That web novelist treated writing like a job, with a daily requirement for themselves: must write, must publish, rain or shine. Sometimes inspiration flowed fast. Most of the time, they simply forced themselves through gritted teeth, suffering terribly. The author found that when you keep trying to prepare everything before starting, the outcome is often poor — you keep failing to prepare fully, keep failing to start, and eventually it all fizzles into nothing.
✍️

Web Novelist's Insight

Must write, must publish, rain or shine. Sometimes inspiration flows fast. Most of the time, grind it out through gritted teeth, suffering. Routine persistence like a day job.

🎬

Zhang Yimou's Candor

One-third in, you know whether it's garbage. But with investors watching, when you know it's garbage, you keep shooting.

03
Growth Curve
Life Is Fragments and Tedium — Every Once in a While, a Small Burst of Progress Erupts
The growth trajectory of a public account perfectly reveals the real rhythm of how things develop. In the first year, the author wrote with passion — only 40,000 followers came. Later, a few posts went viral and the whole thing surged forward. But most of the time, it was just daily updates — long and boring. Two to three thousand followers arrived each day, a few hundred left. Big-company work experience is the same: every day is the same stuff, boring, fragmented routine, with occasional small jumps that slowly lift you higher. Everyone else's situation mirrors this — life is equally fragmented and tedious. Every once in a while, a small burst erupts.

04
Demystifying Truth
Real Work Is Boring and Tedious — Even Stand-Up Comedians Offstage Resemble Programmers
Movies and TV shows create an enormous illusion — the people in them almost never "take the ordinary path"; they all want to leap to the top in one bound. Medical dramas are attractive people hooking up in hospitals, with zero clue about how thick medical textbooks are or that doctors need over a decade of grueling training. Legal dramas are professionals in sharp suits hooking up, when in reality most lawyers slog through boxes and boxes of documents. Programmers lose their hair from college until the company kicks them out, spending most of their time writing docs, replying to emails, reading code, fixing bugs — in a full project cycle, actual coding time is less than a quarter. Stand-up comedians stay up late offstage editing scripts and rehearsing over and over. The jokes that make audiences roar with laughter — the comedians themselves can't laugh at them. Some who went into this line of work later developed depression.
📺

TV/Film's Euphoric Filter

Medical drama = attractive people hooking up. Legal drama = professionals in suits hooking up. Business drama = easy solutions. All shortcut fantasies.

📊

Real Work's True Colors

Doctor = 10+ years of training. Lawyer = brutal bar exam + boxes of documents. Coder = docs, emails, bug fixes → coding under ¼.

"I'm not encouraging everyone to do '996.' I'm saying one thing: life itself is boring and tedious."
05
Comforting Truth
Knowing That Exceptionally Impressive People Also Struggle Somehow Makes You Feel Less Alone
Naturally gifted or haloed people exist in plenty, but most people alike must grind it out. The author writes public account posts sharing experience — it looks like sharing, but it's also a form of reflection. After writing a post and reviewing what happened earlier, he no longer agonizes so much. Anxiety often stems from overthinking. Some things you do for a long time — years, even decades — with zero enjoyment in the process. It's boring and despair-inducing. But fortunately, through voluntary persistence, those daily fragments and intermittent bursts form a complete life curve. Knowing that exceptionally impressive people also grind it out just like you — this in itself is a form of strength.

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY
The key to life is voluntarily embracing hardship. The core of this phrase isn't just "hardship" — it's "voluntarily." Passion invariably becomes painful the moment it turns into a profession — no exceptions. The only weapon against this suffering is daily routine, voluntary persistence — write when inspiration strikes, grind it out through gritted teeth when it doesn't. Life isn't the dramatic twists of a movie. It's long, tedious everyday routine, occasional small bursts, and then back to routine. Programmers spend most of their time on docs and bug fixes — actual coding is under a quarter. Stand-up comedians stay up late editing scripts offstage, unable to laugh at their own jokes. All of this weighs heavy — but knowing that exceptionally impressive people are also grinding it out like you, also hitting creative blocks, also toughing it out — in a way, this makes you feel you are not alone. Voluntarily embracing hardship isn't self-torture. It means accepting life's true face, and then choosing to walk forward anyway.
