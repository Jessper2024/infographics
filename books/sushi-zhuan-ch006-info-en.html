Su Shi Biography · Ch.6
"Singing Aloud, Strolling Slowly, Facing Wind and Rain"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer In 1080, Su Shi arrived at desolate, bleak Huangzhou — "I laugh at myself: a lifetime busy for my mouth; in old age my career turns absurd." From emperor-praised prime-minister material to exiled criminal official, an endless ordeal began. Plunging from heaven's favored son to exiled criminal, how did Su Shi face this endless ordeal?

Su Shi lodged at Dinghui Temple, slept through days and walked alone at night. He wrote "Song of Divination" featuring the lonely wild goose and "Crabapple Song" for the flower from his homeland. Through bathing at Anguo Temple — a ritual of cleansing both body and mind — he slowly healed the psychological wounds inflicted by the Crow Terrace Poetry Trial. "Choosing among cold branches, unwilling to perch — the chill on the lonely sandbar": in solitude, unyielding pride.

01
Life Upheaval
From Prime-Minister Material to Exiled Criminal Official
Huangzhou was a remote, bleak riverside town — Su Shi's official title was "Acting Vice Director of the Water Bureau, Huangzhou Military Vice Commander, locally confined, forbidden to sign official documents," punishment akin to banishment with no end in sight. He recalled his meteoric rise in the capital — the emperor called him prime-minister material, senior officials welcomed him as honored guest. Who could have imagined ending up in this desolate town? What courage, what perseverance would it take to face such a boundless life upheaval?

Past Glory

Present Reality

02
Psychological Trauma
Fear and Withdrawal: Sleeping by Day, Walking by Night
The calamity that fell from heaven gave Su Shi an inexplicable terror toward the outside world. In his early days at Huangzhou, he often stayed indoors all day, sleeping from morning till night, venturing out only under the quiet moonlight. "Drowsy, I lie back down, tossing without satisfaction. Forcing myself to go out, my lonely dream still continues." Everywhere were traps, everywhere unfathomable dangers. Even when he bought rustic wine, he remained vigilant: "The true flavor grows richer with age, but drunken wild words are terrifying when sober."

03
Spiritual Self-Portrait
The Lonely Wild Goose: An Immortal Lyric
One night by the Yangtze, Su Shi saw a solitary wild goose sweep past — among the cold forest's thousand branches, the lonely bird refused to fold its wings casually, finally landing with a mournful cry on the desolate sandbar. This lonely yet proud goose was Su Shi's very incarnation. "A fragment moon hangs on the sparse parasol tree; the water clock stills, all quiet. Who sees the recluse coming and going alone — the shadow of a misty, solitary goose? Startled, it turns back — with regret no one understands. Choosing among cold branches, unwilling to perch — the chill on the lonely sandbar." A declaration of personality: refusing to drift with the current.

04
Life's Sorrow
The Crabapple Self-Portrait: Resonance with a Noble Flower
On a hillside east of Dinghui Temple, Su Shi discovered a crabapple tree in full bloom — a precious flower native to his homeland of Sichuan, inexplicably growing in this backwater where no one recognized its value. Deeply moved, he wrote his immortal "Crabapple Song": "A charming smile within the bamboo fence; among the hills, peach and plum seem utterly vulgar. The Creator must have had deep meaning, sending this beauty to an empty valley. Noble elegance comes from Heaven's endowment — no need for golden platters in ornate halls." The crabapple's innate nobility was a portrait of Su Shi's own circumstances and character.

05
Spiritual Cleansing
Rebirth Through Fire: Bathing as Philosophical Ritual
Su Shi often visited Anguo Temple south of the city to bathe. During this daily ritual, he washed not only the dirt from his body but also cleansed his heart of attachments to glory and disgrace — each time emerging with a sense of total purity of body and mind. "Not only forgetting clean and unclean, but also washing away glory and disgrace." He transformed bathing into a spiritual ritual — in the rising steam, reflecting on his life's journey, letting the shadow of the Crow Terrace Poetry Trial slowly dissipate. The hillside wanderings, the temple baths, the moonlit solitary chanting — all helped repair a spirit shattered by political storms.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Su Shi's early Huangzhou months trace a psychological arc from "Life is truly an unpredictable circle" to "Choosing among cold branches, unwilling to perch." He used the lonely wild goose to symbolize unyielding pride, the crabapple to express life's sorrows, and the bathing ritual to wash away glory and disgrace. These four months of retreat were not depression but accumulation before rebirth. As he wrote: "Clear poems I chant alone, harmonizing with myself; the white wine is gone — who could lend me more?" Poetry and nature became his most reliable salvation in the darkest hour.
