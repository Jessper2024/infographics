Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 24
"A Word of Advice to the Public"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer The business of stock speculation will never die. A speculator's natural enemies — ignorance, greed, fear, and hope — are human animal nature that no laws can eradicate. Worse: the greatest threat isn't naked tips but "sugar-coated rumors" disguised as authoritative information. More absurd: the law punishes bearish rumor-spreaders but not bullish liars. How can the public identify and defend against information manipulation wrapped in anonymous authority?

Livermore exposes how insiders use anonymous media "authoritative sources" to spread bullish talk while dumping shares, then blame the ensuing price decline on "bear raids." This chapter is the most systematic anatomy of information manipulation — revealing techniques and piercingly identifying legal loopholes. Defense requires only three questions — Who said it? Why are they saying it? Is the timing right? Remember: the law does not protect you from bullish lies. The only defense is your own independent thinking.
01
Cognitive Framework
The Speculator's Five Great Enemies: Four Within, One Without
Livermore systematically categorizes the speculator's enemies into two layers. Four internal enemies — ignorance, greed, fear, and hope — are human nature itself; all written laws and exchange regulations in the world cannot eradicate them. One external enemy — deliberately misleading false information wrapped in "semi-official" sugar-coating — is harder to detect and therefore more insidious.
🧠

Four Internal: Human Instinct

Ignorance, greed, fear, hope — these are the nature of the human animal. Accidents can smash the most carefully laid plans to pieces, beyond any rational prediction.

🎭

One External: Sugar-Coated Lies

Unlike naked tips, these come wrapped in "semi-official" disguise — "a banker of high standing says..." Did he really? Why won't he put his name to it?

Institutional Reflection
Legal Asymmetry: Punish the Bear, Indulge the Bull
Livermore poses a question with powerful contemporary relevance — if someone fabricates rumors to depress securities and induce selling, the law surely punishes them. Who punishes those who spread utterly groundless bullish claims? The answer: nobody. This asymmetry creates a natural space for information manipulation.

Defense Guide
The Three-Question Rule: A Retail Investor's Information Filter
Though Livermore did not explicitly name "the Three-Question Rule," the logic of this chapter naturally derives this information-verification framework, applicable in any era. If the first question has no answer, don't bother with the rest.
1️⃣
Who Said It?
Anonymous sources are automatically
untrustworthy. Someone who won't
sign their name — why believe them?
2️⃣
Why Say It?
Are the publisher's interests aligned
with yours? Is that broker recommending
a buy while distributing for insiders?
3️⃣
Right Timing?
Markets lead fundamentals by 6–9
months. Today's good news may already
be fully priced or overpriced.
🔑 Key Takeaway
The information-manipulation mechanisms Livermore exposed 100 years ago remain entirely relevant today: bullish rumors wrapped in anonymous authority are the greatest source of public losses. Defense requires only three questions — Who said it? Why are they saying it? Is the timing right? If the first question has no answer, don't bother with the rest. Remember: the law does not protect you from bullish lies. In a market where bearish rumors are criminal but bullish lies are not, the only defense is your own independent thinking.
