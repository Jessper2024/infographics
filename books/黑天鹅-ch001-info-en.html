The Black Swan · Ch1 "The Self-Deceiving Human"

History does not crawl, it jumps · We are a giant backward-looking machine As a child, adults kept telling me the Lebanese war would last only "a few days" — it lasted nearly 17 years. History and society do not crawl. They make jumps. They go from fracture to fracture, with few vibrations in between. Yet we (and historians) prefer to believe in the small, gradual steps we think we can predict. Black Swans are Black Swans not because they are rare, but because we refuse to see them.

This chapter is the intellectual root of the entire book. Through the twin lenses of the Lebanese war and the 1987 crash, Taleb exposes the "triplet of opacity" in human cognition: ① the illusion of understanding — in a complex and random world, people believe they know what is happening; ② the retrospective distortion — we can only assess matters after the fact, history appears far clearer in books than in lived reality; ③ the overvaluation of factual information — the intelligent and well-informed do not see more clearly than a taxi driver. Recognizing these three mists is the first step toward understanding Black Swan events.

01
Nature of History
History leaps from fracture to fracture, with few vibrations in between
History and society do not crawl — they make jumps. Roman chroniclers barely recorded the rise of Christianity; who could have predicted that a group of horsemen would extend Islamic law from the Indian subcontinent to Spain in just a few years? Historian Georges Duby expressed astonishment at nearly ten Levantine Greek-speaking states being "swept away by the sword." These discontinuities make the historian's job nearly impossible — the most detailed study of the past does not produce historical thinking; it only produces the illusion of understanding history.

02
Cognitive Traps
The triplet of opacity: illusion of understanding, retrospective distortion, and overvaluation of facts
Human thought suffers from three ailments when confronting history. First: the illusion of understanding — in a world far more complex than imagined, people believe they know what is happening. Second: retrospective distortion — we can only assess matters after the fact, like looking through a rearview mirror. Third: overvaluation of factual information — the flaw of authorities and scholars, especially when they engage in "Platonic" categorization. These three mists combine to blind us to Black Swan events.
🌫️

Mist 1: Illusion of Understanding

In a random and complex world, everyone thinks they know what is happening. Adults told children the war would last "only days."

🪞

Mist 2: Retrospective Distortion

History appears far clearer in books than in reality. What seems obvious in hindsight was completely unforeseeable at the time.

03
Shirer's Revelation
The gulf between before and after: Shirer's diary reveals the cognitive chasm
Berlin Diary taught me more than any work of philosophy ever did. Shirer wrote without knowing what would happen next — the French believed Hitler was a temporary enemy, which explains their lack of preparation and rapid surrender. No one at the time imagined a catastrophe. Yet written after the fact, everything appears more "historical." Reading this diary in a basement while shells fell outside, Taleb first recognized: our minds are extraordinary explanation machines, capable of finding reason in almost anything — but only after the fact. The smarter the explainer, the more hollow the explanation.

04
Curse of Knowledge
The well-informed do not see more clearly than a taxi driver
Taleb's grandfather served as Lebanon's Defense Minister, Interior Minister, and Deputy Prime Minister — yet he saw no more clearly than his driver Mikhail. The difference: the driver habitually said "God knows," delegating clarity to a higher power. Elite thinkers believe they know more because they are elite. Almost everyone is familiar with every detail of current events — the overlap between newspapers is so vast that reading another paper adds almost nothing. Yet everyone eagerly devours every detail. All of it is in vain.
🎓

The Elite Thinker's Illusion

"If you are part of the elite, you know more than non-elites." — In reality, their predictions are no better than a taxi driver's.

🚕

The Taxi Driver's Wisdom

"God knows" — admitting ignorance, refusing to pretend to understand the incomprehensible. They know their limits.

05
Birth of a Black Swan
The 1987 crash: when the "impossible" became real, the world trembled
That day was a disaster in world financial history: the largest market crash in modern times. More devastating: it happened when we thought we were sophisticated enough, with Platonic economists and bell-curve equations designed to prevent, predict, and control large shocks. The crash was not even caused by any news. It exceeded anyone's imagination the day before. Walking on Park Avenue, Taleb felt a deafening inner voice saying "I was right" — he trembled at having guessed a rare and unexpected event. This ability came from studying the flaws and limits of models, not the models themselves.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Humans are giant backward-looking machines, always deceiving themselves. History only appears clear, explicable, and predictable in retrospect — in real time it is a dense fog. The triplet of opacity — the illusion of understanding, retrospective distortion, and overvaluation of factual information — is the root cause of why Black Swan events are ignored. You do not need more information or a smarter brain; you need to admit: "Nobody knows what is happening."
