Layout of the World · Chapter 2
「Military Geography of the Four Corners」

Guanzhong, Hebei, the Southeast, and Sichuan — terrain, defenses, and historical destiny of China's four strategic corners

The four corners each possess exceptional geographic conditions: Guanzhong, ringed by mountains and rivers, towers above the eastern plains; Hebei, backed by sea and mountains on three sides, opens southward; the Southeast relies on the Yangtze-Huaihe dual defense; Sichuan, a fortress basin, is called 'Land of Abundance.' This chapter dissects the topography, passes, economic foundations, and social character of each corner, revealing their strategic roles — how the Guanzhong Model unified the realm, why the political center shifted east to Hebei, why southeastern regimes largely settled for half the realm, and why Sichuan never produced a unifying dynasty.

1
Guanzhong
Fortress of Four Passes, Bastion of a Hundred Qin

Guanzhong is shielded on four sides: Qinling to the south, Longshan to the west, the Loess Plateau to the north, and Huashan-Xiaoshan to the east, with the Yellow River encircling. Hangu Pass (Tong Pass) controls the east-west artery; Wu Pass guards the southeast; San Pass defends the route to Hanzhong and Sichuan; Xiao Pass secures the northwest; the Pujin ferry serves as a side door. Its heartland, the 800-li Qinchuan plain, was extraordinarily fertile. The "Guanzhong Model" — "defend on three sides and control the eastern lords with one" — underpinned the Zhou, Qin, Han, Sui, and Tang dynasties.

2
Hebei
Commanding Position, Governing All Under Heaven

Hebei is embraced by mountains and sea on three sides: the Yanshan range shields the north, the Taihang range guards the west, and the Bohai Sea lies to the east. Strategic passes — Juyong, Shanhai, Zijing, Jingxing — control access routes. Sitting at the junction of agrarian and nomadic civilizations, Hebei combined farming with trade. Its martial populace produced "the finest troops under heaven." Eastern Han's Liu Xiu pioneered unification from Hebei; later, the Yuan, Ming, and Qing all built their foundations here. The political center's shift from Guanzhong to Hebei marks a fundamental transformation in China's defense and economic geography.

3
Southeast
Coiling Dragon, Crouching Tiger — Terrain of Advantage

The Southeast's defense rests on the Yangtze-Huaihe dual barrier. The Yangtze runs east-west, linking upstream and downstream; the Huaihe serves as its forward screen — "to hold the Yangtze, first hold the Huaihe." Caishi and Guazhou are critical Yangtze crossings. Shouchun, Zhongli, Shanyang, and Xuyi guard Huaihe tributary junctions. Southeastern regimes were mostly "transplanted courts," preserving orthodox Chinese culture yet beset by complacency — few northern expeditions succeeded. The sole exception: Zhu Yuanzhang's Ming dynasty, which unified from the south after conquering the Yangtze basin, then sweeping north to expel the Mongols.

4
Sichuan
Fortress Basin, Land of Abundance

The Sichuan Basin is ringed by towering mountains — its natural defenses surpass all other regions. The Three Gorges provide the eastern exit, with Fengjie (Kuizhou) as the gateway; the Jinniu and Micang roads head north, with Sword Gate as the portal. Its fertile plains earned the name "Land of Abundance." Yet no Sichuan-based regime ever unified China — two reasons: conflict between immigrant ruling elites and native populations sapped internal strength; the basin's protective geography bred a "basin mentality" of contented secession. Zhuge Liang's northern campaigns came closest to breaking out, but geography and national power ultimately constrained him.

5
Corner Comparison
Rise and Fall: The Interplay of Geography and Social Foundation

Guanzhong and Hebei both produced unifying dynasties — they combined mountain fortresses with powerful social-political forces: Guanzhong's Guan-Long military aristocracy, Hebei's fusion of nomadic martial traditions with Han culture. The Southeast mostly settled for half the realm, constrained by conservative social foundations. Sichuan remained trapped in secession, undermined by native-immigrant conflict and basin mentality. The fate of the four corners reveals an iron law: geographic conditions determine the posture of defense and offense; social foundations determine the ambition to advance. Both are indispensable.

📌 Key Takeaways

The four corners each possessed unique strategic endowments: Guanzhong's ring of passes, Hebei's mountain-sea embrace, the Southeast's Yangtze-Huaihe moat, and Sichuan's fortress basin. All offered bases for both defense and offense — but unifying the realm demanded not just geographic advantage but powerful social-political forces and ambition. Guanzhong and Hebei achieved unification; the Southeast and Sichuan stopped at regional hegemony. This is the four-corner destiny written jointly by terrain and human character.
