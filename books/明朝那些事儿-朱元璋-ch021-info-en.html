Those Ming Matters · Zhu Yuanzhang
Chapter 20 "The Last Great General — Lan Yu"

After Xu Da's death, Lan Yu became Ming's last great general. From a snow-lit surprise attack on Qingzhou in 1387, to Nahachu's 200,000-strong surrender without a fight, to a fatal act of arrogance at the surrender banquet—Lan Yu's brilliance and fatal flaws compose Ming's most magnificent and tragic general's legend.

Situation: Early Ming's great generals are gone—Xu Da dead, Chang Yuchun early-deceased, Li Wenzhong exiled, Feng Sheng aged. Nahachu, the Northern Yuan commander with 200,000 troops in Liaodong, remains Ming's biggest border threat. Complication: Zhu Yuanzhang wants total elimination of the Northern Yuan, but lacks capable commanders. Lan Yu, Chang Yuchun's brother-in-law, is talented but has the shallowest seniority. Problem: Can Lan Yu achieve what his more senior peers could not? Answer: Brilliantly—at Qingzhou and in the surrender of Nahachu. But a cultural misstep at the surrender banquet plants the seed of his future destruction.

01
End of an Era
The Death of Xu Da: A Generation's Swan Song
Xu Da died in 1384, closing the chapter on Ming's founding generation of generals. Li Shanchang was executed in the Hu Weiyong case; Chang Yuchun died young; Li Wenzhong was exiled; Feng Sheng and Deng Yu were elderly. Many of Zhu's best generals died by his own hand. The legend that Zhu killed Xu Da with a steamed goose is implausible—Xu Da posed no threat and was too valuable alive. Xu Da proved that an ordinary peasant could become one of history's greatest military minds through sheer effort.

02
Rising Star
Lan Yu: Chang Yuchun's Brother-in-Law, A Star Is Born
Lan Yu, from Dingyuan, Anhui, was Chang Yuchun's brother-in-law. The proud Chang Yuchun repeatedly recommended him to Zhu, but Zhu was unconvinced—too many established generals. Lan Yu finally proved himself in the 1372 northern campaign, fighting bravely to cover the retreat at Lingbei. But that crushing defeat by Wang Baobao burned in his mind: he must find that man and defeat him completely. Fate denied him that chance—Wang Baobao died in the northern desert in 1375. Lan Yu redirected his ambition: destroy the Northern Yuan entirely.

03
Signature Victory
Snowlit Night at Qingzhou: Lan Yu's Defining Moment
On the second day of the first lunar month, heavy snow blanketed the northern desert. Lan Yu led the vanguard cavalry toward Qingzhou, citing General Li Su's famous snowstorm crossing of Caizhou in the Tang dynasty: "Heaven has sent another blizzard—this is our moment!" The army rode through blinding white, their tracks covered instantly by falling snow. They arrived at Qingzhou to find the gates wide open, Yuan soldiers cooking breakfast, completely unprepared. These ghostly white-clad cavalry struck without mercy—total annihilation, Yuan commander Guo Lai killed, Qingzhou seized.

04
Strategic Masterstroke
Nahachu's Surrender: Money Stronger Than swords
While the army advanced, Zhu deployed a sharper weapon: money. He lured Mongol nobles to migrate south with generous land grants and wealth, exploiting the nomadic economy's structural weakness. Meanwhile, Nailawu—a former Nahachu subordinate—successfully negotiated Nahachu's surrender. Nahachu, facing Feng Sheng and Lan Yu's crack forces, knew resistance meant death. His final lament: "Heaven will not let me keep this army!" Two hundred thousand troops surrendered without fighting. Sun Tzu's ideal—"subduing the enemy without fighting" —achieved.

05
Seed of Doom
The Banquet That Changed Everything
Nahachu brought several hundred men to the surrender ceremony—a show of face for a commander with 200,000 troops. Lan Yu received him warmly, hosting a feast. When Nahachu raised his cup, Lan Yu noticed his tattered clothes and offered his own outer robe. A friendly gesture in Han culture—but Nahachu was Mongol. Refusing the Han lord's cast-off clothing was a matter of tribal dignity. The cultural miscommunication spiraled. Lan Yu abruptly detained Nahachu. This single moment—born of ignorance, not malice—planted the seed of Lan Yu's later arrogance, his eventual political destruction, and the Lan Yu Case that killed 15,000 people.

🔑 Core Takeaway
Lan Yu was the Ming dynasty's finest battlefield commander—his snowlit assault on Qingzhou and the peaceful surrender of Nahachu's 200,000 troops were textbook operations. But military brilliance cannot compensate for political immaturity. The robe incident at the surrender banquet was more than a cultural blunder; it marked the beginning of Lan Yu's arrogance, his growing sense of invincibility, and ultimately his political destruction. Zhu Yuanzhang's fear of the Mongols proved prophetic (his warnings were validated decades later). But Zhu also destroyed the very instrument of that security—Lan Yu—because greatness without political wisdom is intolerable to a paranoid emperor.
