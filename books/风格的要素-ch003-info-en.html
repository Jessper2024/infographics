The Elements of Style · Chapter 003「A Few Matters of Form」

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer Even brilliant content can be undermined by formatting errors. Heading spacing, numeral conventions, parenthesis punctuation, quotation formats, bibliographic references, word syllabication, and title treatment — these seemingly minor details constitute the facade of academic and formal writing. Most writers have only a partial grasp of formatting norms, habitually making errors without awareness. What are the precise conventions for these 7 categories? This chapter answers each in concise entries.

Though the shortest chapter in the book, this section covers 7 categories of formatting conventions essential to formal writing: heading spacing and starting lines, numeral conventions (use Arabic figures, not spelled-out words), punctuation placement inside and outside parentheses, differences between formal quotations, inline quotations, and verse quotations, bibliographic abbreviation and footnote conventions, general rules for word syllabication, and italicization with initial capitals for literary titles. These details shape the first impression your writing makes on professional readers.

01
Headings & Numerals
Heading Spacing & Numeral Conventions
Leave a blank line after the title or heading. On successive pages, begin at the first line if using ruled paper. Do not spell out dates or other serial numbers — use Arabic figures or Roman notation instead.
❌

Avoid

August ninth, nineteen eighteen · Rule Three · Chapter Twelve — spelled-out forms are uneconomical and unprofessional.

✅

Prefer

August 9, 1918 · Rule 3 · Chapter XII · 352nd Infantry — Arabic figures are clear and concise.

02
Parentheses
Punctuation Inside and Outside Parentheses
Punctuate the sentence as if the parenthetical expression were absent. Punctuate the expression inside as an independent unit: omit the final period, but retain a question mark or exclamation point. When a wholly detached expression or sentence is parenthesized, the final stop comes before the last mark of parenthesis.
①
Inline parenthesis: I went to his house yesterday (my third attempt to see him), but he had left town. — comma after the closing parenthesis.
②
Parenthesis with punctuation: He declares (and why should we doubt his good faith?) that he is now certain of success. — question mark stays inside.
③
Detached parenthesis: (A completely independent phrase or sentence follows this pattern.) — period inside the closing parenthesis.
03
Quotations
Three Quotation Formats and Their Usage
Quotation format depends on grammatical function: formal quotations are introduced by a colon and enclosed in quotation marks; inline appositive quotations are preceded by a comma and enclosed in quotation marks; verse quotations are indented on a new line and centered, without quotation marks. Indirect quotations (that-clauses), proverbs, and familiar literary expressions need no quotation marks.
Formal
→ →
Colon + quotes: The provision of the Constitution is: "No tax or duty shall be laid on articles exported from any state."
Inline
→ →
Comma + quotes: I recall the maxim of La Rochefoucauld, "Gratitude is a lively sense of benefits to come."
Verse
→ →
New line, centered, no quotes: Bliss was it in that dawn to be alive, / But to be young was very heaven!
04
References
Bibliographic References in Scholarly Writing
In scholarly work requiring exact references, abbreviate frequently cited titles but give full forms in an alphabetical bibliography at the end. Use parentheses or footnotes. Omit the words act, scene, line, book, volume, page — except when referring to only one of them.
📌
Abbreviation example: In the second scene of the third act → In III. ii (or insert III. ii in parentheses at the proper place).
📌
Citation format: After the killing of Polonius, Hamlet is placed under guard (IV. ii. 14) — act · scene · line.
📌
Biblical citation: 2 Samuel i: 17-27 — book · chapter · verse.
05
Syllabication & Titles
Word Division and Title Formatting
Four syllabication principles: Divide by construction (know-ledge, not knowl-edge); after a vowel (edi-ble); between doubled letters (Apen-nines), except when doubled at word-end (tell-ing); do not divide -ed when e is silent. Title format: Use italics with initial capitals; omit initial A or The when the title follows a possessive.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Formatting conventions may seem like 'minor details,' but they carry the facade of professional writing. These 7 categories — headings, numerals, parentheses, quotations, references, syllabication, and titles — are each a gesture of respect for the reader's time: correct formatting allows the reader to focus on content, not on errors. As Strunk implies: formatting conventions require no creativity, only discipline — and discipline is the foundation of good writing.
