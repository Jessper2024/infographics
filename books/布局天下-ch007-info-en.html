Layout of the World · Chapter 5
「Trajectories of Unification: Guanzhong & Hebei Models」

From the four corners through the four borders to the Central Plains — two fundamental geographic trajectories from fragmentation to unity

The passage from chaos to order and from fragmentation to unity follows a clear geographic trajectory: in widespread turmoil, local orders first crystallize, then expand toward comprehensive unification. The four corners — blessed with defensible terrain (Guanzhong, Hebei, the Southeast, and Sichuan) — are the easiest places to establish secure power bases and accumulate strength. This chapter proposes two 'geographic models' of unification: the Guanzhong Model — 'block three sides and defend, using only the eastern face to dominate the feudal lords' — like a giant eagle spreading its wings; and the Hebei Model — the Great Wall guarding the north while the Grand Canal links north and south, creating a system where 'southeastern grain feeds northwestern troops.' The type of division dictates the trajectory of unification: erosion-type fragmentation passes through the sequence triangular → bipolar → unified; collapse-type chaos sees power centers solidify directly from the four corners before expanding to integrate the whole.

1
Trajectory
The Universal Pattern: Crystallizing Local Order → Integrating the Whole

The geographic trajectory from division to reunification follows this pattern: in widespread chaos, local order crystallizes first, then these local orders converge toward comprehensive unity. China's three great divisions all culminated in bipolar confrontations on the eve of unification: east-west before Qin-Han, north-south before Sui-Tang, and north-south again before Yuan-Ming-Qing. In bipolar contests, geographic advantage lay with West (over East) and North (over South). Every north-conquers-south unification opened the situation from the upper reaches: Western Jin struck via Yizhou and Jingxiang, Sui via Yizhou and Han River, the Mongols via capturing Xiangyang to gain upstream momentum.

2
Guanzhong
The Guanzhong Model: Eagle Spreading Wings to Sweep the Central Plains

Unification based on Guanzhong has unique characteristics — what Rao calls the 'Guanzhong Model.' Zhang Liang summarized its architecture: 'Block three sides and defend, using only one face to dominate the eastern lords; when lords are settled, the rivers and canals convey the realm's resources west to the capital; when lords rebel, one can flow downstream and overwhelm them.' Two critical axes define this model. One is the mountain barrier along the eastern edge of China's second topographic tier — from Yanshan south along the Taihang, through Songshan and Fangcheng to the Dabie range — the military defense line to 'dominate the east,' studded with Juyong, Zijing, Daoma, Jingxing, Fukou, Tianjing, Hulao, and Yique passes. The other is the Yellow River-Wei River axis, the east-west transport line carrying eastern wealth to Guanzhong. Guanzhong was like a giant eagle: the left wing swept Shanxi, the right wing swept Hanzhong and Bashu — both wings deployed, it could envelop the Central Plains. Qin, Western Han, Sui, and Tang all based both their conquest and their governance on Guanzhong.

3
Hebei
The Hebei Model: Great Wall Horizontally, Grand Canal Vertically — the Eastward Shift of Political Gravity

The Hebei Model was key to later imperial China's unification architecture. For the Ming dynasty (Han-founded), two long lines intersected near Beijing: the 'Nine Border Garrisons' of the Great Wall — running from Liaodong in the east through Shanxi to Gansu-Shaanxi in the west, shielding the agricultural heartland from the steppe; and the Beijing-Hangzhou Grand Canal — running from Youyan in the north through Shandong to Jiangnan in the south, vertically traversing the eastern Great Plain. This created the system of 'southeastern grain feeding northwestern troops,' elegantly balancing political-military needs with economic supply. For Yuan and Qing (steppe-origin regimes), the canal line extended northward beyond Hebei to ensure a stable link between the Central Plains and the steppe — their roots lay beyond the border, and if circumstances soured, retreat to the steppe remained an option.

4
Four Corners
The Common Starting Point: The Four Corners as Power Centers

In the early stages of widespread chaos, those who first occupied the four corners — blessed with defensible terrain — could establish local order and secure bases, accumulating strength for future imperial campaigns. After Qin's fall, Liu Bang unified the realm from Guanzhong; after Wang Mang's fall, Liu Xiu unified from Hebei; after Sui's fall, Li Yuan unified from Guanzhong; after Yuan's fall, Zhu Yuanzhang unified from the Southeast. Notably, Qin, Western Han, Sui, and Tang had their bases of conquest and governance coincide (both in Guanzhong), while Eastern Han's political gravity shifted away from its Hebei conquest base to Henan, and Ming's political gravity soon shifted from its southeastern conquest base to Hebei. The base of conquest and the base of governance did not always coincide.

5
Trajectory Contrast
Erosion vs. Collapse: Contrasting Unification Trajectories

Different types of division produce different unification trajectories. Erosion-type unification path: complex quadratic fragmentation → simpler triangular balance → bipolar confrontation → unification. All six triangular configurations of the Wei-Jin era ended in north-south standoffs, and every north-south contest ended with northern conquest of the south. Collapse-type unification path: amid chaos, power centers solidify from the four corners → each radiates control over surrounding regions → eventually one center integrates the whole. Guanzhong powers typically controlled Longxi and Hexi; Hebei powers controlled the North China Plain and often Liaoxi; Sichuan was usually absorbed by the Southeast. Though these two trajectories differ, both unfold within the framework of the chessboard geography, confirming the fundamental law: from corners through borders, converging on the Central Plains.

📌 Key Takeaways

China's path from fragmentation to unity reflects the deep logic of its geographic structure. The Guanzhong Model is the eagle spreading its wings — left wing Shanxi, right wing Bashu — sweeping the Central Plains, with mountain passes 'dominating the east' and the Yellow-Wei waterway conveying the realm's resources. The Hebei Model presents the Great Wall-Grand Canal cross structure — the Wall guarding the north horizon, the Canal linking the south vertical — with southeastern wealth sustaining northwestern military power. Whether through erosion (triangular → bipolar → unified) or collapse (corners consolidate → contend for the Central Plains → integrate the whole), the geographic law remains: the four corners are the cradles of power, the Central Plains the ultimate arena. The base of conquest and the base of governance need not coincide — Guanzhong once served both, but after Yuan-Ming-Qing, political gravity shifted eastward, finding new equilibrium in Hebei.
