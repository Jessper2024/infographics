弹性生长 · Ch.15
「Future Cities: Hegang-ization or Shenzhen-ization?」

Growth Is the Anomaly, Stagnation Is the Norm—Finding a Suitable Niche for Everyone

📖 Chapter Overview

A conversation with the author's hometown city-leader classmate reveals the core thesis: outside tier-1 and tier-2 cities, most places are Hegang—no good resources, no growth industries, continuous brain drain. In static societies, personal connections become currency, and civil service is everyone's dream job. But the author notes: growth is the anomaly; stagnation or micro-growth is the historical norm. Developed countries' "second urbanization" is now playing out in China—talent flowing from small cities to big ones. A good country provides diverse options: ambitious youth can build careers in Shenzhen; others can choose to build their hometowns. Acknowledge being ordinary, and you'll find the right path.

🔍 Deep Dive
Why Hegang Became Hegang
Three lethal factors: no resources or resources exhausted; no high-growth industries; continuous brain drain. A place needs geographic advantage or environmental appeal to develop. Port cities or transportation hubs can trade resources first, then transform before depletion. But resource cities mostly fall victim to dependency—transformation failures abound. Outside tier-1 and tier-2 cities, most places face this same predicament.
Static Culture: When Favors Become Currency
In slow-growth societies, personal connections are another form of currency. Finding jobs for children, handling errands—without connections, one feels uneasy. Ideas of "striving" and "getting ahead" fade; instead, "stability" becomes the obsession. Civil service is the most beloved career—not just in Shandong, but wherever private enterprise is weak. Capable people all flow into the system, further starving private enterprise, reinforcing "only government jobs matter"—a vicious cycle.
The Red Line of Land Finance: People Can No Longer Afford Homes
Real estate as economic engine has a hard limit—when local residents stop buying. If they're still buying, purchasing power remains; if not, they're tapped out. This creates low-price cities: people pooled family resources to buy one home; after years of this, everyone who could buy already has. Developers stop acquiring land; the land-revenue crutch is broken. The cruel irony: cities that most need this model can't sustain it; cities that can sustain it no longer need it.
Positive Feedback: How Tier-1 Cities Amplify Talent Value
A personal case: a PhD in graphics algorithms with world-class research but no domestic manufacturers interested—about to fly to the US. A tech lead at a major company discussed it with management; the leader was open-minded enough to fund continued research. Years later, he has his own lab and team, living in a nice Beijing neighborhood. Such things used to happen only abroad, but increasingly happen here—and almost exclusively in tier-1 cities. More people, broader thinking, quicker talent recognition → investment → positive feedback loop.
Acknowledge Being Ordinary—The Right Choice in a Diverse Society
A good country provides diverse options: ambitious youth build careers in Shenzhen; others choose to build their hometowns. But for now, "lying flat" and living comfortably isn't realistic—unless you're born rich. The country will provide safety nets, enforce labor laws, and exit the rat race. If able-bodied, clear-minded people live better without working than those who work hard, that's a deformed society. Wealth gaps will grow, but quality-of-life gaps won't be enormous. Acknowledge being ordinary—only then can you choose the right path.
💡 Key Takeaway

Growth is the anomaly; stagnation or micro-growth has been the norm throughout human history. China's 40-year miracle came from the convergence of tech cycles, reform, and demographic dividends—all now fading, bringing a "return to normal." A few cities "Shenzhen-ize"—high income, high prices, high pressure, high turnover. Most "Hegang-ize"—low prices, low income, low pressure. Don't panic: a mature society finds a niche for everyone. There's nothing wrong with acknowledging you're ordinary. Adjust your mindset, choose the path that fits you.
