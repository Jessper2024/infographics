Core Pattern
Futures: Slow Rise, Fast Crash — The Carrying-Cost Cycle
Futures prices are steadily pushed upward by carrying costs — storage, financing, and insurance — creating a slow, grinding uptrend. But underlying value hasn't risen proportionally, so the price structure must be interrupted by periodic sharp collapses. Cocoa rises for weeks or months, only to crash back in a single week. Gold climbed $85 over three months, then surrendered 64% of those gains in just three days.

02
Price Anchors
Ceiling and Floor: The Pricing Anchors for Shorting Futures
Unlike stocks, futures have natural pricing boundaries. Commodity price ceilings are set by substitute costs; the floor is production cost. Short near the ceiling, cover near the floor. False breakout + bearish divergence + MACD missing right shoulder = the strongest short setup.
📈

Ceiling · Substitute Cost

The upper limit is set by the cost of alternatives. Prices may briefly spike above but won't stay — precisely when savvy traders short.

📉

Floor · Production Cost

The price floor is production cost. When prices fall below, producers cut output, supply drops, and prices rise. Exceptions don't last.

03
Zero-Sum Game
Options: The Dividing Line Between Winners and Losers
In options, the line separating winners from losers is not about skill — it's between buyers and sellers. Winners are always sellers; losers are always buyers. Options are wasting assets — you can be right about direction, but time decay kills. Options are about trading hope: buy hope or sell hope. Professionals sell hope.

04
Tactics
Covered vs. Naked: Two Paths to Selling Options
Two paths exist: covered writing uses owned shares as collateral to earn premium income; naked writing creates options out of thin air against account capital. The former is conservative income enhancement for large portfolios; the latter maximizes profit but requires $100K–$250K capital and ruthless discipline. Worst case for naked: buying shares at strike price — entirely acceptable to a pro.

Risk Alert
Forex: Bucket Shop Traps and Trend Trading Rules
Forex is the most trend-driven market — currency trends can persist for years. But retail forex dealers harbor severe conflicts of interest: they act as your counterparty, meaning your profit is their loss. They offer insane 400:1 leverage that renders money management meaningless. Currency futures are the more transparent alternative. Every forex trade is a spread trade: buying one currency automatically shorts the other.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The essence of shorting non-equity products lies not in technique, but in understanding each market's game structure: short futures by exploiting the slow-rise/fast-crash pricing rhythm; short options by always standing on the side of time (the seller); short forex by avoiding bucket shops that bet against their own clients. The shared foundation across all three markets: sufficient capital, ruthless discipline, and cutting losses short while letting profits run.
