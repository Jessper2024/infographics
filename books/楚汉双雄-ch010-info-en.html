The Chu-Han Contention · Chapter Ten 「The Climactic Duel: The Battle of Gaixia and the Final Betrayal」

Liu Bang tears up the Honggou treaty, the Guting defeat, Zhang Liang's enfeoffment stratagem, 300,000 allied troops surround Gaixia — the greatest showdown in history

After the Honggou peace, Xiang Yu withdrew eastward. Liu Bang, urged by Zhang Liang and Chen Ping — “What Heaven offers, refusing invites calamity” — tore up the ceasefire and suddenly attacked the retreating Chu army. But at Guting, Han Xin and Peng Yue held their troops back; Liu Bang advanced alone and was routed by Xiang Yu yet again. Zhang Liang proposed the ultimate stratagem: enfeoff them as kings in exchange for their armies. With 300,000 troops surrounding Gaixia, Liu Bang handed full command to Han Xin. Han Xin deployed six battle formations in three echelon layers. For the only time in two millennia of Chinese history, two commanders ranked among the top five of all time faced each other in pitched battle on the Gaixia plain.

01
背信弃义
Echoes Four Centuries Later: Liu Bang Rips Up the Honggou Treaty
After the Honggou peace, Liu Bang withdrew toward Guanzhong. But Zhang Liang and Chen Ping urged: “The realm is already more than half Han's; Chu's provisions are exhausted, its soldiers weary — this is the moment to destroy Chu. What Heaven offers, refusing invites calamity!” Liu Bang immediately agreed — unilaterally tearing up the ceasefire and suddenly pursuing the retreating Chu army. Four hundred years later, Qiao Zhou of Shu Han used this betrayal as key evidence in his essay “On Enemy States,” a masterful polemic that dismantled the last hope of the Liu clan from within, setting the stage for Deng Ai to conquer Shu with a ragged army of ten thousand. History possesses a stunning symmetry — the treaty torn up by Liu Bang boomeranged on his descendants in identical fashion.
02
致命意外
The Guting Debacle: Why Han Xin and Peng Yue Held Back
Liu Bang devised a four-pronged encirclement: his main force frontal pursuit, Peng Yue south to cut supply lines, Han Xin to seize Pengcheng, Ying Bu to block the southern retreat. But at Guting, Han Xin and Peng Yue's armies never appeared. Liu Bang advanced alone into isolation and once again faced the “Demon King” Xiang Yu head-on. The enraged Chu army, united in righteous fury, routed the Han forces, forcing Liu Bang to barricade himself in Yangxia. The man who had always stood others up was now stood up himself. Liu Bang asked Zhang Liang — and Zhang Liang was razor-sharp: “The feudal lords care more about their own interests. Han Xin's kingship was self-requested, leaving doubt in his heart; Peng Yue has long wanted to be King of Wei, and Your Majesty has delayed the enfeoffment. That is the reason.”
03
封王奇计
Zhang Liang's Ultimate Stratagem: Carve Up Territory for Unity Under Heaven
Zhang Liang proposed: “Grant Suiyang to Gucheng to Peng Yue as King of Wei; give Chenyi eastward to the sea entirely to Han Xin.” Liu Bang complied. Peng Yue and Han Xin immediately agreed to send troops. Han Xin ordered Guan Ying's cavalry as vanguard, sweeping southward, breaching Chu defenses, successively capturing Xue County, Pei County, and Liu County, then seizing Pengcheng — the Chu capital — in a single stroke, capturing its defender Xiang Tuo. Han Xin pressed westward victoriously, unstoppable, and joined forces with Liu Bang. Peng Yue's army also rushed south. With the three forces combined, military momentum swelled; they defeated Zhongli Mei at Guting, besieged Chen County, and accepted the surrender of its defender Li Ji. Xiang Yu was forced to abandon his planned decisive battle at Chen, retreating south along the Ying River, preparing to cross the Huai River.
04
史上最强
The Gaixia Showdown: Han Xin's 300,000 vs. the Hegemon-King's 100,000 — China's Only God-Tier Duel
Xiang Yu retreated to Gaixia and fortified, preparing for the final battle. He chose the endless Gaixia plain as the battleground — here, Liu Bang would have to meet him in open field combat. The allied forces surrounding Xiang Yu reached 300,000. Liu Bang knew he lacked the ability to command a battle of this magnitude — he handed full command to Han Xin. Han Xin deployed three echelon layers with six battle formations: the front army led by himself to absorb the Chu cavalry charge head-on; the middle army in three columns — Liu Bang commanding the center, Kong Xi guarding the left, Chen He the right; Zhou Bo and Chai Wu bringing up the rear. Two commanders ranked among the top five in Chinese history finally faced each other. Xiang Yu was like Hong Qigong — the Eighteen Dragon-Subduing Palms, peerless under heaven; Han Xin was like Huang Yaoshi — the arts of deception and misdirection, unfathomable. On equal terms, the outcome would be unknowable.
05
终局之战
The Commander Defeats the War God: The Victory Code at Gaixia
The historical record is extraordinarily concise: Xiang Yu's 100,000 charged. Han Xin engaged, fared poorly, and retreated. Kong Xi and Chen He then surged in from the flanks, and when the Chu army faltered, Han Xin counterattacked and routed them at Gaixia. Two truths hide behind these sparse lines: first, Xiang Yu was tragic and Liu Bang's victory was ignoble, stirring the historian's emotion; second, this battle was commanded by Han Xin — the “Hegemon-King of Western Chu” lost only once in his life, to a later-executed “rebel,” while the emperor was merely taking orders. The more detail, the more the emperor would lose face. Xiang Yu's exhaustion after a hundred victories — every victory postponed a vulnerability that should have detonated; vulnerabilities can only be postponed, never self-eliminated. Gaixia was not a military duel's outcome but the inevitability of systemic confrontation.
📌 KEY TAKEAWAY
The Battle of Gaixia reveals a brutal truth: winning every battle is not genuine advantage. Every one of Xiang Yu's victories postponed a vulnerability that should have detonated — vulnerabilities are only postponed, never self-eliminating. The day you can no longer win, these accumulated vulnerabilities “counter-strike” with fury. Liu Bang's four-pronged encirclement was fundamentally a systemic approach — even after the Guting debacle, Zhang Liang's enfeoffment stratagem could pull the situation back. Xiang Yu relied on individual genius; Liu Bang relied on system-driven operations. The final confrontation between these two models closed at Gaixia. Additionally, Liu Bang's tearing up of the Honggou treaty echoed four centuries later in the form of Qiao Zhou's polemic — history forgives no one.
