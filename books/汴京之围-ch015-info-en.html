The Siege of Bianjing · Ch.13 "Difficult Negotiations"

Part III: The Fall of Bianjing · Surrender → Emperor Visits Qingcheng → Act of Submission → Endless Extortion After the outer wall fell, Emperor Qinzong placed his hopes on negotiation, traveling to Qingcheng to submit an act of surrender. The Jin initially appeared cordial — but their reparation demands were astronomical: 10 million ingots of gold, 20 million of silver, twenty times the previous figure. The Kaifeng Prefecture became an efficient extraction machine — seizing gold and silver, confiscating horses, delivering silk, capturing women as debt payment. The emperor believed satisfying these demands would preserve half the empire. But a larger catastrophe was already brewing.

This chapter records the complete post-surrender negotiation process. On the 30th of the intercalary 11th month, Emperor Qinzong traveled to Nianhan's Qingcheng camp with 300 attendants, bowing four times before a north-facing incense altar to acknowledge vassal status. The surrender document went through four rounds of revision — Nianhan demanded parallel-prose elegance, deleted "Great Song Emperor" replacing it with "your subject," changed "bearing guilt" to "lacking virtue," "universe" to "within the seas." On the 3rd of the 12th month, the Jin delivered their bill: gold 10 million ingots, silver 20 million (equivalent to 100 million taels of gold, 1 billion of silver), plus 10 million bolts of silk. The Kaifeng Prefecture, under Wang Shiyong and Xu Bingzhe, became a ruthlessly efficient extraction machine — and women were priced as debt payment: palace ladies 500 ingots of silver, singers 200, common women 100.

01
Downwind Talks
The Night the Wall Fell: The Emperor Chose to Trust the Jin, Not His Own Army
The night the outer wall fell, only Prince Jing, Mei Zhili, Qin Hui, and a handful of others remained by the emperor's side. The consensus: nothing left but negotiation. Prince Jing was lowered over the wall in a basket to Wolibu's camp. Li Ruoshui returned with Nianhan's message — Jin troops controlled everything within 500 li; the emperor had nowhere to flee. Grand Councilor He Li went to Nianhan: "I refused to come before for the dynasty's sake; I come now for the people's lives." Nianhan called him a loyal minister. Then came the shock: the Retired Emperor must come out in person, or the empress dowager and princesses would be taken hostage. Meanwhile, the city spiraled into chaos — deserters looted, famine drove cannibalism, corpses were stripped of flesh instantly.

02
Emperor's Visit
The Surrender Document: Revised Like a Schoolboy's Essay Under the Conqueror's Red Pen
On the 30th, Qinzong exited the Nanxun Gate with 300 attendants, escorted by Jin cavalry to Qingcheng's Fasting Palace. Nianhan made him write the surrender document first. Sun Di drafted it — Nianhan rejected it. Four rounds of revision: it must be parallel prose with literary merit; retain "Great Jin Emperor" but delete "Great Song Emperor" leaving only "your subject"; change "bearing guilt" to "lacking virtue," "universe" to "within the seas." On the 2nd of the 12th month, the formal ceremony: dragon motifs covered with blue felt, incense altar facing north, emperor bowed four times acknowledging vassalage. Nianhan smiled: the city has fallen — theoretically everything already belongs to the Jin. Distribute the gold and silk you brought among the soldiers.

03
Prince Kang's Refuge
Wax-Pill Edicts and Alum-Ink Letters: The Last Hope of the Song Survives in the Crevices
As early as the 22nd of the intercalary 11th month, Prince Kang received a secret edict from Qinzong hidden in a messenger's topknot — appointing him Supreme Marshal of All Forces. On the 1st of the 12th month, he established his headquarters in Xiangzhou. The Jin pressured the emperor to recall him; Qinzong first used wax-sealed pills, then wrote messages in alum-water on messengers' sleeves — technology defeated Jin searches. But Prince Kang's camp was divided: hawkish Zong Ze vs. dovish Wang Boyan, who urged retreat to distant Dongping for safety. On the 19th, Prince Kang moved east to Dongping. In Xiangzhou, he met a young Yue Fei. Other relief forces fared no better — Fan Zhixu's 200,000-strong army was routed at Qianqiu Town on first contact with the Jin.

04
Endless Demands
Women Priced, Gold Surging, Households Desolate: The Kaifeng Prefecture's Ultimate Extraction
From the 13th of the 12th month, the Kaifeng Prefecture under Wang Shiyong and Xu Bingzhe became an extraction machine. Executive, judicial, and oversight powers merged — nobles and titled ladies were shackled if they refused to surrender gold. Informants got 30% rewards. Gold prices quintupled. Empress Zheng's residence was raided for hidden bullion. Most appalling: the Jin demanded 1,500 young women for the troops, with explicit pricing — palace ladies 500 silver ingots, singers 200, common women 100. Imperial Academy student Xu Kui went alone to the Nanxun Gate with a petition citing the Spring and Autumn Annals — the Jin killed him. The emperor's detached response: "Such matters cannot be settled by words alone!"

05
Extraction Vortex
From the Chancellery Fire to Genyue's Destruction: A City's Spiritual Collapse
When the reparation figures were first made public, Bianjing society shattered. On the 15th of the 12th month, the Chancellery burned — the fire spread to the Ministries of Rites, Personnel, and Justice; people tore down the Chancellery plaque and threw it in to "suppress" the flames. On the 22nd, amid foot-deep snow, the emperor opened the Genyue Imperial Garden for firewood — but gangs seized everything. The next day, the destruction spiraled out of control: pavilions and towers demolished wholesale, hundreds crushed by collapsing buildings, hundreds more killed in brawls, corpses instantly stripped to bone. The Kaifeng Prefecture executed five ringleaders to restore order. Genyue, the masterpiece landscape that had taken years to build, was destroyed in days. The Jin later reduced demands to 1 million gold ingots and 5 million silver — still impossible to fulfill.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Surrender brought not peace but a bottomless pit of extortion. Emperor Qinzong believed that visiting Qingcheng, submitting a surrender document, and satisfying reparation demands would preserve half the empire — but he was facing a promise-delivery system that could never be fulfilled. Every round of demands drained the city's last drop of blood: from gold to horses, from silk to women, from books to antiquities, from craftsmen to musicians. More cruelly, these sacrifices only emboldened the Jin. As the Kaifeng Prefecture operated at peak efficiency, as informants collected 30% rewards, as women were priced like livestock, a civilization's dignity was ground to dust. And the emperor still naively believed: just one more ball game and I can go home.
