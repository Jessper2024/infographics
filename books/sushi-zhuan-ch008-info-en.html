Su Shi Biography · Ch.8
"In Old Age, the Rivers and Lakes Are Never Lost"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer March 1084: the edict transferring him to Ruzhou arrived in Huangzhou — four years and two months on the Eastern Slope came to an abrupt end. "Going home — but where is my home?" In parting, Huangzhou's hills, waters, elders, and horn-drums all turned into the deepest attachment. What did leaving Huangzhou after four years and two months mean to Su Shi? What did he take, and what did he leave behind?

The final month was extraordinarily busy: farewell banquets filled his schedule, friends sought calligraphy keepsakes, and the Pan brothers asked him to copy both Red Cliff Rhapsodies and "Going Home" in minuscule script. The courtesan Li Qi finally gathered courage at the last banquet to request a poem — he inscribed: "Just like Du Fu of West Sichuan — the crabapple is lovely, yet never praised in verse." On departure day, neighbors gathered before Snow Hall, an old man offered wine with trembling hands, and across the river came the haunting sound of Huangzhou's horn-drums. Su Shi stopped, looked back at Dongpo, and wept. "Some year I'll come rowing back — then sound this tune again to welcome me."

01
Turning Point
The Transfer Edict: Sudden Departure After Four Years
In mid-March 1084, Su Shi submitted the customary memorial of gratitude for his transfer to Ruzhou — at last, a glimmer of change in his official career. He entrusted Dongpo, Snow Hall, and his wet nurse's grave to Pan Bing's care, planning to first visit his brother Su Zhe in Junzhou. Once word spread, people came ceaselessly to bid farewell. The Pan brothers' nephews asked him to copy the two Red Cliff Rhapsodies; Su Shi offered instead to write "Going Home," whereupon the young men shamelessly asked for all three — for this, Su Shi delayed his departure by several days.

02
Farewell Poem
Li Qi's Poem: A Witty Classical Allusion
At a prefectural farewell banquet, a courtesan named Li Qi — gifted, well-read, and quietly elegant — had long admired Su Shi but was too shy to ask for a poem. This was her last chance. After several rounds of wine, she finally removed her silk scarf and requested his calligraphy. Su Shi wrote: "Five years Dongpo has dwelled in Huangzhou — why has no word touched on Li Qi?" Then guests from another table interrupted with toasts. As the banquet neared its end, a frantic Li Qi begged again. Su Shi burst out laughing and finished: "Just like Du Fu of West Sichuan — the crabapple is lovely, yet never praised in verse." Of all Huangzhou's courtesans, she alone received such supreme praise.

03
Heartfelt Farewell
"Courtyard Full of Fragrance": The Inner Conflict
On April 1, at a farewell gathering with friends and neighbors, Su Shi wrote the deeply emotional lyric "Courtyard Full of Fragrance." "Going home — but where is my home? My home is ten thousand li away at Mount Min and Emei. Half a century gone, the days ahead are sadly few. What is this life — coming and going like a shuttle!" At nearly fifty, Su Shi truly felt exhausted. He longed to follow Tao Yuanming and cast off official fetters completely, but the Confucian spirit of service was rooted too deeply. The poem ends with an earnest request: "Take good care of the slender willows before the hall — thinking of me, don't trim their tender branches. And tell the elders south of the river: from time to time, sun my fishing rain-cloak for me."

04
Affectionate Horns
Night Crossing: Huangzhou's Horn-Drums Bid Farewell
At dusk on departure day, Su Shi's party crossed the Yangtze toward Wuchang. All was still, when suddenly, faint horn-drum sounds drifted across the river — it was Huangzhou's horn-drums! In four years, how many times had they accompanied his night rambles, roused him from drunken stupor? Su Shi halted, looked back at Dongpo, and tears fell. "Huangzhou's horn-drums are also full of feeling, sending me southward, begrudging no distance... Who says all regions sound the same? Alligators rage and dragons grieve, transformed for me!" He concluded: "Some year I'll come rowing back upstream — then sound this tune again to welcome me." But official life would not permit it — this beautiful wish was never fulfilled.

05
Emotional Home
The Unfulfilled Wish: Dongpo as Spiritual Origin
On departure morning, neighbors and friends gathered spontaneously before Snow Hall. An old man, hands trembling, filled a cup to the brim: "Sir, please drink this parting wine. Don't forget this place — it is your home too." Su Shi took the cup reverently, his throat constricting. He remembered clearing the land: "They followed me to Dongpo, sharing labor and meals"; building the dike: "Neighbors came in droves to help"; planting: "The farmers counseled me." When he arrived four years earlier, he was a terrified criminal official; when he left, he was the Master of Dongpo who could face "a lifetime of mist and rain." Huangzhou did not become where he grew old, but Dongpo had become his eternal spiritual home — the origin-point where he found crystalline joy in the crisp tap of his staff.

🔑 Key Takeaway
Four years and two months in Huangzhou — from the lonely indignation of "choosing among cold branches, unwilling to perch" to the reluctant attachment of "going home — but where is my home?" The Su Shi who left Huangzhou was no longer the man who had arrived. He carried away the personality transformation forged at Dongpo and the literary summit crystallized at Red Cliff, leaving behind Snow Hall, slender willows, and a fishing rain-cloak. The affectionate farewell of Huangzhou's horn-drums, the parting wine offered by trembling old hands, and the earnest request to "sun my fishing cloak from time to time" — together they formed the most heartfelt farewell. As he wrote: "Who says all regions sound the same? Alligators rage and dragons grieve, transformed for me" — the very landscape of Huangzhou was bidding him farewell, and he had confirmed this place as his eternal spiritual home.
