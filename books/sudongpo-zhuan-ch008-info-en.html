Wang Anshi's Reform was China's fourth experiment in state capitalism — and like the three before it, it was doomed by the reformer's own eccentric personality, obsessive idealism, and the timeless truth that "those who defy all human norms are seldom anything but great villains."

Core QuestionWhy was Wang Anshi's Reform — China's last experiment in state capitalism — destined for tragedy from the very beginning?

Core AnswerIn the second year of Xining (1069), the Su brothers arrived in the capital just as China was engulfed by the New Policies. Wang Anshi — a brilliant but eccentric recluse who never changed his robes, ate whatever dish sat closest, and once consumed an entire plate of fish bait at an imperial banquet — admired the Legalist Shang Yang and pursued radical state intervention. Three prior totalitarian experiments in Chinese history (Shang Yang-Qin, Sang Hongyang-Han, Wang Mang-Xin) had all ended in catastrophe. Su Xun had already foreseen the disaster in his essay Discernment of Treachery, warning that anyone so utterly disconnected from normal human sentiment could only bring ruin upon the state.

1
History's Mirror

Four Totalitarian Experiments, All Failures

Over four millennia of Chinese history, four attempts were made at totalitarianism, state capitalism, and radical social revolution — Shang Yang's Legalism briefly united China before collapsing, Sang Hongyang's fiscal state nearly triggered rebellion, Wang Mang's reforms died with him, and Wang Anshi's became the fourth and final experiment.

The earliest Legalist experiment, driven by Shang Yang and enforced by Qin Shi Huang, rested on two principles: "teach war" and "emphasize agriculture" — peasants as soldiers, merchants suppressed. Qin unified China with this system, only to collapse within years. During Emperor Wu of Han, Sang Hongyang's state-capitalist fiscal theory filled the treasury but nearly provoked revolt. Wang Mang's usurpation brought the third attempt, ending with his overthrow.

Lin Yutang notes a telling pattern: all four reforms originated from innovative thinkers determined to demolish the past, each executing their convictions with absolute resolve. And "throughout history, every totalitarian ideology has invoked the name of the nation and the people" — how many political crimes have been committed in the name of "the people."

2
The Eccentric

An Idealist Defying All Human Norms

Wang Anshi was an oddity — a diligent scholar and fine poet who carried a messianic sense of mission yet utterly lacked human warmth. He wore filthy clothes, never noticed when friends secretly swapped his robe, ate only the dish placed directly before him, and once absent-mindedly consumed an entire plate of fish bait at a banquet.

Three anecdotes encapsulate the man: friends hid a clean robe while he bathed — he emerged wearing it without noticing the change. At dinner he ate only the dish closest to him; when rabbit was moved away, he had no idea it existed. Emperor Renzong once set out fish bait for guests to catch their own fish — Wang Anshi ate the entire plate of bait, convincing the emperor he was a hypocrite.

"Dressed like a slave, eating like a beast, with the face of a prisoner and unkempt hair, yet discoursing on the Odes and Documents" — Su Xun, Discernment of Treachery

As a junior aide in Yangzhou, Wang Anshi studied all night and dozed in his chair until dawn, often rushing to the yamen unwashed. Governor Han Qi mistook his disheveled appearance for debauchery and reprimanded him. Later, when Han Qi changed his view and sought to mentor him, Wang Anshi bristled with resentment, writing in his diary: "Han Qi has a handsome face — nothing else worth mentioning."

3
Prophetic Warning

Su Xun's Discernment of Treachery

Su Xun wrote the Discernment of Treachery with extraordinary prescience, declaring that "those whose conduct defies all normal human feelings are seldom anything but great villains" — an essay that became required reading in Chinese schools, and one that history would tragically vindicate.

Su Xun argued that human character is difficult to discern — even the wise can be deceived. Only calm observers can see through a person's nature and predict their future. He cited Wang Yan of the Jin Dynasty and Lu Qi of the Tang — two superficially remarkable men who ultimately destroyed empires. Wang Anshi, Su Xun warned, combined Lu Qi's hideous appearance and cunning with Wang Yan's eloquence.

🔴 Danger Signs of Deception

Reciting Confucius and Laozi while practicing false humility, gathering fame-seekers around him — all while secretly harboring malice. Such a person can deceive even the wisest sovereign.

🟢 Normal Human Conduct

"A dirty face one naturally wishes to wash; soiled clothes one naturally wishes to clean." Behavior so unnatural as to be anti-human is precisely the marker of hidden evil.

Only a few saw through Wang Anshi — Su Xun and his old friend Zhang Fangping chief among them. Zhang had worked with Wang Anshi and dismissed him, never speaking to him again. Ouyang Xiu tried to introduce Wang Anshi to the Su family, but the elder Su refused to meet him. When Wang Anshi's mother died, Su Xun declined the funeral invitation and instead wrote his immortal essay.

4
Path to Power

25 Years of Refusing Office: Big Frog in a Small Pond

From passing the jinshi exam at 21 until assuming power at 46, Wang Anshi spent twenty-five years repeatedly refusing high office in the capital, preferring minor provincial posts — not for fame, but to be "a big frog in a small pond," biding his time to implement his radical vision.

In local governance, Wang Anshi excelled: he built dams, reformed schools, established farmer loans, and piloted elements of his reform agenda. The more he declined high office, the greater his fame grew — until every major official in the capital yearned to meet him. Only in 1060, when offered the powerful post of Finance Commissioner, did he finally accept a capital appointment.

Lin Yutang identifies two reasons for his long wait: first, the capital's senior scholars — Fan Zhongyan, Sima Guang, Ouyang Xiu — would never endorse radical reform; second, Wang Anshi's temperament demanded absolute control. As colleagues Wu Gui and Zhang Fangping recalled, "he was extremely difficult to work with."

5
Character Analysis

The Genius Disguise: Is Filth a Sign of Spirituality?

Lin Yutang delivers a biting observation: "in all countries, eccentrics, visionaries, and schizophrenics believe that slovenliness is the mark of genius" — if filth truly represented higher spirituality, heaven would be filled with dirty, smelly angels.

Wang Anshi's peculiarities extended beyond clothing — he never noticed food before him, studied through the night dozing upright in a chair, and seemed genuinely oblivious to his own physical state. Were these signs of a truly abstracted mind, or calculated self-promotion? Emperor Renzong, after the fish-bait incident, concluded he was a hypocrite. Shao Bowen's account offered a third interpretation: a man so absorbed in thought that he naturally neglected his appearance.

Su Xun's insight cuts deepest: normal people wash dirty faces and change soiled clothes — these are "the ultimate human feelings." Wang Anshi's behavior was so abnormal it crossed into anti-human territory. This very defiance of human norms, Su Xun argued, was the definitive criterion for identifying a "great villain."

6
Reform Ignites

Xining Year 2: The Spark That Set Everything Ablaze

In 1069, the Su brothers arrived in the capital as China was swept into the New Policies — Wang Anshi, with Shang Yang as his spiritual idol, launched reforms whose political aftershocks would reverberate until the Song Dynasty's final days.

As Lin Yutang summarizes: this was not China's first experiment with state capitalism, but it was the last. Wang Anshi openly admired Shang Yang and wrote poetry celebrating Legalist thought. His New Policies — the Green Sprouts Loan, the Market Exchange Law, the Equitable Transport System — were direct descendants of Shang Yang's "teach war, emphasize agriculture," all representing aggressive state intervention in the economy.

What makes this chapter particularly poignant is the gap between those who saw the danger and those in power. Su Xun and Zhang Fangping had diagnosed Wang Anshi's character years earlier. Most senior statesmen — Fu Bi, Wen Yanbo, Han Qi — were deeply skeptical. Yet Emperor Shenzong's ambition and Wang Anshi's obsession would soon converge, unleashing a political storm that would irrevocably alter the Northern Song's trajectory.

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

The tragedy of Wang Anshi's Reform lies in a brilliant but profoundly eccentric figure wielding the power to remake a nation. All four of China's state-capitalist experiments ended in failure. Su Xun's Discernment of Treachery endures as a classic because it reveals a timeless truth: those who defy all normal human sentiments are seldom anything but great villains. The seeds of the reform's failure were sown long before any policy was enacted — they lay in the very personality of the man who would carry it out.
