"Wang Anshi's Reform"

SCQA · Situation → Conflict → Question → Answer "Dressed in rags, eating pig-swill" — Su Xun's prophetic "Discourse on Identifying Villains" foretold that this eccentric man would launch the Song Dynasty's most dramatic state-capitalist experiment. All four of China's totalitarian experiments had ended in disaster. Why was Wang Anshi's reform doomed from the start? The answer lay in the reformer's own grotesque character.

Wang Anshi's reform was the fourth state-capitalist experiment in Chinese history, and the seeds of its failure were already planted in the reformer's eccentric personality, paranoid idealism, and fatal flaw: "Those who deviate from normal human feelings are rarely anything but great villains." In 1069, the Su brothers arrived in the capital just as China was swept into the great wave of new policies. Wang Anshi — a slovenly, socially inept eccentric with a messianic sense of mission — modeled himself on Shang Yang and launched state capitalism. The three prior totalitarian experiments (Shang Yang-Qin, Sang Hongyang-Han, Wang Mang-Xin) had all ended catastrophically. Su Xun's "Discourse on Identifying Villains" had already predicted that if this man gained power, disaster would follow.

01
Historical Mirror
Four Totalitarian Experiments, All Failed
In four thousand years of Chinese history, totalitarianism, state capitalism, and radical social revolution had been attempted four times — Shang Yang's Legalism (Qin, rapid collapse), Sang Hongyang's fiscal policy (Han, near-rebellion), Wang Mang's new order (Xin, ended with his death) — and now Wang Anshi attempted the fourth and final experiment.
📜

First Three: All Failed

Shang Yang-Qin (Legalist totalitarianism, collapsed soon after unification), Sang Hongyang-Han (state capitalism, near-rebellion), Wang Mang-Xin (social revolution, died with him).

⚠️

The Fourth: Wang Anshi

Modeling himself on Shang Yang, he launched the Green Sprout Loans, State Trade System — history repeating, but he and his supporters refused to see it.

02
The Eccentric
An Idealist Devoid of Human Warmth
Wang Anshi was an eccentric — a diligent student and great poet, yet possessed of a messianic mission and completely lacking in human understanding. He wore filthy clothes, never changed his robes, ate an entire plate of fish bait without noticing, and was deemed a "hypocrite" by Emperor Renzong.

03
Prophetic Warning
"Discourse on Villains": The Abnormal Are Rarely Good
Su Xun wrote with astonishing insight that Wang Anshi "dresses in rags, eats pig-swill, with the face of a prisoner and hair of a mourner yet discusses poetry and classics." He concluded: "Those who deviate from normal human feelings are rarely anything but great villains" — and history proved him tragically right.

04
Path to Power
Twenty-Five Years of Refusing Office: Big Frog in a Small Pond
From passing the highest exam at twenty-one to taking power at forty-six, Wang Anshi repeatedly refused high office for twenty-five years, only accepting minor provincial posts — not for show, but to be "a big frog in a small pond," waiting for his moment to realize his radical vision.

Reform Launched
1069: The Spark That Started the Blaze
In 1069, the Su brothers arrived in the capital — the same year China was swept into the great tide of new policies. Wang Anshi, with Shang Yang as his spiritual model, launched reforms that were essentially the same lineage of state-directed economic intervention.

🔑 Key Takeaway
The tragedy of Wang Anshi's reform lay in a paranoid genius without human warmth gaining the power to reshape a nation. China's four state-capitalist experiments had all failed — yet Wang Anshi not only repeated history but, with his "Three Defiances" (see next chapter), pushed the Northern Song into an irreparable factional abyss. Su Xun's "Discourse on Identifying Villains" became an immortal text because it revealed an eternal truth: those who deviate from normal human feeling are rarely anything but great villains.
