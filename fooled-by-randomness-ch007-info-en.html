Fooled by Randomness · Chapter 7
"The Problem of Induction"

How to Survive a Black Swan? — Popper's Falsification and a Trader's Survival Rules

Hume posed the "Black Swan Problem": no amount of white swans can prove that "all swans are white," but a single black swan is enough to disprove it. Popper built his falsificationism on this foundation — scientific theories can only be falsified, never verified. Taleb brings this thinking into financial trading: use data to refute, not confirm, and set stop losses to protect yourself from being destroyed by rare events. The problem of induction matters more in finance than anywhere else — yet it is ignored more there than anywhere else.
01
The Induction Problem
Hume's Warning: Verification Requires Exhaustion, Falsification Requires Only One Counterexample
In his Treatise of Human Nature, Hume pointed out: no matter how many white swans we observe, we cannot infer that all swans are white — unless we are fortunate enough to have observed every swan in existence. But spotting a single black swan overturns the entire proposition. This asymmetry between confirmation and refutation is the fundamental flaw of induction. In financial markets, randomness amplifies this asymmetry dramatically.
🦢

Verification (Induction)

"I observed 4,000 swans, all were white."
→ Cannot conclude "all swans are white"
→ Verification requires exhausting all possibilities

⚫

Falsification (Deduction)

"I found one black swan."
→ Immediately negates "all swans are white"
→ Falsification needs only one counterexample

02
Falsificationism
There Are Only Two Kinds of Theories: Those Known to Be False, and Those Not Yet Proven False
According to Popper, no theory can be verified as "correct"; it can only be tentatively accepted. Newtonian physics is science because it allowed us to prove it wrong — Einstein's relativity indeed overturned it. Astrology is not science because it provides no conditions under which it can be refuted. This is the "problem of demarcation" between science and nonsense.

03
The Data Trap
Niederhoffer Uses Data to Confirm; Taleb Uses Data to Refute
Niederhoffer, like a Bacon of finance, devoted himself to observing data and finding anomalies — he was an empiricist. Taleb, by contrast, inherited Popper: he uses history to refute, not confirm. Their fundamental disagreement lies in how they treat data — one seeks patterns to confirm them, the other hunts for counterexamples to negate propositions.
"Bush never died in 58 years"Induction concludes he is immortal — patently absurd
"Accidents happen near home"People simply drive more near home; it's not that driving near home is more dangerous
"Market never drops 20% in 3 months"Absence from past data does not guarantee absence in the future
04
Trading Discipline
Before Entering a Trade, Determine: What Event Would Prove Me Wrong?
Like Popperian scientists, surviving traders must determine in advance: the cost of being wrong is strictly limited, and the probability of error is not derived from historical data. Once proven wrong, they exit the trade — this is called a stop loss. At the predetermined exit point, they act to protect themselves from the impact of Black Swans.

05
Core Strategy
Pascal's Wager for Statistics: Take the Best the Past Offers, But Don't Be Harmed by It
Taleb borrows Pascal's reasoning: if statistics help with certain things, use them aggressively to place bets; if they pose a threat, never use them to manage risk. He buys out-of-the-money options, betting rare events will occur; Niederhoffer sells them, betting they will not. One enjoys an occasional windfall harvest; the other tries to earn a steady income.
📈

Active Betting (Use Statistics)

Use statistics and inductive methods to find opportunities and bet aggressively. Aim to profit from every imaginable rare event.

🛡️

Risk Management (Don't Use Statistics)

Never use statistical methods to manage the risks you bear. The condition: no single rare event can harm me.

06
Philosophical Extension
An Open Society Allows No Eternal Truth; an Open Mind Is Not Ashamed to Change Opinions
Popper's falsificationism is deeply linked to the idea of the open society. In an open society, no truth is eternal — opposing ideas are permitted. Popper shared this vision with Hayek: all utopian ideals are closed, because they block their own capacity for refutation. Soros understood this profoundly: he proclaims his fallibility everywhere, yet wields enormous power — because he knows his own weaknesses while others think too highly of themselves.

💡 Soros is more Popperian than Popper himself. Popper in private was not open-minded — he was self-centered, couldn't listen, and pursued victory in debate at any cost. Yet Soros, in his professional conduct, perfectly practiced Popper's falsificationism.
