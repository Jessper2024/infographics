📖 Overview

Banishment to Huangzhou, paradoxically, allowed Su Dongpo to find his true self. Stripped from being a capital minister to a disgraced farmer, he tilled wasteland east of the city and called himself "Dongpo Jushi" — the Recluse of the Eastern Slope. He watched the great river flow past his cottage, sat in silent meditation at the Anguo Temple, and divided his monthly expenses into thirty packets hung from the roof beam. It was on this barren land that Su completed his spiritual transformation — shedding the scholar-official's "traps" to reveal the playful child within.

Core Question: What remains of a person when every social identity is stripped away?

Key Insight: Su Dongpo proved in Huangzhou that the person who loses all external trappings comes closest to their essential nature.

01
IDENTITY

Shedding the Armor: From Minister to Farmer

Social identity is a "trap" — remove it, and the true self emerges.

As Lin Yutang wrote: "Society, culture, classical studies, external duties and responsibilities hide a person's true nature. Remove these traps of circumstance and tradition, and the genuine self appears." Su in Huangzhou was like a seal returning to water — a seal flopping on land is only half a seal. Donning a farmer's clothes and walking the Yellow Mud Path, he became unrecognizable to ordinary people.

In 1081, Su began farming wasteland east of the city, taking the name "Dongpo Jushi." A friend had secured several dozen mu of abandoned military land for him — overgrown with brambles, littered with rubble, and hit by drought. The labor of reclamation "exhausted every ounce of his strength."

02
METHOD

Elegant Poverty: The Roof-Beam Budgeting System

4,500 cash divided into thirty packets hanging from the roof beam, retrieved daily with a painting fork — Su Dongpo's personal "financial planning."

In a letter to his disciple Qin Guan, Su detailed his budgeting method: "Daily expenses must not exceed 150 cash. On the first of each month, I take 4,500 cash, divide it into thirty portions, and hang them from the roof beam. Each morning I use a painting fork to take down one portion, then hide the fork away. Leftover money goes into a large bamboo tube, saved for entertaining guests." He calculated his savings could last over a year, and stopped worrying.

He even named a friend's noodle dish "Weishensu" (Why-So-Crisp). Local wine was decent, mutton rivaled northern pork, fish and crab were nearly free. In poverty, he discovered dignity through thrift.

03
DUALITY

Two Faces of Su: Different "Repentance" for Different Audiences

Su Dongpo displayed radically different attitudes of "repentance" depending on his audience — defiant integrity to close friends, feigned contrition to political enemies.

To his confidant Li Chang, he wrote: "Though we are old and poor, principles run through our hearts and loyalty fills our very marrow — we can face life and death with a laugh." To Wang Gong, most severely implicated, he shared Daoist longevity techniques. Yet to his political foe Zhang Dun, he composed a letter so full of self-reproach and humility that it could have been presented to the emperor — claiming he was "beyond the path of regret" and "no longer human" if he did not reform.

"If you pity me in my hardship, you are no different from one who has never studied the Dao… When there is an opportunity to honor the sovereign and benefit the people, I would sacrifice myself without hesitation." — Letter to Li Chang

04
BUILD

Snow Hall: An Exile's Architectural Dream

With his own hands, Su built his world — Snow Hall, dams, fish ponds, orchards — a complete pastoral utopia.

"Architecture was Su Dongpo's instinct." He built a five-room Snow Hall (constructed during snowfall), its walls decorated with his own paintings of forests, rivers, and fishermen in snow. Behind it stood the Prospect Pavilion with panoramic views. He built dams, dug fish ponds, planted trees gifted by neighbors, flowers from friends, vegetables from his hometown. The western neighbor had a giant bamboo grove, stalks seven inches in circumference, so dense they blocked the sky — Su spent summers cooling off there.

05
PHILOSOPHY

The River, the Wind, and the Moon Have No Fixed Owner

In impoverished Huangzhou, Su discovered a startling truth: the most precious things in the world — the river, the wind, the moon — belong to anyone with a leisurely heart.

Living at Lingao Pavilion, just ten paces from the Yangtze, he wrote to a friend: "Windblown waves, misty rain, transforming a hundred times from dawn to dusk. The southern mountains rest upon my table — such fortune has never existed before." To Fan Zhen's son, he added with humor: "The river below Lingao Pavilion, half of it is melted snow from Mount Emei. I drink it, I bathe in it — why would I need to return home? The river, the wind, and the moon have no fixed owner. The leisurely man is their master."

Sometimes he wore straw sandals, hired a tiny boat, and spent the day with fishermen and woodcutters, often jostled and cursed by drunkards — yet he was "delighted that people gradually stopped recognizing him." This freedom of being forgotten by the world was precisely what he treasured most.

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

"Dongpo Jushi" — the Recluse of the Eastern Slope — is not a self-deprecating joke but a declaration. When a man loses his office, his wealth, and his reputation, and builds a home on wasteland with his own hands, what he gains is not failure but a more authentic mode of existence. As he himself wrote: "The river, the wind, and the moon have no fixed owner. The leisurely man is their master."
