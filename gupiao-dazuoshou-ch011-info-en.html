Overview

How to Identify and Follow the Market’s Path of Least Resistance?

Prices, like water, always follow the path of least resistance. Livermore used the breakout of a sideways range to define the path of least resistance: when price breaks above resistance, the upside path is easier; when below support, the downside path is easier. His system rests on patiently waiting for the market to make its own choice—never predicting, never anticipating, never forcing the tape to conform to his will. Pat Hearne’s system was a simple embodiment: add 1% on each rise, stop out on each 1% decline.

Core thesis: The speculator must submit entirely to the market to profit; never stubbornly demand that the tape submit to him.

01
Core Principle

The Path of Least Resistance: A Simple Truth as Old as Water

When the market oscillates sideways in a 10-point range (120-130), don’t be fooled by appearances. At the bottom it looks weak; after rising 8–10 points it looks strong—but neither is a reason to enter. The trader must wait patiently for the market to break its limits: at 130 where buying first exceeds selling, or at 120 where selling first exceeds buying. Traders on the wrong side are forced to cover, which precisely pushes prices further along the path of least resistance.

02
Case Study

Wheat at $1.20: A Perfect Breakout Case

“If you want to make money out of wheat, just watch it. Wait until it crosses $1.20, then buy it—and you’ll get a nice quick play.” Livermore had watched wheat for months as it oscillated between $1.10 and $1.20. One day it closed above $1.19. Next day it opened at $1.20½—he bought immediately. Price rose to $1.21, $1.22, $1.23, $1.25. Europeans had been quietly accumulating; large quantities had been taken out of the market. Breaking $1.20 meant upward force had finally gathered enough power to push through the ceiling.

03
Philosophy

Pat Hearne’s Simple System and the Speculator’s Inner Enemy

“You don’t know till you bet”—Pat Hearne’s trading creed. His system was staggeringly simple: buy 100 shares, if it rises 1%, buy another 100; rise another 1%, another 100. Set a stop 1 point below the last purchase price. A 1% decline triggers the stop and he exits. He didn’t chase inside tips, didn’t expect 20-point moves in a week—he just earned “sure money.” A follower made $100,000 on Lackawanna using this system, then lost it all after abandoning it.

😨 Fear

When the market goes against you, every day you hope it’s the last—refusing the initial loss leads to disaster. When it goes your way, you exit too early, afraid of losing what you’ve gained.

😤 Hope

When losing, you must fear (that losses will grow); when winning, you must hope (that profits will snowball). You must do the opposite of what human nature dictates.

Key Takeaway

The path of least resistance is the cornerstone of Livermore’s trading philosophy: don’t predict, don’t anticipate, don’t gamble—wait for the market to speak for itself. The breakout is the language; price action is the answer. The deeper insight: the trader’s greatest resistance is not the market but himself—fear and hope, the twin brothers, always dominate decisions at precisely the wrong moments. The successful trader must go against human nature: hope when one should fear, fear when one should hope. This internal discipline, more than any external rule, determines success or failure.
