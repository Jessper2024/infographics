Fooled by Randomness · Chapter 5
"Survival of the Least Fit—Can Evolution Be Fooled by Randomness?"

The rise and fall of two traders, and a question that pierces through Darwinism

Carlos made $80 million trading emerging-market bonds using economic analysis — then lost $300 million in a single summer. John amassed a $16 million fortune riding statistical models — wiped out in days. They are not outliers: at any point in time, the highest-earning trader is often the worst trader. Through two true stories, Taleb reveals an unsettling truth: randomness can fool not only individuals but evolution itself. Survival of the fittest? Perhaps just the luckiest who happened to avoid a rare event.
01
Carlos Case
Carlos: A golden road paved by economic intuition, crushed overnight by a rare event
A Harvard economics master's graduate, Carlos entered emerging-market bond trading in 1992. He believed economic analysis explained everything — buy on dips, add more if fundamentals looked good. Over seven years, he accumulated nearly $80 million in profits. In summer 1998, the Russian debt crisis struck. He kept averaging down, shouting at doubters: "It's just liquidation!" In the end, he lost $300 million in one summer and was escorted out by security.

02
John Case
John: A once-in-a-trillion-trillion-year event, arriving in days under leverage
John relied on quant whiz Henry's risk models to run global high-yield bond carry trades. With five-to-one leverage, he managed a $50 million portfolio. The model said: this kind of loss should occur once every 100 trillion trillion years. In summer 1998, the high-yield market crashed, and all his hedges failed simultaneously. Within days, $14 million of personal wealth evaporated — and he lost his job.

03
Common Traits
Traders fooled by randomness share these six fatal traits
Carlos and John are not outliers — nearly all the most successful traders during the 1992–1998 bull run exhibited these traits. They lack precise definitions, but you recognize them instantly when you see them.
1
Overestimating the precision of their beliefs. Carlos believed in economics; John believed in statistics. Neither considered that past success might have been coincidence — economics simply happened to work during that period.
2
Refusal to cut losses. As the saying goes, it's harder to get a bad trader to abandon a position than to get them divorced. They will not admit defeat.
3
Changing the narrative. When losing money, they suddenly call themselves "long-term investors." Conflating trading with investing is just an excuse to avoid taking a loss.
4
Denying reality. They refuse to believe the prices on the screen when losing. "It's just liquidation, panic selling" — continuing to ignore the market's message.
5
No pre-planned exit strategy. They buy more after a crash, but not according to any predetermined plan — driven purely by emotion.
6
Lack of critical self-examination. They won't accept their valuation methods might be flawed. Contrast with Soros, who reexamines his analytical framework when facing adverse outcomes.
04
Core Paradox
The Cross-Sectional Problem: the highest earner may be the worst trader
How did Carlos and John make so much money despite making every mistake? The answer lies in a simple principle of randomness. They profited not only because they were in the right market, but because their "buy the dip" style happened to match the character of the 1992–1998 historical period. During that era, those with that particular trait dominated — crowding out potentially superior traders.
"At any point in time, the highest-earning trader is often the worst trader."
— Taleb · The Cross-Sectional Problem
🔍

Reversing Cause and Effect

We think traders make money because they are good. Perhaps we should reverse it: we think they are good only because they made money. One can profit in financial markets purely through randomness.

🎯

The Sample-Path Trap

Carlos and John both benefited from the market cycle. Their success was over-adaptation to a single sample path — one that happened not to encounter a rare event. The longer without one, the more fragile they become.

05
Evolution Myth
Evolution can be fooled by randomness: adapting to one sample ≠ adapting to all possibilities
Darwin has been misapplied. Evolution concerns reproductive fitness, not short-term survival. Gould identified "negative mutations" — inferior traits that can temporarily persist. When regime change occurs, the previously adapted species may be the first to perish. An animal adapts only to the specific time series it has experienced, not the average of all possible environments. The longer a species goes without encountering a rare event, the more vulnerable it becomes to that event.
"If we extend time to infinity, by the property of ergodicity, that event will certainly occur — and those species will be destroyed!"
"Whatever John and Carlos go on to do, they will remain fools of randomness."
— Taleb · Chapter 5
