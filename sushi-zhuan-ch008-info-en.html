1
Turning Point

The Transfer Edict: Sudden Departure After Four Years

In mid-March 1084, Su Shi submitted the customary memorial of gratitude for his transfer to Ruzhou — at last, a glimmer of change in his official career. He entrusted Dongpo, Snow Hall, and his wet nurse's grave to Pan Bing's care, planning to first visit his brother Su Zhe in Junzhou. The boat was chartered, departure imminent.

Once word spread, people came ceaselessly to bid farewell. Banquet followed banquet, filling his schedule. Many friends asked for calligraphy as keepsakes — Su Shi, always generous with his brush and deeply sentimental, granted every request. His final month in Huangzhou became extraordinarily busy.

Pan Bing's nephews asked Su Shi to copy the two Red Cliff Rhapsodies by hand; Su Shi offered instead to write "Going Home," whereupon the young men shamelessly asked for all three. Copying such long texts in minuscule regular script was something the impatient Su Shi normally detested, yet this time he did not refuse, even delaying his departure by several days.

2
Farewell Poem

Li Qi's Poem: A Witty Classical Allusion

At a prefectural farewell banquet, a courtesan named Li Qi — gifted, well-read, and quietly elegant — had long admired Su Shi but was too shy to ask for a poem. This was her last chance. After several rounds of wine, she finally approached, removing her silk scarf and requesting his calligraphy.

Taking the pure white silk, Su Shi studied this beautiful, faintly melancholy young woman. He had long noticed how she stood apart — it reminded him of the noble crabapple blooming alone on that hillside east of Dinghui Temple. He wrote: "Five years Dongpo has dwelled in Huangzhou — why has no word touched on Li Qi?" Then guests from another table interrupted with toasts. As the banquet neared its end, a frantic Li Qi begged again. Su Shi burst out laughing: "I almost forgot the finale." He finished: "Just like Du Fu of West Sichuan — the crabapple is lovely, yet never praised in verse."

Du Fu lived ten years in West Sichuan, the "Fragrant Crabapple Kingdom," yet never once mentioned a crabapple in his poetry. Su Shi wielded this allusion as a clever non-explanation for his years of silence about Li Qi, while simultaneously comparing her to the crabapple, the flower of supreme quality. Among all Huangzhou courtesans, she alone received such praise. The entire gathering applauded.

3
Heartfelt Farewell

"Courtyard Full of Fragrance": Inner Conflict

On April 1, at a farewell gathering with friends and neighbors, Su Shi wrote the deeply emotional lyric "Courtyard Full of Fragrance," reviewing four years and two months spanning two leap years — "the children all speak Chu and Wu dialects." At nearly fifty, approaching old age, Su Shi truly felt exhausted.

"Going home — but where is my home? My home is ten thousand li away at Mount Min and Emei. Half a century gone, the days ahead are sadly few. What is this life — coming and going like a shuttle!" He longed to follow Tao Yuanming and cast off official fetters completely, but the Confucian spirit of service was rooted too deeply. Neither subjectively nor objectively could he abandon everything now. Reclusion would forever remain a dream just beyond reach.

The poem ends with an earnest request to Huangzhou's elders: "Take good care of the slender willows before the hall — thinking of me, don't trim their tender branches. And tell the elders south of the river: from time to time, sun my fishing rain-cloak for me." The entire lyric unfolds his double-bind and his lingering, reluctant affection.

4
Affectionate Horns

Night Crossing: Huangzhou's Horn-Drums Bid Farewell

At dusk on departure day, Su Shi's party crossed the Yangtze toward Wuchang. All was still, when suddenly, faint horn-drum sounds drifted across the river — it was Huangzhou's horn-drums! In four years, how many times had they accompanied his night rambles, roused him from drunken stupor? Su Shi halted, looked back at Dongpo, and tears fell.

"Clear breeze plays on water, the moon embraces the hill; a recluse crosses Wu Wang Ridge by night. Huangzhou's horn-drums are also full of feeling, sending me southward, begrudging no distance. South of the river, I hear a frontier march again, half-mingled with river sounds, mournful and heroic. Who says all regions sound the same? Alligators rage and dragons grieve, transformed for me!" He concluded: "Some year I'll come rowing back upstream — then sound this tune again to welcome me."

The poem pours forth Su Shi's boundless attachment to Huangzhou — "I remember the withered willows by the river; meeting again before they die, we shall truly know each other." But official life would not permit it. This beautiful wish was never fulfilled. Even years later as Hangzhou prefect, he still reminisced in verse about Dongpo and Huangzhou's elders.

5
Emotional Home

The Unfulfilled Wish: Dongpo as Spiritual Origin

On departure morning, neighbors and friends gathered spontaneously before Snow Hall. An old man, hands trembling, filled a cup to the brim: "Sir, please drink this parting wine. Don't forget this place — it is your home too." Su Shi took the cup reverently, his throat constricting. There was too much to say — how could he possibly say it all?

He remembered clearing the land: "They followed me to Dongpo, sharing labor and meals"; building the dike: "Neighbors came in droves to help raise the rammer, everyone knowing my purse was empty"; planting crops: "The farmers counseled me: don't let the sprouts grow too lush." Through hardship, Huangzhou's elders had given him warm care and help. Their simple, sincere friendship let him feel human warmth at every moment. He drained the cup in one gulp and set forth on a journey of uncertain storms once more.

When he arrived in Huangzhou four years earlier, he was a terrified, trembling criminal official. When he left, he was the Master of Dongpo who could face "a lifetime of mist and rain." Huangzhou did not become the place where he grew old, but Dongpo had become his eternal spiritual home — the origin-point where he found crystalline joy in the crisp tap of his staff. This confirmation was the most precious thing he carried away from Huangzhou.

💡
Key Takeaway

Four years and two months in Huangzhou — from the lonely indignation of "choosing among cold branches, unwilling to perch" to the reluctant attachment of "going home — but where is my home?" The Su Shi who left Huangzhou was no longer the man who had arrived. He carried away the personality transformation forged at Dongpo and the literary summit crystallized at Red Cliff, leaving behind Snow Hall, slender willows, and a fishing rain-cloak. The affectionate farewell of Huangzhou's horn-drums, the parting wine offered by trembling old hands, and the earnest request to "sun my fishing cloak from time to time" — together they formed the most heartfelt farewell. As he wrote in "Night Crossing the River": "Who says all regions sound the same? Alligators rage and dragons grieve, transformed for me" — the very landscape of Huangzhou was bidding him farewell, and he had confirmed this place as his eternal spiritual home.
