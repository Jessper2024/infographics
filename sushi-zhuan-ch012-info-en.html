Su Shi Biography · Ch.12 "Heaven and Earth Mourn Together"

"To strive is already to err" — Su Shi's last words to the world. The great literary master departed in full clarity; the entire realm wept.

In one line: On his journey north, Su Shi endured the shock of Qin Guan's sudden death, agonized over where to settle, forgave Zhang Dun's son who came begging for mercy, and finally succumbed to his own fatal illness. His final words — "To strive is already to err" — encapsulated his enlightenment: no grasping for the Pure Land, no forced devotion, just a serene acceptance of life's end. Li Zhi's funeral oration — "His Way too vast to be contained, his talent too high to escape envy. Heaven above and Earth below bear witness to his lifelong loyalty; the great mountains and rivers receive back his immortal spirit" — became the definitive epitaph for his life.

Core QuestionHow did a cultural titan who had experienced the highest highs and lowest lows face the end of life? How did his death and the ensuing national mourning reflect both his personal greatness and the tragedy of his era?

Core AnswerSu Shi's final year was filled with farewells: reuniting with Qin Guan in Leizhou only to part forever; reconciling with former rival Liu Anshi in Qianzhou; agonizing between settling in Changzhou or Yingchang; inscribing his portrait at Jinshan Temple with "Ask me my life's achievements — Huangzhou, Huizhou, Danzhou." On his deathbed, with astonishing clarity, he rejected the abbot's urging to "remember the Western Pure Land" — "The West exists, but one cannot strive for it here." His serene passing completed the ultimate transformation from scholar-official to awakened being.

1
Fallen Friends

Qin Guan's Death: The Last of Su's Disciples to Fall

In June 1100, after crossing the sea, Su Shi reunited with Qin Guan at Xuwen — after seven years of separation. "Green temples and rosy cheeks? We meet again — two aging men." Qin Guan, ever sensitive, presented his own elegy as a parting gift — Su Shi, having penetrated the nature of life and death, was not alarmed. Who could know that Qin Guan would succumb to heatstroke in Tengzhou on August 12, dying at just 52? Su Shi "could not eat for two days," lamenting: "The foremost literary talent of our age — can such a man ever be found again?"

"A swallow flying south, a wild goose homing north — by chance we meet, our faces gaunt with sorrow. Green temples, rosy cheeks — and now we meet again, two aging men. Ask no more of the endless days since we parted — infinite matters, held silent between us." — Qin Guan, "River City"

Qin Guan was the most gifted of the "Four Scholars of Su School." Su Shi knew that even if he could not "serve greatly in the world," he would "leave writings to enlighten posterity." This 52-year-old genius, at the golden prime of his thought and art, died suddenly just as light was breaking after long darkness. His death resulted from cruel political persecution and harsh natural conditions combined. Su Shi detoured to Tengzhou hoping to mourn at his friend's bier — but arrived half a month too late. Standing alone at the crossroads, he wept in the wind.

⚠️ Qin Guan's death was no isolated case. By this time, the majority of Yuanyou officials exiled with Su Shi had already perished. The "eight lethal prefectures" — "Spring, Dragon, Plum, New — neighbors to death; High, Sin, Thunder, Transform — even speaking their names brings fear" — were Zhang Dun's carefully designed weapons of physical and psychological annihilation.
2
Journey North

Re-climbing Dayu: "Old Man, How Many Exiles Have You Seen Return?"

In the first month of 1101, Su Shi crossed the Dayu Mountains heading north. For Song dynasty officials, this mountain range signified the end of political life — few who crossed south ever returned. Standing on the ridge, poetry surged: "The southern cap I'll wear but briefly — now I follow the northern geese home. Never in life have I dug a rabbit's three burrows — past and present, what's the difference? Foxes all share one hill." A white-haired elder recognized him and said: "I've heard that some have schemed a thousand ways to harm you. Today your northward return — Heaven truly protects the good!"

🟠 Healing in Qianzhou

Stranded waiting for the Gan River to rise, Su Shi carried a medicine bag through city and countryside, "prescribing and giving medicine to all who were ill." Wherever he went, those seeking calligraphy would set out desks, paper, and ink in advance — he'd smile indulgently and write until dusk.

🟠 The "Jade Slab" Prank

Su Shi tricked the famously stern Liu Anshi ("Tiger of the Court") into going to "consult the Venerable Jade Slab" in the mountains — which turned out to be digging bamboo shoots for a picnic. After seven years of hardship, former political rivals became friends. Su Shi praised Liu: "A man of iron and stone."

The meeting with Liu Anshi was particularly meaningful: politically opposed, personally distant — Liu's rigid discipline was the polar opposite of Su Shi's free-spiritedness. But after seven years of hardship, Liu said: "All frivolity and pride have fallen away — he is not the Zizhan of old." A friendship that came late, at the twilight of life.

In Qianzhou, Su Shi also wrote to Huang Shi expressing concern for Zhang Dun — now exiled to Leizhou, the very man who had persecuted Su Shi most viciously. Su Shi wrote: "Haikang though distant has no miasmic sickness; my brother lived there a year quite safely" — asking Huang Shi to comfort Zhang Dun's elderly mother. All grudges had dissolved.

3
Life's Summation

Inscribed at Jinshan: "Ask Me My Life's Work — Huangzhou, Huizhou, Danzhou"

Journeying north, Su Shi agonized between settling in Changzhou or Yingchang — Yingchang meant reunion with his brother, but proximity to the capital meant danger; Changzhou was the second hometown of his dreams. In mid-May at Jinshan Temple, facing Li Gonglin's portrait of himself, he inscribed this quatrain: "My heart — like ashes of spent wood; my body — a boat unmoored. Ask me my life's achievements — Huangzhou, Huizhou, Danzhou."

These four lines are Su Shi's most distilled self-assessment. "Heart like ashes" comes from Zhuangzi — not the nihilism of despair, but the great freedom of "both human and wild creature at ease." "Huangzhou, Huizhou, Danzhou" — for bureaucratic "achievement" this is bitter irony; for literary accomplishment and spiritual maturity, it is proud summation. These three places of exile were precisely where he became Su Dongpo.

💡 The Consensus of the Literati: Zheng Xia sent a poem expecting Su Shi to "clothe China and the barbarians, showering the land with timely rain." Huang Tingjian wrote: "If the Hanlin Academy wants a true academician, summon the bald-whiskered old man from Danzhou." Even Zhang Yuan, son of Su Shi's persecutor Zhang Dun, wrote: "The scholar-officials day and night hope you will enter the council." Boating toward Changzhou, thousands lined the Grand Canal to glimpse him — a scene reminiscent of Sima Guang's triumphal return to the capital years before.

But learning from Cheng Deru that the "Continuing the Sage" faction was regrouping in the capital, Su Shi chose Changzhou. He wrote to his brother: "I regret bitterly that we brothers cannot spend our old age together — this is Heaven's will, what can I do against Heaven?" He added: "If by chance I receive any summons, I shall plead illness and firmly decline — staying home with my sons, farming and cultivating my nature." His ambition for office had died.

4
Final Days

Three Last Exchanges: "To Strive Is Already to Err"

On June 3, Su Shi drank too much cold liquid and suffered violent dysentery through the night. Latent miasmic toxins flared — unstoppable diarrhea, digestive collapse. For over a month his condition seesawed. He self-diagnosed "heat toxins" but his use of warming herbs like ginseng may have worsened things. On July 28, as death approached, Abbot Weilin shouted in his ear: "Excellency, do not forget the West!" Su Shi replied: "The West is not absent — but here one cannot strive for it." When Qian Shixiong added "You must strive harder," Su Shi answered: "To strive is already to err." With that he passed away in perfect serenity.

"To strive is already to err" — these three characters represent the ultimate expression of Su Shi's spiritual universe. Chan Buddhism teaches non-attachment: "The West is not absent" acknowledges the Pure Land's existence, but "one cannot strive for it here" declares his refusal to seek rebirth through forced recitation. "To strive is already to err" pushes this further: any deliberate pursuit violates naturalness itself. He faced death with absolute clarity — his profound understanding of life's meaning dissolved the agony of dying and the terror of death.

"The great affliction comes from having a body — without body, there is no illness. All my life I've laughed at Kumarajiva — those sacred spells, uselessly uttered!" — Su Shi's final verse. Kumarajiva, dying, produced Western Region sacred spells for his disciples to chant for his deliverance — but died before they took effect. Su Shi explicitly rejected such superstition.

Su Shi was born in the third year of Emperor Renzong's Jingyou era (a bingzi year in the sexagenary cycle) and had lived 23,460 days. Before dying, he entrusted his commentaries on the Analects, Documents, and I Ching to Qian Jiming: "I believe in thirty years, there will be those who understand." He reviewed his life calmly: "I have done no evil in life — in death, I shall not fall."

5
National Mourning

Too Vast to Be Contained, Too Talented to Escape Envy

News of Su Shi's death spread across the realm: "The people of Wu and Yue wept together in the markets; their gentlemen mourned together at home; wherever the obituary reached, wise and foolish alike sighed and shed tears" — a spontaneous, nationwide outpouring of grief. Imperial College students gathered at Huilin Monastery; Huang Tingjian hung Su Shi's portrait and paid daily homage; Zhang Lei in white cap and robes sacrificed to his teacher — all four quarters shuddered with grief; mountains and rivers mourned as one.

"His Way too vast to be contained; his talent too high to escape becoming a burden. Heaven above and Earth below bear witness to his lifelong loyalty and righteousness; the great mountains and rivers receive back the spirit of an immortal. Those who knew him and those who did not — who is not stricken with grief? Hearing what we have never heard — where shall we place our hearts?" — Li Zhi's funeral oration, universally recognized as the most precise epitaph for Su Shi's life

🩷 Huang Tingjian

In Jingzhou, barely recovered from serious illness, he hung Su Shi's portrait in the main hall and paid homage each morning. When local scholars held a memorial, he struggled to attend, "hobbling forward clutching one knee."

🩷 Imperial College Students

Hundreds of students spontaneously gathered at Huilin Monastery for a Buddhist memorial ceremony, mourning the falling of a literary giant — before any official response, the people had already organized the most solemn of tributes.

Su Shi's departure marked the end of an era. In Jingzhou, the just-recovered Huang Tingjian struggled to attend the memorial; in Yingzhou, Zhang Lei spent his own salary on Buddhist rites at Jianfu Temple; in Qianzhou, Monk Rongxian "offered vegetarian feasts to the Buddha, weeping with utmost grief"... From scholar-officials to commoners to Buddhist monks — all mourned the passing of this cultural giant.

Before his death, Su Shi had replied at length to Zhang Dun's son Zhang Yuan's plea for mercy — not only holding no grudge, but sharing from experience: "Cultivate inner alchemy; by no means take external elixirs." He sent his own "Sequel to the Treatise on Nourishing Life" and effective medicinal formulas. The Zhang family treasured this letter — decades later, visitors to Zhang Dun's grandson could still see it. All enmity had dissolved, leaving no trace.

💡
Key Takeaway

With his final words "To strive is already to err," Su Shi achieved a breakthrough in the spiritual history of the Chinese scholar-official: he sought no Western Pure Land, performed no forced devotions, clung to no religious form — meeting death in the most natural, clearest state possible. This was of a piece with his lifelong independent spirit — his "belly full of untimeliness." Life and death are one; nothing is sought outside the self. Li Zhi's epitaph — "His Way too vast to be contained; his talent too high to escape envy" — captures not only Su Shi's tragedy but the deeper tragedy of the Chinese intellectual across the centuries. "Heaven above and Earth below bear witness to his lifelong loyalty" declares: politics may destroy a body, but it cannot erase a great soul's radiance. The spontaneous national mourning — by students, commoners, monks, and officials alike — proves Su Shi's transcendence of faction and class. A thousand years later, Su Dongpo remains the most beloved literary figure in the Chinese imagination.
