Core Insight: Beneath Su Dongpo's laughter lay profound sorrow—he witnessed the people's suffering firsthand and turned poetry into advocacy, creating an immortal mid-autumn masterpiece amid the poverty and loneliness of Mizhou.

Core QuestionFacing 17,000 prisoners, locust plagues, and oppressive policies, how would Dongpo choose between pleasure and conscience?

Core AnswerHe chose not to remain silent—pouring the misery of canal laborers and the lament of village women into his verses. Transferred from Hangzhou's paradise to impoverished Mizhou, he reached the very peak of his poetic maturity in despair.

01
Society

The Shadow of Paradise

Hangzhou was not all lotuses and peonies—17,000 debtors and salt smugglers awaited trial in its prisons, alongside locust plagues, famines, and clogged waterways.

On New Year's Eve, he wept as he wrote: "I should have been home by now; official duties keep me here. My brush trembles as I write, thinking of those in chains." He knew most prisoners were commoners who had violated the New Policies—salt smugglers, tax defaulters—policies he fundamentally opposed. To his brother he wrote: "What once shamed me I now feel no shame about, sitting before exhausted people while wielding the whip." Beneath the laughter at banquets lurked anxiety, depression, and even terror.

Su Dongpo secretly dreaded political developments. His poems mixed bird songs with prisoners' groans, water-wheel creaks with old women's wails. Even lakeside revelry was tinged with sighs about his graying hair.

02
Protest

Poetry as a Weapon

Su Dongpo used the plainest language to describe people's suffering—these poems accumulated day by day into charges of "undermining government authority."

He depicted laborers dredging salt canals: "Men like ducks and pigs, splashing mud and stumbling." In "Lament of a Farmer's Wife," he wrote of rice harvested but unsold, families selling oxen to pay taxes and cooking with dismantled roof-beams: "The government wants money now, not grain." His poem "Mountain Village" described farmers taking government loans and spending every coin in the city—because the loan office was conveniently next to the wine shop. He compared those in power to a "night owl" and depicted caged beasts losing their freedom.

"The beast in the marsh, the fish in the lake—once trapped in a pond, there is no return." — Su Dongpo's second response poem, alluding to prisoners

Taken separately, each poem seemed harmless; collected together, they became a volume of protest. They had the effect of mosquito bites: a few were annoying; too many made sleep impossible. His voice finally grew too loud to ignore.

03
Hardship

The Mizhou Ordeal

Transferred from heaven to impoverished Mizhou with a slashed salary, surviving on wild vegetables—this was Su Dongpo's most dejected yet most creatively brilliant period.

Mizhou produced mainly hemp, dates, and mulberries—a world apart from Hangzhou. His salary drastically reduced, he wrote in "Ode to Wolfberries and Chrysanthemums": "After moving to Jiaoxi, I had hoped to eat my fill, but the kitchen was bare. Unable to bear it, I daily walked the old city walls with Deputy Liu, seeking wolfberries and chrysanthemums to eat, patting our bellies and laughing." Children were dying by the roadside; he "followed the city walls wiping tears and picking up abandoned children," personally saving 30–40 starving orphans and placing them with families.

"Cares and troubles fill the human world; people wonder why you're at leisure every day. Last night a fresh breeze lulled you to sleep by the north window; by dawn a crispness fills the western hills." — "The Recluse's Pavilion"

After anger and bitterness passed, only serenity remained. He grew ever closer to Tao Qian—China's great pastoral poet. Poems like "West Studio" and "Cloud-Gazing Tower" achieved a spiritual freedom: "Departing without purpose, returning is also fine."

04
Masterpiece

The Immortal Mid-Autumn Poem

On the Mid-Autumn Festival of 1076, missing his brother Ziyou in Mizhou, Su Dongpo wrote the greatest moon poem in history—critics say it rendered all other full-moon verses obsolete.

"When will the bright moon appear? Wine cup in hand, I ask the blue sky. In the celestial palace above, what year is it tonight? I long to ride the wind and return, yet fear those jade towers and crystal halls—so high and cold. Dancing with my clear shadow, how can this compare to the human world?" On the journey from Hangzhou to Mizhou, he had already sighed in "Spring in the Qin Garden": "The road of life is endless, our toiling life is brief." Now, on this Mid-Autumn night in Mizhou, he elevated personal sorrow to cosmic insight.

📝 Context

Second year in Mizhou (1076). Ziyou was in Jinan; they hadn't met in seven years. Dongpo fused political setback, brotherly longing, and life's philosophy into one work.

🎵 Poetic Innovation

"Prelude to Water Music" is a ci poem—a form popularized by singing girls. Dongpo elevated it from love songs to a vessel for any thought or emotion.

"Men have grief and joy, parting and reunion; the moon has shade and shine, waxing and waning—perfection has never been easy since ancient times. May we live long, sharing the same moonlight though a thousand miles apart." Su Dongpo often said Ziyou drew his best work from him.

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

Heaven and hell coexisted in the same city. Su Dongpo's greatest courage was not in indulging in pleasure—it was in writing the truth for prisoners and village women, knowing full well his poems would reach the capital and invite disaster.
