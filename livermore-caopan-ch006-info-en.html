01
Position Sizing

Scale In Gradually — Never Pile Everything Into One Price

Do not pile all your commitments at a single price. Scale in gradually — buying each lot at a higher level on the long side, selling each lot at a lower level on the short side — this keeps you closer to the right side of the market than any other method.

Too many speculators impulsively buy or sell everything at one price. This is wrong and dangerous. The right way: if you want 500 shares, buy 100 first; after the market rises and confirms, buy the next 100 — every subsequent purchase must be at a higher level than the last.

Same rule for shorts: never sell another lot except at a lower price than the previous one. Following this, every trade starts and stays profitable — and that fact alone is powerful evidence that you are correct. After placing the first commitment, decide in advance how much risk you are willing to accept if proven wrong.

02
Timing > Thesis

Wait for the Pivotal Psychological Moment — Timing Is Everything

Even when your judgment proves correct in the end, acting prematurely is just as deadly. Wait until the market reaches the key point you identified — if you consistently re-enter at the pivotal point, you will be on board when the real move begins.

Livermore's routine: Step 1, estimate the potential extent of the coming move. Step 2, study the price record to determine the entry level. You have already decided the stock must reach a certain price if the move is genuine — when it actually gets there, that is the moment to make your first commitment.

"If you persist in this practice, and if you consistently re-enter on the appearance of your key point, you cannot help but be on board when the real movement starts. You cannot miss it."

You may take a loss once or twice, but stick to the rule and you will be in the market when the genuine move begins. The absolute necessity: patiently choosing the right moment. Impulsive haste costs a fortune.

03
Case Study

The Million-Dollar Blunder — Six Costly Weeks in Cotton

Livermore personally recounts his most humiliating experience: five impulsive cotton trades in six weeks lost nearly $200,000; worse, removing the ticker in disgust caused him to completely miss the subsequent 500-point epic rally — total cost over $1.2 million.

Years earlier, Livermore turned strongly bullish on cotton. He was convinced a big move was coming. But instead of waiting for the market to be ready, he plunged in the moment his opinion crystallized — first purchase of 20,000 bales at the market, which pushed the dull market up 15 points before it fell back within 24 hours to where he started, costing about $30,000.

❌ Attempts 1–3
Each 20K-bale buy → rally → drop back to origin → $25–30K loss per round. Couldn't stand the waiting, sold everything.
❌ Attempts 4–5
Still convinced of the bullish thesis → repeated the pattern → got stopped again. Nearly $200K squandered, zero satisfaction.
☠ Fatal Result
Two days after removing the ticker and losing all interest → market launched a 500-point rally with only one 40-point reaction. Missed $1M in profit.

Livermore writes: "I am ashamed to put this down, so mortified am I." His original plan — accumulate 100,000 bales after the market crossed the key point — would have yielded roughly 200 points of profit. It was one of the most attractive, highest-conviction trading opportunities he had ever identified.

04
Root Cause Analysis

Two Root Causes — The Double Cost of Impatience and Emotional Hijack

The two root causes of the cotton disaster: first, lacking the patience to wait for the psychological moment, trying to "get a little extra" before the key point; second, letting anger at his misjudgment turn into disgust with the market, allowing emotion to displace reason.

❌ Root Cause 1: No Patience

He knew beforehand that only when cotton reached 12½¢ per pound would it signal the real move — but he simply could not wait. Acting before the market was ready not only cost $200K in real money but also forfeited a $1M profit opportunity.

❌ Root Cause 2: Emotional Hijack

Simply because he had misjudged, he let himself grow angry, became disgusted with the cotton market, and even ordered the ticker removed — emotions that are completely incompatible with sound speculative practice.

✅ The Cure: Own Your Mistakes

Don't make excuses for errors. Admit them openly and learn everything you can from them. Study your records to understand what went wrong, then wait for the next big opportunity. What matters is the overall result over a period.

✅ The Stop-Loss Rule

The market tells a speculator when he is wrong — because that is when he is losing money. The moment he first realizes he is wrong is the moment to close out. Take the loss, try to keep a smile, and move on.

05
Advanced Wisdom

Subconscious Danger Signals and the Deadly Lure of Inside Tips

"To know that you are wrong even before the market tells you is a rather advanced faculty." This is a subconscious warning born of long market apprenticeship. Meanwhile, Livermore devotes an entire section to inside tips: "Beware of inside information of every kind."

🔮 Subconscious Danger Signal
After the market close, you feel unsettled → toss and turn all night → next morning everything looks rosy, but a deep unease won't go away. This is a sensitivity developed only through long study and experience. Livermore admits: "By heeding this feeling, I have benefited on many occasions."
⚠️ The Inside-Tip Trap
A friend inside a company "can very easily tell you when to buy. But he cannot and will not tell you when to sell — that would be tantamount to betraying his board." While you dream of paper riches, insiders have been quietly unloading near the top.

📓 What to write on Page 1 of your notebook? Livermore urges you to always carry a small notebook to record market information, ideas, and inspirations. On the first page, print: "Beware of inside information — of every kind, no exceptions." In speculation and investment, success belongs only to those who commit themselves wholeheartedly — nothing falls from the sky.

"Even if money did fall from the sky, nobody is going to force it into your pocket."

💡
Key Takeaway

Impatience is the speculator's most expensive mistake. The two lessons of the cotton disaster are timeless: scale into positions gradually and wait for the pivotal psychological moment — never act before the market is ready. And when you err, don't get angry or make excuses; admit it, study your records, and extract the lesson. More advanced judgment comes from long market apprenticeship — when you feel restless and unable to sleep, your subconscious may be sending a danger signal. Carry a notebook, but let its first page forever read: Beware of all inside information.
