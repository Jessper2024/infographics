Then Rain Came
The Storm of Opposition ignited from three fuses simultaneously: the Baojia militia revolt that drove peasants to sever their own wrists, Heaven's wrath expressed through a mountain collapse and prolonged drought, and the Dowager Empress's pressure on Emperor Shenzong. The fatal blow came from Zheng Xia — a humble gate clerk who painted starving peasants "chained and felling trees to repay government loans" and sent the scroll into the palace with a pledge to be executed if rain did not fall within ten days. Wang Anshi was dismissed, eighteen policies were abolished, and the rain came — as if Heaven rejoiced.

Core QuestionWith Emperor Shenzong's full backing and the entire opposition exiled from the capital, why did the Stubborn Premier ultimately fall? Who ended his political life, and how?

Core AnswerThe storm converged from three directions: popular revolt (peasants disfiguring themselves to avoid the Baojia militia conscription), heavenly omens (Mount Hua's collapse and the drought perceived as divine judgment), and court pressure (the Dowager Empress telling Shenzong directly that "Wang Anshi has made too many enemies"). The killing stroke came from Zheng Xia, an "Anshang Gate" clerk who painted the suffering of the people and sent it to the emperor. The emperor took the scroll into his private quarters; the Dowager Empress spoke first. The next day Wang Anshi was dismissed. The emperor abolished eighteen reform measures — and the rains came.

1
Before the Storm

A Dead Court: The Scattered Luminaries

When Su Dongpo left the capital, the court had fallen into deathly silence — all the great statesmen of the Renzong era had been dismissed and scattered. Ouyang Xiu lived in seclusion in Fuyang, Sima Guang and Lü Gongzhu idled in Luoyang. Fu Bi had been stripped of all honors, and the venerable Lü Hui lay dying.

On his deathbed, Lü Hui sent the emperor a riddle comparing the New Policies to a quack doctor poisoning a patient: "I had no chronic illness, but encountered a physician whose methods were perverse, recklessly prescribing medicines... the affliction has spread to all four limbs, threatening to become an ailment of the heart." Meanwhile, Fu Bi, demoted to Bozhou prefect for failing to aggressively disburse Green Sprouts Loans, was denounced by Deng Wan for "obstructing the New Policies."

Fu Bi said ruefully to Zhang Fangping: "It is hard to know men." His old friend replied: "I don't find him hard to know at all. I once supervised the provincial examinations with him — he overturned every established rule. He had me dismissed. In my old age, I often stare at the ceiling and sigh in silence."

The two dismissed statesmen, meeting in transit, embodied the tragedy of the Renzong era's end. Zhang Fangping had worked alongside Wang Anshi and witnessed firsthand how the eccentric reformer "overturned every established rule." The difficulty was never in knowing the man — it was in the powerlessness that came after knowing.

2
Popular Revolt

The Baojia Uprising: Fathers and Sons Weeping, Severed Wrists to Evade Conscription

Just as Su Dongpo prepared to leave the capital, the suburbs erupted in violence — the Baojia militia system, requiring village men to undergo military training and supply their own weapons, triggered mass protests. Fathers and sons embraced weeping; some peasants severed their own fingers and wrists in horrific acts of self-mutilation to escape conscription.

The Baojia system had been implemented the previous winter, with militiamen kept in their villages for training. Authorities required peasants to provide their own military equipment — merely bows and arrows, in theory — but this became the immediate trigger for revolt. Convinced the training was preparation for a northern campaign, peasants resorted to the most extreme forms of protest.

⚠️ Critical Turning Point: The revolt cost Wang Anshi his last remaining friend, Han Wei — the very man who, as the crown prince's tutor, had once steered the young Shenzong's thinking entirely toward Wang Anshi. Slight policy disagreements had already estranged them; now the Baojia uprising led to Han Wei's dismissal as prefect of the affected region. Wang Anshi was now completely isolated.

The revolt erupted in the capital's suburbs, directly under the court's gaze — impossible to disguise or deflect. If heavenly portents could be dismissed with "even Yao and Shun had droughts," the blood and severed limbs on the ground defied any "enlightened reform" rhetoric. The Baojia system's intent was to embed soldiers among farmers; the farmers' response — reading "training" as "conscription notice" — was brutally clear.

3
Heaven's Judgment

Mount Hua Collapses and the Long Drought

In 1073, the sacred Mount Hua suffered a landslide. From that summer through the following spring, not a drop of rain fell. In the Chinese cosmology of "Heaven-human correspondence," these were not natural disasters — they were Heaven's direct verdict on human affairs. Natural calamity was heavenly wrath.

The emperor, following ritual protocol, moved to a different palace to show reverence and adopted a coarse diet to demonstrate contrition. He asked Wang Anshi what to do. "Droughts and floods are natural disasters," Wang Anshi replied. "Even in the times of Yao and Shun there were droughts and floods. We need only continue good governance." The emperor's response cut to the heart: "That is precisely what I fear — that our governance may not be good. I hear many complaints about commercial taxes. Everyone at court has heard them, even the Empress and the Dowager Empress."

🔴 Wang Anshi's Response

"Droughts and floods are natural disasters. Even Yao and Shun faced them. We need only continue good governance." — Severing natural calamity from human governance entirely, a position that defied the entire Chinese political tradition.

🟢 Feng Jing's Rebuttal

"I have heard them too," Minister Feng Jing contradicted Wang Anshi on the spot. Wang Anshi shot back: "Feng hears complaints because the discontented all flock around him" — but this retort only exposed his defensiveness.

In traditional China, "Heaven's transformations" constituted the highest political veto. The emperor styled himself Son of Heaven; heavenly portents were direct warnings to that Son. Wang Anshi tried to neutralize this threat with rationalism, but Shenzong's fear was genuine — he feared not Yao and Shun's drought, but becoming their opposite.

4
The Fatal Blow

Zheng Xia's Painting: One Humble Clerk's Courage

Zheng Xia, a minor clerk at the Anshang Gate — powerless, without rank or influence — single-handedly accomplished what the entire Censorate dared not: he painted the misery of the New Policies' victims, "chained and felling trees to earn money for repaying government loans," and sent the scroll into the palace with a pledge: "If it does not rain within ten days, behead me outside the Xuan De Gate."

"I have heard that those who campaign south and north all submit paintings of victorious battle formations and mountain terrain. I suspect no one has ever submitted a painting of the people pawning wives and selling children, felling mulberry trees and destroying homes, scattered and fleeing in desperate distress. I have carefully recorded what I daily witness at the Anshang Gate in a single painting. Not even a hundredth of the suffering is captured, yet even a glance should move one to tears." — Zheng Xia's Memorial

Zheng Xia initially tried the proper channels, but palace officials refused to accept it, saying a minor clerk had no standing to communicate with the emperor. So he went to the postal relay station outside the capital and claimed it contained urgent military dispatches, having it rushed into the palace. This "illegal use of official courier services" was later investigated — but by then the political impact was irreversible.

💡 When every censor remained silent, the duty of remonstrating with the government fell upon a palace gate clerk. A departing censor told Zheng Xia: "The Censorate's responsibility to criticize the government now seems to rest on a gate clerk's shoulders" — handing him a packet of famous officials' memorials for safekeeping. Lü Huiqing learned of this through spies, sent Shu Dan to intercept Zheng Xia, searched his luggage, and used the seized documents to identify and prosecute all critics of the regime.

The emperor took the scroll into his private quarters and showed it to the imperial family. The Dowager Empress spoke first. Emperor Shenzong's brother, Prince Qi, added his voice. After a tense silence, the Dowager Empress asked: "Wang Anshi has caused great trouble. What do you intend to do?" The next day, Wang Anshi was dismissed.

5
Court Showdown

The Dowager Empress, Prince Qi, and the Emperor

The confrontation in the emperor's bedchamber was the chapter's dramatic peak: the Dowager Empress exerted grandmotherly pressure, Prince Qi offered fraternal counsel, and Emperor Shenzong lashed out in royal fury — three people representing three verdicts on Wang Anshi's New Policies: rejection, doubt, and anguished ambivalence.

The emperor's resistance was futile. The next day, he dismissed Wang Anshi and — more critically — resolved to abolish eighteen measures: commercial taxes, the Green Sprouts Loan, the Hired Service levy, the Baojia system, and the Land Survey and Equalization Tax. These were the core of the New Policies. Then rain came — as if Heaven rejoiced.

Yet Wang Anshi's hour was not quite over. The emperor retained Lü Huiqing and Deng Wan. Soon after, the New Policies were repealed, but these men — especially Huiqing — turned on all who had criticized the regime. Zheng Xia submitted another illustrated volume, "Portraits of Upright Gentlemen and Crooked Villains." Huiqing wanted Zheng Xia executed; the emperor intervened: "Zheng Xia cared for the nation, not himself. I admire his courage and integrity." Zheng Xia was banished to distant Guangdong instead.

6
Internal Collapse

The Rot After Victory: A Revolutionary Party's Fate

Lin Yutang quotes Su Dongpo's aphorism to reveal the pattern: "Every revolutionary party displays its greatest unity and strength before gaining power; once it achieves power and eliminates the opposition, it begins to splinter through internal strife." After Wang Anshi's downfall, his party members collapsed one after another — Deng Wan and Lü Huiqing "wept together before the emperor," but tears could not mask their mutual backstabbing.

Wang Anshi's tragedy was total. His son burst in barefoot, clutching a woman's sash, suggesting they "chop off Han Qi's and Fu Bi's heads." His brother Anguo quoted Confucius to his face: "Can you not drive away the sycophants?" His most trusted lieutenant, Lü Huiqing, published his private letters to alienate him from the emperor. Deng Wan instantly transferred his loyalty from Wang Anshi to Huiqing. The Zheng Xia affair saw Huiqing deploy Shu Dan to intercept, search, and fabricate mass prosecutions — methods even Wang Anshi could never have imagined.

Su Dongpo's other aphorism: "It is easy to endure poverty and hardship, hard to endure wealth and honor. Easy to bear toil and labor, hard to bear leisure and idleness. Easy to endure pain, hard to endure an itch. One who can bear leisure, endure wealth, and tolerate an itch — that is truly a man of the Dao." — The test of a revolutionary lies not in adversity, but in the aftermath of victory.

Lin Yutang's summation: the aspiration to overthrow the powerful brings forth humanity's finest instincts; the power to rule over others brings forth its ugliest. When things went smoothly and everyone had an office, Deng Wan, Huiqing, and Zeng Bu were too busy to quarrel. When Wang Anshi fell and the situation deteriorated, party members collapsed one after another. The seeds of internal decay had been planted from the very beginning of the New Policies. As Confucius taught: "Drive away the music of Zheng, keep sycophants at a distance." Wang Anshi was consumed by precisely the "Zheng music" and "sycophants" he failed to expel.

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

The Storm of Opposition wove together three threads: the people's blood and tears (the Baojia revolt), Heaven's judgment (Mount Hua's collapse and the drought), and the court's will (the Dowager Empress and Prince Qi). Zheng Xia accomplished with a single painting what the entire opposition could not — not because he had more power, but because he was closest to the people. The deeper tragedy after Wang Anshi's dismissal: the New Policies were abolished, but factionalism had taken permanent root. The Stubborn Premier was gone, but the sycophants he had elevated — Huiqing, Deng Wan — remained in power, turning on all who had criticized the regime. As Su Dongpo observed: overthrowing the powerful brings out humanity's finest instincts; ruling over others brings out its ugliest. The tragedy of revolution lies not in failure, but in what comes after victory.
