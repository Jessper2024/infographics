📖 Overview

After Emperor Shenzong's death, with the Empress Dowager as regent, Su Dongpo's fate underwent a dramatic reversal. In just eight months, he rose from Rank 7 to Rank 3 Hanlin Academician — nearly the highest possible post. The Empress Dowager revealed that the promotion came from Shenzong's dying wish: "Whenever Shenzong put down his chopsticks at meals, it was because he was reading your writings." Su drafted over 800 edicts, working by red candlelight in the palace at night. Yet he discovered that luxurious days and simple days differ not at all in happiness.

Core Question: How does a man who never wanted office conduct himself when pushed to the pinnacle of power?

Key Insight: At his peak, Su wrote: "Joy is envied, suffering is feared — but only before they arrive" — happiness does not depend on status.

01
RISE

The Late Emperor's Dying Wish

When Su asked the Empress Dowager why he was suddenly promoted, the answer was Shenzong's dying wish — the emperor would put down his chopsticks mid-meal whenever he was reading Su's writings.

The Empress Dowager summoned Su and, after verifying his previous and current ranks, asked: "Do you know why you were suddenly promoted?" Su guessed the Dowager's grace, the young emperor's favor, ministerial recommendation — all denied. Finally she said: "It was the Late Emperor's dying wish. All the palace servants knew — whenever Shenzong put down his chopsticks at meals, it was because he was reading your writings. He often praised you as a rare talent and intended to promote you, but passed away before he could." All three wept. She removed the golden lotus candle and gave it to Su to take back to the Hanlin Academy.

Su was promoted three times in eight months: from Rank 7 to 6, jumped to 4, and finally to Rank 3 Hanlin. The Song dynasty had no Rank 1 officials; the Prime Minister was only Rank 2.

02
WORK

Nights as Hanlin: Red Candles and 800 Edicts

As Hanlin Academician, Su drafted over 800 imperial edicts, all elegant, precise, and apt — he never needed to consult reference books.

Hanlin work was done at night, "locked within the palace." Each evening Su walked along the eastern palace wall to his study, connected to the Dowager's quarters. Edicts were dictated by the Dowager; he rendered them in the most classical, dignified prose for release the next day. On cold nights, she sent hot wine. After Su's death, his successor Academician Hong asked an old servant how he compared to Su. The servant replied: "Su Dongpo's literary talent may not surpass yours, but he never had to consult books."

Su's edicts included the dismissal of Li Ding (his accuser), the banishment of Lü Huiqing, and a posthumous citation for Wang Anshi — the latter so subtly worded it was "criticism disguised as ambiguous praise."

03
CONFLICT

Sima Guang's Funeral: A Joke That Sowed a Decade of Hatred

At Sima Guang's funeral, Su Dongpo skewered the neo-Confucian moralist Cheng Yi with a single joke — "Yichuan is truly a rustic Shusun Tong stewing in his own filth" — planting an irreconcilable feud with the Henan school.

Cheng Yi, officiating the funeral, strictly followed "ancient rites" and forbade Sima Guang's sons from standing by the coffin to receive mourners. When Su led the officials to pay respects after the ancestral temple ceremony, Cheng Yi blocked them, citing the Analects: "On a day the Master wept, he did not sing." Su laughed: "But the Analects never said, 'On a day the Master sang, he did not weep.'" He brushed past Cheng Yi, and upon discovering Sima's sons had been barred from receiving guests, publicly cursed Cheng Yi in classical language. Neither man ever forgot this label.

This incident marked the formal beginning of factional struggle between Su's Sichuan faction and Cheng Yi's Henan neo-Confucian faction.

04
LIFE

Capital Splendor and Simple Habits

Su built an official residence near the Eastern Flowery Gate, enjoying a life worlds apart from his Huangzhou farm — yet he still rose at dawn, combed his hair a hundred times, and continued his yoga practice.

The Sus' new life in the capital was rich and colorful. Nearby were silver shops, silk stores, and two- and three-story buildings. Out-of-season fruit sold at any price. Restaurants offered forty to fifty dishes, waiters constantly circulating for selection. Su loved entertaining at home, and restaurants catered banquets with silverware supplied by the establishment. Yet Su maintained his simple habits — rising at dawn, combing his hair a hundred strokes, then lying back down for "a doze so sweet nothing could compare."

05
INSIGHT

Joy and Suffering: Happiness Doesn't Depend on Status

At the peak of his career, Su wrote a startling discovery: luxurious days and simple days differ not at all in happiness.

In his note "Joy and Suffering," he wrote: "Joy is envied, suffering is feared — but only before they arrive. When joy or suffering actually comes and you experience it firsthand, you find the joy you anticipated is not so joyful, and the suffering you feared is not so terrible." He also observed: "Those who don't want office are pursued by the authorities; those who want it lack the qualifications. Once the 'official desire' is satisfied, the pleasures of high office are no greater than those of a successful blacksmith."

"Joy is envied, suffering is feared — but only before they arrive. When joy or suffering actually comes and you experience it firsthand, you find the envied and the feared are nowhere to be found." — Su Dongpo, "Joy and Suffering"

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

Su Dongpo's clarity at the peak of power is astonishing: he knew that "the pleasures of high office are no greater than those of a successful blacksmith." This insight allowed him to be both the Dowager's favorite and a man who refused to play political games. Yet it was precisely this refusal — to conform, to stay silent, to abandon independent thought — that made him the target of factional attacks.
