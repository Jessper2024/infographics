Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 24 "Insiders Blame Short Sellers While Secretly Dumping"

Livermore exposes how insiders use anonymous media "authoritative sources" to spread bullish talk while dumping shares, then blame the ensuing price decline on "bear raids." This chapter is the most systematic anatomy of information manipulation — not only revealing the techniques, but piercingly identifying the legal loopholes.

▎S — Situation
The business of stock speculation will never die. A speculator's natural enemies include ignorance, greed, fear, and hope — all part of human animal nature, which no laws or regulations can eradicate. Markets grow ever more complex: 25 years earlier the NYSE had only 275 stocks; now it had swollen to about 900.
▎C — Conflict
The greatest threat the public faces is not naked tips, but "sugar-coated rumors" disguised as authoritative information — newspapers brim with bullish pronouncements attributed to "a leading insider," "a prominent director," "a banker of high standing." More absurd still: the law severely punishes those who spread bearish rumors, but does not punish those who spread bullish lies. The public loses far more money buying on bullish lies than selling on bearish rumors.
▎Q — Key Question
How can the public identify and defend against information manipulation wrapped in anonymous authority?
▎A — Answer
The Three-Question Rule: One — "Who said it?" Anonymous sources are not credible. Two — "Why are they saying it?" Are their interests aligned with yours? Three — "Is the timing right?" The market leads fundamentals by 6-9 months; today's good news may already be priced in. Ultimately, the law cannot protect you from bullish lies — only your own judgment can.
01
Cognitive Framework

The Speculator's Five Great Enemies: Four Internal, One External

Livermore systematically categorizes the speculator's enemies into two layers:

Four Internal Enemies — Ignorance, Greed, Fear, and Hope: These are part of human animal nature; all written laws and exchange regulations in the world cannot eradicate them. Accidents can smash the most carefully laid plans to pieces, beyond the predictive scope of any rational framework. These internal enemies can only be gradually overcome through long-term self-training.

One External Enemy — Deliberately Misleading False Information: Unlike straightforward tips, these are wrapped in "semi-official" sugar-coating and disguise, making them harder to detect and therefore more insidious and dangerous. Livermore casually pulls a typical example from the day's newspaper: "A banker of high standing says it would be premature to expect a market decline now" — Did he really say it? Why won't he put his name to it?

02
Information Anatomy

"Anonymous Authority": The Industrial Production of Bullish Rumors

Livermore's dissection of "anonymous authority" as an information-manipulation technique is classic — and remains entirely relevant 100 years later:

These messages share three characteristics: Source vague but implying authority ("a leading insider" — which of the dozen-plus directors, exactly?); Content bullish but unverifiable ("business is thriving" — what are the specific numbers? What verifiable facts?); Timing coinciding precisely with the insider distribution window (release bullish news before dumping, then blame the subsequent price decline on "bear raids").

Livermore identifies an even larger asymmetry: the public's losses from selling at low prices under bearish advice during so-called "bear raids" are dwarfed by the losses from buying at inflated prices when insiders spread bullish rumors. But the law only protects against the former, not the latter — bearish rumors are criminal; bullish lies are not. This legal asymmetry creates, in essence, the most hostile information environment possible for retail investors.

The public loses far more money buying stocks at inflated prices after being incited by anonymous insiders than it does selling stocks at below-value prices under the influence of bearish advice during so-called bear raids.
— Jesse Livermore
03
Institutional Reflection

Legal Asymmetry: Punish the Bear, Indulge the Bull

Livermore poses a question with powerful contemporary relevance — if someone fabricates and spreads rumors intended to damage the credit of a person or enterprise, deliberately depressing the value of its securities to induce the public to sell, the law will surely punish them severely. So, to prevent the public from buying at prices above true value: who punishes those who spread utterly groundless bullish claims?

The answer: "Nobody."

This legal asymmetry creates a natural space for information manipulation — insiders can legally disseminate bullish messages through anonymous channels to push up stock prices for distribution, while the natural price decline after distribution is cleverly blamed on imaginary "bear raids." Livermore's call rings out: "If a law were passed punishing the dissemination of bullish lies as current law punishes bearish lies, I believe the public would be saved millions of dollars in losses."

⚖️
The Reality of Asymmetry: Bearish rumors are punished; bullish rumors are exempt — this causes optimistic falsehoods to proliferate while pessimistic truths are suppressed. In a market where the information environment itself is asymmetric, the only thing retail investors can rely on is their own judgment and the habit of questioning information sources.
04
Defense Guide

The Three-Question Rule: A Retail Investor's Information Filter

Though Livermore did not explicitly name "the Three-Question Rule," the logic of this chapter naturally derives this information-verification framework, applicable in any era:

Question One — Who Said It? If the information source is anonymous ("an insider," "an authority," "a prominent director"), automatically deem it untrustworthy. Someone who won't even put their name to a statement — why should its content merit belief? This is not paranoia; it is the most basic form of risk control.

Question Two — Why Are They Saying It? Are the information publisher's interests aligned with those being influenced? A broker's market letter recommends buying — but is his firm simultaneously distributing the same stock for insiders? Conflicts of interest are the most reliable signal of information distortion.

Question Three — Is the Timing Right? Livermore emphasizes — the market leads fundamentals by 6-9 months. Current good news may already be fully priced or even overpriced by the market. When a broker tells you "earnings are great so you should buy," what you should ask is: how long has this earnings information already been reflected in the stock price?

💡
Key Takeaway · BLUF

The information-manipulation mechanisms Livermore exposed 100 years ago remain entirely relevant today: bullish rumors wrapped in anonymous authority are the greatest source of public losses. Defense requires only three questions — Who said it? Why are they saying it? Is the timing right? If the first question has no answer, don't bother with the rest. Remember: the law does not protect you from bullish lies. In a market where bearish rumors are criminal but bullish lies are not, the only defense that can protect you is your own independent thinking.
