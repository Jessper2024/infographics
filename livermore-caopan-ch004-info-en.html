01
Rule One

Never Average Down — the Most Common Fatal Mistake

Livermore strongly advises: never buy more at lower prices to average down a losing position. This is precisely the most common trick — and the trap that has destroyed countless speculators.

His example: you buy 100 shares at $50, then at $47 you buy another 100 to "average down to $48½." On what grounds do you double your worry? If it drops to $44, you're down $600 on the first lot and $300 on the second. If you insist on this path, you must follow it through: 200 more at $44, 400 at $41, 800 at $38, and so on. How many can withstand that pressure?

⚠️ Livermore's Warning: At the risk of repetition and sermonizing, I strongly urge you: do not adopt the practice of averaging down. You must remain perpetually vigilant against this kind of abnormal market action, lest disaster strike.

02
Rule Two

Margin Call = Close Immediately

The only infallible "tip" Livermore ever received from brokers was the margin call. When such a notice arrives, close your position at once.

You are on the wrong side of the market. Why throw good money after bad? Hold onto those good dollars a little longer. Take them to a more attractive venture — don't pour them into a trade that is clearly losing.

❌ Wrong Approach

After receiving a margin call, keep injecting capital to maintain a losing position, hoping for a market reversal

✅ Right Approach

Close immediately upon receiving the call, admit the mistake, preserve remaining capital for the next real opportunity

A successful merchant is willing to extend credit to various customers, but would never sell all his goods on credit to just one. The more customers, the more risk is spread. Likewise, a speculator should risk only a limited portion of capital on each venture.

03
Rule Three

Withdraw Half Your Profits — Let Money Truly Come to Hand

Livermore's core strategy: every time you close out a successful trade, take half the profit and stash it in a safe place. The only money a speculator ever truly makes from Wall Street is the cash withdrawn from the account after a winning trade.

He recalls vacationing at Palm Beach, wiring his broker to deposit $1,000,000 into his bank account. The telegraph operator, with 20 years of experience, had never handled a client's request for a broker to deposit money — usually the wires went the other way: demanding more margin from clients.

"Money in a brokerage account or a bank account is not the same as money in your hand — money your fingers can actually feel. Feeling it gives it meaning. There is a sense of possession in it that slightly dampens the impulse to make reckless trading decisions."

Livermore's only regret: he didn't follow this principle throughout his entire career. In certain places, it would have helped him walk a smoother path.

04
Core Philosophy

Speculation Is a Serious Business, Not Gambling

The common failing of most speculators is impatience — they want to get rich in a very short time. They are not prepared to spend two or three years growing their capital by 500%, but instead attempt to achieve this in two or three months.

Occasionally they succeed. But do these bold traders ultimately hold onto their winnings? No. Because the money comes too easily — easy come, easy go, it only passes through their hands briefly. Such speculators lose their sense of balance. They keep betting everything until, somewhere, something goes wrong — a violent, unforeseeable, devastating event — and the final margin call arrives, too large to meet. The unrestrained gambler vanishes like a shooting star.

❌ Gambler's Mindset

Attempt 500% in 2-3 months; bet everything relentlessly; treat speculation as a get-rich-quick shortcut

✅ Businessman's Mindset

Grow capital 500% over 2-3 years; risk limited capital each time; treat speculation as a serious commercial enterprise

A merchant opening a new shop doesn't expect to earn over 25% in the first year. But speculators want 100%. Their calculations don't hold up — they haven't approached speculation with sound business principles.

05
Ultimate Lesson

Stay in Your Lane — Don't Lose Money Outside Your Competence

Every one of Livermore's investments outside Wall Street failed — Florida real estate bubbles, oil wells, aircraft manufacturing, high-tech product ventures. In all these, he lost every cent. A friend's words snapped him awake.

He once tried to convince a friend to invest $50,000 in a certain business. After listening carefully, the friend said: "Livermore, you can never succeed in any business outside your own. If you want $50,000 to speculate, you can have it — free of charge. But please, stick to speculation and stay far away from that business." The next morning, a check for that amount arrived by mail.

"Speculation is a business in itself, and everyone should treat it as such — with undivided attention. Don't let yourself be swayed by emotion, flattery, or temptation of profit."

Be wary of brokers who unwittingly become the source of many speculators' ruin. They earn commissions — the more you trade, the more they earn — and often encourage overtrading. Never make any trade unless you know with certainty that it is financially safe.

💰
Key Takeaway

In this chapter, Livermore reveals the most essential truth about wealth in speculation: paper profits are not real wealth — only cash withdrawn from your account, money you can actually hold, counts. The three iron rules of preserving wealth — never average down, close on margin calls, withdraw half of every profit — appear simple, yet require transcending the human instincts of greed and fear. Speculation is a serious business. Only by operating like a merchant can you truly make money "in hand" your own.
