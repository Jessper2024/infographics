The New Sell and Sell Short · Chapter 3
Keeping Good Trading Records

Win or lose, a trader must become better after every trade — otherwise all the analysis, time, and risk taken are meaningless.

OVERVIEW

A single winning trade proves nothing — consistent profitability is the real challenge

If a monkey throws darts at a board listing stocks, the stocks it hits could very well earn good returns. What's truly difficult in trading is building a long-term profitable pattern — this requires two record systems: a trader's spreadsheet to track data, and a trading journal to record decisions and charts, combined with a scoring system that turns every trade into compounding improvement. Good records are where the journey from accidental wins to consistent profits begins.

01
Core Insight

Why Trading Records Are Essential

Keeping good records is the best way to learn and improve from experience — they turn fleeting observations into permanent memories and lessons. Money management rules and good records are the two cornerstones of survival and success in the markets. No matter how large a win, a single uncontrolled loss can wipe out all prior results — overconfidence will inevitably cause profits to be given back.

The Trading Journal: Seven-Color Status Codes

Build your trading journal using Outlook Calendar + Snagit screenshots. Record each trade in illustrated form: daily and weekly charts with arrows, lines, and written commentary. Use seven colors to mark trade status — at a glance during monthly review, patterns emerge. It's okay to make mistakes, but never repeat them — the journal is the best way to capture both the joy of winning and the pain of losing in retrievable files.

04
Process & Method

Trade Planning Workflow & Margaret's Method

Write a trading plan before every trade — that's how you stay ahead of the competition. Whether you choose value trading or momentum trading, having a plan gives you an edge over every other market participant. Beyond digital tools, you can also adopt Margaret's low-tech method — print candidate charts and pin them on the wall, where one glance reveals trade-ready signals.

💻
Digital: Outlook + Snagit
  • Capture weekly & daily charts with arrows
  • Paste into Outlook calendar, mark purple
  • Log into broker, set OCO stop & target
  • Add to watchlist, track in chart window
Switch purple → yellow after execution
🖼️
Physical: Margaret's Wall Method
  • Print candidate stock charts
  • Mark trading signals with red pen
  • Pin on board — visible when you walk in
  • Remove after trade completes or cancels
Always keep plans current and updated

Quantitative Review

The Trade Scoring System: Measure Every Trade

Not scoring your trades is like a sprinter claiming to be fast but never using a stopwatch. Elder scores entry quality, exit quality, and overall trade grade separately, targeting above 50% — buy below the day's midpoint, sell above it. Trade scoring forces you to confront your performance honestly and continuously improve execution quality.

📥 Entry Score (Target > 50%)
(Day High − Entry Price) ÷ (Day High − Day Low) × 100%
📤 Exit Score (Target > 50%)
(Exit Price − Day Low) ÷ (Day High − Day Low) × 100%

KEY TAKEAWAY

Record-keeping isn't about looking backward — it's about changing every future trade. Build two systems — a spreadsheet tracking your data, a journal capturing your decisions — then apply the triple scoring framework for entries, exits, and overall trade grades. This turns every trade into a stepping stone. Go from accidental wins to inevitable profits. Go from a tourist in the markets to a professional.
