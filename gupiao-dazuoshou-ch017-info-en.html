Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 17

The Irresistible Allure of Tips Is Human Weakness

A · Answer: Tips are "the alcohol of hope"—they let you substitute someone else's judgment for your own thinking. Only by forging independent thought into an iron rule can you become immune to all tips.
01
Case

Borneo Tin: The Most Carefully Crafted Tip Trap

The chairman of Borneo Tin meticulously orchestrated a "honey trap" to deliver a tip through Livermore's wife—and it spectacularly backfired.

At a Palm Beach dinner party, Borneo Tin chairman Wisenstein deliberately sat next to Mrs. Livingston and whispered a "top secret" tip in her ear: buy Borneo Tin with everything you've got. Mrs. Livingston used the $500 pocket money her husband had just given her to buy 100 shares, which rose 3 points that day. However, Livermore was bearish on the broad market at the time, and noticing the stock's unusual activity, decided to start his short-selling campaign with this very stock—and made a killing instead.

"Of all the tips I ever heard, that one was the most carefully planned and the most painstakingly delivered. Yet, that tip went wrong. It had to go wrong."

Wisenstein made two mistakes: he miscalculated Mrs. Livingston's reluctance to tell her husband, and he mistakenly assumed Livermore was the kind of trader who listened to tips.

02
Psychology

The Psychology of Tip Addiction: Wishful Thinking Syndrome

The root of tip-chasing isn't a lack of intelligence—it's laziness. Having someone tell you what to buy is far easier and more pleasant than independent research.

Livermore hit the nail on the head: tip-seekers are "drunks on hope." They suffer from wishful-thinking syndrome—it's less about greed blinding their eyes than about laziness breeding wishful thinking. As long as someone tells you what to do, you feel happy—"it's almost the best thing after happiness itself."

⚠️ Key insight: Both givers and receivers of tips are relay runners in an endless chain—tip propagation is a never-ending vine of advertisement. Those who distribute tips don't care about credibility because those who seek tips never demand quality. "A tip is a tip."

It's not just the outside public—even professional traders on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange are equally tip addicts. The reason they harbored hostility toward Livermore was precisely that he "never gave anyone any tips."

03
Variation

The Reverse Tip: The Myth of "Bear Raids"

The so-called "bear raid" is nothing but a carefully packaged placebo—designed to spare losers from admitting their own judgment was wrong.

A normal tip tells you what to buy or sell; the reverse tip explains a decline that's already happened. When a stock suddenly drops, brokers and the media tell you "someone is raiding it"—which spares you from having to sell at a loss or admit you were wrong. Livermore exposed this: of 100 alleged "bear raids," 99 are simply the acceleration phase of a legitimate decline. If a raid truly drove a stock below its value, insiders would immediately buy hand over fist—because it would be the deal of a lifetime.

"Attributing most sudden declines to the manipulation of some gambler... is a convenient excuse, easily used to satisfy the speculator's need for a reason—those speculators who are themselves nothing but blind gamblers."

04
Contrast

Independent Thinking vs. Tip-Following: Worlds Apart

The fork in the road for two investors: one made decisions using a scrap-paper basket, the other chased tips relentlessly.

A Pennsylvania German investor noticed that the chairman of Atchison Railroad casually jotted numbers on fine stationery and threw it in the wastebasket—he immediately sold the stock and switched to Delaware Railroad, whose chairman tore up old envelopes for note paper. The latter stock doubled year after year. Meanwhile, J.T. Hood (an NYSE member) advised a friend not to send an angry telegram to an insider whose "tip had failed"—"If you send that telegram, you'll never get another tip from him again!"

"It isn't only the outside public that are tip addicts. The professional traders on the floor of the New York Stock Exchange are just as bad."
05
Anecdotes

The Theater of Tip Absurdity: Four Classic Tales

The absurdity of tips has been proven again and again through different stories—yet it has never stopped anyone from chasing the next one.

🎩 Westlake vs. Gates

Old Westlake solemnly advised Gates to short Reading—Gates went long instead and made $60,000. "I always make money doing the opposite of your tips!" Old Westlake sighed: "If I had your brains, what a fortune I'd make."

🧢 WAR in the Hat

Cartoonist Rogers had "WAR" embroidered inside his bowler hat lining. A broker friend borrowed the hat, saw the word, connected it to the possibility of the Spanish-American War, and decided to go short—war was indeed declared, and he made a killing. He never gave the hat back.

👴 Son-in-Law Traps Father-in-Law

A broker followed a journalist friend's "reliable source" tip and bought G.O.H.—only to lose $3,500. Upon further inquiry, he discovered the source was his own father-in-law. The farther the source, the "purer" the tip seems.

📉 Hood's Absurd Logic

Two floor traders were twice burned by a director's "gratitude tips," suffering heavy losses. When one wanted to send an angry cable, Hood stopped him: "If you send that cable, you'll never get another tip from him again!"

💡
Key Takeaway

Tips are the cheapest and most expensive narcotic in the trading world. They let you temporarily escape thinking—but permanently forfeit the ability to think for yourself. The Rothschild secret to success applies to every trader: "I never buy at the bottom, and I always sell too soon." The deeper meaning: only the judgment you make yourself is profit that truly belongs to you.
