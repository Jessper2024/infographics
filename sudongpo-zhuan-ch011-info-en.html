Golden Period
The Hangzhou and Mizhou period marked Su Dongpo's golden age of poetry — torn between Ziyou's counsel to "be silent" and his own nature of "if something is wrong, it's like finding a fly in your food — you have to spit it out," nourished by Mencius's "flood-like qi" yet facing the ethical dilemma between self-preservation and heroic authenticity, he ultimately chose the latter, launching his most uninhibited creative period.

Core QuestionHow did the Hangzhou period shape Su Dongpo? Between Ziyou's counsel of silence and Mencius's "flood-like qi," which force ultimately guided his life's direction?

Core AnswerIn the summer of 1071, Su Dongpo left the capital with his family for Hangzhou on China's southeastern coast. Over the next eight or nine years, he served with distinction in Hangzhou, Mizhou (near Qingdao), and Xuzhou — his most prolific period of poetry. En route, he stayed seventy days with Ziyou in Chenzhou, celebrating the Mid-Autumn Festival together. Ziyou, knowing his brother's dangerous candor, placed a hand over his mouth and counseled silence. But Dongpo replied: "When I feel something is wrong, it's like finding a fly in my food — I have to spit it out." This "flood-like qi" — Mencius's "vast and unyielding" spirit — sustained Dongpo throughout his life.

1
Brotherly Bond

Seventy Days at Chenzhou: Their Longest Reunion

En route to Hangzhou, Su Dongpo stopped in Chenzhou to visit Ziyou and stayed over seventy days — the longest reunion the brothers would ever share. Ziyou, impoverished, lived with his large family in a low cottage. Dongpo teased him about his height: "Often he bows his head to chant the classics; suddenly he stretches — and bumps the roof!"

Ziyou, ever prolific, had three sons and seven daughters — Dongpo helped marry off his nieces one by one. For over two months the family lived together in reunion, the brothers rowing on Willow Lake or strolling the suburbs, discussing politics, household affairs, and their respective futures. The old friend Zhang Fangping, now retired, lived nearby and joined them often for drinking.

🔴 Dongpo's Weakness

"I often speak without restraint. When I feel something is wrong, it's like finding a fly in my food — I have to spit it out." — His candid nature, the very root of the future Crow Terrace Poetry Trial.

🟢 Ziyou's Wisdom

"But first you must know whom you're talking to. Some people can be trusted — others cannot." Ziyou placed a hand over his brother's mouth, urging silence — a gesture he would repeat after Dongpo's release from prison.

Zhang Fangping was a legendary drinker — "a hundred cups without getting drunk." Dongpo claimed his own capacity was modest but saw no reason to abstain: "I don't envy heavy drinkers. I get tipsy after one or two cups, but am I not just as happy as you?" Ouyang Xiu was also a formidable drinker, though Zhang usually outdrank him. When Zhang proposed drinking, he didn't count cups — he counted days.

2
Character Contrast

Two Brothers: China's Henry and William James

The difference between the brothers was like that between Henry and William James: Dongpo, like William, infused wit and humor into the dry landscape of political reality; Ziyou, like Henry, brought depth of human thought and observation to his quiet prose. Ziyou lacked half his brother's brilliance, but his work carried substance and profundity that made him a major literary figure in his own right.

⚖️
Shared Stance
Their political views were always identical, their position always united. But personality and prose style diverged completely — Dongpo like William James, Ziyou like Henry James.

Among intimates, Dongpo was exuberant, full of wordplay and practical jokes — making practical people everywhere nervous, fearing he might blurt out the truth at any moment. "As if truth were something that could simply be spoken aloud!" His candor terrified the cautious and delighted the brave.

3
Philosophical Roots

The Flood-like Qi: Mencius's Legacy to Su Dongpo

"Qi" (气) — vapor, air, atmosphere, spirit, force, impulse, suppressed fury — is the key concept for understanding Su Dongpo's character. Mencius's "flood-like qi" (haoran zhi qi), akin to Bergson's élan vital, represents the vivid, compelling force within human character — a noble spirit for right action and justice, innate, growing stronger or weaker through the course of a life.

"What is this flood-like qi?" a disciple once asked Mencius.
"It is difficult to describe," Mencius replied. "This qi is vast and unyielding. Nourish it properly without harm, and it fills the space between Heaven and Earth. But it must be accompanied by righteousness and the Way; without these, it withers."

Armed with this quintessential flood-like qi, Su Dongpo continually faced an ethical contradiction: on one hand, the desire to remain true to himself, sustaining that innate, fearless human spirit; on the other, the instinct for self-preservation. At certain periods this conflict grew especially acute — but usually, he chose to remain heroic.

💡 Lin Yutang's insight: The difference between great and small personalities often lies in energy, drive, impact, and vitality. Su Dongpo's critics and admirers alike continually invoked this magnificent spiritual force — "this powerful, intense strength" — sustained by righteousness and truth, fearing nothing in the universe.

Dongpo quoted Mencius's deeper political philosophy: "Goodness alone cannot govern; law alone cannot execute itself... Only the humane should occupy high positions. When the inhumane hold high office, they spread their evil among the multitude." These words read like a precise prophecy of Wang Anshi's New Policies — institutions cannot run themselves; everything depends on who wields power.

4
Political Awareness

"If the Emperor Wants to Kill Me, I Die Without Regret"

Su Dongpo confessed to his brother that when he submitted memorials to the throne, he genuinely feared for his life. He told his examination classmate Chao Duanyan: "If the emperor wants to kill me, I die without regret. But one thing — sacrificing myself to advance you? I won't do that." Both laughed — but the laughter masked genuine fear.

Dongpo explained to Ziyou: "I passed Emperor Renzong's special examination, and only then did senior officials treat me as a friend. Emperor Renzong accepted my frank counsel. Now, if I don't speak, who will?" This "who, if not me?" sense of duty was precisely the flood-like qi applied to political practice. Chao Duanyan listened in silence, his expression grave.

Ziyou offered an intriguing philosophy of time: "Do you know? One day of leisure seems to equal two ordinary days. Therefore, if a man lives seventy years entirely at ease, he has effectively lived one hundred and forty. This is the simplest method of longevity." These words were both counsel to withdraw from political danger and a distillation of Ziyou's own philosophy of self-preservation — a path Dongpo could never ultimately follow.

5
Literary Manifesto

"The Gibbon Cries, the Crane Calls — Unaware of Travelers Below"

Su Dongpo's genius demanded free, unhindered expression. He wrote: "Fancy phrases from past lives can never be ground away, a poet's lingering sentiment still winds its way. The gibbon cries, the crane calls, without intent — unaware of travelers on the path below." This was his literary self-declaration: expression as natural and instinctive as animal calls, utterly indifferent to what "the travelers below" — those in power — might think.

This poem perfectly encapsulates Dongpo's creative philosophy: true poetry is a voice that must emerge from within, like a gibbon's cry or a crane's call — never made to please anyone. The phrase "without intent" (本无意) captures the essence of artistic freedom. If "the travelers below" insist on reading political attack into it, that is the travelers' problem, not the gibbon's or the crane's.

Su Dongpo was naive, free, almost childlike, expressing all his deepest feelings without reserve — and ultimately these laments and angry poems attacking ruling authority would bring upon him great calamity.

Lin Yutang precisely identifies the contradiction: Su Dongpo faced an acute ethical dilemma throughout his life — "on one hand wanting to remain true to himself, preserving humanity's innate fearless spirit; on the other, wanting to protect himself." But usually, he chose the heroic path. Amid West Lake's poetic beauty and Mizhou's administrative achievements, Dongpo chose to "spit it out" — preferring to be the freely crying gibbon, the calling crane, over silence bought at the price of safety.

6
Farewell Night

All-Night Political Debate on the Ying River

Ziyou decided to accompany his brother seventy li downstream to Yingzhou (Fuyang). The two brothers rowed on the Ying River through the night, debating politics and exchanging poems. On the eve of his departure, Dongpo wrote: "The journeying sail catches the west wind; farewell tears drip into the clear Ying." For six years thereafter, he would never again spend the Mid-Autumn Festival with his brother.

That Mid-Autumn and that night on the Ying River became among the most unforgettable moments of Su Dongpo's life. What did the brothers discuss on the boat? Mencius. Together they recalled Mencius's teaching: "To hold the ruler to difficult standards is called reverence; to present goodness and block evil is called respect. To say 'my ruler is incapable of this' — that is to do him injury." In modern terms: to point out difficulties to those in power is respect; to state the right path and block the wrong one is duty; to assume the ruler cannot do better and give up remonstrating — that is betrayal.

Even more profound was another passage of Mencius they both understood: "Incomplete city walls and insufficient weapons are not a state's disaster; uncultivated fields and uncollected wealth are not a state's calamity. When the above lacks propriety and the below lacks learning, when lawless people arise — then destruction is not far off." Read against the backdrop of Wang Anshi's reforms, every word resonates as prophecy.

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

The Hangzhou and Mizhou period was a pivotal turning point in Su Dongpo's life. He left the capital bearing the wounds of political storms, yet found warmth in brotherly love (seventy days of reunion in Chenzhou), strength in Mencius's "flood-like qi" ("vast and unyielding"), and poetic inspiration in West Lake's beauty (his most prolific creative period). Ziyou counseled silence — placing a hand over his mouth — but Dongpo could not comply. As he himself wrote: "The gibbon cries, the crane calls, without intent — unaware of travelers on the path below." His expression was like nature's own sounds, instinctive and free. This flood-like qi — preferring death to silence — would later produce both his greatest poetry and his greatest danger. But Su Dongpo chose to be himself: heroically, consistently, to the very end.
