Reminiscences of a Stock Operator · Ch. 20 "Short Selling & Corners — Famous Market Manipulation Cases in History"

Livermore systematically traces the history of market manipulation: from old-school wash sales and matched orders to modern corners and monopolies — tactics evolve with regulations, but greed and fear remain the manipulator's ultimate weapon for harvesting the public.

▎S — Situation
Manipulation is a common stock-market merchandising process. From Daniel Drew to Jay Gould, from Erie Railroad to Sugar Trust, Wall Street has never lacked manipulation legends. Old-school wash sales, matched orders, and corners each had their own techniques and ecosystems.
▎C — Conflict
The tricks and devices of the old days — wash sales, matched orders — have become obsolete or illegal. Today's exchange rules have been thoroughly transformed, and tax laws have made fictitious transactions prohibitively expensive. But the human nature that makes "suckers always look for a free lunch" has never changed — Ponzi schemes and bucket shops remain impossible to stamp out.
▎Q — Key Question
What should modern traders learn from historic manipulation cases?
▎A — Answer
Abandon the fantasy of replicating specific techniques. Instead, study the unchanging human nature — greed, fear, and credulity — these are the weaknesses manipulators will always exploit. As Woodlock famously said: "In the future, people will continue to repeat the mistakes they have made in the past." Studying human nature is ten thousand times more valuable than studying techniques.
01
Historical Review

The Old-School Trinity: Wash Sales, Matched Orders & Corners

Livermore systematically dissects three classic manipulation techniques that have become obsolete:

Wash Sales: Simultaneously placing buy and sell orders to trade with oneself and create the illusion of active trading. Crude in execution, easy for the NYSE to detect, and difficult to coordinate across different brokers. One famous operator once forgot to cancel a sell order, causing an unwitting broker to push the price up 25 points in minutes — a wash sale turned into a farce.

Matched Orders: Essentially the same as wash sales: the goal is to manufacture trading volume to attract followers. But sweeping tax-code revisions have made all techniques involving fictitious transactions too costly to be viable.

Corner/Monopoly: The Northern Pacific corner of May 9, 1901 is the most famous corner in history — but Livermore specifically notes it was not intentional manipulation, but the result of competitive buying spiraling out of control. The Stutz Motor corner cost all parties dearly. In corner events, "the originator rarely profits from them."

02
Core Principle

Unchanging Human Nature: The Manipulator's Eternal Secret Weapon

Livermore uses a West Point analogy to hit the nail on the head: today's market operators need not study what the ancients did, just as West Point cadets need not study ancient archery to improve their ballistics. Weapons change, but the principles of warfare do not.

Human beings seek comfort and avoid difficulty, preferring to believe what makes them feel at ease. In prosperous times the public rushes into the market — "at such times no attention whatsoever is paid to details" — and ultimately must pay the price for the privilege of pursuing easy money. Ponzi schemes and bucket shops succeed time after time not because their techniques are brilliant, but because the masses forever yearn for a free lunch.

03
Practical Takeaway

Study Human Nature, Not Techniques: The Modern Trader's Defense System

Livermore offers three core insights to help modern traders learn what is truly valuable from history:

First, distinguish means from principles. Specific techniques (wash sales, matched orders) belong to their era and may become obsolete or banned at any time. But principles — exploiting human greed and fear — never expire. Study history for its principles, not to replicate its techniques.

Second, recognize today's "sucker traps." Though old techniques have disappeared, new packaging emerges endlessly. When an investment opportunity makes you feel "particularly comfortable" or that "no thinking is required," ask yourself: is this real, or is it a comfort zone custom-tailored for me?

Third, prosperity periods are the most dangerous. The public enters the market at the height of a boom, "paying no attention whatsoever to details" — this is precisely when the greatest losses occur. Truly safe investments come from rational analysis, not the blind confidence bred by prosperity.

⚠️
Core Warning: "People who seek easy money will ultimately have to pay the price for pursuing this privilege — countless facts decisively prove that such a privilege simply cannot be found in this noisy world." — Livermore's words remain the most precise prophecy in the financial world to this day.

Manipulation techniques may become obsolete, but human nature never does. Abandon the fantasy of replicating century-old techniques and instead learn to identify today's "sucker traps."
