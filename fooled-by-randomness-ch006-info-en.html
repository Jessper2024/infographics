In financial markets, judgments of "bullish" and "bearish" are everywhere. But Taleb shows us: high probability doesn't mean high profit. He publicly declared a 70% chance the market would rise slightly — while heavily shorting it, because the 30% chance of a large drop mattered far more. Probability multiplied by payoff equals expected value, yet most MBAs learned statistics in symmetric environments (coin flips) and can't grasp this asymmetry. Starting from Gould's cancer diagnosis, this chapter reveals how skewness upends our intuitions about risk, prediction, and rare events.
01
Skewness
Gould's Cancer Lesson: Median ≠ Mean
Writer and scientist Stephen Jay Gould was diagnosed with a fatal stomach cancer and told the median survival time was about 8 months. Upon further research, he discovered: about 50% of patients die before 8 months, but those who survive beyond 8 months live much longer — roughly to the normal life expectancy of 73 years. This is asymmetry — the mean and median are radically different. Gould wrote "The Median Isn't the Message," arguing that medical research's reliance on the median obscures the true probability distribution.

Those who survive past 8 months

Core Principle
Probability ≠ Expected Value: Frequency Must Be Multiplied by Magnitude
Consider a bet: 999 out of 1000 times you win $1 (Event A); 1 out of 1000 times you lose $10,000 (Event B). As the table shows — the frequency of winning is 99.9%, but magnitude is everything. Event A is far more probable, yet betting on A is a terrible idea. MBAs don't grasp this because their education takes place in symmetric environments (coin flips), where probability and expected value happen to coincide.

03
Conceptual Critique
Bull and Bear Are Empty Labels
At a Wall Street weekly meeting, Taleb said "the market has a 70% probability of rising slightly." A colleague asked: "Then why are you heavily short S&P 500 futures?" His reply: "I haven't changed my mind — the probability of rising is high, but if it falls, it may fall a lot. I'm bullish on probability, bearish on outcome." The room was baffled — then a few traders got it. He was never invited back. Bull/bear dichotomies belong to TV commentators who bear no risk.

04
Rare Events
Bet Big on One Side: The Crisis Hunter's Asymmetric Strategy
Taleb's trading philosophy is to "make money as infrequently as possible" — lose small often, win huge rarely. Rare events are systematically undervalued in markets because people fixate on narrow historical time series. Legendary investor Jim Rogers said "90% of options lose money, so don't buy options" — ignoring that the remaining 10% might return 50×. The 1987 crash, a rare event, made Taleb a trader. Core strategy: place asymmetric bets and profit from rare events.

05
Statistical Traps
Why Econometrics Is Unreliable
Statistics' core insight: n× more samples → only √n× more knowledge. Worse still is the stationarity problem — if the urn has a hole and a mischievous kid secretly swaps balls, no amount of sampling yields reliable inference. Markets are the same: structures change, and past time series may point nowhere. The Lucas Critique: if rational traders discover a pattern, they'll trade it away until it vanishes. Most of econometrics may be "utterly useless."

⚠️ Pesaran's circular reasoning: When asked whether econometrics can predict the future, he replied — "study more and better econometrics." It's like saying: if you doubt astrology, just study more astrology.
06
History Repeats
The Peso Problem: Killers Always Lurk Beneath Calm Waters
Mexican peso stable for years → banks and funds pile in → "unexpectedly" blow up → new unsuspecting entrants rush in again → the story repeats. In 1998, Taleb spent two hours explaining the peso problem to a major hedge fund manager. The reply: "You're absolutely right. We don't touch pesos — we only invest in Russian rubles." A few months later, the ruble collapsed by 97%. Rare events are always unexpected — otherwise they wouldn't be rare.

💡 Core Takeaway: In an asymmetric world, frequency is irrelevant — magnitude is everything. Probability is for commentators; expected value is what investors care about. Rare events are perpetually undervalued — not because they can't happen, but because people interpret risk through too narrow a time series. History proves again and again: things that never happened before eventually do happen. The broader the perspective, the more lessons we learn.
