"It Is Easier to Buy and Sell Than to Fry an Egg"

By Nassim Nicholas Taleb · Performance Records, Survivorship Bias & The Deceptions of Randomness

📖 Chapter Overview

This chapter reveals a profound paradox: buying and selling securities seems easier than frying an egg — anyone can open an account, start trading, and proudly present a "track record." Yet Taleb, through Monte Carlo simulations, survivorship bias, and data-mining traps, demonstrates that these "performance records" are very likely pure luck. From fund managers to medical research, from book blurbs to cancer cures, randomness wears the mask of "skill" and fools anyone unwilling to dig deeper.

09
Core Paradox

Trading ≠ Egg-Frying: The Fundamental Difference Between Two Kinds of Competence

A dentist who cures your toothache, a pianist who performs at Carnegie Hall, a Canova marble sculpture of breathtaking precision — in these domains, success is almost impossible to attribute to luck. But in financial markets, when a fund manager shows you a "stellar track record," it is very likely the product of random fluctuation. Machiavelli held that luck accounts for at least 50% of life's outcomes — and that was before modern markets even existed.

🧑‍⚕️ Physical-Skill Domains
Dentists, pianists, sculptors — repeated verification makes "getting lucky" astronomically improbable. Skill can be observed and reproduced.
📈 Financial Market Domains
Thousands participate; random fluctuation naturally produces "stars." A profit record alone conveys virtually no reliable information.

Monte Carlo Proof

10,000 Managers: Pure Luck Manufactures "Stars"

Start with 10,000 investment managers. Each year, each has a 50% chance of earning $10,000 and a 50% chance of losing $10,000. Anyone who loses in a given year is eliminated. After five years, purely by luck, 313 managers remain with perfect records — they will be celebrated as "stars," dressed in designer suits, granted illustrious titles.

Even more striking: if all managers have negative expected returns (45% win / 55% loss), after five years 184 managers (under 2%) still emerge as "stars." Market volatility actually helps bad decisions — this is the dark side of ergodicity: in the short run, randomness creates heroes, but time ultimately eliminates randomness.

Survivorship Bias

The Forgotten 98%: Why You Only See the Winners

People always see only the best-performing managers, never the full population. The essence of survivorship bias: the number of "stars" depends primarily on how many people entered the profession, not on their actual ability. If more managers entered in 1997 than in 1993, then 2002 will naturally have more "top managers" than 1998.

Taleb's personal rule: if the original population is only 10 managers, he would entrust half his savings to the top performer. If there are 10,000, he ignores all track records entirely. Without knowing how many tried and failed, no performance record can be evaluated.

Data miners test trading rules against historical prices endlessly — like monkeys at typewriters. A random series will always exhibit some detectable pattern. Taleb is certain: somewhere in the Western world there is a security whose price movements are 100% correlated with temperature changes in Ulaanbaatar, Mongolia.

09
Real-World Fallout

From Book Blurbs to Cancer: Randomness Fools Every Domain

Survivorship bias is everywhere. Book covers feature only the best blurb; backtesting software keeps traders up all night hunting for the "perfect rule"; "spontaneous remission" of cancer is attributed to vitamins or holy water; companies with three consecutive years of earnings growth get "strong buy" recommendations from analysts — they may simply be the 1,250 survivors among an initial 10,000.
