Cultural Bitter Journey · Ch.20 "The Peephole at the Xie Family Home"

The life journey of director Xie Jin—how art, suffering, family, and humanity achieved a magnificent convergence in one man

CORE PROPOSITION

Xie Jin's life is the most profound human interpretation of the 'cultural bitter journey'—the suffering of creation connecting into a life's journey: this is culture's destiny. Xie Jin only 'matured' at sixty, capturing humanity in the cracks of political discourse, and ultimately interrogating the era by the standard of humanity. With three disabled children at home, he transformed a disordered mental vortex into a shrine of humanism. His art, family, friendship, and death together compose the most complete portrait of a cultural creator's life.

01
Image

The Peephole: An Eternal Wait

Xie Jin's intellectually disabled son, A-San, spent every day pressed against the peephole waiting for his father—his eyebrows worn away from rubbing. This is the metaphor of a cultural creator.

Xie Jin adding glimmers of humanity to films in a closed era was just like A-San finding a hole in a closed door and fixing his gaze there relentlessly. 'Your peephole became the peephole of the entire nation's audience.' Two of Xie Jin's three sons were severely disabled. A-San never knew what death was—he was simply forever waiting at the peephole.

“
Director Xie, you ARE A-San! Like your son, you found a hole in a closed door and fixed your gaze there, watching for light, waiting for human connection.
—— Yu Qiuyu to Xie Jin

After Xie Jin died, only A-Si and his 83-year-old mother remained. A-Si's decades-long task was handing his father his bag and shoes. When white flowers covered the shoes, he bent down, retrieved the slippers, and carefully placed them by the door.

02
Art

Maturing at Sixty: Xie Jin's Filmmaking Journey

At sixty, Xie Jin said: 'Now, I've finally matured along with the entire nation!'

His artistic journey divides into an exploratory period and a mature period. During the exploratory period, Stage Sisters moved textile factory women workers to tears—the verdict on 'humanism' vs 'class struggle' was decided by those tears. In his mature period, Hibiscus Town and other works turned the tables, interrogating the era by the standard of humanity.

Xie Jin wasn't a pretty scene scattered on the shore—he was a complete river, allowing everything along the way to find its place relative to him. He was a fiber optic cable of color for colorless historical processes.

03
Hunger

The Hunger to Create: More Than a Brand

Xie Jin's deepest pain in old age was being treated as a 'brand' to be commercialized—a creator's demand for the dignity of self-completion.

Some advised him to retire and become a 'film mentor.' But he was simply hungry, endlessly hungry. He wanted to remake The Peach Blossom Fan, to film the Chinese workers on the Transcontinental Railroad, to make The Diaries of John Rabe. When an entrepreneur called his name 'something to be developed,' he thought: 'Is this all I'm reduced to?'

“
You all say I'm too old and should step back, but I told you long ago—I only matured at sixty. So do the math...
—— Xie Jin

He increasingly performed vitality—speaking louder, challenging everyone to drinking contests. But one day he stumbled on a train platform, face ashen. The age wrapped inside the leather jacket and red scarf could no longer hide.

04
Family

A Shrine of Humanism: Family Suffering and Greatness

Xie Jin transformed a disordered mental vortex into a shrine of humanism—a domestic world unimaginable to ordinary people.

Of his four children with wife Xu Dawen, only Xie Yan was mentally normal. The family was poor and he was impossibly busy. But he never hid it—before Hollywood stars and French directors, he would ceremoniously present his disabled sons with eyes full of appreciation.

⚠️
Fate's Cruelty: The family's only normal child—Xie Yan, an elegant gentleman returned from overseas study—died before his father in 2008. He hid his illness, arranged everything, put on clean clothes, and went to the hospital, never to emerge. Xie Jin stood before the hospital bed like a suddenly desiccated statue.

The day after Xie Yan's burial, Xie Jin embraced a friend who had also lost a son, and wailed—a cry 'like a tiger's roar, a wolf's howl, a dragon's cry, a lion's roar.' That day, even West Lake sobbed.

05
Legacy

The Xie Clan of Dongshan: 1,600 Years of Spirit

Xie Jin's distant ancestor was Xie An, the Eastern Jin prime minister who won the Battle of Feishui. Xie Lingyun, father of Chinese landscape poetry, was Xie An's grandnephew.

Xie An was originally a recluse on Dongshan, drinking and composing poetry with the great calligrapher Wang Xizhi. Forced back into office, he gave China the idiom 'Dongshan Re-Rise' (comeback). Xie Jin cared deeply about this lineage but never spoke of it publicly—what he cherished was the mountain, the village, the house, the name, the spirit.

“
With this inexpressible deep belonging, he would return alone, to draw the final period of his life before that great assembly of ancestors.
—— Yu Qiuyu

On October 18, 2008, Xie Jin returned to his hometown Chunhui Middle School. That night he ate a cold supper, drank no wine, and lay down to sleep—on his left side, never to wake. He was one month and three days short of his 85th birthday.

💡
KEY TAKEAWAY

Xie Jin's life portrait is the most ultimate human interpretation of the 'cultural bitter journey': his art only truly matured at sixty, his family was tested by the fate of three disabled children, his creative hunger remained unsatisfied until death, and his passing in his ancestral Dongshan completed a final homecoming. Xie Jin proved with his life that the true cultural creator is one who builds a shrine of humanism from suffering.
