Fooled by Randomness · Chapter 4
"Randomness and the Scientific Intellectual"

When nonsense wears a lab coat — why humanists keep falling for randomness

In the 1990s, a group of physicists launched the "Science Wars" in Vienna: they insisted that every statement belongs to one of only two categories — deductive (like 2+2=4) or inductive (empirically verifiable). Everything else is worthless dregs. The deeper question: can humanists tell the difference between genuine scientific thinking and eloquent nonsense dressed in jargon? Taleb answers with Monte Carlo generators, Dada engines, and corporate buzzword scramblers — with results both hilarious and sobering.
01
Science Wars
The Vienna Circle: Beyond Deduction & Induction, All Is Dross
The Vienna Circle (Wittgenstein, Popper, Carnap) brought rigor to intellectual life. Their rule: a statement must be either deductively provable from axioms, or empirically verifiable through experience or statistics. Hegelian philosophy — garbage by scientific standards, inferior to music by aesthetic ones. This standard continues to shape philosophy and scientific practice.
🔬

Scientific Intellectuals

Can recognize others' original work; express rigorous reasoning in plain prose — as Dawkins' The Selfish Gene reads like it was translated from mathematics, yet contains not a single equation.

📚

Humanists

Cannot distinguish scientists from eloquent non-scientists; casually invoke "uncertainty principle," "Gödel's theorem," "relativity" — often out of context or with the opposite meaning. A list of scientific references at the end of a paper is enough to fool them.

02
Randomness Test
The Monte Carlo Generator: If a Computer Can Mimic You, You Lack Intelligence
A Monte Carlo generator can produce convincing humanities prose. But you can never write a scientific work at random. Words can be randomly assembled, but genuine scientific knowledge cannot be faked. This is the Turing test in reverse — the ultimate way to separate the bloviators from the real thinkers.

03
Postmodern Hoax
The Dada Engine: Grammatically Flawless, Intellectually Vacuous
The Dada Engine at Monash University uses recursive grammar to randomly assemble grammatically perfect but utterly meaningless sentences — indistinguishable from Derrida or Paglia. Sokal pulled off the ultimate hoax, publishing pure nonsense in a prestigious journal. Taleb laughed so hard on the plane reading it that fellow passengers stared.
"However, the works of Rushdie are not theoretical as the dialectic paradigm of reality would have it, but pre-theoretical. The premise of the new semantic paradigm of narrative ironically suggests that gender identity is significant."
"Thus, the premise of the new dialectic paradigm of expression suggests that if reality and consciousness are distinct, consciousness may be used to reinforce hierarchy; otherwise, we can assume that language has intrinsic meaning."
— Dada Engine output · Monash University

05
Aesthetic Reconciliation
Poetry, Religion & Randomness: We Don't Need to Be Rational Everywhere
Taleb admits he enjoys being fooled by randomness when it comes to art and poetry. After the war, surrealist poets gathered in cafés to play the "exquisite corpse" — each writing a word without seeing the others', letting chance create startlingly beautiful phrases. Religion's power lies in its aesthetic force, not scientific verification. The place for rationality is the market, not poetry or faith.
